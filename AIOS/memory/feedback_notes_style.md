Write substantive, encyclopedic Notes sections on word pages. Go well beyond a one-line gloss. Include:
- Etymology and origin of the term
- Historical or cultural context (dynasties, traditions, texts referenced)
- Cross-linguistic variation where the same concept differs meaningfully across Chinese, Japanese, Korean, Vietnamese traditions — semantic drift, narrowing, homophone collisions, native coinages, etc. are especially worth highlighting
- Numbered lists when the word refers to a canonical set (Nine Ministers, Nine Classics, the Six Hollow Organs, etc.)
- Cross-links to related words in the vault ([[九天]], etc.)

**Why:** User explicitly praised this style ("encyclopedic notes are exactly right") on 2026-05-19 after the 九 word batch.

**How to apply:** Any time a word page is created or edited, write the Notes section with this depth — aim for at least 2–3 substantial paragraphs (or a numbered list for canonical sets), not a "few words" gloss. Good examples discovered worth modeling on: 寵物 (semantic drift: "pet" in CN vs "favored concubine" in JP), 喘息 (semantic narrowing to medical "asthma" in JP/KR vs general "panting" in CN), 教唆/교사 (a striking homophone collision with 教師 "teacher" in Korean — opposite moral connotations from the same sound), 玉葱 (a purely Japanese coinage; other languages use unrelated words), 潮汐 (an old morning/evening tide distinction fossilized into a single modern word for "tide" generally).
