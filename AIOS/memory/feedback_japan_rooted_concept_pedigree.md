A word/concept that originated in Japan (a cultural practice, art form, place-derived name, etc.) is not automatically Japanese-only. Check whether Mandarin, Cantonese, Korean, or Vietnamese dictionaries (Wiktionary etc.) show the *same characters* read with that language's own native pronunciation and a matching definition — even if the definition is literally "this is the Japanese [X]." That counts as genuine pedigree, distinct from a language merely transliterating the Japanese *sound* phonetically (e.g. Korean 유카타 for "yukata" written in hangul, vs. Mandarin 浴衣 read as yùyī).

**Why:** I initially recommended skipping [[猿楽]] (sarugaku, a Japanese theater form) as Japan-only, since its Chinese-origin predecessor was a different word (散楽) and the renaming to 猿楽 happened entirely within Japan. 武明帥 pushed back: "the Chinese definition of 猿樂 is sarugaku... it's a Japanese concept, but an international word." Checking Wiktionary confirmed Mandarin yuányuè is a real character-reading (not a phonetic loan) with the dictionary definition "sarugaku" — i.e. Chinese readers can and do read these hanzi natively to refer to the Japanese concept. This is the same mechanism already used to justify [[浴衣]] (yùyī/juk6 ji1 for yukata) earlier in the same sweep, but I failed to generalize it on the very next analogous case.

**How to apply:** Whenever a candidate word names a Japan-specific referent (food, clothing, art form, place-derived plant/animal name, etc.) and initial research suggests "Japan-only," do one more check before recommending skip: look up the Chinese/Korean/Vietnamese Wiktionary entry for the exact hanzi/hanja string itself. If another language shows a native phonetic reading (pinyin/jyutping/Sino-Korean/Sino-Vietnamese) — not just a katakana-style transliteration of the Japanese sound — treat that as genuine pedigree and recommend create. Contrast with 伊吹 (skipped) where Chinese uses a completely different name (圆柏) for the same referent, with no reading of 伊吹 itself at all — that case is still a legitimate skip.
