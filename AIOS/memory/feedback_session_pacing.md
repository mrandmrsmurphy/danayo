When given a standing directive to work through a long backlog autonomously (e.g. "create everything you can on the broken links output"), pause at natural checkpoint boundaries rather than grinding through the whole list in one unbroken pass.

**Why:** User said "stop when you reach the end of a set" and, separately, "stop after 31-40" mid-session — both signal a preference for periodic check-ins over a single long autonomous run, even when the original directive sounded open-ended ("go to town").

**How to apply:**
- A "set" = a natural grouping the work already produces — most often a syllable page whose entries share a 注音 and get resolved together. Finishing the syllable (perfecting it, setting `date-last-perfect`) is a clean stopping point.
- When such an instruction arrives mid-task, finish the *current* unit of work (the word/character/syllable in progress) rather than stopping abruptly mid-edit, then summarize progress and stop.
- Update the relevant `project_*.md` memory with a clear "stopping point" note (what's done, what's left, where to resume) so the next session can pick up cleanly without re-deriving context.
- Don't wait to be told to resume — but also don't assume "go to town" means "don't stop until literally everything is done."
