> The guiding maxim for Dan’a’yo orthography is “Shōwa determines the look”. This recalls the orthographic reforms carried out in Japan during the Shōwa era, which produced the system of **Shinjitai** “new character forms”）. These simplified characters balanced readability with continuity, standing between Traditional Chinese characters and the far-reaching simplifications of Modern Chinese.
> 
> By adopting Japanese-style simplifications, Dan’a’yo establishes a consistent visual standard that is shared and accessible across the CJKV region.
>
> The motto emphasizes two principles:
> 1. **Japanese orthography as the compromise form** — midway between traditional and simplified.    
> 2. **Visual uniformity across the script** — so that texts are recognizable to all readers.    
>
>Thus **Showa determines the look** stands as a Classical reminder that the authority for the _look_ of Dan’a’yo is derived from the Shōwa reforms.

# Orthography
Dan’a’yo is written with Shinjitai Chinese characters.  Above each character appears a phonetic transcription in Bopomofo letters , set in the style of ruby glosses.  The pronunciation of each character is derived in regular fashion from Middle Chinese.  This derivation aims to stand at equal distance from the modern pronunciations, yet it shows a marked bias toward Middle Korean.  In principle, the character form and the alphabetic form hold equal weight.  Yet, within restrictive media, one form may prevail over the other.

## Rubrics
- **One Character, One Sound**
  Every Chinese character has one, and only one, pronunciation.

- **Per Character, Bright Meaning**
  Every character has a single meaning. Metaphorical derivations are permitted. Distinction between new meaning and extended metaphor allows natural word formation. This accords with the historical tradition of semantic derivation in the region.

- **Simple Character, Familiar Speech**
  Some characters are words when standing alone. Others require companions, forming compounds. In vocabulary terms, there are single-character words and double-character words.

- **Four-Character Phrases**
  Certain fixed expressions are set in four characters. They form a shared idiom across East Asia. Each country also developed innumerable unique phrases, which are not a shared heritage.

These rubrics direct the writing system toward clarity by multiplying characters.  At the same time, Dan’a’yo follows a contrary impulse: to reduce the number of characters as far as possible.  The higher aim of the language is to maintain the minimum set of characters that still ensures clarity.  In practice, this requires a core of about 2,000 characters.  Each core character may gather its variants and archaic forms under one head.  This follows the same practice as Japanese substitute characters and Chinese simplified characters.

## Levels of Characters
More than fifty thousand Chinese characters have been recorded in history.  
In Dan’a’yo  these are divided into six categories:  common characters, advanced characters, naming characters, forbidden characters, variant characters, "loan" characters, and unclassified characters.  Characters that fall outside national standards and official name lists are judged too rare to categorize.  By this restriction, the practical inventory of Dan’a’yo stands at slightly under four thousand characters.

### Common Characters
**Daily Use, Always Needed**

About two thousand characters are classed as common characters.  They form the basic building blocks of the language.  They are usable everywhere without explanation or limitation. This body of characters defines the volume necessary for fluency in Dan’a’yo.  It is subdivided into six graded sets called educational characters.  These graded sets facilitate learning by newcomers.  Outside of textbooks and children’s literature, the six-fold divisions are not recognized.

### Advanced Characters
Advanced characters are drawn from literary sources and difficult words beyond the two thousand common characters.  In ordinary Dan’a’yo texts, such characters must be introduced and defined.  This requirement may be set aside when the audience is not general. 

### Naming Characters
Naming characters are used in technical names of animals, places, persons, anatomical parts, and related categories.  They are not used in ordinary discourse except when referring to those exact things.  When first introduced in a text, a naming character should be followed by a determiner. A character may be designated as naming but also appear in advanced words.  In such cases, its classification shifts from one designation to the other.

### Forbidden Characters 
Forbidden characters are expressly disallowed in Dan’a’yo.   They may belong exclusively to one nation.  They may also stand in direct competition with a common character. This category, though unfortunate, is necessary for the simplicity of the language.  Every forbidden character in another country has a direct substitute within Dan’a’yo.  A complete list is given in [[文法 - 98違法字]].

### Variant Characters
**Every character has only one acceptable form**

Nearly every Chinese character has been written in multiple ways across nations and across time.  Such forms are called variant characters.  Ideally, an input system would recognize a variant when encountered, and replace it with the standardized glyph.

This system sometimes produces striking results.  Obscure characters that are homophones or near-homophones of common characters may be reclassified as variants.  This removes the statistical “long tail” of ultra-rare characters.

For example, the phrase **魑魅魍魎** is visually intimidating.  Yet the last two characters – 魍 and 魎 – have almost no other usage.  By fiat, Dan’a’yo declares 魍 a variant of 罔, and 魎 a variant of 両.  These two are in fact components of the originals.  Thus the idiom therefore appears as **魑魅罔両**, equally expressive but more economical.

### Unclassified Characters
Unclassified characters lie outside all lists.  They are too rare to be assigned a category.  They appear only in dictionaries or historical records.

For practical purposes, Dan’a’yo does not admit them into the active inventory.  They are studied for reference but not employed in writing.

## Historical Derivation
The pronunciation system of Dan’a’yo is based on Middle Chinese.  The Zhengzhang Shangfang rime table serves as the primary reference.  Consensus reconstructions are followed in general.

Rules-of-thumb table for initials and finals is given in Appendix B. From these rules, each character receives a systematic pronunciation.  When the resulting syllable would be overfull, adjustments are made.  For example, the character 殆 would yield /dai/, but Dan’a’yo follows the Cantonese form _doi_ to avoid excess homophony.

Korean influence also guided certain choices.  Many historical /si/ syllables became /sa/ in Dan’a’yo, reflecting Middle Korean shifts.

Occasionally, rare characters were selected solely for their sound value.  For example, 耽 is used to supply ㄉㄛㄇ.  In some cases, inspiration came from unusual Vietnamese readings.  Thus 𦚖 provides the syllable ㄇㄛㄆ, otherwise unattested.

Through such measures, Dan’a’yo establishes a balanced and functional sound system.

## Shinjitai
Over the last century, many reforms of Chinese characters have been proposed and implemented.  Mainland China adopted the most radical simplifications, and a further scheme was rescinded in 1986.  Because many such simplifications depend on Mandarin pronunciation, they are not usable across nations.  Some countries retained traditional forms unchanged.  The Japanese system of **Shinjitai** offers a moderate middle path suitable for Dan’a’yo.

### Exceptions
- **龍 / 竜**  
    Dan’a’yo writes **龍** in all contexts.  
    The simplified **竜** is avoided for cross-regional clarity.    
- **圓 / 円**  
    The semantic term “circle” uses **圓**.  
    The Shinjitai **円** is closely associated with the Japanese currency and is not used for the geometric sense.    
- **弗**  
    Dan’a’yo regularizes the 弗 component: write **佛** not 仏, and **拂** not 払.    
- **桌/卓**      
	- These are distinguished to preserve the rule "One character one meaning".    
- **干 / 乾** 
	- **干** “desiccate; interfere” and **乾** “heavenly; Qian-trigram” remain distinct.
- **果 / 菓**  
    These are **conflated** as in Chinese usage; **菓** is treated as a variant of **果**.
- **貳 / 弍**  
    Standard form is **貳**.  
    **弍** is avoided due to proximity to **弌／弎**.
- **缺 / 欠**  
    These differ in both sound and meaning; they remain distinct.

### Extended Shinjitai
Shinjitai in practice is not fully consistent.  Dan’a’yo therefore applies **extended Shinjitai** where possible, seeking **component-wise consistency** without loss of meaning.

Principles:
1. **Component parity**: when a component is simplified in one common character, comparable occurrences are simplified in parallel.    
2. **Semantic stability**: do not create homographs that violate "one meaning per characer" or compete with a common character.    
3. **Cross-regional legibility**: avoid forms that impede understanding outside Japan.    

Illustrative contrasts: if **區 → 区** and **圖 → 図** are adopted, Dan’a’yo extends comparable component reductions where they do not endanger distinctness.  Conversely, extreme or purely graphic leaps that would break meaning or produce confusion are rejected.  In short, when simplification can be made **consistent, legible, and semantically safe**, Dan’a’yo adopts it; when any of these three are threatened, the traditional form is retained.

## Ruby
In Dan’a’yo, each character carries a ruby gloss above it.  The ruby is written bopomofo.  This shows the intended pronunciation directly.

### Cardinal Vowels
Ruby employs cardinal vowels.  Thus the glyph ㄝ represents /e/, unlike in Chinese usage.

### Y Off-Glide
A dot above marks the _y_ on or off-glide.  

### W Off-Glide 
Dan’a’yo uses the ˇ as the /w/ on or off-glide.

- ㄔ is the onset 'ch'
- ㄕ is the onset 'sh'
- ㄖ is the onset 'r'
- ㄗ is the onset 'z'
