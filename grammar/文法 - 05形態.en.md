> [!tip]
> Morphology in Dan’a’yo consists of **prefixes, suffixes, postpositions, and reduplication** that alter the form or function of content words. These processes do not create new lexical roots but instead **mark grammatical roles, derive new syntactic functions, or adjust aspect and degree**. Unlike function words, which remain independent, morphological elements are **bound forms**: they attach to a host word or phrase.
>
> The guiding maxim for Dan'a'yo morphology is **“Form helps reveal structure”**. This motto highlights how bound forms — prefixes, suffixes, and postpositions — function as **helpers ** that make the underlying **principle** of the sentence visible.
> 
> Two principles flow from this maxim:
> 1. **"Aid through attachment"** — small forms bind to content words to indicate case, aspect, modality, or relation, ensuring clarity across the sentence.    
> 2. **"Revelation of structure"** — what is implicit in Classical Chinese or Vietnamese becomes explicit, while Japanese and Korean intuitions about particles are carried into a new SVO framework.    
>
>Thus **Form Reveals Structure** expresses the balance that defines Dan'a'yo morphology: seemingly minor affixes and particles act as **guides to structure**, ensuring that the language’s classical roots and modern functions remain transparent to all learners.

## Nominal Morphology

### 等 — Associative Plural 
Most Asian languages do not indicate **grammatical number** . The suffix **等** is not a simple plural marker, indicating merely “more than one.” Instead, it functions as an **associative plural**, showing that the main object named by the noun phrase  is expanded to include others associated with it.

This usage is comparable to English expressions such as _et al._ or _etc._, rather than the ordinary plural ending _-(e)s_.

In Dan’a’yo, there is not a separate class of **personal pronouns**, but two words take **等** in a special way.

- **我** (“I, me”) + **等** → **我等** = “we”     
- **君** (“you (singular)”) + **等** → **君等** = “you (plural)”     

These are the only true plural words in Dan’a’yo, apart from **mass nouns**  which are inherently plural.

### 公 — Politeness Marker

The suffix **公** attaches directly to **noun phrases**  and adds a sense of **austerity and politeness**. Dan’a’yo does not preserve the complex politeness systems of Japanese or Korean. In general:

- **Shorter words and sentences** sound intimate and familiar.    
- **Longer forms** sound more polite and distant.    

Certain titles require added formality, and **公** fulfills this role.

As in all cultures, **formality stands in tension with intimacy**. Once a close relationship has been established, a return to politeness is rude and distant.

>[!example]
> At the start of a school year, students would naturally call their teacher  **先生公**, since they do not yet have a personal bond. Later, when speaking of their own teacher among friends, they would simply say **先生** — distinguishing “our teacher” from others, without the added formality.

### Restrictive Suffixes 

Dan’a’yo has three **restrictive suffixes**:

- **丈** “only” 
- **且** “too / also”     
- **就** “just / merely”    

These suffixes attach directly to **noun phrases**. Importantly, they do **not transform the NP into an adverb**. The phrase remains a subject or topic of the sentence, while carrying the restrictive meaning.

>[!info]
> 1. **丈 “only”**
> - 連傑丈 市場於 購買。  
 >   _“Only Jet buys at the market.”_    
> - 我之描丈 寺院於 睡眠。  
>    _“Only my cat sleeps at the temple.”_
> 2. **且 “too, also”**
> - 医生且 市場於 販売。  
>    _“The doctor also sells (things) at the market.”_    
> - 我之描且 木曜日於 嬉遊。  
>    _“My cat also plays on Thursday.”_	
> 3. **就 “just, merely”**
> - 連傑就 寺院於 閲読。  
>    _“Jet just reads at the temple.”_    
>- 医生就 市場於 食。  
>    _“The doctor simply eats at the market.”_

### Nominalization
**Verbal phrases** in Dan’a’yo may be transformed into **noun phrases** through two nominalizing suffixes:
- **–物** “object, result”
- **–事** “event, act”

#### –物 （object/result）
The suffix **–物** marks the **object or product** of an action.
- Example: **食物** “that which is eaten, food, a meal.”    
- Example: **創造物** “that which is created, a creation.”   

#### –事 （event/action）
The suffix **–事** marks the **action itself** as an abstract event.
- Example: **食事** “the act of eating, a meal.”
- Example: **学習事** “the act of studying, study.”   

>[!Warning]
> - **Eventive** may take either **–物** or **–事**, since they can denote both results and actions.    
>- **Statives** may only take **–事**, since they do not govern objects.

>[!info]
> Examples:
>- **絵画物** = “a drawing” （the result of 絵画）    
>- **絵画事** = “the act of drawing”    
>- **大事** = “greatness, importance” （from 性詞 大）

## Verbal Morphology 

### TAM Affixes 

Dan’a’yo verbal morphology expresses **tense, aspect, and mood** through a small set of **affixes**. The system is relative: when no affix is present, the time reference remains underspecified.

#### Suffixes 
- **–已** （_perfective_）  
    Marks an action completed prior to the frame of reference.    
    - Example: **行已** “went, has gone.”        
- **–中** （_continuous_）  
    Marks an action ongoing or in progress.    
    - Example: **行中** “is walking.”        
- **–将** （_prospective/future_）  
    Marks an action in the future, whether intentional, predictive, or irrealis.    
    - Example: **行将** “will go, is about to go.”        

>[!warning]
>**Ordering rule:** Because their definitions do not overlap, **–中** and **–已** may co-occur. The required order is **中已**. If –将 appears, it is always closest to the verb.

#### Prefixes 
Four of the six **copulas**  may also function as verbal prefixes. Remember that copulas are the only verbs that supplete for negation.

>![info]
>- **有– / 無–** （_experiential_）  
>    Indicates whether the subject has ever experienced the action.    
>    - Example: **我 羅馬都市於 無行已也!**  
>        _“I have not been to Rome!”_        
>- **在– / 莫–** （_progressive_）  
>    Indicates an action currently in progress.    
>    - Example: **我 在被食中哉!**  
>        _“OMG, I’m being eaten!”_

In summary,
- TAM is marked by a small, regular set of affixes.    
- Suffixes indicate aspect and tense: **已** (perfective), **中** (continuous), **将** (prospective).    
- Prefixes derived from copulas indicate experiential and progressive aspect: **有/無–**, **在/莫–**.    
- Combinations are permitted with fixed order.

### Negation

Negation of the **verb phrase**  — and therefore of the entire clause — is expressed with the verbal prefix **不–**.

>[!info]
>- Example: **我 市場於 不行。**  
>    _“I do not go to the market.”_

### Ability and Permission 

The verbal prefix **可–** marks **ability or capacity**. It may also co-occur with negation.

>[!info]
>**Classical theological example:**
>- **我 可罪** → _posse peccare_     
>- **我 不可罪** → _posse non peccare_     
>- **我 可不罪** → _non posse peccare_     
>- **我 不可不罪** → _non posse non peccare_   

#### Permission
A similar form, the preposition **可以** , marks **permission**.

>[!info]
>- Example: **宴会於 可以行乎?**  
>    _“May I go to the banquet?”_


### Passive Voice
The verbal prefix **被–** marks the **passive voice**.
- It may only attach to **評詞** transitive verbs）.    
- The former subject may be reintroduced with an adverbial phrase marked by **–以**.

>[!info]
>- **我 被打撃。**  
>    _“I was struck.”_    
>- **和也 被食。**  
>    _“Kazuya was eaten.”_    
>- **熊猫 友以 被愛。**      
>	_“The panda was loved by a friend.”_

## Adnominal Morphology
>[!tip] The guiding maxim for adjectival and adverbial usage is **“Modifiers come first”**. This reflects a fundamental principle of Dan’a’yo grammar: **modifiers precede the elements they qualify**.
>
> **"modifiers comes first** ensures structural predictability: whether in nominal or verbal domains, modifiers guide the reader’s expectations before the head word appears.

### Adnominals and the Particle 之
The particle **之**  is used in Dan’a’yo to link **modifying clauses** to their head noun. It functions as an **adnominal marker**. This is inherited from Classical Chinese but applied more systematically.

#### Basic Pattern 
** (Clause + 之) + Noun**  
→ “the Noun which (Clause)”

1. **捕已魚之男人 此処於 在。**  
    _“The man who had caught the fish is here.”_    
    - Clause: 捕已魚 “caught the fish”        
    - Head noun: 男人 “man”        
    - 之 marks the clause as modifying 男人.        
2. **男人捕已之魚 鮮美。**  
    _“The fish which the man had caught was tasty.”_    
    - Clause: 男人捕已 “the man caught”        
    - Head noun: 魚 “fish”        
    - 之 marks the clause as modifying 魚. 
3. **我 憎悪 捕已魚之男人。**  
    _“I hate the man who caught the fish.”_    
    - Object: 捕已魚之男人 “the man who caught the fish.”        
4. **我食 男人捕已之魚。**  
    _“I am eating the fish the man had caught.”_    
    - Object: 男人捕已之魚 “the fish which the man had caught.”        

#### Commentary 
- In **Classical Chinese**, the particle 之 was often omitted if context allowed. Dan’a’yo requires it systematically, to prevent ambiguity.    
- The structure parallels **Japanese** (の/〜た + 名詞) and **Korean** (관형사형 어미 + 명사), but in Dan’a’yo the marker is **always 之**, regardless of tense or aspect.    
- This makes Dan’a’yo adnominals simpler （簡潔） but also more rigid （硬直的） than J/K.    
- In spoken Modern Dan’a’yo, **short noun–noun compounds** may drop 之 by analogy to texting style （省略）, but in careful Literary register it must appear.

## Reduplication
Reduplication is a productive process in Dan’a’yo where a character or word is repeated to express a special nuance of meaning. This matches patterns seen across Chinese, Japanese, Korean, and Vietnamese, but with its own distinct logic.

### 1. Full vs. Partial Reduplication
- If a **single character** is an independent word, reduplication simply repeats the character:    
    - 山山 = “cute little mountain range” (diminutive).        
- If a **character requires a compound** to make sense, then only the **main character** is reduplicated:    
    - 停止 → 停停止 = “to keep halting.”        
- If a word always requires **two (or more) characters of equal weight**, the **whole word** is reduplicated:    
    - 女児 → 女児女児 = “little daughter” (diminutive).        
- Some lexical items are already inherently doubled (e.g. 人人 “everyone”). These cannot reduplicate again; periphrasis must be used instead.

### 2. Nouns
- **Reduplicating nouns** creates a **diminutive**.    
- Often used in kinship or intimate terms.    
- Examples:    
    - 弟弟 = “little brother” (弟 “younger brother”).        
    - 母母 = “mommy” (母 “mother”).        
    - 女児女児 = “little daughter” (since 女児 is inseparable).        

>[!Warning]
>Reduplicating **respectful terms** may sound childish or disrespectful! For example:
> - 兄兄 = “little big brother” — a younger sibling using this may earn a few bruises.

### 3. Transitives / Eventives
- **Reduplicating transitive verbs** expresses **repetitive or habitual action**.    
- Because Dan'a'yo tense-aspect is relative, reduplication alone is ambiguous. Usually an **adverb** clarifies.   

Examples:
- 咬咬 = “(he) bites and bites,” “(he) keeps biting.”    
- 走走 = “to run about repeatedly.”    
- 敲敲 = “to knock again and again.”

Interpretation depends on context: past habitual, present iterative, or future expected.

### 4. Statives/Adjectives
- **Reduplicating adjectives** indicates **maximum degree / emotional superlative**.   
- This is stronger than 第一 or 最 — it carries an **expressive, often humorous exaggeration**.

Examples:
- 白白 = “as white as possible” (extremely white).    
- 正正 = “utterly correct!”    
- 小小 = “so tiny!”    
In speech, this often conveys the **speaker’s reaction or feeling**.

## Degrees of Comparison
In Dan’a’yo, adjectives and stative verbs do not inflect for degree. Instead, degree is expressed analytically, through particles and adverbs. This structure parallels the practice of CJKV languages, but with Dan'a'yo's distinctive SVO order and preference for postpositions.

### 1. **Equative (Equality)**
- **如** is used to mark equative comparisons: “as … as.”
- Structure: **A  B如 Adj**    
- Example:    
    - 我 君如 高。        
    - _I am as tall as you._        

### 2. **Comparative (Superiority/Inferiority)**
- **比** marks the standard of comparison: “than.”    
- Structure: **A B比 更 Adj**    
- Example:    
    - 我 君比 更好。    
    - _I am better than you._
- The particle **更** “more” has a natural opposite in **少**
- Example:
	- 我之描 君之犬比 少大。
	- _My cat is less big than your dog._
- Technically, **更** is optional (**少** is not) in Classical/Poetic mode.

### 3. **Superlative (Greatest Degree)**
- The universal marker is **第一** “the first, foremost,” paralleling literary Chinese, Korean and Japanese.    
- Structure: **A 第一 Adj**    
- Example:    
    - 此山第一高。        
    - _This mountain is the tallest._ 
- **末一** is parallel to 第一, but “last, lowest”:    
    - 此人末一強。        
    - _This person is the least strong._
