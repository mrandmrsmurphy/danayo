>[!Tip]
> The guiding maxim for Dan’a’yo phonology is “Joseon standardizes the sounds”. This phrase recalls the title "Correct Sounds for the Instruction of the People", the proclamation of King Sejong in 1446 that introduced Hangŭl. Just as Hunminjeongeum established a clear and accessible system of writing for the Korean language, Dan’a’yo adopts Bopomofo as the authoritative script for phonology.
> 
> The motto emphasizes two principles:
> 1. **Old Korean phonology as the reference point**  — back when 어 meant /e/ rather than /ʌ̹/.    
> 2. **Bopomofo as the phonological script**  — ensuring that every sound in Dan’a’yo is represented with clarity and precision.   
>
> Thus **【朝鮮正音】** stands as a Classical reminder that the authority for pronunciation lies with the Korean tradition, continuing the legacy of King Sejong’s reforms.

# Phonology
The phonology of Dan’a’yo  is determined in two ways. In some cases it represents the **average** or **mean value** across the CJKV languages. In other cases, when no middle ground exists, it follows the **principle of simplicity**, choosing the sound easiest for human learners.

The phonological systems of CJKV differ greatly. Chinese, Korean, and Vietnamese are fundamentally based on a contrast between **aspirated and unaspirated initials**, with rimes that often include **central vowels**. Japanese, by contrast, is more similar to Western languages, organized around a contrast of **voiced and voiceless initials** and a system of **cardinal vowels** for rimes.

Phonotactic structure also varies across the region. Some languages prefer the simple **CV pattern** , while others allow **CGVC**, that is Consonant–Glide–Vowel–Consonant.

Dan'a'yo consonants are organized by **four place** and **five manners of articulation**. Consonants may be labial, alveolar, palatal, or dorsal. They may be **nasals, voiced stops, aspirated stops, fricatives**, or **approximants/flaps**. The complete inventory is shown in Figure 2.1.

A high degree of variance is tolerated among the “dialects” influenced by each source language. The phoneme /x/ is often realized as /h/ for non-Chinese speakers. Outside of Japanese, speakers may de-aspirate stops but not consistently voice them. Japanese speakers show the opposite tendency, sometimes failing to aspirate strongly enough. Despite these differences, all realizations are mutually recognizable across the region.
### Sibilants 

The phoneme /s/ undergoes **palatalization** to /ɕ/ before the high front vowel /i/ or the glide /j/. This process is widely found in Asian languages and is not considered phonemic in Dan’a’yo.

Speakers of Chinese often extend this change to syllables beginning with **sw-**, producing a retroflex onset. This extension is optional but should be recognizable for speakers of Dan'a'yo.
### Palatals

The palatal stops may also be realized as **post-alveolar affricates** without restriction.

### Japanese Concerns

For Japanese speakers, certain substitutions are considered acceptable, while others should be avoided.

- The nasal /ŋ/ may be pronounced as /ɴ/, following older Japanese patterns, and this remains fully intelligible.
- The fricative /h/ should not be replaced by /ɸᵝ/, a common Japanese realization, though it may be understood.
- The palatal stop /j/ may be realized as /dʑ/ without difficulty.
- Japanese speakers should be attentive to aspiration, since Dan’a’yo maintains a distinction between aspirated and unaspirated stops, which is weaker in Japanese phonology.
### Vietnamese Concerns 

For Vietnamese speakers, certain features of native pronunciation appear in Dan’a’yo as well.

- Stops may sometimes be produced as **implosives** , though this is non--standard. This remains broadly intelligible across the region.
- The common lenition of /g/ → /ɣ/ should be avoided, since it causes confusion with the distinct phoneme /x/.
- Vietnamese speakers should attend to **final consonants**, since Dan’a’yo retains coda distinctions that Vietnamese phonology tends to neutralize.
### Glottal Stop 

To maintain clear syllable divisions, especially in morphophonological contexts, speakers may insert a **glottal stop** /ʔ/ at the beginning of syllables with a null onset.

However, in practice most words are intelligible without this epenthesis. For example, forms such as 児児 or 異音 are normally distinct enough in context and do not require a glottal onset.

Thus **vowel hiatus** is permitted in Dan’a’yo, as long as the surrounding context provides clarity. This mirrors practices in many East Asian languages, where it is tolerated.

##  Vowels

Speakers of Mandarin must adjust to **simple cardinal vowels** in nearly every foreign language they study — whether Korean, Japanese, English, or others. Dan’a’yo adopts a system that is both simple and broadly inclusive, as shown in Figure 2.2.

### Figure 2.2: Dan’a’yo Vowel Inventory

|          | Front       | Central         | Back        |
| -------- | ----------- | --------------- | ----------- |
| **High** | ㄧ `/i ~ ɪ/` |                 | ㄨ `/u ~ ʊ/` |
| **Mid**  | ㄝ `/e ~ ɛ/` |                 | ㄛ `/o ~ ɔ/` |
| **Low**  |             | ㄚ `/a ~ ä ~ ɑ/` |             |

### Long Vowels

Two so-called **long vowels** are possible in the Dan’a’yo syllable structure: **iy** and **uw** Because not all hearers can reliably distinguish vowel length, speakers must indicate an **off-glide** in _some_ manner.

The exact realization is not fixed. For example, _ii_ may be pronounced as /iɪ/, /iə/, or /iɨ/, while _uu_ may be realized as /uʊ/, /uɔ/, or /uə/. All of these are acceptable variants.

### Epenthesis

Different languages of Asia supply different **epenthetic vowels** when breaking up consonant clusters.

- Korean inserts /ɯ/.    
- Japanese inserts /ɯᵝ/.    
- Mandarin inserts /ɨ/.    
- Vietnamese inserts /ə/.    
- Cantonese inserts /ʊ/.    

In the Latin orthography, epenthetic vowels are written as **ǝ** to distinguish them from the normal vowel _e_. In the Bopomofo orthography, they are written as **ㄜ**.

The transcription of foreign words should follow Dan’a’yo phonotactics, breaking up consonant clusters as necessary.

For example:
- _Bach_ → ㄅㄚㄏㄜ    
- _bibimbap_  ㄅㄧㄅㄧㄇㄅㄚㄆ    
- _natto_  → ㄋㄚㄊㄊㄛ.

## Phonotactics

The basic syllable structure of Dan’a’yo follows a **CV pattern** with several optional elements. An onset is normally required, though a null onset is also possible. After the onset, an optional **on-glide** (y, w) may appear. The nucleus is one of the five vowels. The coda or final may be absent, or may consist of a nasal, a high vowel off-glide, or an unreleased voiceless stop (p, t, k）.

Thus the maximal syllable template is **(C)(G)V(C)**, where G = glide and C = consonant. This system is broadly comparable to other East Asian languages but intentionally inclusive of both Chinese-style finals and Korean-style codas.

### Figure 2.3: Dan’a’yo Syllable Structure

| Position     | Inventory                                |
| ------------ | ---------------------------------------- |
| **Onset **   | m, n, s, h, l, ∅, p, t, c, k, b, d, j, g |
| **On-Glide** | y, w, ∅                                  |
| **Vowel**    | i, e, a, o, u                            |
| **Final**    | m, n, ŋ, w, y, p, t, k, ∅                |
### Velar Nasal

The velar nasal /ŋ/ is permitted in **syllable-final position** but does not occur in **syllable-initial position**.

### Gaps in Distribution

Certain gaps exist in the Dan’a’yo syllable system:

- The consonants **d, t, c, j** may not combine with a **y on-glide**.    
- The consonant **f** may not take any on-glide.
- A **y on-glide** may not be followed by the vowel **i**.    
- A **w on-glide** may only precede the vowels **a, e**.    
- In syllables with a null onset, **wi+** sequences are permitted.    
- A **w on-glide** may not co-occur with the initials **n, l, b, p, m**.

These restrictions reflect articulatory constraints rather than missing phonemes.

### Morphophonology

Because many speakers find coda consonants difficult, Dan’a’yo prohibits **phonological blending across syllable boundaries**.

- Japanese speakers may struggle to distinguish **coda nasals**.    
- Mandarin speakers may struggle to produce **coda stops**.    

Therefore, no **sandhi** or cross-syllable assimilation is permitted. Each syllable remains independent.

Care must be taken to avoid forms like _ningan_ ㄋㄧㄋㄍㄚㄋ, where a velar nasal /ŋ/ would alter meaning. The rule is absolute: there should be no influence of one syllable upon another.

## Intonation and Prosody

Because lexical tone is not present in all East Asian languages and learning new tones is highly difficult, **tone is not phonemic** in Dan’a’yo. Speakers should not attempt to convey semantic or emotional information through intonation alone. Instead, **sentence-final particles** are mandatory to indicate any modality other than the simple declarative.

Dan’a’yo employs **pitch-accent** as a prosodic aid for word separation. Pitch-accent refers to the relative height of syllables across a phrase, rather than lexical tone.  Pitch is realized at the phrase or sentence level, and no minimal pairs of words exist based on pitch alone. For example:

- <ruby>大<rt>ㄉㄚ˙</rt></ruby><ruby>学生<rt>ㄏㄚㄎㄙㄚㄥ</rt></ruby> “big student” → pitch **H HL** 
    
- <ruby>大学生<rt>ㄉㄚ˙ㄏㄚㄎㄙㄚㄥ</rt></ruby> “college student” → pitch **HLL** 
    

### Principles of Pitch

- The **topic** of an utterance is its highest point.
- The **head of a phrase** is its highest point.    
- The **main vowel of a syllable** is its highest point.    
- The **first character of a compound word** is its highest point.    

This system benefits East Asian speakers, but may be confusing for outsiders. A sentence with an object or particle ends with a falling pitch, similar to declaratives in many languages. However, because many sentences end in a verb, the intonation may resemble a tag-question— especially when the verb is monosyllabic.

For this reason, even **non-polar questions** must end with the particle **乎** to ensure clarity.
