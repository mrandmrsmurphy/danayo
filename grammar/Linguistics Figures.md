# Typology
## 1. General Typological Profile

Dan’a’yo is a **topic-prominent, predominantly isolating language** with **agglutinative layering in the functional domain** and **strictly head-final syntax with constrained exceptions**.

More precisely:

- **Morphology**: isolating core + agglutinative functional morphology
    
- **Alignment**: discourse-driven (topic-prominent), not nominative–accusative or ergative in the usual sense
    
- **Word order**: rigid **topic–comment**, internally **head-final (SOV-like)**
    
- **Marking type**: strongly **analytic**, with optional but systematic **postpositional case marking**
    
## 2. Morphology

### 2.1 Core type

Lexical items are **monosyllabic, discrete, and non-inflecting**, corresponding closely to characters. There are:

- no clitics
    
- no phonological fusion across word boundaries
    
- clear morpheme boundaries at all times
    

This places Dan’a’yo firmly in the **isolating typological class** (cf. Classical Chinese, Vietnamese).

### 2.2 Agglutinative layer

A secondary layer introduces **strictly ordered affixation**:

- TAM, mood, evidential, and related categories are expressed via **prefixes and suffixes**
    
- ordering is **fixed and compositional**
    
- each morpheme contributes a single function
    

This is **agglutinative behavior**, but restricted to functional morphology rather than lexical stems.

### 2.3 Derivation

- Productive derivation occurs via **compounding and relational linking (之)**
    
- Affixes such as 化 behave as **true derivational morphemes**, not clitics
    

### 2.4 Reduplication

Reduplication is productive:

- **Nouns**: diminutive / affective
    
    - 山 → 山山
        
- **Verbs**: iterative / intensifying
    
    - 停止 → 停停止
        

Both **full and partial reduplication** occur, aligning with East and Southeast Asian typology.

## 3. Lexical Categories

The system is **rigidly lexicalized** in formal usage:

- 名詞 (nouns)
    
- 事詞 (eventives; transitive predicates)
    
- 性詞 (statives)
    

There is **no zero-derivation across categories** in standard language.  
Informal speech permits drift, but it is not grammatically licensed.

This differs from Classical Chinese, where category fluidity is common, and aligns more with **modern Japanese/Korean rigidity**.

## 4. Syntax: Topic–Comment Structure

### 4.1 Core structure

All clauses follow:

> **Topic + Comment**

This is the **primary syntactic principle**, not subject–predicate.

### 4.2 Subject vs topic

Dan’a’yo allows **simultaneous topic and subject**, analogous to Japanese は vs が or Korean 는 vs 가:

- 荘子 智慧太  
    “As for Zhuangzi, (his) wisdom was great”
    

Here:

- 荘子 = discourse topic
    
- 智慧 = subject within the comment
    

Thus:

- **Topic is extra-clausal / discourse-level**
    
- **Subject is internal to the predicate structure**
    

### 4.3 Argument marking

- No nominative or accusative marking
    
- Core arguments (S, O) are typically **unmarked**
    
- Other roles are marked via **postpositions (関詞)**
    

This yields a **mixed system**:

- syntactic roles are partly inferred
    
- non-core roles are overtly marked

## 5. Case and Postpositional System

### 5.1 Structure

All relational markers are:

- **strictly postpositional** (NP + particle)
    
- never prepositional
    

### 5.2 Inventory behavior

Examples include:

- 于 (animate dative)
    
- 於 (locative)
    
- 以 (instrumental)
    
- 共 (comitative)
    
- etc.
    

### 5.3 Constraints

- **No case stacking** directly
    
- stacking requires **nominalization (e.g., via 之)**
    

### 5.4 Typological classification

This is a **postpositional case-marking system**, typologically similar to:

- Japanese
    
- Korean
    
- Turkic (in structure, though not morphology)
    

## 6. Predicate Structure

### 6.1 Verb classes

- 事詞: require objects
    
- 性詞: stative, often adjectival in function
    

### 6.2 TAM marking

- No inherent tense/aspect in verbs
    
- All TAM categories are expressed via **external morphology**
    
- Markers are:
    
    - **obligatorily ordered**
        
    - **post-verbal (suffixal)** or pre-verbal depending on category
        

### 6.3 No serial verbs

Unlike many Sinitic and Southeast Asian languages, Dan’a’yo:

- **does not permit serial verb constructions**
    
- instead relies on explicit marking and clause structure
    

## 7. Copular and Existential System

Distinct lexemes:

- 是 / 非 (equative)
    
- 有 / 無 (possession)
    
- 在 / 莫 (existence)
    

Key properties:

- **Suppletive negation** (not analytic negation)
    
- No compositional negation of copula
    

### Register variation

- In **classical register**, copula may be omitted
    
- In standard usage, it is typically present
    

## 8. Head Directionality

Dan’a’yo is **overwhelmingly head-final**, but with fixed exceptions.

### 8.1 Head-final domains

- Relative clauses precede nouns
    
- Modifiers precede heads
    
- Adverbs precede verbs
    
- Clausal structure is predicate-final
    

### 8.2 Exceptions

- **Numeral + classifier phrases follow the noun**
    
- **TAM markers follow the verb**
    

This produces a **mixed but rule-governed system**, not free variation.


## 9. Clause Combining

### 9.1 Relative clauses

- Pre-nominal
    
- Marked with **之**
    
- Fully integrated into NP structure
    

### 9.2 Subordination

- Marked by dedicated particles (conjunction-like elements)
    
- Not purely paratactic
    

### 9.3 Coordination

- Distinct forms for:
    
    - NP coordination
        
    - clause coordination
        

This is a **structurally explicit coordination system**, unlike Classical Chinese ambiguity.

## 10. Information Structure

- **Topic = primary discourse anchor**
    
- Focus is not separately grammaticalized
    
- Particles like 丈 (“only”), 且 (“also”) operate within topic/comment structure
    

This yields a system where:

- **focus is interpreted pragmatically**, not morphologically
    

## 11. Typological Positioning

Dan’a’yo sits at an unusual intersection:

|Feature|Typological Affinity|
|---|---|
|Isolating lexicon|Classical Chinese, Vietnamese|
|Agglutinative TAM|Japanese, Korean, Turkic|
|Topic prominence|Japanese, Korean|
|Postpositions|Japanese, Korean|
|No serial verbs|Unlike Sinitic / SEA languages|
|Rigid category system|More like Japanese/Korean than Classical Chinese|

## 12. Summary Classification

Compact typological label:

> **Topic-prominent, isolating–agglutinative mixed language with rigid head-final syntax and postpositional case marking**

# History
_"What alternate history would let a living literary language actually drift from late medieval Sinitic into Dan’a’yo under continuous use?”_

The key is not ordinary sound change alone. The key is a long-lived **supranational written standard** that is read aloud, taught, glossed, reordered, and lightly vernacularized for a thousand years by elites whose native grammars are increasingly Korean-, Japanese-, Cantonese-, and Vietnamese-like. In that setting, the language does not simply descend from spoken Middle Chinese. It is continuously bent by the habits of its readers.

The best model is not “Latin becoming French.” It is more like a fusion of:
- Medieval written Latin under non-native readers
- Kanbun kundoku as a permanent reading technology
- Buddhist/Taoist/Confucian Sinitic as a transregional prestige code
- Classical Chinese surviving long enough to be grammatically regularized by second-language use

So the history should be one of **literary continuity plus slow contact-induced restructuring**.

## 1. The central premise
From about 1000 onward, imagine that the Sinosphere never gives up on a shared classical written medium, but that this medium is increasingly used not as frozen Classical Chinese proper, but as a **readable transregional high language**. It is still written in characters. It is still anchored in Tang literature and commentary. But over the centuries it is spoken aloud in schools, courts, monasteries, examinations, diplomatic settings, and annotated texts in ways that gradually converge.

The speakers do not natively speak it. They bring into it:
- Korean topic marking and clause-final organization
- Japanese modifier-before-head order and rigid postpositional parsing
- Cantonese preservation of compact monosyllabic lexical rhythm and topic prominence
- Vietnamese analytic grammar and tolerance for highly isolating structure

The result is a language that remains visibly Classical in lexicon and script, but whose syntax is slowly **domesticated by East Asian reading practices**.

## 2. Why it moves toward Dan’a’yo rather than toward Mandarin
Because this language is not being shaped primarily by northern colloquial speech. It is being shaped by:
- book reading
- recitation
- glossing
- commentary
- translation habits
- examination prose
- interregional correspondence

A spoken vernacular normally simplifies by ordinary community transmission. A prestige written language simplifies by **pedagogical regularization**. Ambiguities that Classical Chinese could tolerate begin to feel intolerable to non-native readers. They resolve them in the same direction over and over.

Three broad pressures then dominate.

1. First, **topic prominence remains stable**, because Classical Chinese already permits it and Japanese/Korean strongly reinforce it.
2. Second, **head-final parsing grows stronger**, because Japanese and Korean readers habitually understand modifiers before heads and want the predicate to come late.
3. Third, **free function words become fixed particles**, because second-language readers prefer clear slot-based grammar over flexible classical ambiguity.

## 3. A plausible historical sequence
### Phase I: Late classical commonwealth, 1000 to 1200

At this stage the language is still recognizably post-Tang Literary Sinitic. Word order is still relatively flexible. Omission is still common. Many relational words such as 於, 于, 以, 為, 之, 而, 也, 矣 can still function in broad classical ways. Readers in Korea, Japan, Vietnam, and the south China coast still learn the old commentarial tradition.

But the crucial shift has begun: texts are no longer merely decoded. They are routinely **performed _aloud_ through local grammatical filters**.

- In Japan, kundoku habits reorder sentences and make dependency clearer.  
- In Korea, reading traditions increasingly map classical phrases onto topic-comment frames and postpositional logic.  
	- **석독구결** (釋讀口訣, _seokdok gugyeol_)
	- **음독구결** (音讀口訣, _eumdok gugyeol_)
In Vietnam, classical literacy coexists with analytic local grammar and particles.  
In the southern Sinitic sphere, topic prominence remains natural and compact monosyllabic rhythm remains intact.

At this stage the language is still Classical Chinese in form, but in use it has begun to acquire a shadow grammar.

### Phase II: Commentarial domestication, 1200 to 1400
The examination culture and scholarly commentarial tradition now favor prose that is easier to parse across regions. Writers begin to avoid the most compressed ambiguities of ancient style. They do not invent a new language consciously, but they start preferring forms that survive oral explanation well.

Several tendencies harden:

Topic is more often overtly established first. Rather than relying on pure classical juxtaposition, writers favor a clearer topic field before the main assertion.

Modifying material is increasingly kept before the thing modified, because this matches both classical noun modification and Japonic/Koreanic habits. Relative structures with 之 become especially attractive because they can be taught consistently.

Relational words start losing freedom. A form like 以, which in older texts may function with broader semantic range, begins to settle into more fixed uses such as instrumental or means. Likewise 於 and 于 settle into clearer locative or dative niches.

The grammar is still written as classical, but students are increasingly taught that these forms have default slots.

That is the beginning of the move from “loose function word” to “particle/postposition.”

### Phase III: The age of interregional reading norms, 1400 to 1600
Instead of the Sinosphere breaking into separate national philologies, imagine stronger and more sustained scholarly exchange centered on a common “correct reading of the classics.” Not necessarily one pronunciation (yet), but one accepted **way of unpacking syntax**.

Now kanbun-like reading is no longer a merely local Japanese technique. Similar unpacking conventions appear across the shared scholarly world:

- identify the topic first
- hold modifiers before heads
- interpret relational morphemes after the noun phrase they govern
- place TAM and illocutionary force near the end of the predicate
- prefer one overt marker per function

The result is that Classical Chinese is no longer being read as a fluid archaic code. It is being read as a **structured analytic language with recoverable slots**.

Japanese, Korean, and Vietnamese "pull Classical Chinese into becoming Dan’a’yo”, not by replacing vocabulary, but by forcing recurrent parsing decisions:

- noun phrase first, particle after
- dependent clause first, head later
- discourse topic first, assertion last
- verbal nucleus earlier, TAM and force later

Over generations, prose that fits this reading style is copied, praised, and imitated. Prose that resists it begins to feel archaic, poetic, or obscure.

### Phase IV: Early modern koine drift, 1600 to 1800
At this point the shared literary language is no longer Classical Chinese in the old sense. It still quotes the classics and still uses classical lexicon, but its active prose norm has been regularized.

Now you begin to see what would look, in hindsight, like a genuine language:
- topic-comment as default clause architecture
- relative clauses consistently before nouns with 之
- adverbs before verbs
- relational markers obligatorily after noun phrases
- copular distinctions more stable
- suffixal TAM and sentence force more expected in ordinary prose
- less tolerance for category fluidity in formal writing

This is also the point where the language starts to be teachable not just as literary style, but as “the common elegant tongue.” Grammarians begin to write prescriptions. Dictionaries and school texts start classifying words by function more explicitly.

The old language had many things it _could_ do.  
The new language has things it is _supposed_ to do.

That is the mark of grammaticalization under school culture.

### Phase V: Modern Dan’a’yo, 1800 onward
By this stage:

- the phonology has been leveled across regional readings into a compact CGVC-style syllable canon
- retroflexes have vanished
- medials and glides have been regularized
- many old distinctions survive only etymologically

But more importantly, the grammar now looks like a natural endpoint of a millennium of East Asian literary contact:

- topic-prominent throughout
- strongly head-final
- mostly isolating
- but with agglutinative tendencies in a clearly delimited functional layer
- postpositions rather than loose classical coverbs
- stable lexical categories in formal prose
- classical register preserved as an elevated archaism within the same tradition

So the “classical mode” and “modern mode” are not two separate inventions. They are earlier and later states of the same literary civilization.

## 4. What steers it toward CGVC
The main historic, phonological pressures are not random sound changes. They are **literacy-driven syllable discipline**.

A transregional character language wants:
- one stable reading per grapheme
- compact syllables
- recoverable medials
- limited codas
- no retroflex series
- no consonant clusters
- no inflectional endings

That pushes toward a neat canonical syllable shape like CGVC.

A character-based koine reading is repeatedly re-learned from tables, primers, rhyme books, glosses, and recitation. Systems that are too regionally marked or too phonetically messy get leveled away. The surviving reading norm is the one easiest to map onto:

- Per character:
	- onset
	- medial
	- nucleus
	- coda

Japanese and Korean pressure would strongly favor clear syllable boundaries.  
Vietnamese pressure would favor analytic clarity and discrete syllables.  
Cantonese pressure would support preservation of coda discipline and monosyllabic lexical integrity.

So CGVC is not just a phonological outcome. It is a **pedagogical outcome**.

## 5. How loose classical elements become suffixes and postpositions
In Classical Chinese, many function words are versatile. They can be verbs, coverbs, conjunctions, particles, or something between. That flexibility works in a native literary culture with heavy commentary. It works less well in a transregional school language.

So over time, forms are reanalyzed by position.

At first, a form like 以 may still be understood semantically as “take, by means of, with.” But in repeated schoolroom parsing it is taught after a noun phrase as the normal instrumental marker. Once that happens for centuries, speakers stop experiencing it as a lexical word and start experiencing it as a required relational suffix/postposition.

Likewise 于 and 於 cease to be flexible pre-verbal or coverb-like items and become strict NP-final markers of animate goal and location.

丈 and 且 become discourse particles associated with topic phrases, because that is where readers keep encountering and teaching them.

Sentence-final elements such as 矣 harden as clause-final force markers, because end position becomes a stable grammatical slot.

In short, the language evolves not by affixes eroding phonologically, but by **independent words freezing into fixed grammatical positions**. Since word boundaries remain clear, they feel suffix-like or particle-like without becoming clitics.

That is exactly the sort of agglutinative tendency Dan'a'yo language has.

## 6. Why topic prominence survives the entire millennium

Because all four major contact zones support it.

- Classical Chinese already allows discourse-fronted organization.  
- Cantonese is comfortable with topic prominence.  
- Japanese strongly reinforces topic-comment syntax.  
- Korean strongly reinforces topic-comment syntax.  
- Vietnamese is also discourse-friendly and analytic.

So unlike many alternate histories where prestige grammar gets pulled toward subject-centered Indo-European style, this one would be stabilized in the opposite direction. Topic prominence would not merely survive. It would become the one thing everyone across the Sinosphere agrees is natural.

That gives you a very strong continuous spine:
- ancient concise topic-comment in the early period
- pedagogically regularized topic-comment in the middle period
- full modern topic-comment syntax in Dan’a’yo

## 7. Why head-finality grows stronger
This is where Japanese and Korean have the deepest structural influence.

Classical Chinese is not fully head-final. But it already has many pre-head constructions:
- modifiers before nouns
- relative material before nouns
- adverbs before verbs
- genitive/attributive structures before heads

Japanese and Korean readers then interpret ambiguous classical clauses in ways that delay the main predicate and clarify dependency. Over centuries, the prose styles most suited to this reading tradition become normative. This does not mean every head-final trait is copied wholesale. It means the language drifts toward a default expectation:

1. Set the scene first.  
2. Name the topic.  
3. State modifiers and arguments before the main predication.  
4. Finish with predicate plus mood/TAM.

That is enough to give you a strongly head-final language without forcing a fully Japonic or Koreanic structure.

## 8. A cleaner fictional formula

If you want a one-sentence historical claim for the language, it could be this:

***Dan’a’yo is the uninterrupted descendant of a post-Tang transregional literary Sinitic, gradually regularized by a millennium of kanbun-style reading, examination prose, and contact with Korean, Japanese, Cantonese, and Vietnamese grammatical habits.***

That gives continuity without requiring ordinary village-to-village spoken descent.

## 9. What this reveals lexically and stylistically
Older layer:
- looser syntax
- broader function-word semantics
- more omission
- more category fluidity
- more classical ambiguity

Middle layer:
- commentarial regularization
- 之-heavy modification
- clearer particles
- emerging slot grammar

Modern layer:
- explicit postpositions    
- fixed TAM slots
- rigid lexical categories
- strong topic-comment syntax

That lets a word or construction have:
- an archaic classical use
- a medieval commentarial use
- a modern Dan’a’yo use

Which is exactly elucidates semantic and lexical derivation.

## 10. The key idea
***A classical written civilization kept one common language alive long enough that non-native East Asian reading habits gradually regularized it into a real language.***

That is the believable bridge between Tang and Dan’a’yo.

# Kanbun Writ Large
The pieces fit together unusually well because they all solve the same problem: how to make a compact, under-specified classical text speakable inside a very different native grammar. Once that activity is shared across regions and persists for centuries, it stops being a reading technique and starts acting like a slow grammatical engine.

These traditions as **four distinct pressures that converge**

## Korea
First, the Korean layer, especially 석독구결 and later 음독구결, contributes **explicit syntactic disambiguation**. Korean readers need case and clause structure to be visible. So they habitually interpret classical strings as:

- NP + postposition
- clause-final predicate    
- clear topic vs subject distinction  
    Over time, anything that consistently gets read “as if it had a particle after it” begins to behave like it actually does. That is your path from coverbs to true postpositions.

## Japan
Second, the Japanese layer, through kundoku and kanbun training, contributes **ordering discipline**. Japanese readers systematically reorder to:

- modifiers before heads
- predicate at the end
- dependencies resolved before completion  
    Even if the written text does not change immediately, preferred prose begins to align with what is easiest to kundoku. That steadily increases head-finality and stabilizes relative clauses before nouns.

## Vietnam
Third, the Vietnamese layer (chữ Nôm, đọc Hán văn) contributes **analytic clarity and tolerance for overt marking**. Vietnamese is comfortable with:

- explicit particles
- little or no inflection
- stable word classes  
    This pushes against classical ambiguity. It favors one marker per function, discourages multifunctional particles, and helps freeze lexical categories in formal registers.
    

## Southern China
Fourth, the southern Sinitic layer (drawing on Cantonese-like tendencies) preserves **monosyllabic lexical integrity and topic prominence**. That prevents the system from drifting toward polysyllabic erosion or heavy morphology. It also keeps topic-comment natural rather than marked.

When these operate together over a millennium, you get a very specific trajectory:
- Classical flexibility becomes pedagogically intolerable
- Pedagogical conventions become habitual parsing
- Habitual parsing becomes expected grammar
- Expected grammar becomes the language

One way to formalize this is to think in terms of **“interpretive defaults that fossilize.”** For example:

- If every school tradition consistently explains 於 NP as “NP + locative relation,” it will be reinterpreted as a postposition.
- If every reader delays the main predicate to the end when explaining a sentence, prose that already places it late will be favored and imitated.
- If every commentary inserts a topic frame before exposition, authors begin writing with overt topic framing.

That gives you a clean mechanism for grammaticalization without phonological erosion.

Assign each region a dominant grammatical contribution:
- Korea: case-like structure, topic vs subject distinction
- Japan: head-final ordering, relative clause discipline
- Vietnam: analytic transparency, fixed categories
- Southern Sinitic: topic continuity, compact syllable economy

Dan’a’yo then looks less like an invention and more like the **intersection of their long-term compromises**.
