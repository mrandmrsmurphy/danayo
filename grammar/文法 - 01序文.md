<ruby>東亜<rt>ㄉㄛㄥ·ㄙ</rt></ruby><ruby>之<rt>ㄊㄧ</rt></ruby><ruby>言語<rt>·ㄝㄋ·˙ㄛ</rt></ruby> share a common <ruby>文化<rt>ㄇㄨㄋㄏˇㄚ</rt></ruby><ruby>之<rt>ㄊㄧ</rt></ruby><ruby>基盤<rt>ㄍㄧㄅㄚㄋ</rt></ruby>・<ruby>言語<rt>ㄝㄋ·˙ㄛ</rt></ruby><ruby>之<rt>ㄊㄧ</rt></ruby><ruby>基礎<rt>ㄍㄧㄑㄛ</rt></ruby>. For many centuries the <ruby>科挙<rt>ㄎˇㄚㄍ˙ㄛ</rt></ruby> defined <ruby>識字<rt>ㄙㄧㄎㄐㄧ</rt></ruby> and <ruby>学問<rt>ㄏㄚㄎㄇㄨㄋ</rt></ruby> throughout the region. It unified intellectual life around the <ruby>中古<rt>ㄐㄨㄥㄍㄛ</rt></ruby><ruby>漢語<rt>ㄏㄚㄋ·˙ㄛ</rt></ruby><ruby>文法<rt>ㄇㄨㄋㄆㄚㄆ</rt></ruby> and a fixed corpus of <ruby>古典<rt>ㄍㄛㄐㄝㄋ</rt></ruby><ruby>文献<rt>ㄇㄨㄋㄏㄝㄋ</rt></ruby>. In this way, the separate structures of four language families — <ruby>漢蔵<rt>ㄏㄚㄋㄑㄚㄥ</rt></ruby><ruby>語族<rt>˙ㄛㄐㄛㄎ</rt></ruby>, <ruby>日本<rt>ㄋㄧㄊㄅㄛㄋ</rt></ruby><ruby>語族<rt>˙ㄛㄐㄛㄎ</rt></ruby>, <ruby>朝鮮<rt>ㄐㄚㄨㄙ˙ㄝㄋ</rt></ruby><ruby>語族<rt>˙ㄛㄐㄛㄎ</rt></ruby>）, and <ruby>南亜<rt>ㄋㄚㄇ·ㄚ</rt></ruby><ruby>語族<rt>˙ㄛㄐㄛㄎ</rt></ruby> — were set aside, as generations of <ruby>学者<rt>ㄏㄚㄎㄑㄚ</rt></ruby> assimilated a shared written standard.

Over time, however, this unity declined （統一衰退）. <ruby>漢語<rt>ㄏㄚㄋ·˙ㄛ</rt></ruby> diverged into <ruby>普通話<rt>ㄆㄛㄊㄛㄥㄏˇㄚㄧ</rt></ruby> and other regional <ruby>方言<rt>ㄆㄚㄥ·ㄝㄋ</rt></ruby>. <ruby>日本<rt>ㄋㄧㄊㄅㄛㄋ</rt></ruby> and <ruby>韓国<rt>ㄏㄚㄋㄍㄛㄎ</rt></ruby> increasingly oriented themselves toward <ruby>欧羅巴<rt>ㄛㄨㄌㄛㄆㄚ</rt></ruby> rather than <ruby>北京<rt>ㄅㄨㄎㄍ˙ㄝㄥ</rt></ruby>. In the present day, when <ruby>中日韓越<rt>ㄐㄨㄥㄋㄧㄊㄏㄚㄋ·ˇㄝㄊ</rt></ruby> delegates meet, they usually conduct discussion in <ruby>英語<rt>ㄝㄥ·˙ㄛ</rt></ruby>. Thus a region once linked by a common grammar （共通文法） now depends upon a foreign lingua franca （[[外来]][[共通語]]）, with inevitable losses of nuance and shared heritage.

In Europe, <ruby>世界語<rt>ㄙㄝㄍ˙ㄝ·˙ㄛ</rt></ruby> has achieved some measure of success as an 補助語. Its advantages come from three elements: 規則<ruby>之<rt>ㄊㄧ</rt></ruby><ruby>文法<rt>ㄇㄨㄋㄆㄚㄆ</rt></ruby>, [[容易]][[語彙]], and common cultural assumptions （共有文化<ruby>之<rt>ㄊㄧ</rt></ruby>前提）. These principles — <ruby>論理<rt>ㄌㄛㄋㄌㄧ</rt></ruby> and [[共有]]<ruby>文化<rt>ㄇㄨㄋㄏˇㄚ</rt></ruby> — are also essential for East Asia. In a region where much of the common vocabulary is technical （[[専門]]語彙）, the need for systematic devices such as a correlative chart （[[対応]][[表]]） is even greater than in Europe.

Educational practices also reveal contrasts. In <ruby>中国<rt>ㄐㄨㄥㄍㄛㄎ</rt></ruby>, students are often told that grammar <ruby>文法<rt>ㄇㄨㄋㄆㄚㄆ</rt></ruby> need only be studied for <ruby>[[外国語]]</ruby>, not for Mandarin itself. In <ruby>日本<rt>ㄋㄧㄊㄅㄛㄋ</rt></ruby>, students spend years bridging the distance between 漢文, [[上代]]日本語）, and 現代日本語. <ruby>韓国<rt>ㄏㄚㄋㄍㄛㄎ</rt></ruby>／<ruby>朝鮮<rt>ㄐㄚㄨㄙ˙ㄝㄋ</rt></ruby>, historically, occupied an <ruby>中間<rt>ㄐㄨㄥㄍㄚㄋ</rt></ruby><ruby>位置<rt>ˇㄝㄧㄑㄧ</rt></ruby>: geographically, phonetically, and grammatically between <ruby>中国<rt>ㄐㄨㄥㄍㄛㄎ</rt></ruby> and <ruby>日本<rt>ㄋㄧㄊㄅㄛㄋ</rt></ruby>.

From these observations, the design principles of a unifying language （統一語設計原理） can be derived:

1. **[[音韻]]** should favor [[古代]]<ruby>朝鮮語<rt>ㄐㄚㄨㄙ˙ㄝㄋ·˙ㄛ</rt></ruby>, which lies between Chinese and Japanese.    
2. **[[正字法]]** should follow <ruby>新字体<rt>ㄙㄧㄋㄐㄧㄊㄚㄧ</rt></ruby>, a compromise between [[繁体字]] ａｎｄ [[簡体字]].    
3. **<ruby>文法<rt>ㄇㄨㄋㄆㄚㄆ</rt></ruby>** should continue the framework of <ruby>古典<rt>ㄍㄛㄐㄝㄋ</rt></ruby><ruby>漢語<rt>ㄏㄚㄋ·˙ㄛ</rt></ruby>, which historically served all.    

Therefore, Dan’a’yo defined as follows: it adopts <ruby>日本<rt>ㄋㄧㄊㄅㄛㄋ</rt></ruby>字形）, <ruby>韓国<rt>ㄏㄚㄋㄍㄛㄎ</rt></ruby>音声, and <ruby>中国<rt>ㄐㄨㄥㄍㄛㄎ</rt></ruby><ruby>文法<rt>ㄇㄨㄋㄆㄚㄆ</rt></ruby>.

The character base （[[字彙]][[基盤]]） follows the intersection of educational standards （教育標準）: Japan’s Jōyō list （常用漢字表, 2136字）, Korea’s secondary-school list （中高校漢字表, 約1800字）, and China’s HSK vocabulary list （<ruby>漢語<rt>ㄏㄚㄋ·˙ㄛ</rt></ruby>水平考試詞彙表）. In addition, official name-character lists （人名用漢字表, 人名用字表） in both Japan and Korea are consulted. Together these define the maximal set （[[最大]][[集合]]） of allowable characters.

Words may be as small as 一字. When necessary, derivation follows rules of logic （論理規則） and commonality （共通性）. Because much shared vocabulary is technical （専門語）, certain fundamental words must be formed analytically （分析<ruby>之<rt>ㄊㄧ</rt></ruby>造語）; however, preference is given to pan-Asian, widely recognized terms （汎亜普遍語）. Finally, Japanese compounding practice （<ruby>日本<rt>ㄋㄧㄊㄅㄛㄋ</rt></ruby>式複合語）, which shifted to SOV order （主語–目<ruby>之<rt>ㄊㄧ</rt></ruby>語–動詞）, is deliberately reversed, restoring the older VO pattern （動詞–目<ruby>之<rt>ㄊㄧ</rt></ruby>語）.

## <ruby>設計<rt>ㄙㄝㄊㄐㄝㄧ</rt></ruby>[[目標]]
>[!Tip] **【<ruby>[[東亜自通]]<rt>ㄉㄛㄥㄚㄐㄧㄧㄊㄛㄥ</rt></ruby>】** - East Asian Self Communication

<ruby>単亜語<rt>ㄉㄚㄋㄚㄧㄛ</rt></ruby>  is designed as a 補助語. The purpose is not to create a fully 母語 that could become the 第一言語 of an entire region. Rather, it is intended as a **simple tool for communication** （簡易通信工具） between speakers who continue to write 詩歌 and conduct 日常生活 in their own native languages （固有語）.

In this respect, Dan’a’yo is comparable to <ruby>[[教会]]<rt>ㄍㄧㄚㄨㄏㄨㄝ</rt></ruby>[[羅馬語]] in medieval Europe （[[中世]][[欧州]]）. Such a language is not only a practical medium, but also a reminder that there is more shared （共有） among the different languages than there is divided （差異）. A common linguistic heritage （共有言語遺産） can bind peoples together （結束）, without becoming a rigid system （硬直制度） or a burden of obsolete rules （旧規則の枷）.

The design principle （設計指標） is straightforward. Any CJKV speaker （中日韓越話者） should be able to look at a 文章 in Dan’a’yo and visually recognize （視認） or aurally recognize （聴取） more than half of its content. With only a little training （短期学習） or the aid of a simple reference sheet （対照表）, 理解度 should increase rapidly （急速上昇）.

## [[概要]]
Dan'a'yo sounds somewhat like older Korean.  It is written in Shinjitai Chinese characters, with <ruby>注音符号<rt>ㄐㄨㄨㄇㄅㄨㄏㄚㄨ</rt></ruby> above as the phonological alphabet.  Grammar is somewhere between Classical topic-comment, and modern Japanese/Korean forced into SVO word-order.

In **Dan’a’yo**, expression is shaped by two organizing principles: **three modes of style** and **three meta-parts of speech**.

### Modes
1. **<ruby>[[古風体]]<rt>ㄍㄛㄆㄨㄥㄊㄝㄧ</rt></ruby>** — terse, topic–comment, copulas often omitted, written chiefly in <ruby>漢字<rt>ㄏㄚㄋㄐㄧ</rt></ruby>.
2. **<ruby>[[書面体]]<rt>ㄙ˙ㄛㄇ˙ㄝㄋ</rt></ruby>** — explicit and conservative, requires copulas and nominalizers where needed, always written with <ruby>漢字<rt>ㄏㄚㄋㄐㄧ</rt></ruby> plus ruby.    
3. **[[網語]]体** — clipped and context-driven, often omitting particles, written primarily in <ruby>注音符号<rt>ㄐㄨ·ㄨㄇㄅㄨㄏㄚㄨ</rt></ruby>.

## Meta-parts of speech
1. **[[実詞]]** — nouns, verbs, adjectives; any of these may serve as topic or comment.
2. **[[助詞]]** — case markers, connectives, pronouns, aspect/degree markers, sentence-final particles.
3. **[[特詞]]** — numerals, classifiers, determiners, interjections, ideophones; independent but never predicative.
