## Notes
- [汝 (char)](../characters/汝%20(char).md) "you; thou; intimate second-person pronoun"

汝 is the intimate second-person singular pronoun in Dan'a'yo — "thou; you (intimate)." It appears in the personal pronoun table alongside [[我]] (I), [[君]] (thou, standard register), and the archaic [[俺]] (I, humble). Like [[君]], 汝 is marked *ARCHAIC* in the Dan'a'yo grammar, surviving in literary, classical, and highly formal contexts rather than everyday speech. In Classical Chinese, 汝 is one of several second-person pronouns used in dialogue alongside 爾, 若, and 女; 汝 carries an intimate or condescending register depending on speaker–addressee relationship. As a 代詞 (pronoun), 汝 is free-standing and takes case particles and topic-retaining particles directly.

汝 (ㄋㄛㄧ/noi/뇌 — Mandarin rǔ, Cantonese jyu5, Korean 여, Japanese JO/じょ, Vietnamese nhữ): "you; thou; you (intimate)" — MC 日母 (ȵ) + 魚韻 (ɨʌ). The 日母 is a palatal nasal initial (ȵ, sometimes written nj-); Dan'a'yo consistently preserves it as ㄋ (n-), as also seen in [[而]] (日母+之韻 → ㄋㄧ ni/니). The 魚韻 typically gives plain -o in Dan'a'yo (compare [[与]] ⼄ yo, [[語]] ⼄ yo, [[且]] 초 cho), but after the palatal 日母 the palatal resonance adds an -i off-glide, producing -oi (ㄛㄧ) rather than plain -o: 뇌 (noi). Mandarin rǔ reflects the historical merger of 日母 into retroflex r- and 魚韻 into -ü. Cantonese jyu5 drops the initial and preserves 魚韻 as -yu-. Japanese じょ (JO) reflects 日母 → j- with 魚韻 → -o. Korean 여 drops the initial entirely and gives -yeo. Vietnamese nhữ preserves the palatal nasal as nh- with 魚韻 → -ữ (the standard Sino-Vietnamese vowel for 魚韻).

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 뇌 does not match Korean 여. The initial diverges: Dan'a'yo retains MC 日母 as ㄋ (n-뇌), while Korean drops it entirely (여, initial null). The vowel also diverges: Dan'a'yo gives -oi (the 魚韻 -o base with palatal off-glide from 日母, ㄛㄧ), while Korean gives -yeo (魚韻 merged with a palatal onset in place of the lost initial). This initial-dropping pattern — where 日母 is preserved as ㄋ in Dan'a'yo but zero in Korean — follows the same split seen in [[而]] (Dan'a'yo 니 n-i vs Korean 이 i) and [[与]] (Dan'a'yo 요 yo vs Korean 여 yeo, though 与 is 以母 not 日母).
