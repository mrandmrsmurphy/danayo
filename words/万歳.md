## Notes
- [万](../characters/万.md) "ten thousand" + [歳](../characters/歳.md) "year; age"

The compound means literally "ten thousand years!" — a wish for unlimited longevity directed at a ruler or celebrated person. It is the pan-Sinospheric royal acclamation: Chinese 万岁 wànsuì, Korean 만세 manse, Vietnamese vạn tuế, Japanese 万歳 banzai/manzai all derive from the same classical formula. The traditional-character form 萬歲 (alias of this word) was the standard written form through the early 20th century.

In Chinese, 万岁 (wànsuì) was the prescribed form of address to the emperor — 万岁爷 wànsuì yé, "ten-thousand-year lord" — and its use by any non-imperial person was in principle a capital offense. After the fall of the Qing it broadened into a general exclamation of approval or celebration, stripped of its dangerous monarchic charge.

In Korean, 만세 (manse) is best known today through its association with the March 1st Independence Movement (三一運動, 1919), when protesters across Korea shouted 조선독립만세 — "long live Korean independence" — turning the old imperial acclamation into a cry of nationalist resistance. The phrase remains the standard Korean cheer at celebrations.

In Japanese, 万歳 splits into two distinct words with separate readings and histories. **ばんざい** (banzai) is the Sino-Japanese military and celebratory cheer — shouted with both arms raised, it became internationally recognizable through its use in 20th-century warfare. **まんざい** (manzai) is a completely different tradition: a classical New Year's blessing performance involving comic dialogue, song, and dance, now the dominant form of Japanese stand-up comedy. The modern comic genre usually writes 漫才 to distinguish it, but 万歳 is the historical orthography for both.
