## Notes
### Form
放素 is [放](characters/放.md) “to emit; to give off; to release” + [素](characters/素.md) “element.” The suffix follows the main-group naming rule.

### Motivation
The international name _radium_ comes from Latin _radius_ “ray,” because radium was identified by its intense emission of radiation. Among near-synonyms, 放 is broader and more naturally tied to the idea of something giving off energy or rays continuously. That makes it especially suitable for radium, the element whose very identity in early chemistry was bound up with radioactivity.

### Why 放 belongs to radium more than radon
Radium is the primary “ray-emitter” in the historical naming chain. Radon was originally understood downstream, as an emanation from radium. So if one graph is to carry the stronger, more basic radiation sense, it should belong to radium. Then **射素** can remain for radon as a secondary but still etymologically related formation.

So the pair works well:
- **放素** = radium, the source-like emitter
- **射素** = radon, the raying or shooting-out derivative

That is a real semantic distinction, not just arbitrary graph assignment.

### Comparative note
Modern CJKV forms are mostly phonetic:

- Chinese: 镭
- Japanese: ラジウム
- Korean: 라듐
- Vietnamese: rađi

This form restores the original semantic logic better than those do.

### Systemic fit
This is also good design for the one-character symbol requirement. **放** is clearer and stronger as a standalone elemental sign than trying to reserve it for radon, where the historical connection is slightly less direct.

### Conclusion
Saving **放** for radium was the right move. It matches the etymological center of gravity better than using it for radon, and it lets **放素 / 射素** form a tidy, internally differentiated pair.
