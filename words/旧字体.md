## Notes
- [旧 (char)](../characters/旧%20(char).md) "old; former" + [字 (char)](../characters/字%20(char).md) "character; glyph" + [体](../characters/体.md) "form; body" — synonym of [[繁体字]]

**旧字体** (*jiùzìtǐ*, Japanese *kyūjitai*) denotes the traditional, unreformed forms of Sinographic characters — the script as it stood before the simplification reforms of the 20th century. In Japanese the term specifically designates the pre-1946 forms superseded by [[新字体]] (*shinjitai*) under the Tōyō Kanji reforms of the postwar period. The word foregrounds the temporal dimension: 旧 (*jiù*, "old; former") marks these as the forms that came *before*, the script as inherited rather than revised.

The Chinese-language counterpart is [[繁体字]] (*fántǐzì*, "complex-form characters"), the traditional script still in use in Taiwan, Hong Kong, and Macau against the simplified 简体字 (*jiǎntǐzì*) of mainland China. Where 旧字体 foregrounds time, 繁体字 foregrounds structure — these are the forms with more strokes, the fuller shapes. Both terms describe the same broad stratum of the script.

In Dan'a'yo the two are synonymous. The language draws its character inventory from the classical Sinographic tradition, making the pre-reform/traditional forms its native ground, and no distinction between 旧字体 and 繁体字 is required. The terms can be used interchangeably; 繁体字 is the more common reference in Chinese-language contexts, 旧字体 in Japanese-language and comparative orthographic discussion.

A technical caveat exists for specialists: *kyūjitai* proper encompasses some forms that were standard in Meiji-era Japan but diverge from those used in Taiwan or the classical Chinese tradition, while 繁体字 is grounded in the modern Taiwan/Hong Kong standard. Dan'a'yo, operating from Middle Chinese phonology and classical conventions, does not require this distinction.
