## Notes
- [凶 (char)](../characters/凶%20(char).md) "unlucky; sinister" + [悪 (char)](../characters/悪%20(char).md) "bad; evil"

Heinous; atrocious; morally monstrous in a way that provokes visceral revulsion. The compound layers 凶 (calamitous, sinister — the kind of evil that brings disaster) onto 悪 (bad, evil in a general moral sense) to produce something stronger than either alone: not merely wrong, but actively malevolent and deeply threatening to the social order.

In Japanese legal and media discourse, **凶悪犯罪** (kyōakuhanzai, "heinous crime") is a quasi-formal category describing offenses of exceptional brutality or cruelty — premeditated murder, serial killing, and similar acts that go beyond ordinary criminality to something that strikes society as fundamentally monstrous. The phrase appears in sentencing discussion, criminological research, and newspaper coverage. The intensified compound 極悪 (gokuaku, "utterly evil; diabolical") sits one step further — 極悪非道 ("utterly evil and contrary to human decency") being a fixed literary phrase.

In Chinese, 凶恶 functions both as an adjective ("a vicious criminal; a ferocious beast") and a noun ("ferocity; viciousness"). It describes not only human malice but the threatening appearance of dangerous animals, ominous weather, and anything that radiates an aura of danger and ill will. The same word applies to a murderer's expression and to storm clouds that presage destruction.

As a 性詞 in Dan'a'yo, it modifies nouns directly.
