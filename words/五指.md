## Notes
- [五 (char)](../characters/五%20(char).md) "five" + [指](../characters/指.md) "finger; toe"

The five digits of the hand, collectively. Each finger has a traditional name in classical Chinese, still used in modern East Asian languages:

| Finger | Classical name | Meaning |
|--------|---------------|---------|
| Thumb | 大指 / 拇指 (mǔzhǐ) | "big finger" / "mother finger" |
| Index | 食指 (shízhǐ) | "eating finger" (used to gesture at food) |
| Middle | 中指 (zhōngzhǐ) | "center finger" |
| Ring | 無名指 (wúmíngzhǐ) | "nameless finger" |
| Little | 小指 (xiǎozhǐ) | "small finger" |

The "nameless finger" (無名指) is a striking coinage: the ring finger was so rarely used independently, and considered so lacking in individual function, that classical Chinese simply declined to name it. The same logic produced the German *Ringfinger* (ring finger) and the French *annulaire* (from Latin *anulus*, ring) — but the Chinese framing of namelessness is philosophically distinctive.

In Japanese, **五指に入る** (goshi ni hairu, "to enter the five fingers") means to rank in the top five of any category — the image being that all things worth counting can be tallied on one hand. Conversely, 五指に余る (goshi ni amaru, "to exceed the five fingers") means "too many to count on one hand," hence "numerous." This extended meaning is specific to Japanese and is standard in sports commentary and competitive rankings.

The philosophical phrase **五指の別** ("the distinction among the five fingers") expresses the principle that things may be equal in kind but different in degree — all five are fingers, yet they differ in length and function. It is used as a metaphor for equality-within-hierarchy among members of a group.
