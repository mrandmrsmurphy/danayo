## Notes
- [猩](../characters/猩.md) "red ape; vivid red" + [蝿](../characters/蝿.md) "fly" — fruit fly; *Drosophila* spp.; the red-eyed fly

The name traces through the Japanese 猩々蠅 (ショウジョウバエ, shōjōbae) — "the fly of the 猩々," a legendary red-faced ape spirit of Chinese origin. The name was given because the vivid red eyes of *Drosophila* recall the scarlet face of the 猩々; the compound 猩蝿 shortens this to two characters, keeping the key reference. Chinese uses 果蝇 (guǒyíng, fruit fly) or 黑腹果蝇 (the common lab species *D. melanogaster*).

*Drosophila melanogaster* is one of the most important model organisms in the history of biology. Thomas Hunt Morgan's laboratory at Columbia University began using it in genetics research around 1908; its short generation time (ten days at room temperature), large chromosomes, and easy breeding made it ideal for the study of heredity. Morgan's group used *Drosophila* to establish that genes are carried on chromosomes — work that earned Morgan the 1933 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine. The *Drosophila* genome was among the first animal genomes fully sequenced (2000). Today it remains central to research in developmental biology, neuroscience, and aging.
