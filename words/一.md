## Notes
- [一 (char)](../characters/一%20(char).md) "one"

一 is the numeral "one" — the basic unit of the Dan'a'yo decimal counting system and the 22nd entry on the Swadesh list. The character is a 指事 (indicative symbol): a single horizontal stroke represents the abstract concept "one" directly, rather than depicting an object. It is traditionally the first character taught to literate learners across the whole CJKV sphere. In compounds it combines with higher numerals and orders of magnitude to build larger numbers ([[十一]] "eleven", [[二十]] "twenty") and is also placed before the words for larger units — 十, 百, 千, 万, 億, 兆, 溝, 半 — to disambiguate ambiguous quantities (e.g. clarifying "one hundred" against a bare "hundred").

Mandarin 一 (yī, one): 一切 (yīqiè, entirety), 統一 (tǒngyī, unify). Cantonese 一 (jat1). Japanese has a formal/native split typical of low numerals: on'yomi いち (ichi) or いつ (itsu, bound form seen in compounds like 一致 いっち "agreement") versus native ひとつ (hitotsu, used for general counting) — the on'yomi いち is what stands alone as "one." Korean 일 (il) is the Sino-Korean reading used in compounds and formal counting, while 하나 (hana) is the native numeral used in everyday counting and age. Vietnamese nhất is the Sino-Vietnamese reading, reserved for ordinals and set compounds (thứ nhất "first"), while everyday counting uses the native một.

一 also has two coincidental Dan'a'yo homophones sharing its exact reading ㄧㄊ/'id/읻: [[壱]], the complex/formal variant of the numeral used on financial documents and legal instruments specifically to prevent forgery (since 一's single stroke is trivially altered by adding more strokes), and [[逸]] "flee, escape," an entirely unrelated character that happens to share the same Middle Chinese syllable.

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 읻 (id) and Korean 일 (il) diverge in their final consonant even though both derive from the same Middle Chinese source, ʔ (影母, glottal-stop null initial) + iɪt (質三韻, entering tone with a -t coda). Dan'a'yo preserves the entering-tone coda faithfully as -d (읻 = i + ᆮ), the regular Dan'a'yo treatment of MC -t codas (cf. 六 ㄌㄨㄎ→lug, 十 ㄙㄧㄆ→sib, each keeping its own coda type). Sino-Korean, by contrast, shows the well-documented historical shift of MC -t codas to -l in the modern language (일, not the expected 읻) — the same pattern seen in 日 (il), 七 (chil), and 八 (pal). Dan'a'yo and Korean therefore start from an identical MC source but diverge because Korean underwent a coda-lenition sound change that Dan'a'yo's derivation does not replicate.
