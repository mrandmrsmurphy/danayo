## Notes
- [太 (char)](../characters/太%20(char).md) "great" + [学](../characters/学.md) "learn" — "the great school," the highest tier of classical education, contrasted with [[小学]] and built typically alongside lesser regional academies like [[書院]].

The Chinese 太學 was founded in 124 BC under Emperor Wu of Han, on Dong Zhongshu's advice, as the empire's central institution for training officials in the Confucian classics; its enrolled scholars (博士弟子) could rise directly into the bureaucracy, giving the institution from its founding a direct line into state power that schools like the regional 書院 never had at the same scale. It continued, with interruptions and refoundings, through successive dynasties before its functions were eventually absorbed into the later Imperial Academy system known as the 國子監.

The same name and institution were also adopted outside China: [[高句麗]] founded its own 太學 in 372 AD under King Sosurim, two years after promulgating its first legal code and around the time it formally adopted Buddhism — together, often read as a coordinated package of state-building reforms. Goguryeo's 太學 is the earliest national educational institution recorded in Korean history, training the sons of the aristocracy in the Confucian classics and, reportedly, military arts, to staff the kingdom's civil and military administration; it remained in operation for over two centuries. Vietnam's Thái Học names the same tier of state-sponsored higher education, though Vietnamese historiography more often foregrounds the specific institution Quốc Tử Giám as the equivalent of China's later Imperial Academy.
