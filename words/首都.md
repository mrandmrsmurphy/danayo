## Notes
- [首](../characters/首.md) "head; first; leading" + [都](../characters/都.md) "metropolis; all; the great city"

首都 frames the capital city as the "head" of the political body: 首 (the head, that which leads, that which comes first) combined with 都 (the great metropolis, the city that concentrates administrative and cultural authority). The metaphor of the body politic with a capital as its head is ancient and cross-cultural, but the compound 首都 encodes it directly in its graphemic logic. 都 alone historically denoted the imperial capital — Heian-kyō (京都, "capital city") preserves 都 in this sense — but 首都 specifies more precisely the political and administrative capital as distinct from merely the largest or most culturally important city.

The compound is universal across modern CJKV usage. In Chinese 首都 (shǒudū) refers to national capitals, and Beijing (北京) is 中國首都. In Japanese しゅと (shuto) is the standard word, with 首都圏 (shutōken, "capital region") denoting the greater Tokyo metropolitan area; a prolonged political discussion about relocating Japan's capital (首都移転) has run for decades. In Korean 수도 (sudo) is the standard term; 수도권 (sudogwon, "capital region") refers to the Seoul metropolitan area, which contains roughly half the South Korean population. Vietnamese thủ đô is the Sino-Vietnamese equivalent and is the standard formal term for any national capital.

The kwin=false marking reflects the divergence between Dan'a'yo 슈도 (syu + do) and Korean 수도 (sudo) — 首 maps to 수 in Korean Sino-Korean phonology but to 슈 in Dan'a'yo, a consistent vowel difference in this phonological class.
