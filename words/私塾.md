## Notes
- [私](../characters/私.md) "private; personal" + [塾](../characters/塾.md) "private school"

**Classical meaning.** In pre-modern East Asia, a 私塾 was a privately run school operated out of a scholar's home or a local patron's premises, where students were drilled in the Confucian classics, calligraphy, and literary composition. Unlike the state-sponsored examination academies, the 私塾 had no fixed curriculum, no state oversight, and no formal graduation — everything depended on the master. This was the dominant form of basic education in China, Japan, Korea, and Vietnam until the introduction of modern public schooling in the late nineteenth century.

**Modern meaning.** In modern Japanese, 塾 (じゅく, *juku*) has shifted to mean a supplementary private tutoring school — the "cram school" that students attend after regular school hours to prepare for entrance examinations. 私塾 retains this sense: a small, privately run tutoring operation, as opposed to a large commercial chain. The term carries a slight traditionalist connotation, evoking the old master-student relationship more than a modern test-prep centre.

**Korean note.** Korean speakers should take care not to use [[学院]] for this concept. In Korean, 학원 (學院) is the everyday word for a private tutoring academy or cram school — the 학원 industry (hagwon culture) is enormous in South Korea. But in Dan'a'yo, 學院/学院 means a college or formal educational institute, following the Chinese and Japanese usage. The Dan'a'yo word for a cram school or private tutoring school is 私塾.
