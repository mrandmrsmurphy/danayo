## Notes
- [魚 (char)](../characters/魚%20(char).md) "fish"

魚 in Dan'a'yo is the noun for "fish" and serves as the determiner (限定詞) for aquatic creatures — appearing in naming compounds to classify sea animals: 鯤魚 (the mythical giant fish Kūn), 堅魚 (skipjack tuna; bonito), 魚鰭 (fish fin), 魚雷 (torpedo — lit. "fish thunder"), 周魚 (sea bream). The character is a canonical 象形 pictograph of a fish — head at top, tail at bottom, scales visible in earlier forms — and serves as Kangxi radical 195. 魚 appears on the Swadesh list (entry 45: "fish"), reflecting its status as a culturally and linguistically universal concept shared across all human societies. 魚 also serves as the Dan'a'yo stand-in for the character [[魚]].

Mandarin 鱼 (yú, simplified; 魚 traditional): the standard word for fish — 鱼肉 (yúròu, fish meat), 金鱼 (jīnyú, goldfish), 热带鱼 (rèdàiyú, tropical fish). Cantonese 魚 (jyu4): 鱸魚 (lou4 jyu4, sea bass), 金魚 (gam1 jyu4, goldfish); 魚蛋 (jyu4 daan2, fish ball) is an iconic street food. Japanese 魚 (さかな/sakana or うお/uo, native; ぎょ/GYO, Sino-Japanese): 魚介類 (gyokairui, seafood — lit. "fish-shellfish species"), 金魚 (kingyo, goldfish). Korean 물고기 (mulgogi, native: lit. "water meat") is the common word; Sino-Korean 어 (魚) in formal/biological compounds: 어류 (魚類, fish species), 인어 (人魚, mermaid — lit. "person-fish"). Vietnamese cá: con cá (a fish), cá vàng (goldfish); Sino-Vietnamese ngư (魚) in literary compounds: ngư dân (漁民, fisherman), hải ngư (sea fish).

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 요 does not match Korean 어. 魚 reads 요 ('yo/⼄) in Dan'a'yo — MC 疑母 (ŋ) + 魚韻 (ɨʌ) gives a null initial (疑母 drops in Dan'a'yo as in Mandarin and Japanese) with the -ɨʌ vowel yielding -yo in Dan'a'yo, preserving a -y- medial that reflects the MC -ɨ- fronting element. Sino-Korean 어 (eo) maps the same MC vowel to plain -eo without the -y- medial, losing it through a regular historical reduction. The MC 魚韻 (ɨʌ) produced 요 (-yo, with preserved -y-) in Dan'a'yo and 어 (-eo, without) in Korean — the same divergence established in [[於]] (Dan'a'yo 오/ㄛ vs Korean 어), where Dan'a'yo retains a medial element that Korean simplifies.
