## Notes
- [書](../characters/書.md) "writing; documents" + [道 (char)](../characters/道%20(char).md) "way; path; discipline"

**書道** (*shūdào*, Japanese *shodō*) is Japanese calligraphy — the disciplined art of brush-writing, conceived not merely as technical skill but as a complete path of cultivation. The 道 suffix places it alongside [[柔道]], [[空手道]], [[武士道]], and [[茶道]] in the Japanese cultural framework of *dō* disciplines: practices in which mastery of a physical technique becomes inseparable from aesthetic refinement, ethical development, and the cultivation of mind.

The distinction in terminology is meaningful across the Sinosphere. Chinese calligraphy is **書法** (*shūfǎ*, "method/law of writing") — a term emphasising technical principle and the normative standards of the great calligraphic lineages. Korean calligraphy is **書藝** (*seoyě*, "art of writing") — foregrounding aesthetic achievement. 書道, "Way of Writing," foregrounds the disciplinary and formative character of the practice: the process is as important as the product, and a lifetime of study is the expected horizon. All three traditions share a common foundation in the Chinese classical canon of scripts and masters, but each has developed a distinct institutional culture and aesthetic sensibility.

The canonical scripts of 書道 include 楷書 (*kaisho*, block/regular script), 行書 (*gyōsho*, semi-cursive), 草書 (*sōsho*, fully cursive), and the distinctly Japanese forms 仮名 (*kana*, the cursive syllabary derived from Chinese cursive) and 仮名まじり文 (mixed kana-kanji script). The foundational Chinese masters — **王羲之** (*Wáng Xīzhī*, 303–361 AD), universally regarded as the Sage of Calligraphy (書聖), and **顔真卿** (*Yán Zhēnqīng*, 709–785 AD) — remain the primary models in Japanese calligraphic education, their works transmitted through centuries of copying and commentary.

The tools are the **文房四宝** (*bunbō shihō*, "Four Treasures of the Study"): 筆 (*fude*, brush), 墨 (*sumi*, ink-stick), 硯 (*suzuri*, inkstone), and 紙 (*kami*, paper). The preparation of ink — grinding the stick slowly on the stone with water — is itself understood as a meditative preparation, not a mere preliminary.

書道 was a required subject in Japanese compulsory education through much of the postwar period, and remains a standard component of the curriculum. National competitions, licensing bodies, and a tiered rank system (段/kyū) structure the field's institutional life in ways that parallel the martial *dō* disciplines. The practice also carries a strong connection to Zen Buddhism, particularly in the ink-wash tradition where a single brushstroke may be an act of contemplative expression.

Korean 서도 (*seodo*) uses the same compound and overlaps substantially with 書藝 in everyday usage; Vietnamese *thư đạo* is a direct Sino-Vietnamese calque, though the standard term in Vietnamese is *thư pháp* (書法), following the Chinese usage.
