## Notes
- [三 (char)](../characters/三%20(char).md) "three" + [十](../characters/十%20(char).md) "ten" — for anti-forgery, use [[参拾]] instead.

Straightforward "three-ten" compositional numeral, consistent as sānshí/saam1 sap6/samsip across Mandarin, Cantonese, and Korean, with no divergence. Vietnamese is the outlier: everyday and mathematical Vietnamese both use the native numeral ba mươi, not the Sino-Vietnamese reading tam thập — the Sino form survives only in fixed classical contexts, most famously the Confucian phrase 三十而立 (tam thập nhi lập, "at thirty, one stands firm"). The classical single-glyph form 卅 (a fusion of three vertical strokes off a base, distinct from 三十's two-character spelling) survives as an alias, mostly in dating inscriptions and older documents. Japanese carries its own extra wrinkle: alongside the ordinary Sino-Japanese count-word sanjuu, the native-reading 三十路 (misoji) names the age bracket itself — "one's thirties" — the way English might say "the big three-oh."
