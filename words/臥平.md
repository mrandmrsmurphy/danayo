## Notes
- [臥 (char)](../characters/臥%20(char).md) "lie down, crouch" + [平](../characters/平.md) "even, flat"

臥平 is the Dan'a'yo rendering of 躺平 (tǎng píng), the Chinese internet coinage that went viral in April 2021 following an anonymous Baidu Tieba post titled 躺平即是正義 — "Lying Flat Is Justice." The term names a deliberate refusal to participate in the economy of ambition: no overtime, no property purchase, no marriage, no children, the bare minimum and nothing more. Its target was the logic of 内捲 (involution), the exhausting competitive spiral in which effort multiplies but outcomes do not improve.

The metaphor is postural: to lie down and stay down. 躺 (lie face-up) and 平 (flat) together describe a body that refuses to rise. 躺 and 瞓 are too narrowly Chinese to carry the concept across the CJKV sphere; 臥 (recline, prostrate) does the same work while remaining legible in Japanese, Korean, and Vietnamese. The choice of 臥 is not incidental — the character underlies 臥薪嘗膽 (to endure hardship in pursuit of revenge), which makes the contrast pointed: where that idiom demands rising from prostration, 臥平 chooses to remain there.

The concept finds parallels across East Asia. Korean 포기세대 and N포세대 name successive generations abandoning marriage, children, housing, and career. Japanese さとり世代 (the Enlightened Generation, born roughly 1987–2004) are described as having no desires, no ambition, and no appetite for risk — not from despair but from a pragmatic reading of their circumstances. Western "quiet quitting" touches the same surface but is typically framed as a workplace grievance rather than a generational worldview.

In China the authorities moved quickly to censor 躺平 content in 2021, treating it as ideologically dangerous — a repudiation of the productivist narrative the state promotes. This censorship pressure, and the fact that the original viral post was deleted within days, gave the term a sharper political charge than its Korean and Japanese cousins, which circulate freely.
