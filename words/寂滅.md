## Notes
- [寂](../characters/寂.md) "silent, still" + [滅](../characters/滅.md) "wipe out, extinguish" — the extinguishing of desire and suffering into perfect stillness: the Buddhist concept of nirvana. This word is the viable form of [寂](../characters/寂.md): 寂 alone is too bound to stand as a Dan'a'yo word, and 寂滅 is its `stand_in` compound, deliberately chosen to keep 寂's sense the Buddhist/technical one — as seen in 入寂 ("to die," of a monk, lit. "enter stillness") and 圓寂 (a monk's death, "perfect cessation") — in contrast to [[寂寞]] (the ordinary, pedestrian "lonely, desolate" that [[寞]] alone now stands in for, once 寂 is spoken for here).

寂滅 (jakumetsu) opens one of the most famous lines in Japanese literature: 諸行無常、寂滅を楽と為す ("all things are impermanent; nirvana is bliss"), from the Nirvana Sutra and echoed at the start of the Heike Monogatari. Mandarin jìmiè and Cantonese zik6mit6 carry the same Buddhist technical weight — the Sanskrit nirvāṇa is rendered directly as 涅槃, with 寂滅 serving as its native gloss/synonym ("the extinguishing," calqued rather than transliterated). Korean 적멸 (jeokmyeol) preserves the identical Buddhist register, appearing in temple names and sutra translations (積善寂滅, 寂滅寶宮 at Woljeongsa). None of the four daughters use 寂滅 outside religious/philosophical register — it never drifted into ordinary "quiet" the way 寂静 or 寂寞 did.
