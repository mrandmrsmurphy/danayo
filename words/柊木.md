## Notes
- [柊](../characters/柊.md) "holly osmanthus" + [木](../characters/木%20(char).md) "tree; wood" — stand-in for [[柊]], which cannot appear independently
- 柊木 follows the same naming pattern as 桑木, 樺木, and 檀木 — a tree-character bound to the generic 木 "tree" so that it can stand as a free word — and names the holly osmanthus (*Osmanthus heterophyllus*), an evergreen shrub with spiny, holly-like leaves and small white autumn flowers prized in East Asian gardens for both its fragrance and its folk-magical reputation as a ward against evil spirits, traditionally hung at doorways during the Setsubun festival alongside dried sardine heads. The character 柊 itself is a Japanese-coined kokuji-like form (its 06-stroke phonetic 冬 hints at the plant's winter blooming), used overwhelmingly as a name element in Japan — both as the plant-name ヒイラギ (hiiragi, its everyday kun-reading) and as a popular modern given name. The compositional Sino-Japanese しゅうぼく (shūboku) and Sino-Korean 종목 (jongmok) belong to a more bookish, classificatory register — and the latter happens to be a perfect homophone of the everyday Korean word 種目 "event, category, item," a coincidence of sound rather than sense.
