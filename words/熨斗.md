## Notes
- [熨](../characters/熨.md) "to iron, press flat" + [斗 (char)](../characters/斗%20(char).md) "ladle, dipper" — this word is the stand-in for [熨](../characters/熨.md), which cannot appear independently in Dan'a'yo.

熨斗 names the flatiron — historically not the flat sole-plate of the modern electric appliance, but a long-handled bronze or iron pan shaped like a ladle or scoop (hence 斗, "dipper, measuring-cup," the same character as the Big Dipper's 斗), filled with hot coals or heated sand and drawn across cloth to press out wrinkles. The name is transparently descriptive: 熨 "to press with heat" + 斗 "ladle-shaped vessel," describing the tool by its literal shape and function rather than by any metaphor.

**Japanese has drifted to an entirely unrelated meaning.** Modern Japanese does not use 熨斗 for the ironing appliance at all — that's アイロン, a loanword from English "iron." Instead, のし (noshi) names a specific, still-current ceremonial object: a thin strip of dried, stretched abalone (or later, a printed paper facsimile of one) folded into an origami-like decoration and attached to the upper right corner of a formal gift, historically as a token of good fortune and, in its origin, of the giver's own trouble in preparing something for the occasion. The connection to "flatiron" is indirect at best (both involve pressing or stretching something flat), and the semantic distance is wide enough that a Japanese speaker encountering the tool called 熨斗 in Chinese or Korean would not readily connect it to their own のし.

Mandarin (yùndǒu), Cantonese (wan6 dau2), Korean (울두, uldu), and Vietnamese (uất đẩu) all preserve the "iron" meaning directly and transparently, in contrast to Japanese's divergence. The compound's Korean reading uses 熨's *other* attested reading: 熨 has two Korean readings split by sense (per user, 2026-07-19) — 울 (ul) "to iron," the one this compound uses, and 위 (wi) "to apply heat/press medically," the one stored on [the character page](../characters/熨.md) alongside its matching `korean_native` 찜질할. Not a data error, just polyphony; no correction needed on either side.
