## Notes
- [至 (char)](../characters/至%20(char).md) "reach; utmost; arrive at" + [善 (char)](../characters/善%20(char).md) "good; virtuous; excellent" — that at which goodness arrives; the summit of the good.

至善 is most famously anchored in the opening of the Great Learning (《大學》), one of the Four Books of the Confucian canon:

> 大學之道，在明明德，在親民，在止於至善。
> "The way of the Great Learning lies in illuminating illustrious virtue, in renewing the people, and in resting in the highest good."

止於至善 — "to rest/abide in the highest good" — is the teleological aim of Confucian self-cultivation: not merely to improve but to arrive at the summit of moral excellence and dwell there stably. The character 止 ("stop, rest, abide") is crucial: it implies not just reaching 至善 but being settled in it as one's permanent condition.

As a translation of the Latin summum bonum (Cicero's rendering of Aristotle's ἄκρον ἀγαθόν, "the highest good"), 至善 functions across three philosophical traditions simultaneously:
- **Confucian**: the culminating aim of the Great Learning's programme of moral cultivation
- **Aristotelian/Thomistic**: the ultimate end of human action — εὐδαιμονία for Aristotle, the beatific vision for Aquinas
- **Kantian**: Kant's höchstes Gut — the combination of virtue and happiness that is the complete object of practical reason

The convergence of these three traditions in a single compound makes 至善 one of the richest terms in Dan'a'yo's philosophical vocabulary, and a natural site for comparative ethical enquiry.
