## Notes
- [五 (char)](../characters/五%20(char).md) "five" + [角 (char)](../characters/角%20(char).md) "horn; angle" + [形 (char)](../characters/形%20(char).md) "shape; form; type"

A pentagon — a polygon with five sides and five interior angles. Compare [[七角形]] (heptagon) and [[二次元]] (two-dimensional space); the suffix -角形 (-kakukei in Japanese, -gakhyeong in Korean) is the productive East Asian morpheme for polygon names: 三角形 (triangle), 四角形 (quadrilateral), 六角形 (hexagon), and so on.

The **regular pentagon** (all five sides equal, all five angles equal) has interior angles of exactly 108° and is particularly notable in mathematics for its relationship to the **golden ratio** (φ ≈ 1.618): the ratio of a diagonal to a side of a regular pentagon is exactly φ. The five-pointed star (五芒星, pentagram) formed by connecting the diagonals of a regular pentagon subdivides itself recursively into pentagons and golden triangles — a property that fascinated Greek mathematicians and was considered mystical by the Pythagoreans.

The most famous modern pentagon is the headquarters of the United States Department of Defense in Arlington, Virginia — a building known internationally simply as "the Pentagon." In Japanese it is called ペンタゴン (Pentagón, from the Greek) rather than 五角形.

In East Asian tradition, the pentagonal shape appears in the 五芒星 (gobosei in Japanese), a five-pointed star used as a protective symbol in Shinto — most famously associated with the Heian-period onmyōji (陰陽師) Abe no Seimei and still seen today on the Seiman crests of Shinto shrines.
