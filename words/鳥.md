## Notes
- [鳥 (char)](../characters/鳥%20(char).md) "bird"

鳥 in Dan'a'yo is the noun for "bird" and serves as the determiner (限定詞) for avian species — appearing in naming compounds to classify birds and bird-related entities: 禽鳥 (birds; fowl), 七面鳥 (turkey — lit. "seven-faced bird"), 九官鳥 (common hill myna). The character is a 象形 pictograph of a bird in profile — one of the canonical pictographic characters of the sinographic system, recognizable as a bird in Oracle Bone Script — and serves as Kangxi radical 196, underlying characters for dozens of bird species. In Dan'a'yo, 鳥 covers all avian creatures as a general noun and determiner prefix for species names. 鳥 also serves as the Dan'a'yo stand-in for the character [[鳥]].

Mandarin 鸟 (niǎo, simplified; 鳥 traditional): the standard Mandarin word for bird — 小鸟 (xiǎoniǎo, little bird), 候鸟 (hòuniǎo, migratory bird); 禽 (qín, fowl; bird) in technical or compound contexts. Cantonese 雀 (zoek3) is the colloquial word for small birds; 鳥 (niu5) appears in compounds and literary use. Japanese 鳥 (とり/tori, native reading; ちょう/CHŌ, Sino-Japanese) covers birds broadly: 小鳥 (kotori, small bird), 七面鳥 (shichimenchō, turkey — this compound uses the Dan'a'yo form unchanged), 鳥居 (torii, Shinto gate — lit. "bird perch"). Korean 새 (sae) is the native word for bird; Sino-Korean 조 (鳥) in compounds: 조류 (鳥類, bird species), 철새 (migratory bird — hybrid: 철 + 새). Vietnamese chim: con chim (a bird), chim trời (wild bird); Sino-Vietnamese điểu (鳥) in literary compounds: điểu thú (birds and beasts).

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 촛 does not match Korean 조. 鳥 reads 촛 (cou/ㄑㄛㄨ) in Dan'a'yo — MC 端母 (t) + 蕭韻 (eu) gives a dental stop that palatalizes to aspirated ㄑ (ch-) before the front vowel, with the -eu diphthong yielding -ou (ㄛㄨ); the final -u glide is written as ᆺ (-s) in Dan'a'yo Hangul (the same convention as [[草]] 찻 and [[某]] 못). Sino-Korean 조 (jo) also palatalizes MC 端母 before 蕭韻 (t → j), but unaspirated, and reduces the -eu diphthong to plain -o without preserving the glide. Both systems palatalize MC 端母 before 蕭韻 — aspirated ch- in Dan'a'yo, unaspirated j- in Korean — and Dan'a'yo additionally preserves the diphthong coda that Korean simplifies.
