## Notes
克/극/khắc share the "overcome, restrain" core across Mandarin, Korean, and Vietnamese (克己/극기/khắc kỷ, "self-restraint"; 克服/극복, "to overcome"). Mandarin alone carries a second, everyday sense entirely unrelated to that core: 克 (kè) is the standard Chinese word for the metric unit "gram," adopted when China formally switched to the metric system in 1929 — likely chosen for its rough phonetic resemblance to the Western word. Cantonese hak1 follows Mandarin's lead here, appearing as the gram unit on packaging and nutrition labels. Japanese, Korean, and Vietnamese all went a different route for the same unit, borrowing the Western word directly instead of repurposing their own cognate character: グラム (guramu), 그램 (geuraem), and gam are all loanwords, leaving 克/극/khắc to mean only "overcome, restrain" in those three languages, with no unit sense at all.
