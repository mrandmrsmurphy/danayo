## Notes
- [苦 (char)](../characters/苦%20(char).md) "bitter" + [味](../characters/味.md) "taste, flavor" — bitter taste; bitterness as a flavor quality

苦味 is one of the five canonical flavors (五味) in East Asian taste taxonomy: 酸 (sour), 甘 (sweet), 苦 (bitter), 辛 (pungent/spicy), and 鹹 (salty). In the five-phase (五行) system of traditional Chinese medicine, bitter corresponds to the heart (心) and fire (火), and is held to drain heat, dry dampness, and consolidate the yin. Bitter herbs — 黄連 (coptis), 苦参 (sophora root), 黄柏 (phellodendron bark) — form a core category of medicinal plants precisely because of their 苦味.

Evolutionarily, sensitivity to bitter taste is a defense mechanism: most plant-derived toxins, including alkaloids and glucosinolates, are bitter. The tongue's bitterness receptors (T2R family) are unusually numerous and diverse compared with other taste receptors, reflecting the evolutionary pressure to detect a wide range of toxic compounds. The 苦味 of 苦瓜 (bitter melon), tea, coffee, and dark chocolate lies along a spectrum from warning signal to acquired pleasure.

Japanese にがみ (nigami) uses the native kun reading rather than the on'yomi くみ (kumi), suggesting the word was naturalized into Japanese at the level of direct sensory experience rather than through literary borrowing.
