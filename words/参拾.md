## Notes
- [参](../characters/参.md) "three (anti-forgery form)" + [拾](../characters/拾.md) "ten (anti-forgery form)" — the formal, fraud-resistant way of writing the numeral [[三十]] ("thirty").

Ordinary numerals 三, 十, 百, 千, etc. are visually simple and easy to alter with an extra stroke — a single brushstroke can turn 三 into 二 or 五, or 十 into 千. To prevent tampering on financial documents, checks, contracts, and receipts, the Sinosphere developed a parallel set of "anti-forgery" or "formal" numeral characters (大寫數字), of which [[参]]/[[拾]] are the most common substitutes for 三/十. **参拾** is therefore not a separate word so much as a formal orthographic variant of [[三十]], used wherever a number must be written in a way that resists alteration — most prominently on banknote amounts, legal documents, and accounting ledgers.

The practice and its vocabulary are shared across Mandarin, Cantonese, Japanese, and Korean financial and legal writing, though the degree to which formal numerals are still used in everyday life varies — they remain essentially mandatory on Japanese and Chinese checks and many legal instruments, while more informal in casual Korean usage.
