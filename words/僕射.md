## Notes
- [僕](../characters/僕.md) "servant" + [射](../characters/射.md) "shoot" — the office title is a fossil of its Qin-dynasty origin, when it named the chief steward in charge of the ruler's household archers. In this word 射 takes an exceptional reading — yè in Mandarin rather than its ordinary shè — and this exception propagates unusually consistently across the Sinosphere: Japanese reads it ぼくや (BOKU + YA, not BOKU + SHA), Korean 복야 (not 복사), and Vietnamese most often Bộc Dạ (with Bộc Xạ also attested as a more conservative, regularized alternative). The Dan'a'yo reading bogsya does not reproduce this exception, following the usual convention of mechanical concatenation from each character's ordinary bound-form value — the same treatment given to 句's exceptional gōu reading in [[高句麗]].

僕射 grew from this minor household post into one of the most powerful offices in the imperial bureaucracy. Under the Han, the 僕射 served as deputy to the Minister Steward; by the Sui and Tang dynasties, the title had attached itself to the Department of State Affairs (尚書省) as 尚書左僕射 and 尚書右僕射 (Left and Right Vice Directors), the two officials who, alongside the Left and Right Chancellors, formed the executive core of the entire central government — at their peak under the early Tang, the Tang Vice Directors' authority rivaled that of the Chancellor himself. The office was gradually eclipsed and eventually abolished as the Song and later dynasties reorganized central administration around other titles, leaving 僕射 as a historical term rather than a living one.
