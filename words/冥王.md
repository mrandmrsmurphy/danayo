## Notes
- [冥 (char)](../characters/冥%20(char).md) "dark; gloomy; the underworld" + [王 (char)](../characters/王%20(char).md) "king" — "King of the Underworld"; the shared Sinographic title for the ruler of the realm of the dead across traditions; see also [[冥王星]] (the planet Pluto)

**冥王** (*míngwáng*) is the title of the sovereign ruler of the underworld — the lord of the realm of the dead — applied across three major traditions: the Greek **Hades** (Ἅιδης), the Roman **Pluto** (Plūtō), and the Hindu-Buddhist **Yama** (閻魔/閻羅). The compound is compositionally transparent: 冥 (darkness, obscurity, the unseen world beneath) + 王 (king). The 冥 component alone functions as a term for the underworld or the realm of death in classical Chinese; 冥王 then parallels the compound logic of 天王 (heavenly king) and 海王 (sea king).

**Hades** (Greek Ἅιδης, "the unseen") is the ruler of the Greek underworld — not the underworld itself, though the name came to denote both the god and his realm. In the Olympian cosmology, the three sons of Kronos divided the world by lot: Zeus received the sky, Poseidon the sea, and Hades the underworld. Hades is not the personification of death (that is Thanatos) but its administrator — implacable, permanent, and strictly neutral. His realm receives all the dead without exception. He is associated with the cap of invisibility (the Helm of Darkness), the three-headed dog Cerberus who guards the entrance, and his queen **Persephone** (Περσεφόνη), abducted from the upper world and bound to the underworld for a portion of each year — the mythological explanation for winter.

**Pluto** (Latin Plūtō, from Greek Πλούτων, "the wealthy one") is the Roman name for the same figure, derived from a Greek euphemism: the dead were said to go to the "Wealthy One" because all things eventually return to the earth, and the earth contains all mineral riches. The name Pluto gradually displaced Hades in Greek usage as well, as the euphemism felt less ominous than the direct name. The planet **冥王星** — discovered by Clyde Tombaugh in 1930 AD and named Pluto for the god — carries the compound forward into astronomy; it was reclassified as a dwarf planet by the International Astronomical Union in 2006 AD.

**Yama** (Sanskrit यम, "twin; restrainer") is the Hindu and Buddhist lord of death. In the Rigveda, Yama is the first mortal to have died, and as such he became the ruler and judge of the dead — the one who established the path to the afterlife that all subsequent mortals follow. Buddhist transmission carried Yama into East Asia as **閻羅王** (Chinese: Yánluówáng; Japanese: Enma-ō; Korean: 염라대왕, Yeomna Daewang), the presiding judge of the dead who weighs the merit of the deceased and assigns their rebirth. In the developed East Asian Buddhist cosmology, he heads the **十王** (Ten Kings of Hell), each governing a specific court of judgement through which the dead must pass. This figure is iconographically distinct from the Vedic Yama — he acquired a green or black face, fierce demeanor, a mirror (業鏡, the karma mirror that reflects all deeds), and a register of deeds — and became a fixture of East Asian popular religion, literature, and visual art.

The convergence of all three figures under 冥王 reflects the cross-traditional role of the term as a Sinographic umbrella: the concept of a sovereign ruler of the underworld is near-universal in world mythology (compare Egyptian Osiris, Mesopotamian Ereshkigal, Norse Hel), and 冥王 serves as the generalising title when the specific tradition is not at issue or when a single term is needed to span all of them.

Japanese **冥王** (*meiō*) is used for Hades and Pluto in classical and literary contexts; for the Buddhist Yama, Japanese prefers 閻魔大王 (*Enma Daiō*). Korean **명왕** (*myeongwang*) functions similarly — used for Pluto/Hades in Western-mythology contexts; Yama is typically 염라대왕 (*Yeomna Daewang*). Vietnamese **minh vương** is the Sino-Vietnamese compound; for Yama, Vietnamese uses 閻羅 (*Diêm La*) or 閻王 (*Diêm Vương*).
