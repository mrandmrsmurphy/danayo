## Notes
- [万](../characters/万.md) "ten thousand" + [年 (char)](../characters/年%20(char).md) "year" — a span of ten thousand years; by extension, eternity or "all ages."

**万年** (*wànnián*) sits at the top of the small family of durational time-compounds alongside [[百年]] (a hundred years) and [[千年]] (a thousand years), but functions less as a literal measurement and more as a hyperbole for permanence — much as English "for ten thousand years" or "forever" exaggerates rather than counts. 萬年不變 ("unchanging for ten thousand years") and 萬年青 (lit. "ten-thousand-year green," the common name for several evergreen plants prized as symbols of longevity) are typical examples.

Japanese **まんねん** (*mannen*) carries this same sense of "eternal, perpetual," and is the source of several familiar compounds: 万年筆 (lit. "ten-thousand-year pen," i.e. "fountain pen" — so named for its durability compared to dip pens), and the wry 万年補欠/万年〜 pattern used for someone perpetually stuck in a role (e.g. 万年最下位, "perpetually in last place"). Korean **만년** (*mannyeon*) and Vietnamese **vạn niên** preserve the same "eternal, perpetual" extension — Korean 만년설 ("perpetual snow," i.e. permanent snowcap) parallels the Japanese 万年雪 directly.

Geologically and astronomically, 万年 also serves as a convenient round unit for deep time in popular science writing across the Sinosphere — "百万年" (a million years), "一億年" (a hundred million years) — extending the same "year"-counting logic established by 百年/千年/万年 indefinitely upward.
