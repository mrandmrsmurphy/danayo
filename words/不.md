## Notes
- [不 (char)](../characters/不%20(char).md) "not; un-; non-"

不 is the primary negation particle in Dan'a'yo — "not; un-; non-" — prefixing to a verb, adjective, or noun to negate it: 不食 (not eat; does not eat), 不可 (cannot; not permissible — cf. [[可]]), 不有 (does not have; lacks). Among the Dan'a'yo mood particles, 不 is the core negation word, appearing in the compounds [[可不]] (able to not; can decline), [[不可以]] (cannot; may not), and the double negation [[不可不]] (cannot not = must necessarily). As the second most used character in Classical Chinese, 不 carries an extensive range of negation functions inherited in Dan'a'yo compounds. 不 also serves as the Dan'a'yo stand-in for the character [[不]].

Mandarin 不 (bù, not — general negation before most verbs/adjectives: 不吃 doesn't eat; 不好 not good), 没/没有 (méi/méiyǒu, not — negates completed actions and 有: 没吃 didn't eat), 无/非 (wú/fēi, without; not — literary; cf. [[無]] and [[非]]). Cantonese 唔 (m4, not — general spoken negation: 唔食 doesn't eat), 冇 (mou5, not have; without), 否 (fau2, no; not — formal). Japanese 〜ない (nai, not — verbal negative ending: 食べない doesn't eat), 不 (FU/BU — Sino-Japanese prefix: 不可能 fukanō impossible; 不満 fuman dissatisfied). Korean 않다/안 (-ant-da/an, not — general negation: 먹지 않다 doesn't eat), 못하다/못 (cannot), 불- (不, Sino-Korean prefix: 불가능 impossible; 불만 dissatisfied). Vietnamese không (not; no — general), chẳng/chả (not at all), bất- (不, Sino-Vietnamese prefix: bất khả impossible; bất an uneasy).

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 볻 does not match Korean 부. 不 reads 볻 (bod/ㄅㄛㄊ) in Dan'a'yo — MC 幫母 (p, bilabial stop) + 物韻 (ɨut, entering tone with -t coda). Both Dan'a'yo and Korean give plain bilabial initial b- (ㄅ/ㅂ). The MC entering tone ɨut: Dan'a'yo preserves both the entering-tone coda (-ㄊ in 注音, rendered as final ᆮ in Hangul: 볻 = b-o-t) and a distinct vowel (-o/ㄛ), giving 볻 (b-o-d). Sino-Korean 부 (bu) drops the entering-tone coda entirely and shifts the vowel to -u, leaving an open syllable. Dan'a'yo's systematic retention of entering-tone codas (ㄆ/ᆸ, ㄊ/ᆮ, ㄎ/ᆨ) distinguishes it from both modern Chinese pronunciations and Sino-Korean readings that have reduced them.
