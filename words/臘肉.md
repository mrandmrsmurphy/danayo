## Notes
- [臘](../characters/臘.md) "Laba" + [肉 (char)](../characters/肉%20(char).md) "meat"

臘肉 is salt-cured, air-dried or smoked pork — typically belly or shoulder — made during the cold months of 臘月 when the dry winter air inhibits spoilage. The curing takes weeks: the meat is rubbed in salt, Sichuan pepper, sugar, and spices, then hung in the open air or suspended over a smoldering fire. The result is dense, deeply flavored, and shelf-stable for months.

The etymological sequence runs the other direction from what the name implies: the character 臘 did not originally mean "festival" — it meant this dried preserved meat, specifically the animal offerings central to the ancient year-end 臘 sacrifice. The festival borrowed its name from the ritual food, not the reverse. By extension the 12th month became 臘月 and the 8th of that month became [[臘八節]]. The character's radical is ⺼ (meat radical), carrying the original meaning plainly.

Regional varieties are sharply distinct. Sichuan 川味臘肉 is smoked over pine, cypress, or orange peel, developing a deep amber rind and an intensely resinous flavor; it is essential in 臘肉炒蒜苔 (cured pork with garlic shoots). Hunan 臘肉 is air-dried without smoke and tends to be leaner and saltier. Yunnan versions incorporate minority-group spice traditions and sometimes use yak or goat.

The word acquired a second, politically charged life on Chinese internet platforms. The embalmed body of Mao Zedong, displayed in the Mausoleum on Tiananmen Square, became known colloquially as 臘肉 — a deliberately crude comparison between preserved meat and a preserved corpse. The coinage functions as both a physical description and a political dismissal, and its opacity to automated censorship gave it staying power. The parallel exists in Russian dark humor about Lenin's mausoleum, but the Chinese usage is notably more savage in register, inflected by memory of the Great Leap Forward famine and the Cultural Revolution.
