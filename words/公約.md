## Notes
- [公 (char)](../characters/公%20(char).md) "public; official; held in common" + [約](../characters/約.md) "to bind; agreement; pact"

公約 denotes a binding commitment made openly to the public — literally, a binding (約, from the image of tying or constraining) that is 公 (open, held in common, transparent, not private or partial). The compound straddles two distinct registers: the electoral promise (campaign pledges made to voters) and the formal legal instrument (multilateral treaty or international covenant). This dual sense is productive and sometimes ironic — the 公約 that politicians make before elections and the 公約 of the UN Convention on Climate Change are both called 公約, which allows the gap between them to be pointed at with a single word.

The compound is active across all CJKV languages. In Chinese, 公約 (gōngyuē) names both election pledges and international agreements (联合国海洋法公约, UN Convention on the Law of the Sea), and also appears in mathematics as 公約數 (greatest common divisor, literally "common divisor/measure"). Japanese こうやく (kōyaku) is heavily used in political journalism for election promises, with a cultural undertone of skepticism about whether they will be kept; 公約違反 (breaking a pledge) is a frequent compound in political coverage. Korean 공약 (gong-yak) operates identically — it is one of the most common words in Korean electoral discourse. Vietnamese công ước leans toward the formal treaty sense rather than the electoral promise.

The kwin=true marking reflects the exact phonological match between Dan'a'yo 공약 and Korean 공약 — both 公 and 約 align identically across the two systems.
