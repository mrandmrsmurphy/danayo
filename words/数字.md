## Notes
- [数](../characters/数.md) "number; count" + [字 (char)](../characters/字%20(char).md) "character; written symbol"

数字 is the Dan'a'yo term for "numeral; digit; number" — both the abstract concept of a number and the written graphic symbol that represents it (1, 2, 3; 一, 二, 三; ①, ②, ③). In Dan'a'yo metalinguistics, 数字 designates the members of the numeral POS class ([[数詞]]): a 数字 is the lexical item, while 数詞 is the grammatical category to which such items belong. The alias 數字 is the traditional-form spelling (with 數 for 数). The compound is shared across Mandarin (数字, shùzì) and Japanese (数字, すうじ suuji) with identical characters and closely parallel meaning. Vietnamese reverses the order: chữ số — chữ (字, character; written form) + số (数, number) — placing the head noun first per Vietnamese grammar, a noteworthy mirror-image of the Chinese-origin compound.

数 (ㄙㄨ/su/수 — Mandarin shù, Cantonese sou3, Korean 수, Japanese SŪ/すう, Vietnamese số): "number; count; several; fate; enumerate" — MC 書母 (ʃ) + 虞韻 (ɨo); a 形声 of semantic 攴 (strike; action) + phonetic 婁, originally depicting careful tallying or enumeration, generalized to "number; count." The character serves as the root of 数学 (mathematics), 数量 (quantity), [[数詞]] (numeral POS). 字 (ㄐㄧ/ji/지 — Mandarin zì, Cantonese zi6, Korean 자, Japanese JI/じ, Vietnamese tự): "character; glyph; written form; letter; courtesy name" — MC 從母 (d͡z) + 之韻 (ɨ); a 形声 of semantic 宀 (roof; house) + phonetic 子 (child), originally "a child born under a roof → to give birth to; to nurture," extended to "written character" — the written form that gives birth to meaning. In [[新字体]] and [[注音符号]], 字 carries the same "written character" sense.

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 수지 does not match Korean 숫자. The first syllable converges: 数 gives 수=수 (MC 書母+虞韻, identical in both). The second syllable diverges: 字 gives Dan'a'yo 지 (ji, MC 從母+之韻 → -i vowel, ㄐㄧ) vs Korean 자 (ja, same MC 之韻 → -a vowel). Dan'a'yo preserves the MC 之韻 (ɨ) closer to its high central quality as -i, while Korean reflects it as -a — the same split seen in [[新字体]] (字: Dan'a'yo 지 vs Korean 자). Note also that Korean writes the compound 숫자 rather than 수자: the intervening ㅅ (siot) is inserted by Korean's 사이시옷 rule when the second element begins with a plain stop after a Sino-Korean syllable ending in a vowel, a phonological phenomenon with no parallel in Dan'a'yo.
