## Notes
- [而 (char)](../characters/而%20(char).md) "and; but; clausal conjunction"

而 is the clausal conjunction in Dan'a'yo — "and; but; yet" — connecting two clauses or predicates, particularly when the second follows sequentially from or contrasts with the first: 食而去 (eat and then go), 大而美 (large and beautiful), 欲行而未能 (wished to go but couldn't yet). Among the Dan'a'yo linkers, 而 is the clausal connector — linking whole propositions — contrasting with [[与]] (nominal "and"), which connects nouns and noun phrases. In classical Chinese, 而 (OC *njɯ) was one of the most versatile particles, serving as conjunction, adversative ("but; however"), modal ("if; when"), and even second-person pronoun ("you; your") — of which Dan'a'yo retains only the conjunctive function. 而 also serves as the Dan'a'yo stand-in for the character [[而]].

Mandarin 而 (ér, and; but — literary/formal: 学而不思则罔 "to learn without thinking is futile"), 而且 (érqiě, moreover; furthermore; and also), 并且 (bìngqiě, and; in addition — parallel clauses), 但是/可是 (dànshì/kěshì, but; however). Cantonese 而 (ji4, and; but — formal), 而且 (ji4 ce2, moreover; furthermore), 但係 (daan6 hai6, but; however). Japanese そして (soshite, and then; and so), 〜て (te, and then — gerund conjunction: 食べて帰る "eat and go home"), しかし (shikashi, however; but), が (ga, but; however — clausal contrastive). Korean 그리고 (geurigo, and; and then), 하지만 (hajiman, but; however), -고 (-go, and; gerund conjunction: 먹고 가다 "eat and go"). Vietnamese và (and — connecting clauses or nouns), nhưng (but; however), rồi (and then; after that — sequential).

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 니 does not match Korean 이. 而 reads 니 (ni/ㄋㄧ) in Dan'a'yo — MC 日母 (ȵ, voiced palatal nasal) + 之韻 (ɨ). Dan'a'yo maps MC 日母 to ㄋ (n-, preserving the nasal quality of the initial); Sino-Korean 이 maps the same MC initial to ㅇ (null initial), dropping it entirely. MC 之韻 (ɨ) gives -i in both systems (Dan'a'yo ㄋㄧ = n-i; Korean 이 = i) — the vowel is identical, only the initial differs. The retention of MC 日母's nasal quality (ȵ → n-/ㄋ) in Dan'a'yo vs its complete loss in Korean is a systematic divergence — contrast [[已]], where both systems drop the MC 以母 glide initial, leaving a pure vowel.
