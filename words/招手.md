## Notes
- [招](../characters/招.md) "beckon; recruit" + [手](../characters/手%20(char).md) "hand" — stand-in for [[招]], which cannot appear independently
- 招手 names the small, universal gesture of raising a hand and moving it — to call someone closer, to flag down a passing taxi, to say goodbye across a widening distance. Mandarin zhāoshǒu and Cantonese ziu1 sau2 keep it as the plain, everyday verb-object phrase it has always been, found already in pre-Qin texts like the *Xunzi*, where "登高而招" ("climbing high and beckoning") shows the gesture already doing the work of long-distance signaling before words could carry that far. The compound travels less easily into Japanese and Korean as a fixed loanword — both languages prefer to gloss the action with native phrases (手招きをする, 손짓하다) rather than borrow 招手 wholesale — so its Sino-Japanese しょうしゅ and Sino-Korean 초수 readings are compositional, built the same transparent way Dan'a'yo builds any compound from its parts. Vietnamese chiêu thủ preserves the literal "beckon-hand" sense intact, rounding out a word that, in every language it touches, stays close to the body: a small motion of the hand standing in for an entire sentence of greeting, summons, or farewell.
