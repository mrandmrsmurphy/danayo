## Notes
- [孝 (char)](../characters/孝%20(char).md) "filial piety" + [廉 (char)](../characters/廉%20(char).md) "upright, incorrupt" — "filial and incorrupt," the name of a recommendation category for entry into the Han-dynasty civil service, and by extension the term for a candidate recommended under it.

Emperor Wu of Han established the system in 134 BC on the advice of the Confucian scholar Dong Zhongshu (董仲舒), instructing each commandery (郡) and kingdom (國) to annually nominate men noted for filial devotion to parents and personal incorruptibility — qualities held to be evidence of the moral character needed for office, in the absence of any standardized examination at this stage. The system, part of the broader 鄕擧里選 ("recommendation from village and ward") framework, became one of the principal channels into the Han bureaucracy alongside the parallel 茂才 ("flourishing talent") category, the difference being that 孝廉 candidates were nominated by a commandery's governor (太守) while 茂才 candidates were nominated by a regional inspector (刺史).

Over time the system was formalized rather than left purely to local reputation: in 132 AD, on the recommendation of the Imperial Secretariat's Zuo Xiong (左雄), a minimum nomination age of forty was imposed, and nominees were required to pass a written test — classical scholars on Confucian texts (經術), career clerks on administrative documents (箋奏) — turning 孝廉 into something closer to a regular civil-service entry route. Despite these reforms, the system remained vulnerable to the same problem dogging recommendation-based selection generally: local officials and powerful clans increasingly used their nominating power to advance their own relatives and clients, and by the late Han the 孝廉 designation had become as much a marker of family connection as of demonstrated virtue, contributing to the loss of legitimacy that helped end the dynasty.
