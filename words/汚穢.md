## Notes
- [汚 (char)](../characters/汚%20(char).md) "dirty" + [穢](../characters/穢.md) "filthy, foul, impure" — synonym compound doubling down on defilement; stand-in for [[穢]], which cannot appear independently.

Both halves independently mean roughly "dirty," but they arrive there by different roads. 汚 is water (氵) plus a phonetic element, an image of water gone stagnant or fouled. 穢, by contrast, is built on 禾 ("grain") plus phonetic 歲: a field left to run to weeds, filth as agricultural neglect rather than liquid pollution. Stacked together, the compound generalizes past either original image into filth or defilement as such — physical, ritual, or moral.

The word's register varies noticeably by tradition. In Japanese, 汚穢 (おわい) narrowed hard toward the concrete and unglamorous: its primary modern sense is night soil / human waste, the term historically used for waste collection (汚穢屋, おわいや, "night-soil collector," a real trade into the 20th century) before more clinical vocabulary took over. Korean 오예 stayed at the more general register attested in Chinese, "dirty, filthy" as a broad descriptor without the Japanese sanitation-trade narrowing. Vietnamese ô uế went the opposite direction from Japanese: rather than narrowing to the physical, it broadened toward the abstract, becoming the standard term for ritual and moral impurity in formal, literary, and religious Vietnamese writing — "unclean" in something close to the Levitical sense — while everyday physical dirtiness is left to native bẩn/dơ instead.
