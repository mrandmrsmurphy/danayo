## Notes
- [鎌 (char)](../characters/鎌%20(char).md) "sickle" + [倉](../characters/倉.md) "granary" — Kamakura; seat of the first Japanese shogunate (1185–1333)

The name 鎌倉 is read with native Japanese pronunciations (かま + くら) rather than Sino-Japanese on'yomi, suggesting the place predates the Sino-Japanese layer of naming. The etymology is uncertain: the most commonly cited theory links 鎌 to the sickle-shaped contour of the surrounding hills and the bay; another interprets it as a compound meaning "storehouse in the hills." What is undisputed is that Kamakura's geography — a coastal basin enclosed on three sides by steep hills, open only to the sea — made it exceptionally defensible, and it was this that drew Minamoto no Yoritomo (源頼朝) to establish his base there in 1180.

Yoritomo's seizure of political power and the subsequent establishment of the Kamakura shogunate (鎌倉幕府) in 1185 marks the beginning of Japan's medieval period and a structural shift that would define Japanese governance until 1868: the replacement of imperial court administration (公家政治) by military rule (武家政治). The emperor remained a ritual center; actual power moved to the shogun and his council of warriors. The Kamakura settlement also produced Japan's first systematic law codes for the warrior class (御成敗式目, 1232).

The period saw an extraordinary flowering of new Buddhist traditions introduced from Song China. Rinzai Zen (臨済宗), brought by Eisai (栄西), found its institutional home in Kamakura's great temple complexes — Kenchoji (建長寺) and Engakuji (円覚寺). The Great Buddha (鎌倉大仏) cast in 1252, now exposed to the sky after its hall was destroyed by typhoon, remains Kamakura's most iconic image. The Mongol invasions of 1274 and 1281 (元寇) — repelled in part by typhoons that the Japanese called 神風 (kamikaze, divine winds) — reinforced a sense of Japan's protected distinctness, a self-understanding that would echo for centuries.

The shogunate fell in 1333 when Emperor Go-Daigo (後醍醐天皇) led a coalition that destroyed it, briefly restoring imperial rule before a new military regime arose under Ashikaga Takauji (足利尊氏) in Kyoto.
