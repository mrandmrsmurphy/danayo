## Notes
- [単](../characters/単.md) "single; simple" + [色](../characters/色.md) "color" — contrast 多色 (polychrome) and 彩色 (full color)

**単色** (*dānsè*) describes something rendered in a single colour or hue — monochrome in the fullest sense: not necessarily black-and-white, but confined to one colour and its range of tones and shades. The opposite is 多色 (*duōsè*, "multicolour, polychrome") or 彩色 (*cǎisè*, "full colour").

The word operates across several registers. In **photography and printing**, 単色 designates monochrome output — black-and-white or single-tone imagery — as opposed to colour reproduction. 単色印刷 (*dānsè yìnshuā*, "monochrome printing") and 単色照片 ("monochrome photograph") are standard compounds. In **physics and optics**, 単色光 (*dānsè guāng*, "monochromatic light") denotes radiation of a single wavelength — the ideal of a laser, as opposed to the polychromatic spectrum of white light. In **art and design**, 単色 describes works executed in a single pigment or hue, including not only greyscale but also sepia, cyanotype, and ink wash. In **display technology**, 単色 screens (single-colour LED or LCD) contrast with full-colour displays.

The 単 component carries the sense of *simple, undivided, singular* — as in 単独 (alone, solo), 単純 (simple, uncomplicated). Applied to colour, it reduces what is naturally complex (the spectrum, the mixing of pigments) to its irreducible element: one. There is an aesthetic tradition across East Asia — particularly in ink-wash painting (水墨画, 수묵화) — that treats the reduction to a single tone not as impoverishment but as discipline: the monochrome image as the most concentrated form of visual expression.

Japanese たんしょく (*tanshoku*) is the standard technical term used in photography, printing, and optics. Korean 단색 (*dansaek*) is equally standard across those fields, and also appears in everyday speech when describing objects of a single colour ("a plain, solid-colour fabric"). Vietnamese *đơn sắc* is the Sino-Vietnamese compound and is used in scientific and technical contexts; vernacular usage may prefer *một màu* (literally "one colour").
