## Notes
唳 names specifically a bird's cry — almost always a crane's — rather than birdsong in general; it survives today mainly in the compound 鶴唳 (hèlì, "a crane's cry") and, through that, in one of Chinese's most famous idioms: 風聲鶴唳 (fēngshēng hèlì, "wind and crane-cries"), from the *Book of Jin*'s account of the Battle of Fei River (淝水之戰, 383 AD). The routed army of Former Qin, fleeing in the dark, mistook the sound of wind and distant cranes for the pursuing Eastern Jin army and stampeded in panic — the idiom has meant "a state of extreme, self-generated paranoia, jumping at shadows" ever since. Japanese and Korean both retain 唳/려 largely as a literary, dictionary-level character rather than everyday vocabulary, mirroring how narrow and archaic its Chinese use has become outside that one idiom.
