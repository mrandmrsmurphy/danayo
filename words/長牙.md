## Notes
- [長 (char)](../characters/長%20(char).md) "long" + [牙](../characters/牙.md) "fang" — stand-in for [[牙]], which cannot appear independently — literally "long tooth," a tusk.

**Genuine Mandarin heteronym pair, not a typo**: 長/长 reads *cháng* ("long," an adjective) in this compound's actual "tusk" sense, but the identical written form 长牙 is also a common verb meaning "to teethe, to grow teeth" — there, 長/长 instead reads *zhǎng* ("to grow"). Same characters, same tone-bearing syllable, two unrelated grammatical functions distinguished only by the reader's knowledge of context; verified this compound's noun sense specifically uses cháng before confirming the frontmatter tone.

**Cross-linguistic asymmetry, disclosed rather than forced into false symmetry** (same standard as [[男人]] earlier in this sweep): Cantonese coeng4 ngaa4 parallels Mandarn's noun-sense reading directly, but none of Japanese, Korean, or Vietnamese appear to actually use a 長牙-based compound as their real word for "tusk." The frontmatter values (ちょうが, 장아, trường nha) are honest compositional on'yomi/Sino-Korean/Sino-Vietnamese readings only — not claims of real usage. What each language actually says: Japanese uses bare 牙 alone (きば, kiba); Korean uses the fully native 송곳니 (songgotni, literally "awl-tooth," i.e. canine tooth); Vietnamese uses the native compound răng nanh (răng "tooth" + nanh "fang/canine").

**Aliases corrected**: removed a self-referential `長牙` (a word can't be its own alias, same slip as [[健康]] earlier in this sweep) and `尖牙`, which is not an orthographic variant of this word at all but a distinct, narrower concept — "canine tooth" (a sharp pointed human/carnivore tooth) rather than "tusk" (an elongated, often protruding overgrown tooth, as in elephants/walruses/boars) — a real near-synonym that deserves its own entry if this vault ever creates one, not a spelling variant of 長牙. Kept `长牙`, the genuine simplified form.
