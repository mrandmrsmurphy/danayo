## Notes
- [擬 (char)](../characters/擬%20(char).md) "imitate; mimic" + [詞](../characters/詞.md) "word; term"

擬詞 is the Dan'a'yo term for *ideophone* — any word whose phonological form iconically represents some aspect of reality: sound, movement, texture, appearance, psychological state, or intensity. On the grammar page, 擬詞 are classed as a subgroup of [[修飾語]] (Modifiers): they modify comments rather than topics, and unlike ordinary statives they cannot modify nouns directly. They are typically formed by reduplication — a word doubled to intensify or sustain its iconic quality — and tend toward informal register, with many 擬詞 attested only in [[網語体]] (internet-register writing). The category is a deliberate superordinate coinage, not borrowed directly from any single CJKV tradition.

擬 (ㄧ/'i/이 — Mandarin nǐ, Cantonese ji5, Korean 의, Japanese GI/ぎ, Vietnamese nghĩ): "imitate; simulate; mimic; draft" — MC 疑母 (ŋ) + 之韻 (ɨ); originally "to compare; to imitate," extended in grammatical metalanguage to mean "mimetic; imitative." 詞 (ㄙㄚ/sa/사 — Mandarin cí, Cantonese ci4, Korean 사, Japanese SHI/し, Vietnamese từ): "word; term; lyric" — MC 從母 (z) + 之韻 (ɨ); the CJKV character for "word" used in grammatical terminology (品詞, 名詞, 動詞, etc.) as opposed to 語, which stresses usage and utterance over classification. The two characters share the same MC rhyme (之韻 ɨ) but different initials: 擬 has the nasal velar ŋ (weakened to null in Dan'a'yo and to /ŭi/ in Korean), while 詞 has the voiced fricative z (→ Dan'a'yo /s/, → Korean /s/). No CJKV language uses 擬詞 for this concept in established usage. The nearest is Mandarin 拟声词 (nǐshēngcí = 擬聲詞, "sound-mimicking word"), which shares 擬 and 詞 but inserts 聲 to specify the sound-imitating subtype. Japanese uses 擬音語 (giongo) and 擬態語 (gitaigo) with the suffix 語 rather than 詞, and distinguishes sound-imitation (音) from manner-imitation (態). Korean uses 擬聲語 (의성어) and 擬態語 (의태어) — agreeing with Japanese on 語 and 態 but using 聲 (like Mandarin) rather than 音 for sound-imitation. Vietnamese uses Sino-Vietnamese 象聲 (tượng thanh, "sound-resembling") and 象形 (tượng hình, "form-resembling") without the 擬 initial element. Dan'a'yo synthesizes: it takes the 詞 suffix from the Mandarin tradition (not 語 from the Japanese/Korean tradition) and drops the specifying medial character, producing a superordinate covering all mimetic word types.

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 이사 does not match Korean 의사. The second syllable converges: 詞 gives 사=사 (identical — MC 從母+之韻 yields the same reading in both). The first syllable diverges: 擬 gives Dan'a'yo 이 (i, MC 疑母+之韻 → null initial + plain -i vowel) vs Korean 의 (ui/ŭi, null initial + diphthong -ui). MC 疑母 (ŋ) weakens to null in both, but the 之韻 vowel resolves differently: Dan'a'yo gives plain -i (ㄧ), Korean gives the diphthong -ui (의). Note that Korean 의사 (擬詞, ideophone) is a homophone of 의사 (醫師, physician) and 의사 (意思, intention/will) — a collision that would create ambiguity in any Korean-language discussion of 擬詞, and one reason Dan'a'yo's coined 이사 stands as a distinct form.
