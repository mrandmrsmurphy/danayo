## Notes
- [形 (char)](../characters/形%20(char).md) "form, shape" + [声](../characters/声.md) "sound" — one of the [[六書]] (liùshū), the traditional six-category classification of Chinese characters; by far the largest of the six.

**形声** (*xíngshēng*, lit. "form and sound") characters are built from two components: a semantic element (the "radical") that hints at the character's meaning or semantic field, and a phonetic element that hints — often only approximately, after millennia of sound change — at its pronunciation. The vast majority of characters in any standard dictionary, by some estimates over 80%, belong to this category. Examples include 河 ("river": 水 "water" + phonetic 可), 媽/媽 ("mother": 女 "woman" + phonetic 馬), and countless others.

Because the phonetic components were chosen based on Old Chinese pronunciation, the "hint" they give in modern Mandarin, Cantonese, or Sino-Vietnamese readings can be weak or entirely opaque — a frustration for learners but a goldmine for historical phonologists, since the phonetic series preserved in 形声 characters are one of the principal sources of evidence for reconstructing Old Chinese.

In Japanese the term 形声文字 (*keisei moji*) is taught as one of the four "structural" categories of 六書, alongside 象形, 指事, and 会意. The same framework, including this term largely unchanged, is preserved in Korean (형성문자, *hyeongseong munja*) and Vietnamese (chữ hình thanh) traditional grammar pedagogy, again tracing back to the *Shuowen Jiezi*'s original six-fold scheme.
