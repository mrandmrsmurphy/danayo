## Notes
- [草 (char)](../characters/草%20(char).md) "grass; plants"

草 in Dan'a'yo is the noun for "grass" and serves as the determiner (限定詞) for herbaceous plants and grasses — appearing in naming compounds to classify non-woody vegetation: 艾草 (mugwort), 芝草 (grass; lingzhi mushroom), 茜草 (Indian madder), 草木 (greenery; vegetation), 草原 (grassland; steppe; prairie). The character is a 形声 compound — phonetic 早 (OC *tsaːwʔ) under the grass radical 艸 (艹), itself a 象形 depicting grass shoots growing from the ground. 草 denotes the broad class of herbaceous plants: grasses, herbs, weeds, and low-growing leafy plants, as distinct from [[木]] (the woody-plant/tree determiner). A secondary meaning — "rough; hasty; draft" (草稿, rough draft; 草書, cursive script) — derives from the sense of unfinished vegetation. 草 also serves as the Dan'a'yo stand-in for the character [[草]].

Mandarin 草 (cǎo): grass; herb; weed — 草地 (cǎodì, grassland; lawn), 草原 (cǎoyuán, steppe; meadow), 杂草 (zácǎo, weeds), 草莓 (cǎoméi, strawberry — lit. "grass berry"), 草稿 (cǎogǎo, rough draft), 草书 (cǎoshū, cursive script). Cantonese 草 (cou2): 草地 (cou2 dei6, lawn; grassland), 草莓 (cou2 mui4, strawberry). Japanese 草 (くさ/kusa, native; そう/SŌ, Sino-Japanese): 草原 (sōgen or kusahara, grassland), 草食 (sōshoku, herbivorous — lit. "grass-eating"), 雑草 (zassō, weeds), 草書 (sōsho, cursive script). Korean 풀 (pul) is the native word for grass and herb; Sino-Korean 초 (草) in compounds: 초원 (草原, grassland), 잡초 (雜草, weeds), 초식 (草食, herbivorous). Vietnamese cỏ: cỏ dại (wild grass; weeds); Sino-Vietnamese thảo (草) in formal compounds: thảo nguyên (草原, steppe), thảo mộc (草木, vegetation).

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 찻 does not match Korean 초. 草 reads 찻 (cau/ㄑㄚㄨ) in Dan'a'yo — MC 清母 (t͡sʰ) + 豪韻 (ɑu) gives an aspirated affricate onset with the -au diphthong; the -u glide is written as ᆺ (-s) in Dan'a'yo Hangul (the same convention as [[鳥]] 촛 and [[某]] 못). Sino-Korean 초 (cho) maps the same aspirated initial to ㅊ (ch-, aspirated) — both systems preserve the MC aspiration, which is unusual — but reduces the -au diphthong to plain -o, losing the glide. The MC 豪韻 (ɑu) produced ㄚㄨ (-au/찻) in Dan'a'yo and -o (초) in Korean — a diphthong preserved in Dan'a'yo and simplified in Korean.
