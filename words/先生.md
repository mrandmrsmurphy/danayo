## Notes
- [先](../characters/先.md) "first; before" + [生](../characters/生.md) "life; born" — an independent compound; neither character's own `stand_in` points here (先's is `優先`, 生's is `生活`); literally "born before [you]," an honorific for someone senior in age or experience

**A genuine cross-linguistic sense split, corrected from a wrong-first gloss rather than a wrong-word substitution**: 先生's primary living sense differs sharply by language. In modern Mandarin, xiānsheng chiefly means "Mr., sir" — a general polite title for a man (regardless of profession), with the "teacher" sense archaic/historical (superseded today by 老师, lǎoshī) — reordered the English gloss to lead with this Mandarin-primary sense, verified via search. Cantonese sin1 saang1 follows the same Mandarin-like pattern (also "husband," in some dialectal use). Japanese せんせい (sensei) and Korean 선생 (seonsaeng), by contrast, genuinely and overwhelmingly mean "teacher" as the living, primary sense in both languages (선생님 being the everyday respectful address for teachers).

**Real bug corrected**: `vietnamese: giáo viên` was the generic native/Sino word for "teacher" (built from unrelated roots, 教員), not a reading of 先生 at all — corrected to `tiên sinh`, a real, richly documented Sino-Vietnamese honorific (verified via search) carrying the exact same range of senses found across the sphere — teacher, respected elder/expert, and even (in the form tiên sanh) a wife's term of address for her husband.
