## Notes
- [私](../characters/私.md) "private; personal" + [讐](../characters/讐.md) "personal grudge" — this word is the stand-in for [讐](../characters/讐.md), which cannot appear independently in Dan'a'yo.

私讐 names a grievance or enmity held against another person on purely personal grounds, as opposed to a grudge arising from official duty, clan feud, or public wrong. The distinction is old and functional: classical Chinese statecraft and law drew a sharp line between 私讐 (a private matter, to be resolved or restrained by the individual) and public wrongdoing that fell under the state's jurisdiction — a magistrate who let 私讐 color an official judgment, or who used his post to settle one (公報私仇, "using public office to avenge a private grudge"), was committing a real and named abuse.

Modern Mandarin and Cantonese generally write the same word with 私仇 rather than 私讐 — 讐 (and its variant 讎) survives mainly in literary and classical registers, while 仇 is the everyday character for "enmity, foe" in the modern language (it is also its own independent Dan'a'yo character, [[仇]], with its own separate stand-in [[仇恨]] "hatred"). 讐/讎 and 仇 are not graphemic variants of one character — they are two historically distinct characters that happen to overlap in this one register-shifted sense, which is why 私讐 keeps 讐 here (matching this vault's own derivation for the character) rather than borrowing 仇's reading.

Japanese ししゅう (shishū) and Korean 사수 (sasu) both preserve the literary Sino-form directly, consistent with 讐/讎 being the learned register's default even where the vernacular has moved on. Vietnamese tư thù (also written 私讐 in Hán tự) is likewise still current, without the same literary/vernacular split seen in Chinese.
