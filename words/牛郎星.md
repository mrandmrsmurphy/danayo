## Notes
- [牛 (char)](../characters/牛%20(char).md) "ox; cow" + [郎](../characters/郎.md) "young man" + [星 (char)](../characters/星%20(char).md) "star" — literally "the Cowherd Boy's Star"; sub-compound [[牛郎]]

**牛郎星** (*Niúláng xīng*) is the East Asian name for **Altair** (α Aquilae), the brightest star in the constellation Aquila and one of the three vertices of the Summer Triangle. At approximately 16.7 light-years from Earth, Altair is among the nearest naked-eye stars visible; it rotates so rapidly — completing one full rotation in under nine hours — that it is measurably oblate, visibly flattened at the poles and bulging at its equator.

The name derives from the legend of [[牛郎]] (*Niúláng*), the Cowherd Boy, paired with [[織女]] (*Zhīnǚ*), the Weaver Girl — a myth attested as early as the *Shijing* and fully elaborated by the Han dynasty. The two lovers are separated by the Milky Way (天河, *tiānhé*) and permitted to meet only once a year on the seventh night of the seventh lunar month — the festival known as [[七夕]] (*Qīxī*) in Chinese, *Tanabata* (七夕) in Japanese, 칠석 (*Chilseok*) in Korean, and *Thất Tịch* in Vietnamese. On that night, according to legend, a bridge of magpies (鵲橋, *quèqiáo*) forms across the Milky Way to reunite them. Altair and its two flanking stars (β and γ Aquilae) represent 牛郎 carrying his two children on a shoulder pole.

The Japanese Tanabata tradition calls the star **彦星** (*Hikoboshi*, "Lad Star"), while the Sino-Japanese reading 牛郎星 (*ぎゅうろうせい*) is reserved for formal astronomical contexts. Korean tradition uses **견우성** (牽牛星, *Gyeonuseong*, "Star of the Ox-Driver") — the compound 牽牛 ("lead the ox") rather than 牛郎 ("cowherd boy"), an older stratum of the myth. Vietnamese knows the star as *Sao Ngưu Lang*, with the native word *sao* ("star") prepended in Vietnamese syntactic order rather than appended as Sino-Vietnamese *tinh*.

In traditional Chinese astronomical cataloguing, Altair was identified as **河鼓二** (*Hégu Èr*, "Second Drum at the River"), the central of three stars beside the Celestial River, a classification independent of the Qixi legend. The 牛郎星 name reflects the later popular identification of the astronomical catalogue star with the mythological figure.
