## Notes
- [但 (char)](../characters/但%20(char).md) "but; however; only; merely"

但 is the primary adversative linker in Dan'a'yo — "but; however; yet" — connecting a clause that contrasts with or qualifies what preceded: A 但 B ("A, but B"). In Classical Chinese 但 also functions as a limiter meaning "only; merely; no more than," a sense that survives in the Dan'a'yo compound [[不但]] ("not only") and in classical quotation. As a pure adversative it is sharper and more immediate than [[不過]] ("however; that said"), which tends toward a discursive hedge; 但 marks direct contradiction.

但 (ㄉㄚㄋ/dan/단 — Mandarin dàn, Cantonese daan6, Korean 단, Japanese TAN/たん, Vietnamese đản): "but; however; only" — MC 定母 (d) + 寒韻 (ɑn). A 形声 compound: semantic 亻 (person) + phonetic 旦 (dawn, OC *daːns); 旦 depicted the sun (日) rising above the horizon (一), meaning "daybreak," and 但 was phonetically borrowed for the adversative/limiter particle, drifting from an earlier sense of "bare; plain; unadorned." Mandarin 但 (dàn) remains the standard literary adversative, typically extended as 但是 (dànshì, but; however). Cantonese 但係 (daan6 hai6, but; however) retains 但 as the core element. Japanese ただし (tadashi, "however; provided that") and ただ (tada, "only; merely") are the kakikudan readings; たん (TAN) is the on-yomi. Korean 단 (但) survives in formal and legal registers — frequently seen as a proviso marker after the main clause: 단, ～의 경우에는 ("however, in the case of..."). Vietnamese đản carries the classical limiter sense.

kwin is true: Dan'a'yo 단 matches Korean 단 exactly. MC 定母 (d) → Dan'a'yo ㄉ (d-), Korean ㄷ (d-). MC 寒韻 (ɑn) → Dan'a'yo -an (단), Korean -an (단). Both the initial and the rhyme map identically, with no aspiration split, no vowel shift, and no coda change. 但 is among the cleaner kwin: true cases — a single-syllable word where all three phonological dimensions converge.
