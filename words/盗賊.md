## Notes
- [盗](../characters/盗.md) "burgle; steal" + [賊](../characters/賊.md) "thief; bandit" — stand-in for [[賊]], which cannot appear independently
- 盗賊 pairs two near-synonyms for "thief" into the standard Sinospheric word for an armed robber or brigand — someone who steals by open force rather than stealth, the figure of highway banditry and pirate raids rather than the cutpurse or burglar (that narrower sense belongs instead to 盗's other compound, 窃盗). The pairing is ancient and stable: Mandarin dàozéi, Cantonese dou6 caak6, Sino-Korean 도적 (dojeok), Sino-Vietnamese đạo tặc, and Japanese とうぞく (tōzoku) all preserve both the form and the meaning essentially unchanged from Classical Chinese, where 盜賊 already named the bandit-rebels who threatened dynastic order — a usage that lent the word a faint political charge it retains in historical and literary registers, where "盗賊" can shade from common criminal toward "rebel against the state," depending on whose chronicle is doing the naming.
