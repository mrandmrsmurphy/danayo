## Notes
- [人 (char)](../characters/人%20(char).md) "person" + [等 (char)](../characters/等%20(char).md) "etc., rank"

人等 is "persons" in the formal/classical collective sense — 等 here functions as a classical plural/collective suffix ("and others; of that class/rank"), the same pattern seen in 我等 ("we," classical/formal), 尔等 ("you all," classical/formal), and set legal/bureaucratic phrases like 有関人等 ("the persons concerned") and 閑雑人等 ("unrelated/miscellaneous persons, i.e. those with no business being here"). This is a distinctly formal, classical-register plural, different in register and connotation from the everyday colloquial plural 人們 (built with 們 instead, not currently a separate vault entry) — the two are easy to conflate since both translate to "people" in isolation, but 人等 belongs to legal/classical Chinese phrasing rather than everyday speech.

Mandarin 人等 (rénděng) survives mainly inside set legal/classical phrases rather than as a freestanding word — 闲杂人等 (xiánzá rénděng, "unrelated persons; riffraff"), 各色人等 (gèsè rénděng, "people of every stripe/class"). Cantonese 人等 (jan4dang2) patterns the same way. Neither Japanese nor Korean nor Vietnamese appear to have a well-attested standalone equivalent of this specific compound — the frontmatter readings above (じんとう, 인등, nhân đẳng) are straightforward on'yomi/Sino-Korean/Sino-Vietnamese compositions from each language's own readings of 人 and 等, given here as a formal cross-reference rather than as claims of natural everyday usage. Japanese does use 等 (とう) productively as a formal pluralizing suffix on other nouns in legal and bureaucratic writing (関係者等 "the parties concerned, etc."), the same function 等 performs here, just not conventionally attached directly to 人 itself.
