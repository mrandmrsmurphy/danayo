## Notes
- [唾](../characters/唾.md) "saliva; spit; to spit" + [液](../characters/液.md) "liquid; fluid"

唾液 is "saliva" — the formal and technical term for the oral fluid produced by the salivary glands, used in digestion, speech articulation, and oral health. The compound is anatomical: 唾 (saliva, spit — the substance) + 液 (liquid, fluid — the category). It is used in medical and scientific contexts across CJKV, contrasting with more colloquial expressions: Mandarin 口水 (kǒushuǐ, "mouth water"), Japanese 唾/つば (tsuba), Korean 침 (chim, spit), all of which are the everyday register equivalents.

Mandarin 唾液 (tuòyè) is the medical and formal standard — 唾液腺 (salivary gland), 唾液分泌 (saliva secretion). Japanese 唾液 (だえき, daeki) is the anatomical term; everyday speech uses つば (tsuba) or 침 for spit. Korean 타액 (taek) is the formal medical term; 침 (chim) is the colloquial word. Vietnamese thóa dịch is the Sino-Vietnamese compound (thóa = 唾, dịch = 液); natural Vietnamese uses nước bọt (literally "mouth/foam water").

The Dan'a'yo 諺文 transcription 타역 differs from Sino-Korean 타액: 液 in Dan'a'yo reads 역 (yek, ⼶ㄎ) rather than the Sino-Korean 액 (aek). This reflects different Middle Chinese inheritance paths — Dan'a'yo preserves the velar initial of MC *ʔjak or similar, while Sino-Korean has smoothed this to 액. 唾液 belongs to the pan-CJKV medical vocabulary stratum where all five language forms share the same written compound even as phonological details diverge, making the Sinographic characters the reliable cross-linguistic anchor.
