## Notes
- [綏](../characters/綏.md) "soothe; calm; pacify" + [靖](../characters/靖.md) "pacify; bring to peace" — a synonym compound for the act of restoring order and stability; stand-in for [[靖]], which cannot appear independently; note that Korean 수정 is a homophone of [[水晶]]

**綏靖** (*suíjìng*) is the standard Sinographic term for both *pacification* — the suppression of internal unrest and restoration of order — and *appeasement* — the granting of concessions to an aggressive external power to avoid conflict. These two senses run together historically and diverged sharply in political valence during the 20th century.

In the **classical and imperial** register, 綏靖 meant pacification of frontier regions or internal rebellions: the process of 綏靖 a newly conquered territory involved disarming resistance, installing administrative structures, and gradually integrating the population into the imperial system. 綏 carries the sense of soothing, calming, reassuring — a ribbon or cord that ties things together — while 靖 means bringing disorder to a settled, quiet state. The compound is thus both military (suppress the resistance) and political (settle the population).

The word's modern resonance is almost entirely defined by its use as the translation of **appeasement** in the context of 1930s European diplomacy. **綏靖政策** (*suíjìng zhèngcè*, appeasement policy) is specifically the policy associated with British Prime Minister Neville Chamberlain — the systematic granting of territorial and political concessions to Nazi Germany in the hope of satisfying Hitler's demands and avoiding war. Its defining moment was the **慕尼黑協議** (Munich Agreement, September 1938 AD), in which Britain and France agreed to the annexation of the Sudetenland. When Germany violated the agreement and occupied the remainder of Czechoslovakia in March 1939 AD, 綏靖政策 was discredited, and "appeasement" became a permanent byword for the failure of concession-based diplomacy in the face of ideological aggression.

This semantic collapse — from a neutral word for pacification to a pejorative for dangerous weakness — occurred simultaneously in English ("appeasement") and in its Sinographic translation (綏靖). In post-WWII political discourse across the Sinosphere, accusing a policy of 綏靖主義 (*suíjìng zhǔyì*, appeasementism) is a serious rhetorical attack. The charge has been levelled in discussions of policy toward North Korea, in the Taiwan Strait context, and in Chinese official rhetoric regarding what it characterises as foreign interference.

Japanese **すいせい** (*suisei*) is the reading of 綏靖 in the context of 綏靖天皇 (*Suizei-tennō*), the legendary second Emperor of Japan — the name being a posthumous honorific bestowed by the Chinese-educated court in the Nara period. In the political sense of appeasement, Japanese tends to use **宥和** (*yūwa*, appeasement) or **融和政策** alongside 綏靖政策. Korean **수정** (*sujeong*) is an exact homophone of 水晶 (*sujeong*, rock crystal) — the two words share pronunciation entirely in Korean, differentiated only by context and written form. Vietnamese **tuy tịnh** is the Sino-Vietnamese compound; in political discourse, Vietnamese uses the loanword *chính sách nhượng bộ* (policy of concession) more commonly than the classical compound.
