## Notes
- [回 (char)](../characters/回%20(char).md) "return, turn" + [天 (char)](../characters/天%20(char).md) "heaven, sky"

回天 describes the act of reversing the will of heaven — turning around a situation so dire that fate itself seemed fixed. The compound is literary and carries a sense of heroic impossibility: to achieve 回天 is to accomplish something that should not have been possible. In Chinese classical writing, 回天 appears in contexts where a minister saves a dynasty, a physician restores a patient given up for dead, or a general reverses a rout. The related compound 回天之力 ("the strength to turn heaven") denotes the capacity for such reversal.

In Japanese (かいてん, kaiten), the compound is perhaps best known from 回天 (回天), the Imperial Japanese Navy's crewed torpedoes deployed in the final phases of World War II. Named deliberately for their supposed power to "turn the tide of heaven" in Japan's favor, the Kaiten were modified Type 93 torpedoes enlarged to carry a single pilot, who would guide the weapon to its target with no means of escape. The program was a product of the same desperation that produced the kamikaze aircraft. Approximately 106 Kaiten pilots died in combat operations between 1944 and 1945.
