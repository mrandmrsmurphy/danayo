## Notes
- [祭](../characters/祭.md) "ritual, offer sacrifice; ceremony" + [物 (char)](../characters/物%20(char).md) "thing, object" — the object presented in a ritual; that which is offered.

祭 is one of the two great activities of the ancient Chinese state. The Zuo Zhuan (左傳) states:

> 國之大事，在祀與戎。
> "The great affairs of state are ritual sacrifice and warfare."

Ritual (祀/祭) and war (戎) were the twin pillars of Zhou legitimacy — sacrifice to heaven, earth, ancestors, and the spirits of grain and soil maintained the cosmic order that made governance possible. The Chinese sacrificial system was elaborate: 郊祭 (suburban sacrifice to Heaven and Earth at the solstices), 廟祭 (ancestral temple sacrifice at seasonal festivals), and 社稷 (altar of the Earth deity and the grain deity, whose loss symbolised the fall of a dynasty). 祭物 — the physical offerings — included grain, wine, silk, livestock, and incense.

In Japanese Shinto, 祭物 (さいもつ, saimotsu) refers to ritual offerings presented at shrines: 幣帛 (heihaku, cloth and paper offerings), 神饌 (shinsen, food offerings including rice, sake, fish, and vegetables). Shinto ritual preserves an archaic relationship between the deity (神), the offering (祭物), and the officiating priest that has formal parallels with the Zhou Chinese sacrificial system.

In biblical translation into Chinese, 祭物 covers the range of offerings described in Leviticus: grain offerings (素祭, sùjì), burnt offerings (燔祭, fánjì), peace offerings (平安祭), and sin offerings (贖罪祭). The compound bridges East Asian ritual and biblical theology without contradiction.
