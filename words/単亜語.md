## Notes
- [単](../characters/単.md) "single; simple" + [亜](../characters/亜.md) "Asia" + [語](../characters/語.md) "language"

単亜語 is the name of Dan'a'yo itself — the constructed zonal auxiliary language designed for speakers of the East Asian cultural sphere: Mandarin, Cantonese, Japanese, Korean, and Vietnamese. The name compounds three characters whose meanings are transparent: 単 (single; unified; simple), 亜 (Asia; the East Asian cultural sphere), 語 (language; speech) — giving "unified East Asian language" or "simple Asian language." The romanization dan'a'yo marks each morpheme boundary with an apostrophe: /dan/ (単), /ʔa/ (亜), /ʔjo/ (語). Dan'a'yo is both the name of the language and a vocabulary item within it — the first word a speaker of Dan'a'yo needs to know.

The three components function as a semantic compound. 単 (ㄉㄚㄋ/dan/단 — Mandarin dān, Cantonese daan1, Japanese TAN/たん, Korean 단, Vietnamese đơn): "single; simple; sole" — MC 定母 (d) + 寒韻 (ɑn); depicts a Y-shaped sling weapon holding one projectile. In the compound, 単 carries both "unified" (one language for the region) and "simple" (accessible across the CJKV sphere). 亜 (ㄚ/'a/아 — Mandarin yà, Cantonese aa3, Japanese A/あ, Korean 아, Vietnamese á): "Asia; secondary; sub-" — MC 影母 (ʔ) + 麻二開韻 (ɣa); abbreviation for 亜細亜 (Asia), designating the shared sinographic cultural region. 語 (⼄/'yo/요 — Mandarin yǔ, Cantonese jyu5, Japanese GO/ご, Korean 어, Vietnamese ngữ): "language; speech" — MC 疑母 (ŋ) + 魚韻 (ɨʌ); the standard CJKV suffix for naming languages: 日語 (Japanese), 英語 (English), 漢語 (Chinese), 韓語 (Korean), 越語 (Vietnamese).

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 단아요 does not match Korean 단아어. The first two syllables converge: 単 (ㄉㄚㄋ/단) is kwin: true (Dan'a'yo 단 = Korean 단), and 亜 (ㄚ/아) is kwin: true (Dan'a'yo 아 = Korean 아). The divergence is entirely in the third: 語 (⼄/'yo/요) is kwin: false — Dan'a'yo gives 요 (yo, via MC 疑母+魚韻 → -yo, the 魚韻 → -o pattern also seen in [[与]] and [[且]]), while Korean gives 어 (eo). Dan'a'yo's name for itself ends with a syllable that distinguishes it from the Sino-Korean reading — 단아요 rather than 단아어 — a built-in marker of the language's distinctive phonology.
