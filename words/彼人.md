## Notes
- [彼 (char)](../characters/彼%20(char).md) "yon; distal demonstrative; he/she (literary)" + [人 (char)](../characters/人%20(char).md) "person; human being"

彼人 is the distal correlative in the person column — "that person yonder, that other person (away from both speaker and listener)." In classical Chinese, 彼 was the standard distal demonstrative and third-person pronoun; 彼人 appears in philosophical and contrastive prose (彼人之道 "that person's way"; 彼人如此 "that person is like this"). It occupies the far-field deictic slot, contrasting with 此人 (this person, proximal) and 其人 (that person, medial).

In Mandarin, 彼人 (bǐrén) is found in classical and formal texts meaning "that person (distal)" but has no everyday colloquial use; modern Mandarin would say 那個人 (nàge rén, that person) for both medial and distal. Japanese あの人 (ano hito, that person — distal) is the natural spoken equivalent and is also the most common polite way to refer to an absent third party; it contrasts directly with この人 (this person) and その人 (that person, medial). Korean 저 사람 (jeo saram) is the distal person phrase, contrasting with 이 사람 and 그 사람.

The distinction between 其人 and 彼人 maps to different social and narrative uses. 其人 (medial, near addressee) tends to be used for people who are part of the shared discourse — "the person you mentioned, the person we both know." 彼人 (distal) tends to refer to people outside the immediate conversational frame — third parties, absent individuals, or people discussed at arm's length. In narrative prose, 彼人 can function as a more distanced third-person form compared to 其人, marking emotional or social distance as well as spatial or deictic distance.
