## Notes
- [檀](../characters/檀.md) "sandalwood; hardwood" + [香](../characters/香.md) "fragrant; incense; aroma"

Sandalwood is the dense, slow-growing heartwood of trees in the genus *Santalum* and related genera, valued across the entire East Asian cultural sphere for three properties: its penetrating, long-lasting fragrance; its workability as a fine hardwood; and its role in medicine and cosmetics. The heartwood — 檀香 — is the innermost and most aromatic part of the trunk, forming only in mature trees and becoming more fragrant as it ages.

In Buddhist practice, sandalwood incense (檀香 as a material; see also [[檀木]] for the tree itself) is among the most prestigious offerings. Its smoke is understood to purify the space, carry prayers, and please the Buddha — and its name appears throughout liturgical texts, temple architecture (elaborate carved sandalwood altarpieces), and religious poetry across China, Japan, Korea, and Vietnam. The Sanskrit word for sandalwood, *candana*, was transliterated into Chinese as 栴檀 (*zhāntán*), with 檀 becoming the character associated with the tree.

Japanese uses the compound 白檀 (びゃくだん, *byakudan*, "white sandalwood") as the more common name for the species *Santalum album*; だんこう is the on-yomi reading of 檀香 as a Sino-Japanese compound. Vietnamese *đàn hương* derives directly from the Sino-Vietnamese readings of both characters and is the standard literary term.
