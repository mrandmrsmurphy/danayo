## Notes
- [臘](../characters/臘.md) "Laba" + [八 (char)](../characters/八%20(char).md) "eight" + [節 (char)](../characters/節%20(char).md) "holiday"

The Laba Festival falls on the 8th day of the 12th lunar month ([[臘八]]) and has two distinct historical roots that fused over time.

The older layer is the ancient Chinese *腊祭* (là jì), the great year-end sacrifice to the gods and ancestors. By the Qin and Han dynasties this ritual had been systematized into the state calendar — the 12th month was renamed 臘月 in its honor. The character 臘 itself (OC \*raːb) carries the sense of preserved, dried meat, reflecting the animal offerings central to the rite.

The Buddhist overlay arrived with the introduction of Chan Buddhism in China. The 8th of the 12th lunar month was taken to commemorate Śākyamuni's attainment of enlightenment under the Bodhi tree after forty-nine days of meditation — an event known in Japanese Buddhism as 成道会 (じょうどうえ, *jōdōe*). Tang-dynasty monasteries began cooking 臘八粥 (*Laba zhōu*), a thick porridge of seven or eight grains, legumes, nuts, and dried fruits, as a ritual offering. The Song court institutionalized the practice, distributing the congee to officials.

By the Song dynasty 臘八節 had become the popular anchor of the Spring Festival season. The proverb 過了臘八就是年 — "after Laba, it's already the New Year" — captures its role as the countdown's starting gun. Regional 臘八粥 recipes vary widely: northern China favors millet, red dates, and peanuts; Sichuan versions include chili; temples add lotus seeds and longan.

In Japan, December 8 on the solar calendar is observed as 成道会 in Zen temples, liturgically equivalent though calendrically shifted. The Vietnamese parallel *Lễ Lạp Bát* is largely absent from popular practice outside Buddhist communities.
