## Notes
- [永](../characters/永.md) "eternal; perpetual; forever" + [久 (char)](../characters/久%20(char).md) "long; for a long time"

永久 is a semantic dyad of temporal endlessness: 永 means "forever, eternally" (the character depicts a river spreading out into multiple currents, traditionally symbolising unceasing flow), and 久 means "for a long time, of long duration." Together they express permanence without qualification — not merely lasting for a long time, but lasting without end. The redundancy is intensifying: 永 declares that something will never end, and 久 confirms that it has already been persisting for a long time, and will continue to do so.

In scientific and technical vocabulary, 永久 is highly productive. 永久磁石 (yǒngjiǔ cí shí / えいきゅうじしゃく / 영구자석) is a permanent magnet; 永久歯 (えいきゅうし) are permanent teeth (as opposed to deciduous milk teeth); 永久保存 is permanent storage or archival preservation. In legal contexts, 永久追放 is permanent exile and 永久欠番 is a retired jersey number. In Buddhist philosophy, 永久 appears in discussions of nirvana and the eternal dharma — the question of whether any phenomenon can truly be 永久 is itself a central doctrinal concern in some traditions.

Across the CJKV sphere: Mandarin 永久 (yǒngjiǔ) is standard; Japanese えいきゅう (eikyū, historical kana えいきう) is common in formal and scientific registers; Korean uses the Sino-Korean 영구 (yeonggu), though everyday speech for "for a long time" often prefers the native 오랫동안 (oraetdongan). Vietnamese vĩnh cửu uses the Sino-Vietnamese readings. The kwin=false marking reflects the divergence between Dan'a'yo 윙규 and Korean 영구 — 永 maps to 영 in Korean but to 윙 in Dan'a'yo.
