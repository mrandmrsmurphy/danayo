## Notes
- [五 (char)](../characters/五%20(char).md) "five" + [射](../characters/射.md) "to shoot" — the Five Archery Techniques, the third of the Six Arts (六芸) of Zhou-dynasty noble education.

五射 (wǔshè) is the third item in the **《周禮·地官司徒·保氏》** curriculum, following [[五礼]] and [[六楽]]. The five techniques of ceremonial archery are:

1. **白矢** (bái shǐ) — "white arrow": the arrow strikes with such force that its white-feathered tip passes clean through the target
2. **參連** (cān lián) — "three in succession": a first arrow is followed by three more shot in rapid, unbroken sequence, like a string of pearls
3. **剡注** (yǎn zhù) — "sharp release": a swift, precisely-aimed shot released without hesitation
4. **襄尺** (xiāng chǐ) — "yielding a foot": when shooting alongside a ruler or superior, the subordinate steps back by one *chi* (尺) out of deference
5. **井儀** (jǐng yí) — "well pattern": four arrows land in the formation of the character 井 ("well"), each hitting its mark

These techniques were not merely martial; in the Zhou ritual curriculum, archery (射禮) doubled as a ceremony of etiquette and moral cultivation, where precision and deportment mattered as much as marksmanship. The tradition was later transmitted to Japan and Korea as 弓道/궁도, where ceremonial archery retains a similar emphasis on form and decorum.

As one of the Six Arts, 五射 sits alongside [[五礼]], [[六楽]], [[五馭]], [[六書]], and [[九数]].
