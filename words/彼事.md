## Notes
- [彼 (char)](../characters/彼%20(char).md) "yon; distal demonstrative; he/she (literary)" + [事 (char)](../characters/事%20(char).md) "act; matter; affair"

彼事 is the distal correlative in the action column: it refers to a matter away from both speaker and addressee — "that business over there," "that affair of theirs," or "what we were discussing before." The character 彼 (bǐ in Mandarin, Dan'a'yo *bǝ*) was the classical Chinese third-person distal pronoun and demonstrative, frequently used in philosophical prose for the contrasted party or the counterexample (彼…此… "on that side…on this side…"). Its distal deictic sense survives in Japanese あ-series forms (あれ, あの, あそこ) and Korean 저-series (저, 저기, 저것), while Mandarin has largely collapsed the three-way distinction into 那 covering both medial and distal.

In Mandarin, 彼事 (bǐ shì) appears in classical and formal prose meaning "that affair" (彼事已過 "that matter has passed"; 勿管彼事 "do not concern yourself with that affair"). Japanese あのこと is the natural spoken equivalent — あの (adnominal distal demonstrative) + こと (matter) — and carries a nuance of emotional distance or unresolved tension: あのことは忘れましょう ("let us forget about that matter"). Korean 저 일 (jeo il) uses the distal native demonstrative 저. Vietnamese *việc kia* (that matter yonder) employs the distal particle kia.

The three-way deictic system (此/其/彼 = proximal/medial/distal) is one of the features Dan'a'yo retains from classical Chinese that most modern East Asian languages have simplified. Japanese preserves all three tiers fully (ko/so/a → これ/それ/あれ), Korean preserves all three (이/그/저), Mandarin has largely reduced to two (这/那), and Cantonese similarly operates with proximal/distal (呢/嗰). Dan'a'yo's mandatory three-way system in the correlative table aligns with Japanese and Korean usage while using sinographic lexemes accessible across the entire CJKV sphere.
