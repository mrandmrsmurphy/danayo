## Notes
- [家](../characters/家.md) "house; family" + [鼠](../characters/鼠.md) "rat; mouse"

Commensal rodents — those that have adapted to live in and around human dwellings and settlements. The term contrasts with [[田鼠]] (field rodents). The principal species:

- **House mouse** (*Mus musculus*) — the small, grey-brown mouse of households, granaries, and warehouses worldwide. Origin: Central Asia; now cosmopolitan.
- **Black rat / roof rat** (*Rattus rattus*) — historically the dominant urban rat of Europe, the Middle East, and Asia; associated with ships and high places (attics, rafters). Primary carrier of plague.
- **Brown rat / Norway rat** (*Rattus norvegicus*) — larger, more aggressive, burrowing; now the dominant urban rat worldwide, having displaced the black rat in most regions.

The relationship between house rodents and human disease is one of the most consequential in recorded history. *Rattus rattus* carrying *Yersinia pestis* — transmitted via flea bite — was the primary vector of the **Black Death** (1347–1353), which killed an estimated 30–60% of Europe's population and comparable proportions across the Middle East and parts of Asia. House rats also carry leptospirosis, hantavirus, salmonella, and numerous other pathogens.

Yet the **Chinese zodiac** places 鼠 (Rat) first among the twelve animals — a position of honor reflecting the rat's association with cleverness, resourcefulness, adaptability, and fecundity in East Asian tradition. The Year of the Rat is considered auspicious for business ventures; people born in rat years are stereotyped as quick-witted and resourceful.

The **laboratory mouse** — a domesticated, inbred strain of *Mus musculus* — has become one of the most important model organisms in biomedical research, second only to humans in the breadth of research conducted. Genetically defined mouse strains (C57BL/6, BALB/c, and thousands of others) underlie the vast majority of mammalian genetics, immunology, oncology, and pharmaceutical testing worldwide.
