## Notes
- [噌](../characters/噌.md) "whoosh; clamorous sound" + [噌](../characters/噌.md) "whoosh; clamorous sound" — stand-in for [[噌]], which cannot appear independently
- 噌噌 doubles a single onomatopoeic syllable to capture a quick, sharp friction-sound — the scrape and scrabble of claws on bark as a cat darts up a tree, the brisk *whoosh-whoosh* of something moving fast and close. 噌 by itself already names a harsh or clamorous noise (its phonetic component 曾 carries an old sense of "to scold, to shout at"), and reduplication does what it always does in these onomatopoeic compounds: it turns a single instance into a rhythm, a repeated texture of sound rather than one sharp burst. The doubled form chēngchēng sits naturally alongside the language's broader stock of sound-words — what Chinese calls 拟声词, Japanese 擬声語 (giseigo), Korean 의성어 (uiseongeo) — categories every one of these languages maintains richly, reduplicating single syllables into vivid little soundscapes that dictionaries can gloss but never quite capture.
