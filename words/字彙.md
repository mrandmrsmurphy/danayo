## Notes
- [字 (char)](../characters/字%20(char).md) "character; glyph" + [彙 (char)](../characters/彙%20(char).md) "collection; class"

In its general sense 字彙 is a collected body of characters — a lexicon, the full inventory of written signs belonging to a language or domain.

The word also names one of the most consequential works in Chinese lexicographic history. The *Zìhuì* (字彙), compiled by Mei Yingzuo (梅膺祚) and published in 1615 during the Ming dynasty, catalogued 33,179 characters organized under a system of 214 radicals ordered by stroke count. This radical-stroke framework — radical first, then number of remaining strokes — became the standard for Chinese dictionary organization and was adopted almost unchanged by the *Kangxi Dictionary* (康熙字典, 1716), which in turn was the basis for the Unicode CJK Unified Ideographs block. The 214 Kangxi radicals, still in wide use, descend directly from Mei Yingzuo's system.

The simplified alias is 字汇, using the simplified form of 彙 (汇). Japanese じい is the regular on-yomi concatenation (JI + I).
