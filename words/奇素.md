## Notes
**奇素** is a semantic translation for the noble gas **Xenon**. It seamlessly merges the 19th-century chemical discovery with the CJKV lexical system through the core concept of "strangeness and rarity."

### 1. Perfect Etymological Alignment: The Stranger
The name **[Xenon](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/xenon#Etymology)** was coined by William Ramsay and Morris Travers in 1898.

- **Original Greek Meaning:** **[ξένος](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/xenon#Etymology)** means "foreigner," "stranger," or "strange thing."
- **Translation Logic:** **奇** (Mandarin: _qí_; Japanese: _ki_; Korean: _gi_; Vietnamese: _kỳ_) encompasses "strange," "rare," and "unusual." By using **奇素**, we aren't just translating a name; we are describing the sense of wonder—the "rare and strange"—that humans felt when first discovering this extremely scarce, non-reactive gas.

### 2. "Strange" Physical and Chemical Behavior
Although it is a noble gas, Xenon behaves quite "strangely" within its group:

- **The Non-Inert Noble Gas:** Xenon was the first noble gas discovered to [form chemical compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xenon#History) (such as xenon hexafluoroplatinate). In a group originally thought to be "absolutely stable," it is the "strange" outlier that breaks the rules.
- **Optical Applications:** Xenon gas produces an extremely high-intensity white light, commonly used in [camera flashes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xenon_flash_lamp) and movie projectors. This "strange light" that mimics daylight further justifies the name **奇素**.
