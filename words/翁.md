## Notes
- [翁 (char)](../characters/翁%20(char).md) "venerable old man; elder; honorific for an elderly gentleman"

翁 is "venerable old man; elder of standing" — a literary and honorific term for an elderly man of dignity or position, carrying connotations of age, wisdom, and respect rather than mere oldness. The character combines 公 (public, venerable, gentleman) with 羽 (feathers), with the feather component traditionally interpreted as evoking the white hair of age. 翁 is not the neutral word for "old man" (老人, 老頭) — it is an elevation, used in poetry, classical literature, and formal address for an elder of learning or standing.

Mandarin 翁 (wēng) is literary and classical: 漁翁 (the old fisherman — a stock figure in classical poetry), 老翁 (venerable old man), 塞翁失馬 (the old man at the frontier loses his horse — the famous idiom about fortune's reversals). Cantonese 翁 (jung1) appears primarily in compounds and names. Japanese 翁 (おきな, okina) is the term for a venerable old man in classical and Noh theatrical contexts — the 翁 mask (翁面, okina-men) is the most sacred mask in Noh drama, representing a divine elder associated with good fortune and longevity; 翁 (おう, ō) appears in classical poetry and names. Korean 옹 (翁, ong) functions as a Sino-Korean honorific suffix for elderly men: 金翁 (Venerable Mr. Kim) in formal or literary address.

The kwin: true reflects that Dan'a'yo's Hangul transcription 옹 exactly matches Sino-Korean 옹 — both derive from Middle Chinese through parallel phonological paths, and as a single syllable, Dan'a'yo and Sino-Korean converge precisely. Vietnamese ông deserves note: it is the standard second-person pronoun for addressing or referring to an older male (grandfather, elderly man, male superior), with its Sino-Vietnamese origin in 翁 — one of the clearer cases where a classical Sinitic literary character became an everyday Vietnamese pronoun.
