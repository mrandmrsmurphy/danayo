## Notes
- [始](../characters/始.md) "to begin; origin" + [祖](../characters/祖.md) "ancestor; founder"

始祖 denotes the founding ancestor — the first person in a lineage, the original practitioner of an art or school of thought, or the progenitor of a species. 始 marks the point of origin; 祖 (graphemically 示, "spirit altar" + 且, "ancestral tablet") evokes the altar tablets through which descendants venerate forebears, grounding the concept of ancestry in ritual practice rather than mere biological fact. Together they cover both the genealogical sense (first ancestor of a clan) and the institutional sense (founding master of a tradition or art form).

The compound is stable across the CJKV sphere. In Chinese, 始祖 (shǐzǔ) appears in genealogical records, art-historical writing, and evolutionary biology — most prominently in 始祖鳥 (Archaeopteryx, literally "first ancestor bird"), the fossil species long considered the link between dinosaurs and birds. Japanese 始祖 (shiso) is used for founders of philosophical schools, artistic lineages, and dynasty lines. Vietnamese thủy tổ uses the Sino-Vietnamese readings (thuỷ for 始, tổ for 祖). A notable homonym arises in Korean: 시조 (sijo) is simultaneously the Sino-Korean reading of 始祖 (progenitor) and the name of the sijo (時調) — the classical three-line Korean verse form — which are entirely unrelated etymologically but phonologically identical in modern Korean.

The kwin=false marking reflects the phonological divergence between Dan'a'yo 시즈 (si + jǝ, reduced vowel for 祖) and Korean 시조 (sijo), where 祖 maps to 조 in Korean Sino-Korean phonology but to 즈 (jǝ) in Dan'a'yo.
