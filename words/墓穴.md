## Notes
- [墓](../characters/墓.md) "grave, tomb" + [穴](../characters/穴.md) "cave, pit, hole" — a burial pit or grave cavity; stand-in for [[墓]], which cannot appear independently

**墓穴** (*mùxué*) is the burial pit — the excavated cavity that receives the coffin or interred remains. Where 墓 names the tomb as a whole, 穴 specifies the pit itself: the hollow dug into the earth. Together they form the standard compound for a grave cavity, used in archaeological description, mortuary ritual specification, and everyday speech about graves.

The 穴 component covers a range of hollow, enclosed spaces — cave (洞穴), acupuncture point (穴位), and here burial pit. In funerary architecture, 墓穴 designates the rectangular or vertical shaft cut into the earth to receive the dead. Han-dynasty tombs expanded this from simple pits into elaborate underground chambers with side chambers, lacquered coffins nested within coffins, and full inventories of grave goods. The compound now extends to any grave cavity regardless of scale or elaboration, from a field burial to an imperial mausoleum.

The most widely known idiom built on the compound is **挖自己的墓穴** (*wā zìjǐ de mùxué*, to dig one's own grave) — to take an action that brings about one's own destruction. Japanese preserves the same image as **墓穴を掘る** (*boketsu wo horu*, to dig a grave), used identically as a metaphor for self-destructive action. The force of both idioms derives from the image of the condemned man digging his own execution pit: an end prepared in advance, sealed before it arrives.

Korean **묘혈** (*myohyeol*) is the Sino-Korean form, used mainly in historical, archaeological, and specialist funerary contexts; everyday Korean uses **무덤** (native Korean, burial mound) or **묘지** (墓地, graveyard/cemetery). Vietnamese **mộ huyệt** (*mộ* from 墓, *huyệt* from 穴) is the standard compound: *huyệt* covers both acupuncture cavity (穴位 sense) and burial pit, and is the normal word for the prepared grave in contemporary Vietnamese funerary usage.
