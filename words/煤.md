## Notes
煤's original classical sense was "soot, smoke-dust" — even, per the Shuowen Jiezi tradition, the black residue used to make ink — not "coal." Mandarin alone shifted this character to its now-dominant modern sense: 煤 by itself commonly just means "coal" in everyday speech (煤炭, 挖煤, "to mine coal"; 煤矿, "coal mine"), with "soot" surviving mostly in the compound 煤烟/煤灰. Japanese すす keeps the older, original sense exactly — soot only, never coal, which Japanese instead calls 石炭 (sekitan). Korean and Vietnamese pattern the same way as Japanese: Korean 매 stays bound in compounds like 매연 ("smoke, exhaust") rather than meaning "coal" on its own (coal is 석탄), and Vietnamese môi (one of several Sino-Vietnamese readings, alongside mai and muồi) survives mainly in classical compounds like môi đài (煤炱, "soot") — the everyday Vietnamese word for coal is the fully native than đá, unrelated to any Sino root at all.
