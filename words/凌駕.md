## Notes
- [凌](../characters/凌.md) "to surpass, to transgress" + [駕](../characters/駕.md) "carriage; to ride" — literally "to ride over, to surmount," extended to overriding a bound or authority one should properly stay beneath; stand-in for [[凌]], which cannot appear independently.

凌 alone denotes ice overflowing its banks — encroaching past a natural limit — and by extension any transgression of a proper boundary. 駕 as the second element carries the sense of "to drive, to command" (as of a carriage), so 凌駕 literally pictures riding *over* something that should instead be carrying or bounding you, and settled into its modern sense of "to override, to surpass, to place oneself above." It appears constantly in legal and political registers across the sphere — the stock phrase 沒有人能凌駕於法律之上 ("no one is above the law") uses it in exactly this structural sense, government/authority *over* a system meant to bind everyone equally.

Cross-linguistically the word tracks closely: Japanese 凌駕する (りょうがする) is used for one thing surpassing or eclipsing another in scale or power (権力を凌駕する "to exceed [someone's] authority"), and Korean 능가하다 is, if anything, the *more* common member of the family in everyday modern usage — freely applied to a rival, a record, or a market leader being outdone. Vietnamese lăng giá, by contrast, stays firmly literary/Hán Việt register; a contemporary Vietnamese speaker would usually reach for a native phrase (vượt qua, vượt trội) instead, making it the outlier of the four living languages in how naturalized the term became.
