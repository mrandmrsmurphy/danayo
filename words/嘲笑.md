## Notes
- [嘲](../characters/嘲.md) "ridicule; scorn; jeer at" + [笑](../characters/笑%20(char).md) "laugh; smile" — stand-in for [[嘲]], which cannot appear independently
- 嘲笑 takes the universally human act of laughing and turns it pointedly against someone — not shared mirth but laughter aimed downward, the cruel chuckle of mockery and derision. The compound's logic is transparent in every language it touches: Mandarin cháoxiào and Cantonese zaau1 siu3 keep "ridicule-laugh" as a single inseparable idea, Sino-Japanese ちょうしょう (chōshō) and Sino-Korean 조소 (joso) both name the contemptuous laugh or sneer leveled at a target — 조소 glossed in Korean dictionaries with the homegrown synonym 비웃음, "the laugh of looking down on someone." Across the region the word marks the precise point where laughter stops being warmth and becomes a weapon — the line between laughing *with* and laughing *at* that every culture seems to feel keenly enough to need its own word for.
