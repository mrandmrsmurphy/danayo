## Notes
- [性](../characters/性.md) "nature; quality; intrinsic disposition" + [詞](../characters/詞.md) "word; the basic unit of speech"

性詞 is Dan'a'yo's word class for statives: words that describe what a referent *is* or *is like*, as opposed to what it *does* (事詞) or what it *is called* (名詞). The character 性 — graphemically 心 (heart/mind) + 生 (to arise naturally), denoting an innate, inherent quality — precisely captures the defining feature of this class: statives name properties that inhere in their subjects rather than events performed by them. A 性詞 like 太 (great), 大 (big), or 公平 (fair) describes a state of being, not an action taken.

Within the Dan'a'yo tripartite content-word system, 性詞 occupy a distinct syntactic niche. Unlike 名詞, which require a copula (是, 有, 在) to function as predicates, 性詞 can predicate directly without a copula — the stative reading is their default function, not a derived one. Unlike 事詞, which require an object (they are basically transitive), 性詞 are intransitive and typically adnominal: they most naturally appear as pre-nominal modifiers within a noun phrase. In formal Dan'a'yo, there is no zero-derivation between these classes; a 性詞 cannot be used as a 名詞 or 事詞 without explicit derivational morphology (such as nominalization with 化 or a relative-clause structure with 之).

The cross-linguistic picture is varied. In Mandarin, adjectives (形容詞 xíngróngcí) directly predicate without a copula — 山很高 ("the mountain is very high") — which aligns with the 性詞 pattern; but Mandarin permits category fluidity that Dan'a'yo explicitly blocks. In Japanese, adjectives are a morphologically complex class divided into い-adjectives (inflecting like verbs for tense and mood) and な-adjectives (requiring な before nouns and だ as predicate copula); Dan'a'yo 性詞 have no such morphological distinction. In Korean, adjectives (형용사 hyeongyongsa) inflect fully like verbs, directly predicate, and take TAM suffixes — the same behavior as verbs except they cannot take an object — a system closer to the 性詞/事詞 distinction than either Mandarin or Japanese. Vietnamese is the most direct parallel: 性詞 in Dan'a'yo corresponds functionally and lexically to **tính từ** (性詞 in characters), which is the standard Vietnamese linguistic term for adjective — a productive stative word class that directly predicates without inflection in a strongly analytic language. The coincidence of Dan'a'yo's coinage with Vietnamese's existing term is not accidental: Vietnamese analytic structure was one of the pressures that shaped Dan'a'yo's preference for stable, rigid word classes.
