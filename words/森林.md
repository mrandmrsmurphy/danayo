## Notes
- [森](../characters/森.md) "forest" + [林 (char)](../characters/林%20(char).md) "grove" — stand-in for [[森]], which cannot appear independently

Both halves of this compound are 会意 tree-multiplication graphs: 林 duplicates 木 ("tree") to give "grove," and 森 triples it for a denser, larger "forest" — the same iconic-repetition strategy as 品 (三口) or 晶 (三日), with the graph itself encoding a quantity. 森林 pairs the two escalating forms into a single word covering the general concept of forest/woodland, from a stand of trees to dense wilderness.

Japanese しんりん (shinrin) is the standard, everyday register word for "forest" (森林浴, shinrin-yoku, "forest bathing," is now an internationally borrowed term in its own right). Korean 삼림 (samnim) is likewise real and current, but sits in a more technical/institutional register — 삼림욕 "forest bathing," 산림청/삼림청-style forestry agencies — next to the far more common everyday native word 숲 (sup), which has no Sino-Korean root at all. Vietnamese shows a similar split: the field value rừng is the ordinary, everyday native word for "forest," while sâm lâm is the real Sino-Vietnamese compound built from the same two characters — attested but narrower and more literary, glossed specifically as "dense forest, thicket" rather than the general term.
