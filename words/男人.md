## Notes
- [男](../characters/男.md) "man" + [人 (char)](../characters/人%20(char).md) "person" — stand-in for [[男]], which cannot appear independently

男人 is "man, male" — the same construction pattern as [[女人]] ("woman"), reinforcing 男 with the general noun 人. 男 cannot appear as an independent vault entry on its own, so 男人 is what legitimizes it.

Mandarin 男人 (nánrén) is everyday and neutral, the direct counterpart of 女人, alongside the more formal/clinical 男性 (nánxìng, "male"). Cantonese 男人 (naam4jan4) patterns the same way. Unlike its counterpart, though, this word doesn't have the same well-attested archaic/ritual-register life in Japanese, Korean, or Vietnamese: [[女人]] found a real, independently attested literary reading in all three (にょにん, 녀인/여인, nữ nhân), tied specifically to women being singled out as a ritually marked category (女人禁制, "forbidden to women," a real historical Buddhist practice) — men, as the unmarked default in that same religious context, apparently never needed a parallel coined term the same way, so 男/男子 sufficed on their own. The readings above (だんじん, 남인, nam nhân) are therefore given as compositional cross-references rather than claims of natural everyday or literary usage. **Korean 남인 deserves a specific caution**: it is homophonous with 南人 ("southerner"), the name of a real and historically significant Joseon-dynasty political faction (Namin, one of the major factional parties) — a genuine ambiguity risk if this reading were ever used in running Korean text.

kwin is false: 男 alone matches its own Sino-Korean reading exactly (남=남), but 人 does not (Dan'a'yo 닌 vs Korean 인), so the compound comes out false under the AND-rule established earlier in this sweep.
