## Notes
- [眼](../characters/眼.md) "eye, eyeball" + [鏡 (char)](../characters/鏡%20(char).md) "mirror, lens" — an eye-mirror; a lens held before the eye.

The compound 眼鏡 tells the history of optics in East Asia: spectacles arrived from Europe in the 16th century and were understood as small mirrors or lenses placed before the eye. The first Chinese records of eyeglasses date to the Ming dynasty, where they appear as luxury imports — Emperor Zhengde (r. 1505–1521) is said to have owned a pair. The framing as 鏡 ("mirror, reflective surface") rather than as a lens or a new optical category reveals how European glass technology was assimilated into existing Chinese concepts.

Korean 안경 (an-gyeong) is the universal Korean term for glasses — a case of Sino-Korean vocabulary completely displacing any native alternative, and one of the most stable loanwords in the Korean lexicon.

Japanese めがね (me-gane, written 眼鏡 or 目鏡) uses the same characters but an entirely native reading: me (eye) + kane/gane (metal fitting). Japanese thus uses Chinese characters while pronouncing them with unrelated native words — a phenomenon called jukujikun (熟字訓). The modern forms めがね (hiragana) and メガネ (katakana) are both common, with the katakana form increasingly used when discussing glasses as fashion accessories.

Vietnamese kính mắt reverses the morpheme order: kính (from 鏡, Hán-Việt) + mắt (native Vietnamese for "eye") — a hybrid that mirrors the logic of 眼鏡 with native vocabulary in second position.
