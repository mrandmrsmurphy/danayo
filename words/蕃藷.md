## Notes
- [蕃](../characters/蕃.md) "flourish; foreign" + [藷](../characters/藷.md) "potato, yam"

The sweet potato (*Ipomoea batatas*) is a New World crop, brought to East Asia by Spanish and Portuguese traders in the late 16th century — first to the Philippines and Fujian, then spreading through the Sinosphere as a famine-resistant staple. The character 蕃, whose core sense is "to flourish, multiply" but which also carries the older meaning "foreign, barbarian" (as in 蕃人, 蕃語), marks the plant's exotic origin, much like 番薯 (the more common Mandarin variant, listed here as an alias) uses 番 in the same "foreign" sense. 藷 itself is the general character for tuberous, yam-like plants, related to 薯蕷 (Chinese yam, *Dioscorea*).

In Japanese, 蕃藷 (ばんしょ) is a literary synonym for 薩摩芋 (*satsuma-imo*), the everyday word for sweet potato — named for Satsuma domain, through which the crop entered Japan via the Ryukyu Islands in the early 17th century. Korean has no native Sino-Korean equivalent in everyday use; the common word is the native term 고구마, borrowed in turn from a Tsushima dialect form of Japanese 孝行芋. The Sino-Korean reading 번저 given here is therefore a learned/compositional reading rather than an attested everyday word — a reminder that not every Dan'a'yo compound corresponds to a living loanword in all five languages.
