## Notes
- [死](../characters/死.md) "die, death" + [骸](../characters/骸.md) "corpse, skeleton"

This word is the stand-in for [骸](../characters/骸.md), which is too bounded to appear alone in Dan'a'yo discourse; 死骸 carries the same sense of a bodily remain.

死骸 denotes a corpse or the physical remains of a dead creature — the body after the animating principle has left it. The compound is matter-of-fact rather than euphemistic: 死 (death) + 骸 (the body as physical structure, bones and flesh). In Japanese (しがい, shigai), 死骸 is the most direct and clinical term for a dead body, used in news reporting, legal writing, and biological contexts — more neutral than 遺体 (itai, "mortal remains," used respectfully for human dead) and more concrete than 屍 (shikabane, the literary and poetic word for corpse). The alternative spelling 屍骸 uses 屍 (an alias of 死 in Dan'a'yo) and appears in classical and literary Japanese.

In Chinese (sǐhái), the compound is literary and somewhat archaic in modern usage; everyday speech more often uses 屍體 (shītǐ) or 遺體 (yítǐ). In Korean (사해), the Sino-Korean reading is less common than the native 시체 (siché) or 주검 (jugeom).
