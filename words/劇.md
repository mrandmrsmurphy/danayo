## Notes
- [劇 (char)](../characters/劇%20(char).md) "drama; intense, severe" — stand-in word for the character.

劇 carries two related senses: a specific theatrical performance or genre, and a broader sense of intensity or extremity. The theatrical meaning is the primary Dan'a'yo sense; the intensifying sense surfaces in compounds like 劇痛 (violent pain), 劇変 (dramatic change), 劇薬 (powerful/dangerous drug). This dual extension mirrors the Greek δρᾶμα, which also shifted from theatrical performance toward general vividness in some modern usages.

The four great theatrical traditions of the Sinosphere each build on 劇 or a near-cognate:
- **京劇** (jīngjù, Peking Opera) — the classical form developed under Qing patronage in the 18th–19th centuries; heavily codified in role types, vocal register, and costume colour symbolism.
- **歌舞伎** (kabuki) — Japanese popular theatre from the Edo period; the character 劇 appears in compounds like 劇場 (theatre building) and 劇団 (theatre company) throughout its institutional vocabulary.
- Korean **연극** (演劇, yeongeuk) — the modern Korean word for drama/theatre; 극 (劇) is the colloquial root and appears in drama criticism, script terminology, and genre classification.
- Vietnamese **kịch** — the direct Hán-Việt descendant of 劇, covering modern spoken drama (kịch nói) and opera-style theatrical forms.

The compound 劇場 (theatre building) and 劇団 (theatre company) are central to performing arts vocabulary across the Sinosphere.
