## Notes
- [導](../characters/導.md) "guide, lead" + [演](../characters/演.md) "perform, practice" — to guide a performance; by extension, the person who does so.

導演 is transparent and specifically Chinese: a director *leads* (導) the *performance* (演) of a film, play, or production, and the compound covers both the act of directing and the director themself with no separate word needed for either. Japanese and Korean both reach for a completely different metaphor for the same role — 監督 (かんとく / 감독), literally "supervise, oversee," borrowed from its older sense of overseeing work or sport (a baseball 監督 is a team's manager, not its "director"). Korean also has a second native option, 연출 (演出, "staging/direction"), used especially for stage and broadcast directors as distinct from film. Vietnamese sides with Chinese here rather than Japanese/Korean, keeping the same guide-plus-perform logic in đạo diễn (導 → đạo, 演 → diễn).
