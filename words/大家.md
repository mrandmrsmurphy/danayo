## Notes
- [大 (char)](../characters/大%20(char).md) "big" + [家](../characters/家.md) "house; family" — neither character's own `stand_in` points here (both are bare self-standing characters). `kwin: true` per the AND-rule, since both are individually `kwin: true`.
- **Major content bug corrected**: `english` previously listed only "noble, lord, lady, gentleman," entirely missing this word's actual HSK-1-relevant sense. In Mandarin and Cantonese, 大家 is an extremely common, grammaticalized pronoun meaning "everyone; everybody" (大家好! "Hello everyone!") — corrected the primary gloss accordingly.
- **A striking four-way cross-linguistic divergence, documented rather than forced into agreement**: the same two characters have split into unrelated senses in each language (all verified via search) — Mandarin/Cantonese "everyone" (a grammaticalized pronoun, the sense this entry is for); Japanese おおや (ooya) "landlord" (owner of rented property; a different reading, たいか, means "master, authority in a field," but neither means "everyone"); Korean 대가 "master; distinguished expert or authority" (음악의 대가 "a master musician"); and Vietnamese đại gia, a modern, extremely common slang word for "tycoon; wealthy business person." None of the latter three retain "everyone" as a sense — Vietnamese instead expresses "everyone" with the entirely native, unrelated mọi người, so `vietnamese` was left blank rather than force đại gia into a gloss it doesn't carry.
- Japanese and Korean fields were kept as their own real, attested words (not removed) since they are genuine readings of this exact compound — just documented as unrelated senses rather than silently presented as if they meant "everyone" too.
