## Notes
- [泥 (char)](../characters/泥%20(char).md) + [婆](../characters/婆.md) + [羅](../characters/羅.md) — phonetic rendering of Sanskrit *Nepāla*

泥婆羅 is the Tang dynasty Chinese rendering of *Nepāla*, attested in the *Tang Shu* (唐書) and in diplomatic records of Nepali embassies to the Tang court. Xuanzang's *Dà Táng Xīyù Jì* (大唐西域記, 629–645 AD) uses the closely related variant 尼波羅 for the same kingdom. These are competing phonetic approximations of the same Sanskrit source; 泥婆羅 is preferred here over the modern PRC transcription 尼泊爾, which has no deeper claim to accuracy and lacks the textual pedigree. The traditional Chinese form (as used in Taiwan, Hong Kong, and classical scholarship) is 尼泊爾, included here as an alias.

The Nepal valley was one of the most important nodes on the Buddhist transmission route between India and Tibet. Situated in the high Himalaya between the Gangetic plain and the Tibetan plateau, it served as a way-station for pilgrims, texts, and teachers moving in both directions. The Licchavi dynasty (c. 400–750 AD), which ruled during the Tang period, maintained simultaneous relations with both the Tang court and the early Tibetan Yarlung dynasty; the Nepali princess Bhrikuti is credited in Tibetan tradition with bringing Buddhism to Tibet alongside the Tang princess Wencheng (文成公主). This triangular relationship — Tang, Tibet, Nepal — gave 泥婆羅 a significance in the spread of Mahāyāna and Vajrayāna Buddhism disproportionate to its size.
