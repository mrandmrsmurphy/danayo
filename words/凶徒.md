## Notes
- [凶 (char)](../characters/凶%20(char).md) "unlucky; sinister" + [徒](../characters/徒.md) "disciple; follower; member of a group"

A villain, ruffian, or member of a criminal band; collectively, a mob or gang. The character 徒 (follower, group member, disciple) is notable for appearing in both morally positive and negative compounds: [[使徒]] ("apostle, one who is sent") and 信徒 ("believer, adherent of a faith") describe devoted followers of a cause or creed, while 凶徒 and the related [[暴徒]] ("mob, rioters") describe group membership defined by violence and criminality. The suffix thus marks **collective identity** — 凶徒 are not merely individual villains but people who belong to a group characterized by its malevolence.

In classical Chinese historiography, 凶徒 described **rebel factions, bandit gangs, and organized criminal confederacies** — those who took up arms against legitimate authority or preyed systematically on civilians. It appears in official documents condemning uprisings, in dynastic histories describing the suppression of outlaws, and in military dispatches identifying enemy forces as illegitimate. The term carries a tone of official condemnation: to be called 凶徒 is to be denied legitimacy as a political actor and classified instead as a criminal mob.

In modern usage, 凶徒 has a slightly archaic, literary flavor compared to colloquial terms like 歹徒 (dǎitú, "criminal, thug") in Chinese or ヤクザ (yakuza) in Japanese. It appears in newspaper editorials, historical fiction, formal legal condemnations, and literary descriptions of villainy where a more elevated register is appropriate. In Japanese, the close synonym **暴徒** (bōto, "mob; rioter") is more common in modern contexts for group criminal violence, while 凶徒 retains its classical resonance.
