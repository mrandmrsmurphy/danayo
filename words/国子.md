## Notes
- [国](../characters/国.md) "nation; state" + [子](../characters/子.md) "child; son"

国子 is "princes; sons of the state" — 国 (nation; state) + 子 (child; son). The compound originates in the 周禮 (Rites of Zhou), where 國子 referred to the sons of the feudal nobility — the sons of the lords (諸侯) and high ministers who were educated at the court academy. Literally "children of the state," 國子 named those whose fathers held sovereign or near-sovereign authority over a feudal domain. From the Han dynasty onward the sense broadened: 國子 came to denote the students of the imperial national university, the 國子監, named after them — the highest institution of classical learning in the dynastic East Asian state.

The compound survives most visibly in 国子監 (Guózǐjiàn / こくしかん / Quốc Tử Giám): the imperial academy maintained in the capitals of the major CJKV dynasties. The Beijing 國子監, founded in the Yuan dynasty and maintained through Ming and Qing, is now a museum and Confucian temple complex at the heart of the city's historical education district. The Quốc Tử Giám in Hanoi, established in 1070 under the Lý dynasty, is Vietnam's oldest university — still standing and a UNESCO World Heritage site. In Joseon Korea, the equivalent institution was the 성균관 (成均館, Sungkyunkwan), whose name survives in Sungkyunkwan University. Japanese 国子 (こくし, kokushi) appears in historical and classical contexts.

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 곡즈 differs from Korean 국자 in both syllables. 国 reads 곡 (gog/ㄍㄛㄎ) in Dan'a'yo but 국 (guk) in Sino-Korean — MC 見母 (k) + 德合韻 (wək) gives Dan'a'yo ㅗ (o-vowel) while Sino-Korean gives ㅜ (u-vowel); this 곡/국 split is consistent across all 国-containing compounds in Dan'a'yo (cf. 中国, 中国人, 中华民国). 子 reads 즈 (jǝ/ㄐㄜ) in Dan'a'yo but 자 (ja) in Sino-Korean — the same MC 之韻 (ɨ) → Dan'a'yo schwa vs Sino-Korean -a split found across the 陽子/様子/養子 homophone set.
