## Notes
- [射](../characters/射.md) "shoot; eject; emit" + [出 (char)](../characters/出%20(char).md) "exit; issue forth" — to shoot out, emit, or eject; stand-in for [[射]], which cannot appear independently

**射出** (*shèchū*) describes the forceful emission or ejection of something from a source — light from a lamp, liquid from a nozzle, a projectile from a barrel. The compound is a verb-complement construction (射 "shoot" + 出 "out"), making the directionality explicit: the thing is not merely emitted but sent outward.

The dominant technical use of 射出 is in **射出成形** (*shèchū chéngtǐng*; Japanese: 射出成形 *shashutsu seikei*, injection moulding): the manufacturing process in which molten plastic, metal, or glass is injected under high pressure into a mould cavity and allowed to solidify into the desired shape. Injection moulding is the foundational process of modern plastic manufacturing — virtually every plastic component in consumer electronics, automotive interiors, and medical devices is produced by some form of 射出成形. The process was patented by John Wesley Hyatt and Isaiah Hyatt in 1872 AD for celluloid, and modern thermoplastic injection moulding developed through the 1930s–1950s AD.

In **physics and optics**, 射出 describes the emission of radiation, particles, or energy: 光射出 (light emission), 電子射出 (electron emission, as in the photoelectric effect). In **biology and medicine**, 射出 appears in contexts of forceful expulsion — related compounds include [[射精]] (ejaculation) and 射出量 (ejected volume, output volume in fluid dynamics).

Japanese **しゃしゅつ** (*shashutsu*) is the standard technical term in manufacturing, physics, and engineering. Korean **사출** (*sachul*) is equally standard in the manufacturing sense — 사출 성형 (injection moulding) is a major sector of Korean industry — and also appears in the physics sense of emission. Vietnamese **xạ xuất** is the Sino-Vietnamese compound; in technical contexts, the loanword terminology from French or English tends to dominate in Vietnamese manufacturing discourse.
