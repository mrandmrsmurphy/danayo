## Notes
- [桃](../characters/桃.md) "peach" + [山 (char)](../characters/山%20(char).md) "mountain" — literally "peach mountain," a native Japanese place name (もも + やま) read with native readings rather than borrowed Sino-Japanese values, the same pattern as [[安土]]. It names a hill in the Fushimi area of southern Kyoto.

The name is in fact younger than the historical period it lends its name to. Toyotomi Hideyoshi built Fushimi Castle (伏見城) on this hill in 1594 as his retirement residence and de facto seat of power after he had unified Japan; at the time, the site was known simply as Fushimi, not Momoyama. The castle was dismantled early in the Edo period (1623), and peach trees were planted across its ruins; only afterward, once the hill had become known for its peach blossoms, did the name 桃山 (Momoyama) attach to it. Historians later paired this retrospective name with [[安土]] (Azuchi) to label the whole Azuchi–Momoyama period (1568–1600/1603) — meaning the period's own name was coined after the fact, from a name that did not yet exist while the events it describes were taking place. The period itself covers Oda Nobunaga's and then Hideyoshi's drive to reunify Japan after the [[室町|Muromachi]] shogunate's collapse into the Sengoku era, ending with Hideyoshi's death in 1598 and the consolidation of power by Tokugawa Ieyasu after the Battle of Sekigahara (1600).
