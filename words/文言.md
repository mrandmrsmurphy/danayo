## Notes
- [文](../characters/文.md) "culture, writing" + [言 (char)](../characters/言%20(char).md) "speech, words" — literally "written language."

文言 (wényán) refers to Literary Chinese: the conservative written register used across the Sinosphere from the end of the Han Dynasty onward, modeled on but distinct from the [[古文]] ("Ancient Writing") of the pre-Han classics. Where 古文 names the language of the classical canon itself, 文言 names the broader literary register — the formal, archaic-flavored written Chinese that remained the standard for scholarship, administration, and belles-lettres for nearly two millennia, only displaced by vernacular written Chinese (白話) in the early 20th century.

The Japanese ぶんげん and Korean 문언 both carry the more general sense of "wording" or "the text of a document" — the specific phrasing or language used in a piece of writing — a natural extension of "written language" to "the words on the page." The Vietnamese văn ngôn, by contrast, follows the Chinese usage closely and refers specifically to Literary Chinese as a register, as studied in traditional Vietnamese education alongside [[古文]].

[[Lookup/CC/Classical Chinese]] introduces 文言 as the name for "the language used after the fall of the Han Dynasty," in contrast to 古文.
