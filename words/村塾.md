## Notes
- [村](../characters/村.md) "village" + [塾](../characters/塾.md) "private tutorial school" — a village-level private school; the grassroots educational institution of pre-modern rural East Asia

**村塾** (*cūn shú*) is a rural private school — a 塾 (private tutorial establishment) operating at the village level rather than in a city or under official patronage. The 塾 was the fundamental unit of private education throughout pre-modern East Asia: a scholar, often without a government post, teaching reading, writing, and the classical curriculum to students who paid fees or offered goods in lieu. The 村塾 specifically is this institution at its most local: a single teacher, often in a single room, serving the children of one village or cluster of villages.

In **China**, the rural equivalents went by several names — 私塾 (*sīshú*, private school), 義塾 (*yìshú*, charity school supported by a clan or philanthropist), and 村塾 (*cūnshú*) for the specifically rural variant. Before the Qing dynasty's failed attempts at a national school system and the eventual establishment of modern schools in the early Republic, these institutions were virtually the only path to literacy outside official academies (書院, *shūyuàn*). Their curriculum centred on the Three Character Classic (三字經), the Hundred Family Surnames (百家姓), the Thousand Character Classic (千字文), and then the Four Books and Five Classics.

The most celebrated 村塾 in East Asian history is **松下村塾** (*Shōka Sonjuku*, "Pine-Under Village School"), established by Yoshida Shōin (吉田松陰, 1830–1859 AD) in Hagi, Chōshū domain. From this small thatched building Yoshida taught — for less than two years before his execution — the generation that led the Meiji Restoration: Itō Hirobumi, Yamagata Aritomo, Inoue Kaoru, and others who became the architects of modern Japan. The 松下村塾 was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage site in 2015 AD as part of the Sites of Japan's Meiji Industrial Revolution, marking the distance between its humble physical form and its historical consequences.

In **modern Japan**, the 塾 has been reborn as the 学習塾 (*gakushūjuku*, cram school) — the private supplementary education industry that now operates alongside, and often in tension with, the public school system. The 村塾 is the historical ancestor of this institution, though the modern 塾 is typically urban, commercially operated, and oriented toward examination performance rather than classical formation. Korean **촌숙** (*chonseuk*) is the direct Sino-Korean equivalent and appears in historical contexts; Vietnamese **thôn thục** is the Sino-Vietnamese form, used in scholarly discussion of the region's shared educational heritage.
