## Notes
- [慣](../characters/慣.md) "habitual, accustomed" + [用](../characters/用.md) "use" + [句 (char)](../characters/句%20(char).md) "phrase, clause" — a phrase whose meaning has become fixed by habitual use, rather than by the literal sum of its words.

慣用句 is a precise technical sibling of [[成語]] and its subtype [[四字成語]]: where 成語 specifically denotes a phrase with a literary or classical pedigree (often four characters drawn from a canonical source), 慣用句 is the broader linguistic category for any expression — of any length, any register, any origin — that speakers reproduce as a fixed unit rather than generate freely. English examples: "kick the bucket," "by and large," "as the crow flies." These are 慣用句: their meanings are opaque to literal analysis and must be learnt as wholes.

[[熟語]] (jukugo / 숙어) overlaps with both terms and is sometimes used as a near-synonym of either, depending on the tradition. 慣用句 is well attested in exactly this technical sense in Japanese linguistics (慣用句) and Korean linguistics (관용구), but Mandarin and Cantonese favour 惯用语 (with 語/语 rather than 句) or simply 成语 for the broader category, so the precise three-character form 慣用句 is a Japanese–Korean coinage that Dan'a'yo has adopted as its general term for idiomatic expressions.

The compound 慣用句 is itself meta-linguistic — it names the category to which Dan'a'yo [[四字成語]] and [[成語]] belong, and also the category that includes phrases from the grammar book that are taught as fixed units. In a constructed language vault, distinguishing 慣用句 from 成語 is practically important: not every fixed expression has a classical source or cultural weight.
