## Notes
- [遡](../characters/遡.md) "go upstream; trace back" + [及 (char)](../characters/及%20(char).md) "reach out to"

Retroactivity; the principle of applying a law, decision, or effect to events that occurred before the rule was established. The compound is metaphorically precise: 遡 (to go upstream, to trace a river back to its source) + 及 (to reach, to arrive at) = "to reach backward upstream in time." To 遡及 a law is to send it upstream against the current of time, making it arrive at events it did not govern when they occurred.

In **law**, the question of retroactivity (遡及性, sokyūsei) is fundamental to legal certainty. The principle of **non-retroactivity** — that criminal laws must not be applied to acts committed before the law existed — is enshrined in most modern legal systems as a basic protection against arbitrary state power. In Japan, Article 39 of the Constitution explicitly prohibits retroactive criminal punishment. The phrase **遡及適用** (sokyū tekiyō, "retroactive application") describes the exceptional cases where laws do apply backward: remedial legislation, civil procedure reforms, certain tax provisions.

The legal taxonomy contrasts **遡及効** (sokyūkō, "retroactive effect") with **不遡及効** (fu-sokyūkō, "non-retroactive effect; prospective effect only"). A law with 遡及効 reaches into the past; one with 不遡及効 applies only from the moment of enactment forward. This distinction matters enormously in transitional justice, post-war tribunals, constitutional amendments, and contract law.

In Korean, **소급** (遡及, sogeup) is used in 소급 적용 ("retroactive application") and 소급 입법 ("retroactive legislation"), parallel to the Japanese. Both legal systems share this Chinese-derived vocabulary for discussing temporal scope of law, reflecting the common classical Chinese foundation of East Asian legal traditions.

Also written 溯及 (using 溯 rather than 遡, both meaning "to go upstream"); the 溯 form is more common in Chinese contexts.
