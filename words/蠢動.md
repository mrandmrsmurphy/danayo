## Notes
- [蠢](../characters/蠢.md) "squirm; wiggle; foolish" + [動](../characters/動%20(char).md) "move" — stand-in for [[蠢]], which cannot appear independently
- 蠢動 starts with the lowliest kind of motion — the blind wriggling of worms and insects, captured in 蠢's radical 虫 ("bug, worm") and its core sense of squirming, witless movement — and lifts that image into a pointed metaphor for human misbehavior. To say that troublemakers, bandits, or enemy forces are "蠢動-ing" is to liken their stirrings to vermin working their way through the dark: aimless from a distance, but ominous up close, the first restless signs of trouble before it fully breaks into the open. The compound carries this same derogatory shading everywhere it appears — Mandarin chǔndòng and Cantonese ceon2 dung6, Sino-Korean 준동 (jundong, often paired with descriptions of bandits or enemy infiltrators "준동하다"), Sino-Japanese しゅんどう (shundō), and Sino-Vietnamese xuẩn động all describe not mere movement but unwelcome, unsettling movement — the twitch in the grass that tells you something is there before you can see what it is.
