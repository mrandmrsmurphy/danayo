## Notes
- [感](../characters/感.md) "to feel; to sense; to be affected" + [冒](../characters/冒.md) "to brave; to venture into; to expose oneself"

感冒 encodes a classical Chinese medical theory of illness: 感 (to be affected by an external force, to sense something penetrating inward) and 冒 (to expose oneself to the elements, to venture recklessly into something harmful) together describe the act of exposing one's body to pathogenic external forces — wind-cold (風寒) or wind-heat (風熱) — that then penetrate the weakened defensive qi (衛氣). The compound thus names the illness through its cause in traditional medicine rather than through its symptoms.

In modern Mandarin, 感冒 (gǎnmào) is the everyday term for the common cold, functioning as both noun (我有感冒 — I have a cold) and predicate (我感冒了 — I've caught a cold). It appears on drug packaging (感冒藥), calendars (感冒季節, cold season), and in Taiwan Mandarin has also developed the informal meaning "to dislike" or "to be put off by" something. In Japanese, 感冒 (kanbō) is formal and clinical; ordinary speech uses 風邪 (kaze, "wind-evil"), a native Japanese term. A significant lexical divergence occurs in Korean: the Sino-Korean form would be 감모 (gammo), but Korean instead uses the word 감기 (gamgi), which has displaced the sinographic compound entirely in everyday speech. This is why kwin=false: Dan'a'yo 감맛 (gam + mau, concatenated sinographic readings) differs from Korean 감기 not only phonologically but lexically.
