## Notes
- [道 (char)](../characters/道%20(char).md) "Way; Logos" + [成 (char)](../characters/成%20(char).md) "become" + [肉 (char)](../characters/肉%20(char).md) "flesh; meat" + [身](../characters/身.md) "body" — the theological doctrine that the divine Word or Logos took on human flesh and became a person

道成肉身 translates John 1:14: 「道成了肉身，住在我們中間」("And the Word became flesh and dwelt among us"). The choice of 道 for the Logos is itself a profound theological and philosophical decision by the early Chinese Bible translators — 道 in classical thought is the foundational ordering principle of the cosmos, the Way that underlies all things, and using it for the Johannine Logos bridges Greek metaphysics and Chinese cosmology.

The compound 肉身 (flesh-body) is a well-attested classical expression for the human physical body, particularly one subject to mortality and sensation, as opposed to 靈魂 (spirit-soul). To say the 道 became 肉身 is to say the divine principle took on the full weight of mortal embodiment.

Across the Sinosphere, 道成肉身 is shared in Catholic, Protestant, and Eastern Orthodox theology. The Korean 도성육신, Japanese どうじょうにくしん, and Vietnamese đạo thành nhục thân all follow the same character readings directly.
