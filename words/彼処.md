## Notes
- [彼 (char)](../characters/彼%20(char).md) "yon; distal demonstrative" + [処 (char)](../characters/処%20(char).md) "place; location"

彼処 is the distal place correlative — "yonder; over there; at that distant place." It refers to a location away from both speaker and addressee: a place not in the immediate vicinity of either participant in the conversation, whether physically remote or simply outside the shared spatial frame. The Chinese alias 那邊 (nàbiān, "that side/direction") captures the directional-distal sense particularly well — not just "that place" but "toward/at that side over there."

Japanese offers two equivalents: あそこ (asoko, the standard distal place pronoun — "over there") and あちら (achira, the polite form, also meaning "that direction/way"). あちら is used in formal speech and business contexts where あそこ would be too blunt: あちらへどうぞ ("please go this way — over there"). Korean 저기 (jeogi, "there — distal") is the distal member of the 이기/거기/저기 (here/there/yonder) set. Vietnamese đằng kia (over there, distal) explicitly marks the three-way distinction that Mandarin has mostly lost: Mandarin 那邊 (nàbiān) covers both medial and distal, which is why the alias 那邊 is more precise than 那裡 for the distal slot.

The 処 row (此処/其処/彼処) and the Japanese ko/so/a place series (ここ/そこ/あそこ) align almost perfectly — both maintain the full three-way proximal/medial/distal distinction for places. This alignment makes the 処 row one of the easiest sections of the Dan'a'yo correlative table for Japanese speakers to internalise, since the deictic semantics map one-to-one. Korean 여기/거기/저기 similarly provides a ready three-way parallel.
