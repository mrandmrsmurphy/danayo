## Notes
- [道 (char)](../characters/道%20(char).md) "way"

道 is "way, path, road" — and, in its extended philosophical sense, "the Way" or "doctrine," the central concept of Daoism ([[道教]]) and a term borrowed across the region for any codified discipline or practice: [[武道]] "martial arts," [[茶道]] "the Way of Tea," [[書道]] "the Way of Writing (calligraphy)," and the many Japanese martial/artistic "-dō" disciplines ([[柔道]] judo, [[剣道]] kendo, [[弓道]] archery). The character is 会意 (a compound of meaning): [[Radical 162|辵]] "walk, road" plus [[首 (char)|首]] "head" — literally "the head leading the way," which the character page glosses as "path, way, doctrine."

Mandarin 道 (dào). Cantonese 道 (dou6). Japanese on'yomi どう (dō) is what appears both standalone and in nearly every compound (道路 dōro "road," 道徳 dōtoku "morality"); the native kun'yomi みち (michi, "path, road") is common in everyday speech and place names but doesn't carry over into the Sino-Japanese compounds above. Korean 도 (do) is the Sino-Korean reading used in compounds (도로 "road," 도덕 "morality"), while the everyday native word for "road, path" is 길 (gil). Vietnamese đạo is the Sino-Vietnamese reading, prominent in religious and philosophical vocabulary (Đạo giáo "Daoism," Phật đạo "the Buddhist path"), alongside the native everyday word for "road," đường.

道 shares its exact Dan'a'yo reading (ㄉㄚㄨ/dau/닷) with one true homophone, [[悼]] "to lament, mourn" — an unrelated character that happens to fall on the same Middle Chinese syllable (d + ɑu). The syllable page [[syllables/ㄉㄚㄨ|ㄉㄚㄨ]] lists several other same-reading characters (到, 導, 桃, 盗, 稲, 逃, 陶), but all of those require a disambiguating compound to be used at all (bound morphemes) rather than standing alone as words, so only 悼 needs the reciprocal homophone callout.

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 닷 and Korean 도 diverge — Dan'a'yo's transcription of the MC 豪韻 diphthong ɑu (with no coda) into Hangul takes the coda-marking form 닷, while the corresponding Sino-Korean reading 도 has no coda at all. Both ultimately derive from the same MC syllable (d + ɑu, 定母 + 豪韻), but Dan'a'yo's Hangul convention for this diphthong and Korean's own historical development of it don't produce matching forms.
