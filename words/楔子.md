## Notes
- [楔](../characters/楔.md) "wedge" + [子](../characters/子.md) "small thing" (nominal suffix)

楔子's literal sense is the carpenter's wedge — a small tapered piece of wood or metal driven into a gap to tighten a joint, fix a tool-head to its handle, or split wood. 楔 is built from the wood radical 木 with phonetic 契 (qì, also seen in 契約 "contract"), and the bound noun-forming suffix 子 (as in [[椅子]] "chair" and [[孫子]]) turns the bare character into a free-standing word for the small physical object.

From this literal "small wedge driven into a larger structure," 楔子 developed a literary, structural sense: a short framing piece — prologue, preface, or interlude — wedged in before the main body of a work to set its context. This usage is most associated with Yuan-dynasty zaju drama, where a 楔子 was a brief scene (often just one act) inserted before the first act or between acts to bridge the plot, and it carries over into vernacular novels: both *Water Margin* (水滸傳) and *Journey to the West* (西遊記) open with a chapter explicitly labeled 楔子, framing the story before the main narrative proper begins — the textual equivalent of the carpenter's wedge holding the larger structure together.
