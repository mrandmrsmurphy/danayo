## Notes
- [赦](../characters/赦.md) "pardon; forgive" + [免](../characters/免.md) "excuse; exempt; release" — a synonym compound for the formal remission of punishment; stand-in for [[赦]], which cannot appear independently

**赦免** (*shèmiǎn*) is the formal remission of a penalty — the act by which an authority with the power to punish instead releases the offender from that punishment, whether fully or in part. The compound joins 赦 (to pardon, to forgive a wrong) and 免 (to excuse, to exempt from an obligation or penalty), both pointing toward the same act of release.

In **political and legal** contexts, 赦免 typically refers to one of two instruments. A **大赦** (*dàshè*, general amnesty) is a blanket pardon issued by a sovereign or head of state to a class of offenders — historically issued on occasions of imperial accession, dynastic celebrations, military victories, or natural disasters as an act of benevolence resetting the state's relationship with its subjects. A **特赦** (*tèshè*, special pardon) is an individual grant of clemency to a specific person or group. In modern constitutional systems, the power of pardon (赦免権 in Japan, 사면권 in Korea) is typically vested in the head of state and exercised on the advice of the executive.

In East Asian imperial governance, amnesty was a regular instrument of statecraft. The Han, Tang, Song, Ming, and Qing dynasties all issued amnesties frequently — the *Veritable Records* (實錄) of any dynasty contain numerous edicts of 赦免, typically tied to the ritual calendar and auspicious events. Critics of this practice argued that predictable amnesties undermined deterrence; defenders held that clemency expressed the moral character of the throne and restored social harmony.

In **Christian theology**, 赦免 translates the Latin *absolutio* and *remissio peccatorum* — the absolution of sins in the sacrament of penance, and the general forgiveness of sin through grace. Japanese 罪の赦免 (*tsumi no shamen*, "remission of sins") appears in liturgical and catechetical contexts. Korean **사면** (*samyeon*) carries both the legal (amnesty) and theological (absolution) senses; South Korean presidential amnesties, issued periodically and sometimes controversially, keep the legal sense of 사면 in active public discourse. Vietnamese **xá miễn** is the Sino-Vietnamese compound; the more common everyday terms for forgiveness and pardon in Vietnamese are *tha thứ* (forgive) and *ân xá* (amnesty, clemency).
