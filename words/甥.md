## Notes
- [甥 (char)](../characters/甥%20(char).md) "sister's child; sororal niece or nephew"

甥 is "sororal niece or nephew" — a sister's child, specifically. The character is phono-semantic: 生 (life, birth — the phonetic component, giving the reading) + 男 (male — the semantic component suggesting a male offspring). The original meaning likely specified a sister's son, but the compound 甥姪 (a sister's children collectively) and classical usage show the character broadening to cover any child of one's sister regardless of sex. The distinction between 甥 (sister's child) and 侄/姪 (brother's child) reflects the classical Chinese kinship system's careful differentiation between uterine and agnatic relatives.

Mandarin 甥 (shēng) specifically means a sister's child in classical usage, but the everyday word is 外甥 (wàisheng) — literally "outside nephew" — which is the standard colloquial term for a sister's son; 外甥女 (wàishengnǚ) for a sister's daughter. Bare 甥 appears in formal, literary, or compound contexts. Cantonese 甥 (sang1) similarly. Japanese 甥 (おい, oi) is the standard word for "nephew" — but Japanese does not distinguish a brother's son from a sister's son; おい covers both, with sex (not lineage) being the relevant distinction. Korean uses 생질 (甥姪, saengjil) as the standard term for a sister's child; the bare 생 is the Sino-Korean reading. Vietnamese sinh is the Sino-Vietnamese reading.

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 諺文 상 does not match Korean 생. The Middle Chinese final ɣæŋ (庚二開) gives Dan'a'yo ㄙㄚㄫ (*sang*/상) — preserving the 아 (a) vowel quality — while Sino-Korean yields 생, with an 애 (e) vowel. This a/e split in the 庚 final is a regular phonological correspondence between Dan'a'yo and Sino-Korean. The English gloss "sororal" (from Latin soror, sister) is one of those precision kinship terms that exists because common English "nephew/niece" does not specify the parent's sex — a distinction that classical Chinese encodes by default.
