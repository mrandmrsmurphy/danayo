## Notes
- [主](../characters/主.md) "lord, master" + [従 (char)](../characters/従%20(char).md) "follow, obey"

主従 names the fundamental hierarchical relationship of East Asian feudal and Confucian social organization: the lord and the retainer, the master and the servant, the principal and the subordinate. The compound encapsulates the dyadic bond — not just authority (主) but the reciprocal loyalty (従) that gave it legitimacy. In Japanese (しゅじゅう, shujū), 主従 is the central concept of bushido: the 主従関係 (master-retainer relationship) defined the ethos of the samurai class, demanding absolute loyalty in exchange for protection and recognition. The term appears throughout Japanese historical fiction, drama, and ethical writing.

In Chinese (主从, zhǔcóng), the compound extends beyond the feudal into the grammatical: 主从句 (main and subordinate clauses), 主从关系 (primary-secondary relationship), reflecting the same structural logic of hierarchy applied to language. In Korean (주종), the word carries both feudal and modern connotations — 주종 관계 can describe corporate hierarchies and institutional power structures as naturally as historical lord-retainer bonds.
