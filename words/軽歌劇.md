## Notes
- [軽 (char)](../characters/軽%20(char).md) "light" + [歌](../characters/歌.md) "song" + [劇 (char)](../characters/劇%20(char).md) "drama" — light opera; a musical-dramatic form distinguished from grand opera by its comic or romantic tone, spoken dialogue, and accessible subject matter.

The term is a calque of German *Operette* and French *opérette*, coined during the Meiji period as Japan systematically translated Western theatrical genres. The form reached East Asia via two routes: the European operetta tradition (Johann Strauss II, Franz Lehár, Offenbach) and an indigenous Japanese light theatrical tradition that absorbed Western musical elements. The distinction from grand opera (歌劇) turns on register and intent — 軽歌劇 entertains rather than elevates, favoring romantic comedy and social satire over tragic grandeur.

The 宝塚歌劇団 (Takarazuka Revue), founded in 1914, shaped Japanese popular musical theatre in ways that diverged sharply from Western operetta conventions: an all-female cast, pastel aesthetics, and an audience culture unlike anything in Vienna or Paris. Korean 경가극 follows the same structural logic. Vietnamese *ca kịch* is a looser equivalent covering various musical-dramatic fusions, though it lacks the precise contrast with grand opera that 軽歌劇 carries.
