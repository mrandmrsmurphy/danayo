## Notes
- [倭](../characters/倭.md) "small, pygmy" + [猩](../characters/猩.md) "ape" — bonobo (*Pan paniscus*); shortened from 倭猩猩

倭猩 is the Dan'a'yo shortening of 倭猩猩, the Sinitic term for bonobo — the "pygmy chimpanzee." 倭 names what is smaller than expected of its kind; the bonobo is slightly more slender and lighter than the common chimpanzee, with a flatter face, longer legs, and a parted black hair-cap. It was recognized as a distinct species only in 1929 by Ernst Schwarz, making it the last great ape to be scientifically described.

*Pan paniscus* is endemic to the Congo Basin south of the Congo River — a geographic barrier that separates it entirely from *Pan troglodytes*. Bonobos are notable for a social structure in which females form strong coalitions and occupy high rank, and in which sexual behavior serves as a social lubricant reducing tension. The bonobo has become culturally significant in discussions of human evolution and primate behavior as a counterpoint to the more hierarchical and violent patterns observed in common chimpanzees — a reminder that the great ape most closely related to humans can organize society in radically different ways. Wild populations are restricted to the Democratic Republic of Congo and are endangered.
