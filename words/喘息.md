## Notes
- [喘](../characters/喘.md) "pant; gasp; asthma" + [息](../characters/息.md) "breath; rest" — stand-in for [[喘]], which cannot appear independently
- 喘息 starts life as a literal description — labored breathing, the gasping pant of exhaustion or distress — and the Sinosphere has split cleanly down the middle over how literally to take it. Mandarin chuǎnxī and Cantonese cyun2 sik1 keep the older, more general sense: to pant, to catch one's breath, sometimes used figuratively for "a moment's respite" (一息喘息的機會). Sino-Japanese ぜんそく (zensoku) and Sino-Korean 천식 (cheonsik), by contrast, have both narrowed the compound into a precise medical term — "asthma," the chronic disease rather than the act of panting itself — a shift typical of how clinical vocabularies in Japan and Korea absorbed and specialized older Chinese compounds during the rapid modernization of medicine in the 19th and 20th centuries. Sino-Vietnamese suyễn tức threads a middle path, glossed as "to breathe heavily, to pant" (thở hổn hển) — and notably lends its first half, suyễn, to hen suyễn, the everyday Vietnamese word for asthma, echoing the same drift toward the medical sense found in Japan and Korea.
