## Notes
- [灯 (char)](../characters/灯%20(char).md) "lamp, lantern" + [心 (char)](../characters/心%20(char).md) "heart; centre; core" — the heart of the lamp; the central thread.

The metaphor of 心 ("heart" or "centre") for the structural core of a functional object recurs throughout Chinese: 螺絲心 (screw core), 鉛筆心 (pencil lead), 電池心 (battery cell). A lamp's 心 is its sustaining element — the wick that draws fuel upward by capillary action and feeds the flame.

Traditionally, lamp wicks in China were made from the pith of the rush plant (燈心草, dēngxīncǎo — "lamp-heart grass," Juncus effusus), dried and peeled to expose the spongy white interior. The same pith was used in Chinese herbal medicine as a mild sedative and diuretic. In Europe, "rush lights" — thin candles made by soaking rush pith in grease — served the same function in peasant households for centuries. The convergence across civilisations reflects identical material conditions: the rush plant grows abundantly near water wherever people need cheap, steady light.

灯心 also appears in 灯心絨 (dēngxīnróng), the Chinese word for corduroy fabric — named because the raised parallel ridges of corduroy resemble rows of lamp wicks laid side by side. The metaphor is exact: both the wicks and the fabric's cords are parallel cylinders that channel and hold.
