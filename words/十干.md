## Notes
- [十](../characters/十%20(char).md) "ten" + [干](../characters/干.md) "stem, shield" — the ten Heavenly Stems (甲乙丙丁戊己庚辛壬癸), a cyclical numbering system used in combination with the [[十二支]] (twelve Earthly Branches) to form the sexagenary (60-term) cycle that has counted years, months, days, and hours across the Sinosphere for over two millennia.

**十干**, more commonly called **[[天干]]** ("heavenly stems") in running text — the two terms are synonyms, with 十干 emphasizing the count of ten and 天干 emphasizing their cosmological pairing with the "earthly" branches — originated as a Shang-dynasty system for naming the ten days of the week-equivalent (旬) and for ancestral ritual names, before being generalized into the calendrical cycle still used today for years (e.g. 甲子, 乙丑 …), and historically for the hours of the day and compass directions in combination with the Five Phases (五行) and Yin-Yang.

The Japanese reading **じっかん** (*jikkan*), rather than the expected *jūkan*, reflects the same gemination found in 十回 (*jikkai*) and 十階 (*jikkai*) — 十 + a /k/-initial morpheme regularly surfaces as じっ in Sino-Japanese compounds. Korean 십간 (*sipgan*) and Vietnamese *thập can* preserve the transparent two-morpheme reading. In modern usage, the ten stems survive most visibly in the naming of years (used in historical dating and in names like 甲午 for the 1894 Sino-Japanese War, 壬辰 for the 1592 Imjin War), in school grading sequences (甲乙丙丁 as "A, B, C, D"), and in traditional Four Pillars (四柱) fortune-telling.
