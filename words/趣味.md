## Notes
- [趣](../characters/趣.md) "interest; direction of inclination" + [味](../characters/味.md) "taste; flavor" — the flavor of one's interest; what one finds engaging and pleasurable; stand-in for [[趣]], which cannot appear independently

**趣味** (*qùwèi*) names the quality of interestingness and, by extension, the personal interests and hobbies through which that quality is pursued. The compound joins 趣 (the direction toward which one is drawn, one's aesthetic inclination) and 味 (taste, the experiential quality of something), producing a word that is at once more refined than "hobby" and more personal than "interest" — it suggests a cultivated sense of what is worth one's attention.

In **Japanese**, 趣味 (*shumi*) is the everyday word for hobby, one of the first words taught in any introductory Japanese course. ご趣味は何ですか ("What are your hobbies?") is among the most standard of conversation-opener questions. But 趣味 carries more aesthetic weight than the English "hobby": a person's 趣味 reveals their sensibility — what they find beautiful, engaging, or worth spending time on. 趣味が悪い ("bad taste") and 趣味が合う ("we have compatible tastes") use the word in the evaluative sense, while 趣味の問題 ("a matter of taste") echoes the philosophical *de gustibus* tradition. The word also appears in the compound 趣味人 (*shumijin*) — a person of refined taste and wide-ranging cultural interests, an aesthete.

In **Chinese**, 趣味 (*qùwèi*) is used more in the evaluative sense — the quality of being interesting or tasteful — than as a noun for a personal hobby. 有趣味 means "has interest, is engaging"; 生活趣味 means "the flavour and interest of daily life." For personal hobbies, Chinese more commonly uses 愛好 (*àihào*, what one loves and is drawn to) or 業餘愛好 (*yèyú àihào*, leisure interest). The Japanese sense of 趣味 as a personal leisure pursuit — "my 趣味 is calligraphy" — is somewhat calqued back into Chinese from Japanese influence, but 愛好 remains more natural in that usage.

Korean **취미** (*chwimi*) directly mirrors the Japanese usage: the standard word for hobby in both everyday conversation and formal self-introduction. Vietnamese **thú vị** (*thú* from 趣, *vị* from 味) functions primarily as an adjective — "interesting, enjoyable" — rather than as a noun for a hobby; for hobbies, Vietnamese uses *sở thích* (what one likes) or *niềm đam mê* (passion).
