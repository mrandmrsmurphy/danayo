## Notes
- [水 (char)](../characters/水%20(char).md) "water" + [晶](../characters/晶.md) "crystal; sparkling" — frozen water; the transparent quartz that resembles solidified water; stand-in for [[晶]], which cannot appear independently

**水晶** (*shuǐjīng*) is rock crystal — transparent, colourless quartz (SiO₂) — named across the Sinosphere for its resemblance to frozen, perfectly clear water. The compound encodes a theory of origin: 水晶 is water that has crystallised, congealed over millennia into a permanent solid form. This belief was not merely folk etymology but a considered natural-philosophical position held across ancient Eurasia. Pliny the Elder, writing in the 1st century AD, argued that *crystallus* (< Greek κρύσταλλος, *krystallos*, "ice, crystal") was formed from intense freezing of water over great stretches of time; Chinese natural philosophy arrived at an equivalent conclusion through independent observation of the same phenomenon — the visual identity of clear ice and clear quartz.

The 晶 component is itself a triplication of 日 (sun) — three suns, read as the quality of extreme brightness and crystalline radiance. 晶 compounds appear across the vocabulary of transparent, reflective, and luminous things: 結晶 (*jiéjīng*, crystallisation, crystal lattice), 晶体 (*jīngtǐ*, crystal as a material form), 晶体管 (*jīngtǐguǎn*, transistor — literally "crystal tube").

In classical Chinese cosmology and literature, 水晶 carries a distinctly magical resonance. The **水晶宮** (*shuǐjīng gōng*, Crystal Palace) is the palace of the Dragon King (龍王) at the bottom of the sea — the realm from which Sun Wukong famously stole his staff in *Journey to the West* (西遊記). This mythological 水晶宮 pre-dates the Crystal Palace of the 1851 Great Exhibition in London by centuries, and the coincidence of names became a literary and journalistic touchstone when Chinese writers encountered the iron-and-glass Victorian building for the first time.

In modern usage, 水晶 covers both natural rock crystal (quartz) and manufactured lead crystal glass (鉛水晶, lead crystal — used in cut glass, tableware, and optical instruments). The two are distinguished in technical contexts but merged in everyday speech. 水晶球 (*shuǐjīng qiú*, crystal ball) is the fortune-teller's globe; 水晶鞋 (*shuǐjīng xié*, crystal shoes) are the iconic footwear of Cinderella.

Japanese **すいしょう** (*suishō*) is standard for both natural quartz crystal and crystal glass; the word also appears adjectivally for crystal-clear quality (すいしょうのような, "like crystal"). Korean **수정** (*sujeong*) is the standard term for quartz crystal; the same Korean pronunciation 수정 is also the reading of 綏靖 (see [[綏靖]]), making these two compounds exact homophones in Korean. Vietnamese **thủy tinh** (*thủy* from 水, *tinh* from 晶) now primarily means "glass" in modern Vietnamese — a semantic shift from its original reference to rock crystal — making the Sino-Vietnamese compound somewhat misleading in contemporary usage; *thạch anh* (石英, quartz) is used specifically for the mineral.
