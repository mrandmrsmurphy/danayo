## Notes
叫/규/kêu diverge along an interesting axis: Mandarin jiào and Cantonese giu3 both carry a second, extremely common everyday sense that has nothing to do with shouting — "to be named/called" (他叫王明, "his name is Wang Ming") and "to order/hire" (叫車, "to hail/order a car") — uses so frequent they arguably outweigh the literal "cry out" sense in daily speech. Korean 규 (from Sino root 叫) stays confined to the literal shouting/crying-out sense, mostly bound in compounds like 규환 (絶叫, agonized screaming) rather than standing alone. Vietnamese kêu keeps the "shout, cry out" core (kêu la, "to shout/scream"; kêu cứu, "to cry for help") and extends it to animal sounds (a rooster's crow, a dog's bark) much like Mandarin, but the more literary Sino-Vietnamese reading khiếu is reserved for classical/poetic register — kêu is the word used in ordinary speech.
