## Notes
- [呪](../characters/呪.md) "spell, incantation" + [文](../characters/文.md) "writing, culture" — this word is the stand-in for [呪](../characters/呪.md), which cannot appear independently in Dan'a'yo.

呪文 names the spoken or written formula itself — the specific string of words believed to carry a magical or ritual effect when recited or inscribed, as distinct from the general practice of spellcasting or the bewitchment it produces. The compound is transparent: 呪 "curse, incantation" + 文 "writing, text," literally "incantation-text." The word spans real-world ritual registers (Shinto and Onmyōdō invocations, Daoist talismanic chants, shamanic formulas across the wider East Asian sphere) as well as its enormously productive modern life in Japanese fantasy fiction, tabletop and video games, and anime, where 呪文 is the standard term for a spoken "spell" a character casts.

**Japanese じゅもん (jumon) is a lexicalized reading, not the plain concatenation of each character's on'yomi** (呪 ju + 文 mon/bun would suggest *jubun*) — same gotcha as fused calendar words like 八日 (yōka). じゅもん is simply the fixed, dictionary-attested reading of this specific compound.

Korean 주문 (jumun) and Mandarin/Cantonese zhòuwén/zau3 man4 all preserve the same compound transparently. Modern Chinese generally writes it 咒文, using 咒 — the same simplified/popular-register variant already recorded as an alias on [[呪]] itself — rather than the more literary 呪; both forms are read identically and refer to the same word.
