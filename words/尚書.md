## Notes
- [尚 (char)](../characters/尚%20(char).md) "esteemed; ancient; still" + [書](../characters/書.md) "book; document" — "the esteemed/ancient documents"

尚書 (*Shàngshū*) is the older title of the text now more commonly known as the [[書経]] (*Shūjīng*, *Book of Documents* or *Classic of History*), one of the [[四書五経]]. The name 尚書 — literally "esteemed documents" or "documents of antiquity" — emphasises the text's character as a collection of speeches, pronouncements, and records attributed to legendary and early historical rulers (Yao, Shun, Yu, and the founders of the Xia, Shang, and Zhou dynasties). 書経, by contrast, simply marks the text's canonical status with the generic 経 ("classic") suffix, in parallel with [[易経]] and [[詩経]]. The two names refer to the same underlying text and are largely interchangeable in scholarly usage, though 尚書 is the historically prior title and remains the more common name in premodern bibliographic and commentarial traditions.

Independently of its use as the name of the classic, 尚書 was also, from the Qin and Han dynasties onward, the title of a powerful court office — originally a palace secretary handling the emperor's documents, the position grew in importance until, by the Tang dynasty, the **六部尚書** (Ministers of the Six Boards/Ministries — Personnel, Revenue, Rites, War, Justice, and Works) were the heads of the central government's executive departments, a role roughly equivalent to a modern government minister. This administrative sense of 尚書 survived in various forms across the Sinosphere; the Vietnamese **thượng thư** and Korean **상서** both carry this "minister" sense as well as the textual one, while in Japanese しょうしょ is used essentially only for the classic text.

The textual transmission of 尚書/書経 is itself a famous problem in classical scholarship: the "Old Text" (古文尚書) version, long accepted as authentic, was demonstrated in the Qing dynasty to be a later forgery, while only portions of the "New Text" (今文尚書) — based on texts that survived the Qin-dynasty burning of books ([[焚書坑儒]]) — are considered genuinely ancient. This makes 尚書/書経 one of the central case studies in Chinese textual criticism.
