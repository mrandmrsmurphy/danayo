## Notes
- [有 (char)](../characters/有%20(char).md) "have; to possess"

有 is the possession copula in Dan'a'yo — "to have; to possess" — asserting that the subject possesses or is accompanied by the predicate: 我有書 (I have a book), 此地有水 (this place has water). Among the six Dan'a'yo copulas, 有 occupies the possessive/existential function for things that are possessed or present; its antonym is [[無]] (to lack; to not have). In classical Chinese, 有 blends possession and existence into a single form — 有人 (there is someone; someone exists here) vs 有書 (there is a book; one has a book) — a pattern that carries into Mandarin and Cantonese. 有 is one of the most frequent characters in the Classical Chinese corpus (12th most used) and appears in high-frequency compounds across all five CJKV languages: 有名 (famous), 有機 (organic), 希有 (rare). 有 also serves as the Dan'a'yo stand-in for the character [[有]].

Mandarin 有 (yǒu): the core copula for possession and existence — 我有书 (I have a book), 这里有人 (there is someone here — existential), 有时候 (sometimes — lit. "having time-intervals"), 没有 (méiyǒu, to not have; there isn't). Cantonese 有 (jau5): 我有書 (I have a book); 你有冇？(do you have it? — the existence question uses the 有/冇 opposition: jau5/mou5). Japanese ある (aru, inanimate existence and possession) and いる (iru, animate existence): 本がある (there is a book / I have a book), 時間がある (there is time); 有 (ゆう/YŪ) in Sino-Japanese compounds — 有名 (yūmei, famous), 有機 (yūki, organic). Korean 있다 (itda) covers both possession and location: 책이 있다 (there is a book / I have a book), 거기 있어요 (it's there); Sino-Korean 유 (有) in compounds — 유명 (有名, famous). Vietnamese có: Tôi có sách (I have a book), Có người đây (there is someone here); Sino-Vietnamese hữu (有) in literary compounds: hữu dụng (有用, useful), hữu danh (有名, famous).

kwin is true: Dan'a'yo 유 exactly matches Korean 유. 有 reads 유 ('yu/⼜) in Dan'a'yo — MC 云母 (ø) + 尤韻 (ɨu) gives a null initial (云母 collapses to zero in Dan'a'yo as in Mandarin and Japanese) with the -yu vowel. Both Dan'a'yo and Sino-Korean map MC 云母 to a null initial and MC 尤韻 (ɨu) to the -yu vowel (ㅠ), producing identical 유 forms. The MC 尤韻 is among the more regularly preserved vowels in both systems — a straightforward kwin: true case with null initial and a clean diphthong vowel.
