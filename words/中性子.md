## Notes
- [中 (char)](../characters/中%20(char).md) "middle; neutral" + [性](../characters/性.md) "nature; quality; charge" + [子](../characters/子.md) "particle; small thing (suffix)"

中性子 is "neutron" — the electrically neutral subatomic particle found in the nucleus of all atoms except hydrogen-1. 中 (middle, neutral) + 性 (nature, quality, charge) + 子 (particle suffix, literally "small thing" — see 電子, 陽子). The compound was coined in Japanese (中性子, chūseishi) to render the physics concept: a particle (子) of neutral (中性) charge. The naming parallels 電子 (electron, "electric particle") and 陽子 (proton, "positive particle") in the same Meiji–Taishō era system of Sino-Japanese scientific coinage.

Japanese 中性子 (ちゅうせいし, chūseishi) is the standard scientific term for neutron. Mandarin uses the shortened form 中子 (zhōngzǐ, "neutral particle") rather than the full 中性子, making the compound effectively a Japanese-coined word. Cantonese 中子 (zung1 zi2) likewise. Korean 중성자 (中性子, jungsongja) retains the full three-character form. Vietnamese nơtron is a phonetic borrowing from the French/English "neutron" rather than a Sino-Vietnamese compound — one of the cases where Vietnamese scientific vocabulary chose direct phonetic borrowing over morphological translation.

The neutron was discovered in 1932 by James Chadwick; the East Asian naming reflects the 20th-century wave of nuclear physics terminology coined primarily by Japanese physicists and translators. kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 중싱즈 diverges from Korean 중성자 in both the second and third syllables — 性 reads 싱 (sing/ㄙㄧㄫ) in Dan'a'yo vs Sino-Korean 성 (seong), and 子 reads 즈 in Dan'a'yo vs Sino-Korean 자 (ja). Both are regular correspondences: 性 in Dan'a'yo preserves the vowel from MC 清 final (sing/싱), while Sino-Korean gives 성 with a different vowel development.
