## Notes
- [二 (char)](../characters/二%20(char).md) "two" + [十](../characters/十%20(char).md) "ten"

Compositional across Mandarin, Cantonese, and Sino-Korean (èrshí/ji6 sap6/isip), but Japanese and Korean both carry a second, native-vocabulary layer this numeral shares no cognacy with. Korean 스물 (seumul, bound form 스무) is the ordinary native count-word for 20 — used for counting objects and marking age (스무 살, "twenty years old") — alongside the Sino-Korean 이십 used for math, dates, and phone numbers. Japanese has an even richer native layer: はたち (hatachi) is the everyday word for "20 years old" specifically, built from ancient はた ("twenty," related to ふた "two") plus the old counting suffix ち; the same ancient system produces the rare literary reading ふたそじ (futasoji, "twenty" using そ as an archaic "-ty" suffix, the same pattern behind みそ "thirty" and よそ "forty"). Vietnamese, like the other tens, uses its native numeral hai mươi in all registers — the Sino-Vietnamese nhị thập is a classical-only form.
