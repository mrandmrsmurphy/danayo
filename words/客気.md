## Notes
- [客](../characters/客.md) "guest, traveller" + [気 (char)](../characters/気%20(char).md) "feeling" — neither character's own `stand_in` points here (客's is 客人, 気's is bare 気). `kwin: false` per the AND-rule, since both are individually `kwin: false`.
- **A striking cross-linguistic sense divergence, documented rather than forced into agreement**: Mandarin kèqì/Cantonese haak3 hei3 mean "polite, reserved, standing on ceremony" (as in 别客气, "don't be so polite" = "you're welcome") — extremely common and the sense this entry is titled for. Vietnamese khách khí matches this same sense (verified via search). Japanese かっき (also read きゃくき/きゃっき) and Korean 객기, however, mean something entirely different: "rash impulsiveness; hot-blooded bravado; foolhardy pride" (e.g. 객기 부리다, "to act recklessly out of misplaced pride") — verified via search on both. Kept both Japanese and Korean as their own genuine, attested readings of this exact compound, documented as an unrelated sense rather than silently implying they mean "polite" too.
