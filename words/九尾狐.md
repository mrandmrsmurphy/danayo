## Notes
- [九 (char)](../characters/九%20(char).md) "nine" + [尾 (char)](../characters/尾%20(char).md) "tail" + [狐](../characters/狐.md) "fox"

A mythological creature of East Asian folklore: a fox that has lived for centuries and cultivated such spiritual power that it has grown nine tails, each additional tail representing a further century of life and an increase in supernatural ability.

In **Chinese tradition**, the nine-tailed fox appears as early as the *Classic of Mountains and Seas* (山海経, pre-Qin), where it is described as "a beast whose form is like a fox but with nine tails," initially an auspicious omen associated with righteous kingship. Over time, however, Chinese literary tradition increasingly portrayed it as a dangerous shapeshifter — often taking the form of a beautiful woman to seduce and drain the life force of men.

In **Korean tradition**, the 구미호 (九尾狐) is typically an entirely malevolent shapeshifter: a fox that has lived 1,000 years and seeks to become human by consuming human liver or hearts. It can transform into a beautiful woman but may be detected by certain signs (a tail, a reflection, etc.). The kumiho is one of the best-known supernatural creatures in Korean folklore and continues to appear in modern film, television, and fiction.

In **Japanese tradition**, the 九尾の狐 (kyūbi no kitsune, 九尾狐) is central to the legend of **Tamamo-no-Mae** (玉藻前), a beautiful noblewoman at the court of Emperor Toba (12th century) who was revealed to be a nine-tailed fox in disguise. After being driven away, she became the *killing stone* (殺生石) of Nasu, releasing poisonous vapors until shattered by the monk Gennō.
