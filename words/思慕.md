## Notes
- [思](../characters/思.md) "think, consider" + [慕](../characters/慕.md) "long for, admire" — to yearn for someone or something absent; stand-in for [[慕]], which cannot appear independently

**思慕** (*sīmù*) is the compound for longing — specifically the kind directed toward a person: someone who has died, someone far away, someone above one in status, someone one admires from a distance. 思 provides the cognitive dimension (to think of, to hold in mind) and 慕 the emotional one (to yearn toward, to admire upward). Together they describe a feeling with an object: one thinks of and reaches toward someone who is not reachable.

The character 慕 combines 莫 (none, absent, without) and 心 (heart) — the heart without its object, longing for what is gone or inaccessible. This graphic etymology directly encodes the compound's meaning: 思慕 is the mind and heart directed toward someone whose absence is what defines the feeling. It differs from 思念 (*sīniàn*, to miss someone) in its upward or admiring orientation: one 思念 a friend; one 思慕 a mentor, a deceased ancestor, a beloved who is beyond reach. The compound is not typically used for reciprocal romantic longing in everyday speech — that register belongs to 愛慕 (*àimù*) or 戀慕 (*liànmù*) — but it appears in classical verse and formal prose for deep, sustained reverence-tinged longing.

In Korean, **사모** (*samo*) is the most direct and emotionally resonant word for the feeling. It is used for longing toward a deceased parent or grandparent, for reverence toward a teacher or elder one can no longer reach, and — in a distinctly Korean literary register — for the unrequited longing of a woman for an absent or unattainable man. The word appears prominently in *sijo* and *gasa* poetry traditions as a marker of yearning that cannot be fulfilled. That Korean 사모 is identical to the Dan'a'yo 諺文 concatenation (사 + 모) reflects the stability of both components' Sino-Korean readings.

Japanese **しぼ** (*shibo*) is the Sino-Japanese compound reading, used in literary and formal contexts; the native Japanese equivalents — **したう** (*shitau*, to yearn after, to follow longingly), **あこがれ** (*akogare*, longing, aspiration) — are more common in everyday speech and carry overlapping but not identical ranges of meaning. Vietnamese **tư mộ** (*tư* from 思, *mộ* from 慕) is the Sino-Vietnamese literary compound; modern Vietnamese typically uses **nhớ** (to miss) or **mến mộ** (to admire) in contexts where 思慕 would appear in classical Chinese.
