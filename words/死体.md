## Notes
- [死](../characters/死.md) "die" + [体](../characters/体.md) "body"

死体 (したい, shitai) is the ordinary Japanese word for a dead body — a corpse, whether human or animal, and the standard term in everyday, journalistic, and forensic use alike (床下から死体が発見された, "a body was found under the floor"). The compound is transparent: 死 ("to die") plus 体 ("body"), read on'yomi-on'yomi as shi + tai. The character 死 here also functions as the modern substitute (daiyōji) for the older, more literary 屍 ("corpse"), which survives as an alternate spelling, 屍体.

Mandarin does not use this compound in ordinary speech — 死體/死体 is attested chiefly as a Taiwanese Hokkien form (sí-thé), likely an orthographic borrowing from Japanese, while standard Mandarin prefers 屍體, 遺體, or plain 死人 for the same referent. Korean, by contrast, borrowed the Japanese compound directly as 사체 (死體, sache), and uses it in the same registers (forensic, clinical, veterinary) as Japanese 死体. This is one of several corpse-related terms in the sphere where each language settled on a different head character — 屍, 體, 遺, 死 — despite sharing the same small pool of morphemes for "dead" and "body."
