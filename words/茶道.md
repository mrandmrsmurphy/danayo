## Notes
- [茶 (char)](../characters/茶%20(char).md) "tea" + [道 (char)](../characters/道%20(char).md) "the Way; discipline" — same 道-suffix pattern as [[書道]], [[弓道]], [[剣道]], [[空手道]]

**茶道** (*chadō* or *sadō*, "the Way of Tea") is the Japanese tea ceremony — a highly formalised practice of preparing and serving powdered green tea (抹茶, *matcha*), conducted according to prescribed sequences of movement, utensil handling, and seasonal aesthetic considerations. Like [[書道]], [[弓道]], and [[剣道]], it belongs to the *dō* family of disciplines in which technical mastery is inseparable from spiritual and aesthetic cultivation; 茶道 in particular is closely associated with Zen Buddhism, having developed historically in close connection with Zen temple practice.

The ceremony's modern form is largely shaped by the legacy of **千利休** (Sen no Rikyū, 1522–1591 AD), who codified the aesthetic of *wabi-sabi* (侘寂) — rustic simplicity, asymmetry, and the beauty of the imperfect or transient — as the guiding spirit of the tea gathering (茶会, *chakai*; or the more formal 茶事, *chaji*). The major schools descending from Rikyū's tradition, collectively the **三千家** (San-Senke, "Three Sen Houses": Omotesenke 表千家, Urasenke 裏千家, and Mushakōjisenke 武者小路千家), continue to transmit distinct variations of procedure and aesthetic emphasis to the present day.

The setting matters as much as the procedure: the tea room (茶室, *chashitsu*), typically a small space accessed through a low crawling entrance (にじり口, *nijiriguchi*) that requires guests to stoop and thereby leave status and formality behind; the garden path (露地, *roji*) leading to it; and the careful arrangement of a hanging scroll and seasonal flowers (花入, *hanaire*) in the tea room's alcove (床の間, *tokonoma*). The whole sequence — from the host's preparation through the washing and presentation of utensils to the final cleaning — is understood as a single unified aesthetic and spiritual act, often summarised by the phrase 一期一会 (*ichi-go ichi-e*, "one time, one meeting"): each gathering is unique and unrepeatable.

Both readings *chadō* and *sadō* are current in Japanese, with *sadō* often considered the more traditional reading within tea-practicing circles and *chadō* more common in general usage. Korean **다도** (*dado*) and Vietnamese **trà đạo** denote both the Japanese tradition specifically and, more broadly, the cultivated practice of tea appreciation within their own tea cultures (Korean 다도 has its own distinct historical tradition centred on Korean tea ceremony, particularly associated with Buddhist monastic practice). Mandarin and Cantonese 茶道/*chádào* likewise covers both the Japanese tradition and the (largely modern, partly revivalist) Chinese tea ceremony tradition, which is more often termed **茶藝** (*cháyì*, "tea arts") to distinguish it from the Japanese practice.
