## Notes
- [監](../characters/監.md) "oversee" + [督](../characters/督.md) "supervise" — stand-in for [[監]], which cannot appear independently.

Japanese and Korean both broadened this compound well beyond plain oversight: かんとく/감독 cover "supervisor," a sports team's "coach/manager," and — most distinctively — a film's "director," all under the same word. Mandarin jiāndū never made that last leap; Chinese keeps 监督 strictly in the administrative/regulatory sense ("supervise, oversight, supervisor") and uses a separate word, 导演, for a film director. Cantonese gaam1 duk1 follows Mandarin here rather than Japanese/Korean. Vietnamese giám đốc narrows the compound a different way again: rather than "supervisor" in general, it's the everyday, extremely common word for a company's director or CEO — the specific business-executive sense has crowded out the broader oversight meaning in ordinary usage.
