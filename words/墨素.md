## Notes
**墨素** is a binomial neologism that identifies Antimony by its ancient use as a pigment and its distinctive physical appearance. It replaces the phonetic transcription **锑** (_tī_) with a descriptor that bridges 3,000 years of cosmetic and alchemical history.

### 1. The Etymological Anchor: Ancient "Kohl"
The name "**Antimony**" has a convoluted history, but its chemical symbol **Sb** comes from the Latin **[stibium](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/stibium#Latin)**.

- **The Root:** In the ancient world, the primary ore of antimony, [stibnite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stibnite), was ground into a fine black powder called **[kohl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kohl_\(cosmetics\))**.
- **The Translation:** **墨** (_mò_) literally means "ink" or "black pigment." By using **墨**, we are referencing the element's most famous historical role as the "black ink" used by ancient Egyptians and others to [line their eyes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antimony#History).

### 2. Physical Property: The "Inky" Luster
While pure Antimony is a [silvery, lustrous metalloid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antimony#Properties), its most common natural form (stibnite) occurs as [striking, lead-gray metallic crystals](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/antimony#Noun) that leave a dark, inky smudge on the hands.

- **The Logic:** Calling it **墨素** ("Ink Element") perfectly describes the experience of handling the mineral. It is the element that "writes" or "stains" like ink.

### 3. Single-Character Symbol: 墨
In the formula system, **墨** serves as a unique and high-contrast symbol:

- **The Symbol:** **墨**
- **The Formula:** Antimony Trisulfide ($Sb_2S_3$), the chemical name for the mineral stibnite itself, is rendered as **$墨_二硫_三$** (Ink (x2) + [Sulfur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur) (x3)). This reads as a recipe for "three parts sulfur and two parts ink-stone."
