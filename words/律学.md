## Notes
- [律](../characters/律.md) "statute" + [学](../characters/学.md) "learn" — "legal studies."

In the Tang-dynasty Imperial University system, 律学 named one of the Six Schools (六學) operating under the central Directorate of Education (國子監) alongside 國子學, [[太学|太學]], 四門學, 書學, and 算學 — together with two further institutes, 弘文館 and 崇文館, this formed the full "Six Schools and Two Institutes" (六學二館) structure of central state education. 律学 specifically trained statute clerks and judicial specialists: enrollment of around fifty students was open both to lower-ranked officials' sons and to commoners who already showed aptitude in law, and its course of study ran six years, shorter than the nine years prescribed for the more prestigious classical schools — reflecting law's lower status relative to the Confucian classics in the era's academic hierarchy, even as legal expertise remained indispensable to actually running the state.

The system, and the name 律学 with it, was exported wherever the Tang educational model was adopted: [[高麗]] established its own 國子監 in 992 AD under King Seongjong, with an equivalent Six Schools structure (京師六學) that included its own 律学, and the northern state of Balhae's 胄子監 likewise oversaw legal study alongside its own 太學. In modern usage the term has shed its institutional specificity and simply means the academic discipline of law — Vietnamese Luật Học in particular survives today as the live, ordinary term for legal studies.
