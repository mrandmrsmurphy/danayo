## Notes
- [番 (char)](../characters/番%20(char).md) "time; turn; occasion"

番 is the Dan'a'yo classifier for occurrences, turns, and episodes of effort — 量詞, counting instances of a repeatable action: 一番 (one time; once), 三番苦労 (three instances of hardship), 毎番 (every time; each occasion). The character is a 象形 of fields (田) being turned (番), giving the sense of rotation or alternation — one cycle, one turn; hence "a time; a turn; an occasion." The classifier function is closely connected to the sense of counted repetitions or scheduled rounds: unlike 度 (which neutrally counts occurrences), 番 implies a sequence of turns or a particular episode requiring effort or attention. 番 also serves as the Dan'a'yo stand-in for the character [[番]].

The CJKV classifier landscape for occasions and turns shows notable divergence in productivity. Mandarin 番 (fān): 一番话 (one set of words; one speech), 一番苦心 (one instance of painstaking effort), 三番五次 (time and again; repeatedly — a fixed expression); the colloquial 次 (cì) and 遍 (biàn) carry greater everyday frequency for "time; occasion." Cantonese 番 (faan1): 搞番次 (do it one more time — colloquial); 次 (ci3) and 回 (wui4) are more common in speech. Japanese 番 (ばん/BAN) primarily marks ordinal position or guard duty — 一番 (ichiban, number one; first), 番組 (bangumi, broadcast program), 番号 (bangō, number) — rather than occasions; the classifier for counted occurrences is 回 (かい/KAI): 三回 (san-kai, three times). Korean 번 (番) is active and productive: 한 번 (one time; once), 두 번 (twice), 몇 번 (how many times) — it is the standard counter for numbered sequence items as well as counted occurrences. Vietnamese phiên (番): một phiên (one session; one turn), phiên dịch (翻譯, translation — lit. "turn the language"), phiên tòa (court session); lần (turn; time) is the more colloquial classifier.

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 빤 does not match Korean 번. 番 reads 빤 (fan/ㄈㄚㄋ) in Dan'a'yo — MC 敷母 (fʰ) + 桓韻 (uɑn) gives a labiodental fricative onset with the plain -an final and -a vowel. Sino-Korean 번 (beon) uses a bilabial stop (b-, since Korean lacks f-) and maps the -uɑn final to -eon, a different vowel grade. The MC 桓韻 produced ㄚㄋ (-an/빤) in Dan'a'yo but -eon (번) in Korean — a vocalic mismatch despite the shared labial initial. This 桓韻 divergence is also seen in [[段]] (ㄉ⺢ㄋ/돤 vs 단), where Dan'a'yo preserves the MC -u- medial and plain -a nucleus while Korean reduces to plain -an/-eon.
