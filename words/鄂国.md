## Notes
- [鄂](../characters/鄂.md) "E (state)" + [国](../characters/国.md) "nation"

The State of E (鄂国) was an ancient Chinese polity that existed from roughly the twelfth century BCE until its destruction in 863 BC — a span of approximately three hundred years. Its territory shifted over that period: it originated in southern Henan province, was displaced westward toward Shanxi during the early Western Zhou, and eventually settled in what is now Hubei province near the modern city of Ezhou (鄂州).

E was a vassal of the Shang dynasty; its ruler held a position among the senior ministers of the last Shang king, Di Xin (紂王). After the Zhou conquest around 1046 BC, E continued under Zhou suzerainty but occupied an increasingly precarious position on the southern frontier. In 863 BC, under pressure from Zhou expansion, the ruler of E led a coalition of Huaiyi peoples in open rebellion against King Yi of Zhou (周夷王). The rebellion was crushed, and the state was annihilated. The Zhou victory was commemorated on the *Yu Ding* (禹鼎), a bronze ritual tripod whose inscription records the campaign in detail and is one of the primary sources for E's history.

After E's absorption into Chu, its former heartland became central to Chu's expansion southward. The character 鄂 endured as Hubei province's one-character abbreviation (Hubei is still colloquially called 鄂), a rare case of an obliterated ancient state leaving its name permanently embedded in Chinese administrative geography. In 1211, the Southern Song dynasty posthumously conferred on the general Yue Fei (岳飛) the title *King of E* (鄂王), borrowing the ancient name to honor the man who had spent his military career defending Hubei from the Jin dynasty.
