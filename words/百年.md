## Notes
- [百 (char)](../characters/百%20(char).md) "hundred" + [年 (char)](../characters/年%20(char).md) "year" — a span of one hundred years.

**百年** (*bǎinián*) is the plain durational compound for "a hundred years," parallel in formation to [[千年]] (a thousand years) and [[万年]] (ten thousand years). Like its larger cousins, it denotes a *length* of time rather than a named historical unit — for the latter, Sinitic languages instead borrowed the Western "century" as a calque, 世紀 (*shìjì*/せいき/세기), which counts from a fixed epoch rather than from an arbitrary starting point.

百年 carries a strong idiomatic charge of "a very long time" or "a lifetime," well beyond its literal hundred years: 百年大計 ("a plan for a hundred years," i.e. a long-term strategic plan), 百年好合 (a traditional wedding wish, "may you be in harmony for a hundred years"), and 百年樹人 ("it takes a hundred years to cultivate a person," on the long timescale of education) are all common set phrases. The Japanese reading **ひゃくねん** (*hyakunen*) is the regular on'yomi compound; an older poetic reading **ももとせ** (*momotose*, using native とせ "years") survives in classical and ceremonial contexts. Korean **백년** (*baengnyeon*) and Vietnamese **bách niên** carry the same "a very long time / a lifetime" idiomatic extension, as in Korean 백년해로 ("to grow old together for a hundred years," a traditional wish for a married couple) and Vietnamese trăm năm (the native equivalent, used in the same wedding-vow context).
