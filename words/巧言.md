## Notes
- [巧](../characters/巧.md) "clever, skilful, artful" + [言 (char)](../characters/言%20(char).md) "speech, word" — artfully crafted but insincere speech.

巧言 is best understood through the line in which Confucius fixed it as an ethical category:

> 子曰：「巧言令色，鮮矣仁。」(《論語·學而》1.3)
> "The Master said: 'Fine words and an insinuating appearance — rarely are they associated with true virtue.'"

巧言令色 (qiǎoyán lìngsè) — "clever speech and agreeable appearance" — is one of the most quoted two-word pairs in the Confucian tradition. The target is not eloquence per se but the performance of virtue: speech polished to please rather than to illuminate, appearance arranged to charm rather than to reflect inner character. Confucius returns to the theme at Analects 17.17, adding excessive bodily deference (足恭) as a third variant of the same vice.

The moral contrast is with 訥 (nè) — slowness or plainness of speech — which Confucius consistently associated with genuine virtue. The pairing 巧言 / 訥言 encodes a deep Confucian suspicion of rhetoric: the man who speaks fluently and pleasingly is likely trimming his words to his audience rather than to truth. This is the opposite of Aristotle's view, where cultivated eloquence (ῥητορεία) is a neutral instrument; for Confucius, the very polish of 巧言 is already a moral signal.

巧言令色 is itself used as a four-character compound with the force of a chengyu — a fixed phrase for sycophancy, flattery, or smooth-talking insincerity.
