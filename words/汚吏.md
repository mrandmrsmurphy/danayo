## Notes
- [汚 (char)](../characters/汚%20(char).md) "dirty; filthy; corrupt" + [吏](../characters/吏.md) "officer; government functionary"

汚吏 is "corrupt official" — 汚 (dirty; filthy; corrupt) + 吏 (officer; government functionary). In classical usage, 吏 referred specifically to lower-ranking officials who administered day-to-day governance below the level of appointed ministers; 汚吏 thus names the corrupt functionary at any level of the bureaucratic hierarchy who abuses their position for personal gain through bribery, embezzlement, or extortion. The compound carries classical resonance across all CJKV literary traditions as a stock figure of moral reproach, most commonly encountered as the second half of the 成語 [[貪官汚吏]] — "greedy officials and corrupt functionaries" — where the pair 貪官/汚吏 forms a fixed rhetorical doublet condemning venality in government.

Mandarin 污吏/汙吏 (wūlì) is literary and classical; the everyday term for a corrupt official is 貪官 (tānguān) or 腐敗官員 (fǔbài guānyuán, corrupt official). The 成語 [[貪官汚吏]] (tānguān-wūlì) is the standard fixed phrase in which 汚吏 most often appears — as a set pair rather than a standalone word. Cantonese 汚吏 (wu1 lei6). Japanese 汚吏 (おり, ori) is a literary term for a corrupt official; modern Japanese uses 汚職者 (oshokusha) or 汚職官吏 (oshoku kanri, corrupt government official). Korean 오리 (汚吏, ori): 탐관오리 (貪官汚吏, greedy officials and corrupt clerks) is the standard fixed phrase. Vietnamese ô lại (Sino-Vietnamese: 汚=ô, 吏=lại) — lại is the standard Sino-Vietnamese reading of 吏 as "clerk; functionary," as in 관리/quan lại (官吏, officials and clerks).

kwin is true: Dan'a'yo 오리 exactly matches Korean 오리. 汚 reads 오 in both Dan'a'yo and Sino-Korean — MC 影母 (ʔ) + 模韻 (uo) gives a vowel-initial o-syllable in both systems. 吏 reads 리 in both — MC 來母 (l) + 之韻 (ɨ) gives li in both. A notable coincidence: 오리 (汚吏, corrupt official) is homophonous in Korean with the native word 오리 (duck), a collision that has not gone unnoticed in Korean political satire and commentary on bureaucratic corruption.
