## Notes
- [天 (char)](../characters/天%20(char).md) "heaven" + [神 (char)](../characters/神%20(char).md) "god; spirit" + [地 (char)](../characters/地%20(char).md) "earth" + [祇](../characters/祇.md) "earth-spirit" — literally "heavenly gods, earthly spirits"

天神地祇 is a paired compound built from two further compounds, 天神 ("heavenly gods/spirits") and 地祇 ("earthly spirits"), expressing the totality of divine beings by distinguishing those associated with the heavens from those associated with the earth. The construction parallels [[天地]] itself — a merism in which two complementary halves stand for an exhaustive whole — but applied to the population of [[神霊]] rather than to the cosmos as a physical structure.

地祇 specifically denotes spirits of the earth: mountain gods, river gods, soil gods, and other localized numinous powers tied to particular places, as distinct from 天神, the celestial deities associated with the sky, sun, stars, and weather. Together, 天神地祇 functions as an all-encompassing formula — "every god there is, of heaven and of earth" — used in oaths, prayers, and invocations where completeness of divine witness or protection is the point.

The phrase is especially prominent in **Japanese Shinto** (てんじんちぎ / てんしんちぎ, *tenjin chigi*), where it appears in ritual prayers (祝詞, *norito*) and in formal oaths, invoking the totality of *kami* — both the heavenly deities (天津神, *amatsukami*) descended from the Age of the Gods and the earthly deities (国津神, *kunitsukami*) native to the land — as witnesses or guarantors. This Shinto usage closely parallels the structure of the Chinese term while mapping it onto the specifically Japanese mythological distinction between *amatsukami* and *kunitsukami* found in texts like the *Kojiki* and *Nihon Shoki*.

In Mandarin, Cantonese, Korean, and Vietnamese, 天神地祇/천신지기/thiên thần địa kì is used in more general religious and literary contexts as a comprehensive reference to "all the gods" — comparable to English "heaven and all its powers" — without the specific institutional anchoring that the term has in Japanese Shinto liturgy. See also [[神霊]], of which 天神 and 地祇 are, respectively, the heavenly and earthly subsets.
