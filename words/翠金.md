## Notes




**Form.** 翠金 is 翠 “emerald-green; kingfisher green” + 金 “metal,” following your rule for lanthanides.

**Etymological situation.** _Terbium_ is named after **Ytterby** (a Swedish locality), part of the quartet yttrium, terbium, erbium, ytterbium. The place-name etymology is not practically reducible to a single useful graph in your system, so a direct toponymic strategy is weak here.

**Motivation.** You instead select a **color-based property**:

- terbium compounds (notably Tb³⁺) are known for **bright green luminescence**
- 翠 denotes a vivid, saturated green (classically associated with jade/kingfisher feathers), more specific and elevated than generic 緑/青

Thus the name captures a **distinctive optical property**.

**Type of formation.**  
A **property-based designation** replacing an unusable place-name etymology:

- aligns with your fallback strategy (cf. 硬金, 磁金, 吸金)
- maintains internal coherence by staying within the color/spectrum semantic field already used elsewhere

**Fit within the system.**

1. Meaning-based, not phonetic.
2. Uses 金 appropriately.
3. Provides a unique, visually and semantically distinctive symbol (翠).
4. Integrates well with other spectrum-related names (青, 紫, 茜, 霓).

**Comparative CJKV forms.**

- Modern Chinese: 铽 (phonetic)
- Japanese: テルビウム (phonetic)
- Korean: 터븀 (phonetic)
- Vietnamese: terbi (phonetic)

Modern forms are phonetic; 翠金 restores semantic content.

**Clarity and issues.**

- 翠 is somewhat literary but precise.
- The association “green luminescence” is not universally known at an elementary level, but it is accurate and distinctive.

**Conclusion.**  
翠金 is a strong solution: it replaces an opaque toponym with a precise, high-quality semantic descriptor, maintaining both distinctiveness and coherence within your system.
