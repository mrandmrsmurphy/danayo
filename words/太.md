## Notes
- [太 (char)](../characters/太%20(char).md) "great; extreme; excessive"

太 is the emphatic superlative of [[大]] — graphically, a 指事 character created by adding a small stroke to 大 to mark "the utmost degree of greatness." The same logic produced [[天]] (大 with a stroke above the figure's head, marking heaven) and 太 (大 with a stroke marking excess at the base). In oracle-bone and bronze script, 太 and 泰 were written interchangeably; they later differentiated, with 泰 taking on the sense of "peaceful, smooth" and 太 retaining the superlative and cosmogonic uses.

In Classical Chinese, 太 functions primarily as a prefix of the highest degree, not as a standalone adjective. The cosmogonic sequence 太易 → [[太初]] → [[太始]] → [[太素]] → [[太極]], derived from texts like the Liezi and the Zhuangzi, uses 太 to name stages of primordial existence beyond form, substance, or differentiation — 太 here means something closer to "ultimate" or "primordial" than simply "great." This cosmogonic cluster gives 太 a philosophical weight absent from its near-synonym 大.

In modern Mandarin, 太 is most familiar as the degree adverb "too; excessively" (太好了 tài hǎo le "that's too good; great"), a semantic narrowing from the classical superlative prefix into an adverb of excess. Japanese uses 太 (TAI/TA) in compounds as a prefix of greatness or antiquity — 太古 (taiko) "remote antiquity," 太平 (taihei) "great peace" — but the native kun reading ふと/ふとい (futoi) drifts into a different semantic field entirely, meaning "fat; thick." Korean 태 (tae) in Sino-Korean compounds tracks the classical Chinese superlative meaning closely.
