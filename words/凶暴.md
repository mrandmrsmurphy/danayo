## Notes
- [凶 (char)](../characters/凶%20(char).md) "unlucky; sinister" + [暴 (char)](../characters/暴%20(char).md) "violent; brutal; fierce; tyrannical"

Ferocious and brutal; combining sinister intent with overwhelming physical force. Where [[凶悪]] emphasizes the moral dimension of evil — its wickedness, its offensiveness to ethical order — 凶暴 emphasizes the **behavioral and physical dimension**: not merely evil in character but violently, dangerously savage in action.

凶暴 is applied across a wide range of subjects:
- **Animals**: 凶暴な動物 (a ferocious animal) — a predator that attacks without provocation, a dangerous wild creature
- **Criminals**: 凶暴な犯人 (a violent criminal) — someone who uses disproportionate, reckless physical force
- **Rulers and regimes**: 凶暴な支配 (brutal rule) — tyrannical governance characterized by violent suppression

The compound intensifies both components: 凶 (sinister, bringing calamity) amplifies 暴 (violent, explosive) and vice versa, producing a word that sits near the upper limit of descriptions of dangerous physical menace.

In Japanese, 凶暴 (kyōbō) functions as a **な形容詞** (na-adjective): 凶暴さ (kyōbōsa, "ferocity; brutality") is its nominalized form. Related adjectives occupy a severity spectrum: 危険 (dangerous) < 乱暴 (rough, rowdy) < **凶暴** (ferocious, brutal) < 残忍 (cruel, sadistic). 凶暴 specifically implies the combination of dangerous intent and physical capability — something that will hurt you and wants to. Wildlife documentaries use it for apex predators and aggressive species; crime reports use it to flag individuals considered physically dangerous during arrest.

The related compound [[暴徒]] ("mob; rioters") shares the 暴 character and describes the collective expression of the same energy — organized group violence.
