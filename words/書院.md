## Notes
- [書](../characters/書.md) "book" + [院](../characters/院.md) "institution, courtyard" — literally "book institution," the traditional East Asian private academy of classical and Confucian learning, distinct from state-run institutions like [[太学]].

書院 originated in Tang- and Song-dynasty China as private libraries and lecture halls, often founded by individual scholars or endowed by local gentry rather than the imperial state, and became centers for Neo-Confucian philosophical instruction and debate — Zhu Xi himself taught at several. The institution spread to Korea, where 書院 (seowon) became central to Joseon-period local education and ancestor veneration of revered Confucian scholars from the 16th century onward; nine Korean seowon were inscribed together as a UNESCO World Heritage site in 2019 for their role in spreading Neo-Confucianism and their distinctive integration of lecture halls, shrines, and scenic natural settings. In Japan, 書院 (shoin) took a more architectural turn, naming both a style of study room with built-in shelving and a writing alcove (床の間) that became a standard feature of upper-class residential design (書院造), and by extension the academies housed in such spaces.

The Vietnamese reading thư viện preserves the same two Sino-Vietnamese morphemes (書 thư, 院 viện) but has narrowed in modern usage to simply mean "library," reflecting how, across the Sinosphere, 書院-type institutions blurred the line between a place of study, a place of lecture, and a place where books were kept.
