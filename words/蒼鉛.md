## Notes
### Form
蒼鉛 is a compound of [蒼 (char)](characters/蒼%20(char).md) “dark bluish-green; ashen; gray” + [鉛 (char)](characters/鉛%20(char).md) “lead.” Unlike the 素/金 suffix system, this is a traditional binome, comparable to older mineralogical naming.

### Historical basis
This is not a modern invention. 蒼鉛 (and variants such as 蒼鉛礦) appears in classical Chinese and Japanese scientific vocabulary as a designation for bismuth. It reflects early confusion or association with lead ores: bismuth was long treated as a kind of “impure” or variant lead in European alchemy and mineralogy as well.

The European name _bismuth_ likely derives from German _Wismut_, possibly from _weiße Masse_ (“white mass”) or a mining term; it is not semantically transparent in the same way as many Greek-derived names. East Asian traditions instead chose a **descriptive analogy**.

### Motivation
- 鉛 anchors the substance in the familiar category of heavy, lead-like metals.
- 蒼 modifies it by color: a dull bluish, grayish, or ashen tone, which matches the appearance of bismuth in many forms (especially oxidized surfaces and crystalline tarnish).

So the compound effectively means “bluish/ashen lead,” distinguishing it from true lead while acknowledging similarity.

### Within the Dan'a'yo system
1. It satisfies the first rule: use established CJKV names when available.
2. It avoids forcing an artificial etymology onto a name that is historically opaque in Indo-European languages.
3. It provides a unique leading character 蒼 for symbol use, distinct from 鉛.

### Comparative CJKV forms

- Modern Chinese: 铋 (phonetic simplification; older usage includes 蒼鉛)
- Japanese: ビスマス (phonetic), but 蒼鉛 appears in historical/technical contexts
- Korean: 비스무트 (phonetic)
- Vietnamese: bitmut (phonetic)

Thus 蒼鉛 represents an older, meaning-based layer largely replaced by phonetic loans in modern usage.

### Clarity and ambiguity
- 蒼 has a broad color range (blue, green, gray), but in metallurgical context it reads as “dull bluish-gray,” which is appropriate.
- The “lead” component could mislead a beginner into thinking it is a form of lead, but this reflects genuine historical classification rather than a flaw in the term.

### Conclusion
蒼鉛 is a historically attested, semantically grounded name that fits our preference for inherited CJKV terminology. It encodes both appearance and historical classification.
