## Notes
- [苦 (char)](../characters/苦%20(char).md) "bitter, suffering" + [肉 (char)](../characters/肉%20(char).md) "flesh, meat" — flesh made to suffer; desperate self-sacrifice as a stratagem

苦肉 is most familiar as the basis of the 苦肉計 (苦肉之計), the thirty-fourth of the Thirty-Six Stratagems (三十六計): to harm oneself in order to gain the enemy's trust. The logic is that no one would willingly injure themselves unless the injury were real — and a credibly injured party can be believed when they claim to have defected.

The canonical instance is Huang Gai (黄蓋) of the state of Wu at the Battle of Red Cliffs (赤壁之戦, 208 AD). He arranged to be publicly flogged by his own commander Zhou Yu (周瑜) as punishment for insubordination, then sent a message to Cao Cao (曹操) claiming to defect with a fleet. Cao Cao, convinced by the apparent humiliation, accepted the offer — and Huang Gai's fire ships burned the Wei fleet, turning the battle. The story, dramatized in the San Guo Yan Yi (三国演義), made 苦肉計 a proverbial expression for tactical self-harm.

In modern usage, 苦肉 (and especially 苦肉之策 or 苦肉の策 in Japanese) has generalized to mean any painful or self-damaging measure taken because no better option exists — a last resort that costs the person using it.
