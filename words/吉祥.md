## Notes
- [吉 (char)](../characters/吉%20(char).md) "lucky, good omen" + [祥](../characters/祥.md) "auspicious sign, felicitous"

One of the most pervasive auspicious phrases in East Asian culture, 吉祥 pairs two independent words for good fortune — 吉 (OC *kiʔ, originally a ritual vessel on an altar, hence "propitious") and 祥 (OC *sŋaŋ, "auspicious sign, presage of good") — into an intensified formula. The compound appears on ceramics, textiles, calligraphy scrolls, city names, and as a given name across the CJKV sphere.

In Japanese, the compound has two readings reflecting different on'yomi preservation paths: きっしょう (kisshō, more formal, with the entering-tone -t of 吉 producing the geminate っ) and きちじょう (kichijō, with じょう for 祥 via the 上古 long vowel). The Buddhist goddess 吉祥天 (Kichijōten, "Lady of Auspiciousness") — the Sinitic name for the Sanskrit Lakṣmī — has kept the kichijō reading prominent in religious contexts.

In Vietnamese, cát tường (吉祥) is attested in literary and Buddhist usage. The term 吉祥如意 ("may auspiciousness fulfill your wishes") is a set phrase found on New Year's greetings across the Chinese-speaking world.
