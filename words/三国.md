## Notes
- [三 (char)](../characters/三%20(char).md) "three" + [国](../characters/国.md) "nation" — literally "three countries," a term used across the Sinosphere for two distinct historical periods as well as a literal generic sense.

1. The Three Kingdoms period of China (220–280 AD): the division of the collapsed Han empire's territory among Wei (魏), Shu Han ([[蜀国|蜀漢]]), and Wu (呉/吴), a period of constant warfare among the three that nonetheless became one of the most romanticized eras in Chinese historiography, popularized far beyond the period itself by the Ming-dynasty novel Romance of the Three Kingdoms (三国演義).
2. The Three Kingdoms period of Korea (1st century BC – 7th century AD): the centuries-long rivalry among [[高句麗]] (Goguryeo) in the north, 百済 (Baekje) in the southwest, and [[新羅]] (Silla) in the southeast for control of the Korean peninsula, ending when Silla, allied with Tang China, conquered Baekje (660) and Goguryeo (668) and unified the peninsula as Unified Silla (統一新羅, [[統一]] + [[新羅]]). This is the sense used in [[歴史綱要]].
3. The generic literal sense, "three countries." In Japanese, the term was historically also used elliptically for 日本・中国・天竺 (Japan, China, and India) — the three civilizational centers recognized in pre-modern Japanese geography — and by extension came to mean "the whole world."

Korean 삼국 and Vietnamese Tam Quốc both read the characters with their own Sino-Korean/Sino-Vietnamese values rather than transliterating a foreign name, unlike the native-Japanese place names seen in words like [[奈良]] — 三国 is a learned Sino-Sphere compound, not a native toponym, so all five languages converge on cognate Sino-readings of the same two morphemes.
