## Notes
- [無 (char)](../characters/無%20(char).md) "without; not" + [恥](../characters/恥.md) "shame; sense of shame"

無恥 negates 恥, the Confucian moral faculty of shame. Mencius listed 羞惡之心 — the heart of shame and aversion — as one of the four beginnings (四端) of virtue, the innate seed from which righteousness (義) grows. To be 無恥 is therefore not merely to have done something shameful, but to be morally deficient at a foundational level: lacking the capacity for shame that makes ethical life possible. The compound carries this philosophical weight, making it a stronger charge than "impudent" or "brazen" in isolation.

Its most common expansion is the four-character set phrase 厚顔無恥 (thick-faced and without shame), which adds 厚顔 ("thick-faced; brazen") to reinforce the idea. The phrase is attested across all CJKV literatures and functions as a formulaic condemnation.

Across the Sinosphere the compound is near-universal but with uneven everyday use. In Mandarin, 无耻 (wúchǐ) remains common in both everyday speech and political invective. In Japanese, 無恥 (muchi) is largely literary and most alive inside 厚顔無恥 (kōgan muchi); the stand-alone form is slightly archaic. In Korean, the Sino-Korean reading 무치 (muchi) exists but is infrequent in everyday speech — modern Korean prefers the native 뻔뻔하다 ("to be brazen"), which the korean field records here as 뻔뻔한; this native substitution accounts for kwin=false. In Vietnamese, vô sỉ (無恥) remains an active Sino-Vietnamese term in formal and literary registers.
