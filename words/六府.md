## Notes
- [六 (char)](../characters/六%20(char).md) "six" + [府](../characters/府.md) "hollow organ" (Dan'a'yo stand-in for 腑)

In Dan'a'yo, 府 serves as the graphemic and phonetic stand-in for 腑 (viscera, hollow organ), which does not have its own character entry. 府 carries 腑 as an alias; the pronunciation is identical in Dan'a'yo.

六腑 (six hollow organs) is a foundational concept in traditional Chinese medicine, paired with the 五臟 (five solid organs). The six *fu* are the yang, hollow organs responsible for receiving, transforming, and transmitting: the stomach (胃), small intestine (小腸), large intestine (大腸), gallbladder (膽), urinary bladder (膀胱), and the triple burner (三焦), a functional organ unique to TCM with no direct anatomical equivalent. Together, 五臟六腑 became a fixed phrase meaning "all the internal organs" and by extension "every part of one's being."

The term appears across all five CJKV literary traditions in medical, philosophical, and literary writing. In Japanese (ろっぷ, roppu), it remains in use in discussions of TCM and is embedded in idiomatic expressions such as 五臓六腑に染み渡る ("to permeate one's entire being"). In Vietnamese (lục phủ), it appears in Hán-Việt medical terminology.
