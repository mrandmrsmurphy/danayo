## Notes
- [誰 (char)](../characters/誰%20(char).md) "who; which person; interrogative person pronoun"

誰 is the interrogative pronoun for people, occupying the 何-column slot of the 人 row in Dan'a'yo's correlative table. It is the one cell in the correlative grid that uses a monomorphemic word rather than a 何+X compound — the grammar notes that 何人 (*hanin*) exists but is considered rude, while 誰 is the neutral form. A parallel register distinction holds: 誰 (neutral) / 何人 (rude) / 何名 (very polite) — the only three-way politeness gradient in the correlative table, inspired by the Japanese (だれ / どなた) and Korean (누구 / 어느 분) pronoun systems.

Across CJKV, 誰 has a remarkably consistent phonological history: Mandarin shéi (sometimes shúi in careful speech), Cantonese seoi4, Sino-Vietnamese thuỳ, Sino-Japanese すい (sui). All reflect the Middle Chinese form reconstructed as *ʑwi or similar (齊韻 with 禪母 initial). The native Japanese word 誰 (dare) shares the character but is phonologically unrelated — a coincidence of graphic assignment. Korean uses the native form 누구 (nugu, "who"), with the Sino-Korean reading 수 (su) surviving only in archaic contexts. Dan'a'yo reads 誰 as *syei*/셰/ㄙ⼶ㄧ, preserving the Middle Chinese phonology.

The choice of 誰 over 何人 for the interrogative person slot reflects pragmatic economy: "who" is one of the most frequent interrogative words in any language, and giving it a single-character form prioritises fluency. This is consistent with how natural languages handle high-frequency pronouns — they tend to be short and phonologically simple. The longer forms 何人 and 何名 exist for their register functions (rude and polite respectively) but do not displace 誰 as the unmarked default for "who?"
