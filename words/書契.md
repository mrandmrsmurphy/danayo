## Notes
- [書](../characters/書.md) "writing; documents" + [契](../characters/契.md) "contract; deed; carved tally; to engrave" — together: writings and notched tallies, the full range of written record-keeping

**書契** (*shūqì*) denotes the system of writing — specifically the invention of written records as a civilisational threshold, the moment when pre-literate record-keeping gave way to the inscribed sign. The compound pairs 書 (writing, documents) with 契 (engraved tally, notched record, contract), spanning from incised marks on bone and wood to formal written instruments. Together they name writing in its totality: marks made to survive the moment of inscription.

The locus classicus is **易·繫辭下** (*Yì, Xì Cí Xià*, "Appended Judgements, Part II" of the *Book of Changes*), §2:

> 上古結繩而治，後世聖人易之以書契，百官以治，萬民以察。

*"In high antiquity they governed by knotted cords; later sages replaced this with 書契; the hundred officials were thereby ordered, the ten thousand people thereby observed."*

The contrast — **結繩** (knotted cords, 結繩之政) versus **書契** — is one of the most cited formulations in Classical Chinese historiography for the transition from pre-literate to literate governance. Knotted cords served as mnemonic devices and accounting tallies; 書契 superseded them with inscribed signs capable of carrying propositional meaning. A similar progression appears in the **淮南子** and the preface to the **說文解字** (Shuowen Jiezi), where the invention of writing (書契) is attributed to 倉頡 (*Cāng Jié*), the legendary scribe of the Yellow Emperor, said to have derived the first characters from the tracks of birds and beasts. The Shuowen preface states: 倉頡之初作書，蓋依類象形 — "when Cangjie first created writing, he took the forms of things as his models."

The 契 component is etymologically significant: it originally meant a notched tally — a piece of wood or bone cut with marks to record quantities or agreements, split between two parties as a primitive contract. Oracle bone script (甲骨文) and bronze inscriptions (金文), the earliest attested Chinese writing, share this material substrate of inscription into hard surfaces. 書契 thus captures both the act (engraving) and the product (document), both the archaic tally and the developed literary tradition.

In East Asian diplomatic history, **書契** acquired a specific technical meaning in the Edo period (1603–1868 AD): it was the standard term for official written communications exchanged between the Tsushima domain and the Joseon Korean court, mediating Japan-Korea relations across the sea. The format, seal, and wording of 書契 were the subject of elaborate diplomatic negotiation, and breakdowns in the accepted form of 書契 could trigger major incidents. Korean 서계 (書契) carries this diplomatic sense directly.

Vietnamese *thư khế* is the Sino-Vietnamese compound; in everyday usage *văn tự* or *chữ viết* cover the sense of "writing system," while *khế* (from 契) survives in *khế ước* (contract, agreement).
