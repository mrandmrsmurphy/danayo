## Notes
- [及 (char)](../characters/及%20(char).md) "reach out to" + [第 (char)](../characters/第%20(char).md) "-th; ordinal"

To pass — originally and most specifically to succeed in the imperial civil service examination system (科挙, 과거, kēju / gwageo). The word literally means "to reach the ranking" — to achieve a place on the list of successful candidates. Its antonym is **落第** (luòdì / rakudai, "to fall from the ranking; to fail").

The examination system ran from roughly 605 CE (Sui dynasty) to its abolition in 1905 (late Qing), a span of 1,300 years across China and its cultural sphere. The highest degree, **進士** (jìnshì), was awarded to those who passed the final metropolitan examination (殿試) in the imperial capital, held in the presence of the emperor. Passing this examination — 進士及第 — was the supreme academic achievement of traditional East Asia, transforming the candidate's social standing overnight and obligating the state to assign him a government post.

Stories of imperial examination culture permeate classical Chinese literature: candidates who attempted the exams for decades before succeeding; families that bankrupted themselves to support a promising son through years of study; the wild celebrations that greeted news of 及第; and the devastating shame of repeated 落第. The poet Meng Jiao (孟郊, 751–814) wrote one of the most celebrated poems in Chinese about his 及第 at age 46 after multiple failures: 春風得意馬蹄疾，一日看盡長安花 ("Riding high on spring wind, the horse's hooves fly — in a single day I survey all of Chang'an's flowers").

In Korean, 급제 (急第) referred to passing the gwageo — the Korean adaptation of the Chinese system, administered from 958 to 1894.

In modern Japanese, **きゅうだい** (kyūdai) has shed its imperial exam context entirely and now simply means to pass an academic grade or level — the opposite of 落第 (rakudai, failing a year in school). The historical weight is gone; the structural logic of "reaching the rank" remains.
