## Notes
**Form.** 穀金 is [[穀]] “grain; kernel” + [金 (char)](characters/金%20(char).md) “metal,” following your rule for metals.

**Etymological chain.**
- _cerium_ is named after the asteroid **Ceres**
- Ceres is the Roman goddess of **agriculture and grain**
- core semantic: “grain; harvest; cereal”

- Ceres → agriculture/grain
- “grain” → 穀
- - 金 → element name

**Motivation.**  
We extract the **mythological domain** (grain, fertility) and map it to a concrete, high-frequency Sinitic graph.

穀 is the correct classical term for grain/cereals, more precise than generic 食 or 米, and appropriately elevated in register.

**Type of formation.**  
A **mythological semantic reduction**:

- deity → domain (agriculture)
- domain → core object (grain)
- object → graph (穀)

This parallels the treatment of palladium (護金), where the myth is reduced to its functional meaning.

**Fit within the system.**

1. Meaning-based, avoiding phonetic borrowing.
2. Uses 金 appropriately for a metal.
3. Provides a unique and semantically rich symbol (穀).
4. Maintains consistency with other indirect etymologies (護金, 狼金).

**Comparative CJKV forms.**

- Modern Chinese: 铈 (phonetic)
- Japanese: セリウム (phonetic)
- Korean: 세륨 (phonetic)
- Vietnamese: xêri (phonetic)

Modern forms are phonetic; 穀金 restores a semantic link to the original myth.

**Clarity and issues.**

- The connection “cerium → Ceres → grain → 穀” is not immediately obvious without explanation.
- However, once established, it is systematic and memorable.
- 穀 is classical and slightly formal, but appropriate for your register.
