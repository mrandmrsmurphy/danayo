## Notes
- [芻](../characters/芻.md) "grass; fodder" + [狗](../characters/狗.md) "dog" — a dog woven from straw

芻狗 (*chúgǒu*, "straw dog") were ritual objects woven from straw or grass, made in the shape of dogs and used as sacrificial substitutes in ancient Chinese ceremonies. Like other straw or paper effigies used across East Asian ritual traditions, a 芻狗 stood in for a real sacrificial animal — it was treated with great ceremony and reverence during the rite itself, then discarded without further regard once the ceremony concluded.

The term is best known from its appearance in Chapter 5 of the _Daodejing_: 天地不仁，以萬物為芻狗 ("Heaven and Earth are not benevolent; they treat the ten thousand things as straw dogs") — see [[天地不仁]]. The image captures the Daoist view of the cosmos as impartial and non-anthropocentric: just as the straw dog is honored during the ritual and then cast aside without sentiment, Heaven and Earth bring the myriad things into being and let them pass away again without partiality or attachment, following the natural course (自然) rather than human notions of kindness or cruelty.

By extension, 芻狗 has become a literary figure of speech for anything — or anyone — that is valued and made much of while useful, then carelessly discarded once its purpose is served. This figurative sense survives in Mandarin, Cantonese, and Sino-Korean/Sino-Vietnamese literary registers, though the term is rare outside of classical and philosophical contexts; everyday speech in all five languages uses more transparent expressions for "a used-and-discarded thing" or "a scapegoat."
