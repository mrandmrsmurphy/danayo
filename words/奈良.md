## Notes
- [奈](../characters/奈.md) "apple tree" + [良](../characters/良.md) "good" — an ateji spelling of the native Japanese place name Nara; the characters were chosen for their sound, not their sense. The etymology of the name itself is uncertain, but one common theory ties it to an old root *nar-* found in words meaning "flat," fitting the broad, level basin the city occupies in the Yamato region.

Nara (710–794) was Japan's first fixed, permanent capital, established as Heijō-kyō (平城京) on a grid plan explicitly modeled on the Tang dynasty capital Chang'an. Before this, the seat of government had moved with each new sovereign; Heijō-kyō's permanence reflects the maturing Ritsuryō (律令) state, which had adopted Tang-style law codes and centralized bureaucracy over the preceding Asuka period. The period saw the compilation of Japan's foundational texts — the Kojiki (古事記, 712), the Nihon Shoki (日本書紀, 720), and the poetry anthology Man'yōshū (万葉集) — and a flowering of Buddhist scholasticism organized into the so-called Six Schools of Nara Buddhism (南都六宗). The Great Buddha of Tōdai-ji (東大寺大仏), cast in 752 under Emperor Shōmu as a state act of Buddhist devotion, remains the period's most visible monument.

The same Buddhist establishment that had given Nara its religious prestige also became politically overbearing — the monk Dōkyō's (道鏡) influence over Empress Shōtoku is the period's emblematic case — and concern over clerical power in the capital was a major factor in Emperor Kanmu's decision to relocate the court, first briefly to Nagaoka and then in 794 to Heian-kyō (平安京), opening the Heian period. The city of Nara survived this loss of political centrality as a religious center, and today gives its name to Nara Prefecture.

The Japanese reading なら is native rather than Sino-Japanese on'yomi, the same pattern seen in [[鎌倉]] — a Yamato-era place name predating the Sino-Japanese reading layer, fitted afterward with kanji chosen for sound. Korean 나라 and Vietnamese Nại Lương diverge from each other in kind: Korean directly transliterates the Japanese name (as with 가마쿠라 for Kamakura), while Vietnamese instead reads the characters with their own Sino-Vietnamese values, the convention typically used for premodern Japanese era and place names in Vietnamese historical writing.
