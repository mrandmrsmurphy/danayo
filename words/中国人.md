## Notes
- [中 (char)](../characters/中%20(char).md) "middle; China" + [国](../characters/国.md) "nation; country" + [人 (char)](../characters/人%20(char).md) "person"

中国人 is "a Chinese person; a person from China" — 中国 (the Middle Kingdom; China) + 人 (person). The compound follows the productive CJKV pattern for nationality words: country name + 人 = a person of that nation. 中国 itself means "Middle Kingdom" (中=middle + 国=nation), the traditional Chinese self-designation rooted in the concept of China as the central state surrounded by peripheral kingdoms. The compound is primarily a legal-citizenship term: 中国人 = a national of the People's Republic of China or a person of Chinese ethnicity.

Mandarin 中国人 (zhōngguórén; traditional 中國人) is the standard term for a Chinese person. Cantonese 中國人 (zung1 gwok3 jan4). Japanese 中国人 (ちゅうごくじん, chūgokujin). Korean 중국인 (中國人, junggugIn). Vietnamese Người Trung Quốc (người = person + Trung Quốc = China). The ethnic Chinese population living outside China is called 華人 (huárén) or 海外華人 (overseas Chinese) — a distinct term from 中国人, which implies PRC citizenship or mainland-Chinese identity rather than ethnic Chinese ancestry.

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 중곡닌 diverges from Korean 중국인 in both the second and third syllables. 国 reads 곡 (gog/ㄍㄛㄎ) in Dan'a'yo but 국 (guk) in Sino-Korean; 人 reads 닌 (nin/ㄋㄧㄋ) in Dan'a'yo but 인 (in) in Sino-Korean. Both are regular sound correspondences: the 国 divergence (곡/국) reflects different vowel development from MC *-wək; the 人 divergence (닌/인) reflects Dan'a'yo preserving the MC ȵ (日 initial) as ㄋ (n-), while Sino-Korean merges the ȵ with the following vowel-initial to give vowel-initial 인.
