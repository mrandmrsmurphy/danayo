## Notes
- [刀](../characters/刀.md) "blade, knife" + [鞘](../characters/鞘.md) "scabbard, sheath"

A transparent compound: 刀 (the iconic knife/sword character) + 鞘 (形声: 革 "leather" + 肖 "resembling/hollow" — a leather sleeve shaped to fit). The scabbard is thus etymologically a skin (革) that resembles the blade (肖), an apt image for a fitted protective covering.

In Japanese sword tradition, the 鞘 (さや, saya) holds a significance equal to the blade itself. Lacquered and decorated 鞘 are craft objects in their own right, and the 鞘師 (sayashi, scabbard maker) is a distinct artisan role separate from the swordsmith. The ritual of 鞘入れ (saya-ire, "sheathing") and the sound a well-fitted 鞘 makes when the blade is drawn or returned are matters of connoisseurship. Japanese reads 刀鞘 as とうしょう using both Sino-Japanese on'yomi, though in practice さや alone names the scabbard in ordinary speech.

In Korean, 도초 (刀鞘, doCho) is the hanja compound reading; the everyday Korean word for a sword scabbard is 칼집 (kaljip, literally "knife-container"), showing how the native language produced a transparent compound parallel to the Sinitic one.
