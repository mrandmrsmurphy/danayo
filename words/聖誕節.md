## Notes
- [聖](../characters/聖.md) "holy; sacred" + [誕](../characters/誕.md) "to be born; birth" + [節 (char)](../characters/節%20(char).md) "seasonal festival; holiday; joint; node"

聖誕節 — "holy birth festival" — is the sinographic compound coined to name Christmas in East Asian Christian communities. The three characters are transparent: 聖 (holy, the quality of sacred excellence — graphemically an ear over a mouth over a person, suggesting one who hears and speaks divine truth) + 誕 (to be born, birth — note 誕 also carries the sense of "fantastic, outlandish" as in 誕生 but here the birth sense dominates) + 節 (a seasonal festival or holiday — from 竹 bamboo node, generalised to the "nodes" of the calendar year). The compound renders "the feast of the holy birth" directly.

The word was created by Christian missionaries in China during the nineteenth century as part of the translation of Christian vocabulary into literary Chinese — a project that also produced 聖父/聖子/聖靈 (Father/Son/Holy Spirit), 聖経 (Bible), and 聖誕 (Christmas, the first two characters alone). 聖誕 spread through Chinese-speaking Christian communities in China, Hong Kong, and Taiwan, where 聖誕節 remains the standard term. In Korea, 성탄절 (Sino-Korean reading) exists alongside the borrowing 크리스마스 (from English "Christmas"); in Japan, the sinographic compound せいたんせつ (seitansetsu) is known but クリスマス is overwhelmingly dominant in everyday usage. Vietnamese Giáng Sinh (降生, "descended birth") is a different sinographic rendering of the same concept, using 降 (to descend) rather than 聖 (holy).

The kwin=false marking reflects the divergence between Dan'a'yo 싱단젇 (concatenated from 聖 sing + 誕 dan + 節 jed) and Korean 성탄절 (seongtanjeol, the Sino-Korean reading), where all three characters map to different phonological forms across the two systems.
