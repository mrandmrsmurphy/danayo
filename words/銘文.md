## Notes
- [銘](../characters/銘.md) "inscription, engrave" + [文](../characters/文.md) "writing, text"

This word is the stand-in for [銘](../characters/銘.md), which is too bounded to appear alone in Dan'a'yo discourse; 銘文 carries the same sense of engraved or inscribed text.

銘文 denotes text that has been engraved, cast, or inscribed into a durable material — bronze vessels, stone steles, tomb walls, coins, seals, and monuments. The character 銘 (metal radical 金 + phonetic 名) depicts writing into metal: the original sense was an inscription cast into a bronze vessel, typically recording a royal command, a gift, or a commemorative event. Bronze inscriptions from the Shang and Zhou dynasties (甲骨文 and 金文) are among the earliest surviving forms of Chinese writing and the principal source for reconstructing Old Chinese phonology and grammar.

In modern usage across the CJKV languages, 銘文 is the standard term in archaeology, epigraphy, and numismatics for any inscribed text. In Japanese (めいぶん, meibun), the word appears widely in museum labeling and archaeological writing. In Korean (명문), the Sino-Korean reading is a homophone of 名文 (명문, fine or celebrated writing) and 名門 (명문, a prestigious school, family, or institution) — three entirely distinct words distinguished only by context and hanja.
