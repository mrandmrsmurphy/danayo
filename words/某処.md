## Notes
- [某 (char)](../characters/某%20(char).md) "some; a certain; indefinite" + [処 (char)](../characters/処%20(char).md) "place; location"

某処 is the indefinite place correlative — "somewhere; at some unspecified location." Like all 某-column entries, it refers to a place the speaker either does not know or chooses not to specify: the location is presumed to exist, but its identity is withheld. This contrasts with the interrogative 何処 (asking for the location's identity) and the distal 彼処 (pointing to a known but distant place). 某処 sits between them: the place exists and may even be known, but the speaker declines to name it.

Mandarin 某處 (mǒu chù) is the direct compositional equivalent and is well attested in formal and journalistic Chinese: 藏在某處 ("hidden somewhere"); 發生於某處 ("occurring at a certain location"); 駐紮在某處的軍隊 ("troops stationed at some undisclosed location"). Japanese どこか (dokoka, "somewhere" — 何処+か indefinite suffix) is the natural spoken equivalent; Vietnamese ở chỗ nào (at some place or other) is the analytic equivalent. Korean 어떤 곳 (eotteon got, "a certain place") or 어딘가 (eodin-ga, "somewhere — indefinite") cover the same ground.

某処 fills a real lexical gap visible in the source languages: none has a single dedicated indefinite place pronoun in the same way Dan'a'yo's correlative system provides one. Japanese どこか is the closest but is formed by suffix (不 -ka + どこ), not a separate compound; Chinese 某處 is formal; Korean requires circumlocution. In Dan'a'yo, 某処 is as compact and compositionally transparent as 此処, 其処, or 何処 — the indefiniteness is simply a column property of 某, applied uniformly across all nine semantic rows.
