## Notes
- [束 (char)](../characters/束%20(char).md) "bundle of"

束 is the Dan'a'yo classifier for bundled or bound objects — 量詞, counting flowers, vegetables, straw, firewood, papers, and any collection of discrete objects tied or gathered together: 一束花 (one bunch of flowers), 三束稻草 (three sheaves of straw), 兩束蔬菜 (two bundles of vegetables), 一束光 (one beam of light). The character is a 象形 pictograph of a bundle of sticks with a cord tied through the middle — the visual structure directly encodes what it classifies. From the concrete sense of "a bound bundle," 束 extended to the verb "to bind; to constrain" (拘束, 結束, 束縛) and to abstract restraint, but Dan'a'yo 束 as a classifier preserves the original material sense. 束 also serves as the Dan'a'yo stand-in for the character [[束]].

The CJKV classifier landscape for bundles shows moderate convergence. Mandarin 束 (shù) classifies flowers, plants, and ray-like things: 一束花 (one bunch of flowers), 一束光 (one beam of light), 一束头发 (one lock of hair); 捆 (kǔn) covers tightly bound bundles of firewood and rope. Cantonese 束 (cuk1): 一束花 (one bunch of flowers); 紮 (zaak3) is the colloquial classifier for tightly bound bundles: 一紮蔥 (one bundle of spring onions). Japanese does not use 束 (そく/SOKU) productively as a classifier — its main appearances are in verb compounds (拘束 kōsoku, restrain; 結束 kessoku, unite). The native Japanese reading 束 (たば/taba) functions as a classifier: 一束 (hitotaba, one bundle), using the native pronunciation, not the on-reading. Korean 속 (束) appears in Sino-Korean compounds but not as an active classifier; 묶음 (mukkeum, "a tied-together set") is the native Korean equivalent. Vietnamese bó: một bó hoa (one bunch of flowers), một bó củi (one bundle of firewood); Sino-Vietnamese thúc (束) appears in kết thúc (結束, to conclude; to finish) and câu thúc (拘束, to restrain).

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 쇽 does not match Korean 속. 束 reads 쇽 (syog/ㄙ⼄ㄎ) in Dan'a'yo — MC 書母 (ɕ) + 燭韻 (ɨok) gives a palatal fricative onset with the compound -yok coda. Dan'a'yo preserves the MC -ɨ- medial, producing 쇽 (syog, ending in a stop coda -k); Sino-Korean 속 (sok) maps the same final to plain -ok, dropping the -ɨ- medial through a regular historical reduction. The MC 燭韻 (ɨok) produced ⼄ㄎ (-yok/쇽) in Dan'a'yo and plain -ok (속) in Korean — the -ɨ- medial is consistently preserved in Dan'a'yo and consistently dropped in Korean from this MC final.
