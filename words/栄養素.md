## Notes
- [栄](../characters/栄.md) "flourish; glory" + [養](../characters/養.md) "nourish; foster" + [素](../characters/素.md) "element; basic substance" — sub-compound [[栄養]]

**栄養素** (*róngyǎngsù*) denotes a nutrient: any substance that an organism requires from its environment to sustain life, support growth, and maintain health. The compound extends [[栄養]] (nourishment) with 素 (*sù*, "element; basic substance"), yielding "the elemental substances of nourishment" — a precise and transparent coinage. The six major classes recognised in nutrition science are carbohydrates, proteins, fats, vitamins, minerals, and water; in some frameworks dietary fibre is added as a seventh.

The suffix 素 follows a well-established East Asian pattern for naming fundamental substances: compare [[養素]] (oxygen — literally "nourishing element"), 炭素 (carbon), 窒素 (nitrogen), and 水素 (hydrogen) in the chemical nomenclature that spread across the Sinographic world in the 19th century via Japanese scientific translation. In each case 素 marks an irreducible elemental thing, giving 栄養素 the sense of "the elemental units that constitute nourishment."

In modern usage, the term is standard in Japanese (栄養素, *eiyōso*), Korean (영양소, *yeongyangsо*), and Chinese nutritional science (营养素, *yíngyǎngsù* — note that standard Chinese uses 营养 rather than 栄養; see [[栄養]] for the character distinction). Vietnamese dinh dưỡng tố is the parallel Sino-Vietnamese construction, though vernacular usage often prefers *chất dinh dưỡng*.
