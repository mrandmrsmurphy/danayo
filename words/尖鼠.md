## Notes
- [尖](../characters/尖.md) "sharp; pointed" + [鼠](../characters/鼠.md) "rat; mouse"

Shrews — small, insectivorous mammals of the order Eulipotyphla, family Soricidae. The name is apt: 尖 (sharp, pointed) + 鼠 (mouse/rat) = "pointed-nose mouse," describing the shrew's most distinctive visible feature, the long tapered snout used for probing soil and leaf litter for prey. The Japanese name とがりねずみ (togarinezumi, "sharpened mouse") is a direct parallel.

Despite superficial similarity to mice, **shrews are not rodents**. They belong to a completely separate mammalian order, more closely related to moles and hedgehogs. Their lineage is among the most ancient of placental mammals, diverging from other groups in the early Cenozoic era, long before rodents diversified.

Shrews have the **highest metabolic rate of any mammal**: their hearts beat up to 1,500 times per minute (compared to a resting human's 60–100), and their body temperature drops overnight if they cannot find enough food. Some species must eat every two to three hours or die; many consume more than their own body weight in food daily. A shrew that goes without food for several hours is at serious risk of starvation.

Several shrew species possess **venomous saliva**, making them among the very few venomous mammals. The North American short-tailed shrew (*Blarina brevicauda*) and the Eurasian water shrew (*Neomys fodiens*) can immobilize invertebrates and small vertebrates with a toxic bite, allowing them to cache paralyzed prey for later consumption.

In East Asian folk tradition, shrews appear as small, ferocious creatures — properties that parallel the English secondary meaning of "shrew" (a quarrelsome, ill-tempered person, famously in Shakespeare's *The Taming of the Shrew*). Chinese and Japanese usage of 尖鼠 / とがりねずみ remains primarily biological and lacks this figurative dimension.
