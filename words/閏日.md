## Notes
- [閏 (char)](../characters/閏%20(char).md) "intercalary" + [日 (char)](../characters/日%20(char).md) "day; sun"

The extra day inserted into the calendar in a [[閏年]] (leap year) to keep the civil calendar synchronized with the solar year. In the Gregorian calendar, this is **February 29**, occurring in years divisible by 4 — with the correction that century years are leap years only if also divisible by 400 (making 2000 a leap year but not 1700, 1800, or 1900).

The need arises from arithmetic: the solar year is approximately **365.2422 days**, not 365. The residual 0.2422 accumulates over years; without correction, the calendar would drift through the seasons over centuries. The Julian calendar (46 BCE) addressed this simply by inserting a day every four years (365.25 average). The Gregorian reform of 1582 fine-tuned this to 365.2425 by dropping three leap days per 400 years, reducing the drift to about 26 seconds per year — an error that will only require correction after roughly 3,300 years.

The Roman origin of the insertion is preserved in the name: the Julian calendar's leap day was not added at the end of February but inserted *before* February 24 (the sixth day before the Kalends of March, *ante diem sextum*), creating a doubled sixth day — *ante diem bis sextum*, "the twice-sixth" — which gives the Latin word **bissextile** for leap year, still used in French (*année bissextile*) and other Romance languages.

People born on February 29 — called **leaplings** or leap-year babies — have a calendar birthday only once every four years. Irish and Scottish tradition held that February 29 was the one day women could propose marriage to men without social censure; a man who refused was obliged to buy the woman a gift. The custom was attributed by some to St. Brigid's negotiation with St. Patrick, though its historical origin is murky.
