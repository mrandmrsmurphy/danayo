## Notes
- [叉 (char)](../characters/叉%20(char).md) "fork; prong" + [勺 (char)](../characters/勺%20(char).md) "spoon; ladle"

A spork — a hybrid eating utensil combining the shallow bowl of a spoon with the tines of a fork along its leading edge. The Chinese name is architecturally transparent: 叉 (fork) + 勺 (spoon) = fork-spoon. The English "spork" is similarly a portmanteau of *spoon* and *fork*.

A U.S. patent for a "combined spoon and fork" was filed as early as 1874, and similar designs appeared in European silverware catalogues in the same era. The spork's mass-market breakthrough came in the mid-20th century through **disposable single-use packaging** — airlines, fast-food chains (most iconically KFC), and institutional catering adopted the spork as a cost-saving measure, providing one utensil instead of two. It became so associated with these contexts that "spork" carries connotations of disposability and convenience in many languages.

In Chinese, 叉勺 competes with the synonym **叉匙** (chāchí, using 匙 *chí*, the more formal register word for spoon) and with phonetic borrowings of the English *spork*. Neither the Sino-Japanese compound nor a standard Korean equivalent is firmly established, reflecting the spork's status as a modern, primarily packaging-driven utensil rather than a traditional East Asian implement.

In English-speaking popular culture, the spork has acquired mild ironic status as an emblem of compromise and half-measures — a utensil that does two things adequately rather than one thing well. The standard observation: *a spoon is better at being a spoon than a spork is; a fork is better at being a fork than a spork is.* The counter-argument, advanced by spork advocates: for food that is simultaneously scoopable and pierceable (pasta, rice dishes, salads with chunky ingredients), the spork outperforms both.
