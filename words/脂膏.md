## Notes
- [脂](../characters/脂.md) "fat" + [膏](../characters/膏.md) "grease, oil" — stand-in for [[膏]], which cannot appear independently

脂膏 is a classical Chinese compound meaning fat, grease, or rich oil — a near-synonymous pairing of two words for animal and vegetable fats. 脂 (zhī) typically refers to solid animal fat or tallow, while 膏 (gāo) covers softer, ointment-like fats, medicinal salves, and fertile richness in a figurative sense (膏腴, "fat and fertile land"). Together the compound denotes richness,润澤, and the essence of nourishment.

In classical literature 脂膏 appeared both literally (the fat rendered from sacrificial animals, the ointment applied to wounds) and figuratively (the wealth squeezed from the common people — 民脂民膏, "the fat and grease of the people", a phrase for unjust taxation). The figurative sense became the more literary and politically charged one, appearing in dynastic remonstrance literature as a shorthand for the people's livelihood that rulers must not squander. In modern Chinese 脂肪 has largely displaced 脂膏 for the literal meaning of fat, leaving 脂膏 primarily in literary and historical contexts.
