## Notes
- [高 (char)](../characters/高%20(char).md) "high" + [等 (char)](../characters/等%20(char).md) "rank, grade" — of a higher rank; used adjectivally before an institution or discipline to mark it as advanced.

高等 keeps its plain "high-level, advanced" sense across Chinese, Japanese, and Korean, almost always as a modifier before an institution or field: 高等教育/こうとう教育/고등교육 "higher education," 高等法院 "High Court," 高等数学/고등수학 "advanced mathematics." Japanese also builds [[高校]] (こうこう) as a contraction of 高等学校 ("high school"), the everyday word for secondary school.

Vietnamese *cao đẳng* has narrowed sharply away from this general sense into a specific institutional category: modern Vietnamese education uses *cao đẳng* for junior-college-level, vocationally-oriented tertiary education — typically 2-3 years, practical rather than theoretical, and explicitly ranked below *đại học* ("university") rather than meaning "advanced" in general. A Vietnamese speaker hearing *cao đẳng* thinks of a specific tier of school, not the broader adjective its Chinese/Japanese/Korean cognates still carry.
