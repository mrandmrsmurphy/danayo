## Notes
- [新 (char)](../characters/新%20(char).md) "new" + [字 (char)](../characters/字%20(char).md) "character; glyph" + [体](../characters/体.md) "form; style; type"

新字体 is the term for the simplified character forms standardized in Japan from 1946 onward — "shinjitai" — as opposed to the traditional forms known as [[旧字体]] (kyūjitai, "old character forms"). Dan'a'yo adopts shinjitai as its canonical character set: where a shinjitai and traditional (kyūjitai) form both exist, Dan'a'yo uses the shinjitai — for example, 体 (shinjitai) over 體, 来 over 來, 国 over 國. The term 新字体 is Japanese in origin but applies directly within Dan'a'yo metalinguistics: when discussing character forms, script reform, or the orthographic choices of the vault, 新字体 is the standard term. The alias 新字體 is the traditional-form spelling of the same word (with 體 for 体).

新 (ㄙㄧㄋ/sin/신 — Mandarin xīn, Cantonese san1, Japanese SHIN/しん, Korean 신, Vietnamese tân): "new; novel; fresh" — MC 心母 (s) + 眞A開韻 (iɪn); 新 depicted chopping new wood (originally the phonetic source for 薪 "firewood"), borrowed for "new." 字 (ㄐㄧ/ji/지 — Mandarin zì, Cantonese zi6, Japanese JI/じ, Korean 자, Vietnamese tự): "character; glyph; written form" — MC 從母 (d͡z) + 之韻 (ɨ); a 形声 of semantic 宀 (roof) + phonetic 子 (child), originally depicting a child born in a house and later generalized to "written character." 体 (ㄊㄝㄧ/tei/테 — Mandarin tǐ, Cantonese tai2, Japanese TAI/たい, Korean 체, Vietnamese thể): "form; body; style; type" — MC 透母 (tʰ) + 齊開韻 (ei); in character-script terminology, 体 denotes a particular form or style: 楷体 (standard), 草体 (cursive), 新字体 (simplified), 旧字体 (traditional). Notably, 体 is itself a shinjitai — the simplified form of traditional 體.

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 신지테 does not match Korean 신자체. The first syllable converges: 新 (ㄙㄧㄋ/신) is kwin: true (Dan'a'yo 신 = Korean 신). The divergence is in 字 and 体. 字 gives Dan'a'yo 지 (ji, MC 從母+之韻 → ㄐㄧ) vs Korean 자 (ja, different vowel — -i in Dan'a'yo vs -a in Korean). 体 gives Dan'a'yo 테 (tei, MC 透母+齊開韻 → ㄊㄝㄧ, dental initial with -ei diphthong) vs Korean 체 (che, MC 透母 palatalized to ㅊ before the front vowel, with monophthong -e). The 体/체 divergence is notable: Dan'a'yo preserves the dental initial (ㄊ tʰ-) and the diphthong (ㄝㄧ -ei), while Korean palatalizes to ㅊ (ch-) and simplifies the vowel to plain -e.
