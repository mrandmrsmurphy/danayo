## Notes
- [教](../characters/教.md) "teach" + [唆](../characters/唆.md) "incite" — stand-in for [[唆]], which cannot appear independently
- 教唆 takes the neutral, even noble verb "to teach" and bends it toward something darker — not instruction toward virtue but the deliberate planting of an idea toward wrongdoing, a teaching aimed at making someone else commit the crime so the instigator's own hands stay clean. The compound carries this precise legal weight everywhere it appears: Mandarin jiàosuō and Cantonese gaau3 so1 name straightforward incitement, Sino-Japanese きょうさ (kyōsa) appears in criminal codes describing the instigator (教唆犯) who is punished alongside the principal offender, and Sino-Korean 교사 (gyosa) carries identical legal force in Korean criminal law, where 교사범 (instigator) receives the same sentence as the person who actually commits the crime. That last reading hides a small trap for the unwary: 교사 is a perfect homophone of 교사 (敎師, "teacher") — one of those quiet collisions where two compounds built from the same root character land, by sheer coincidence of sound, on opposite ends of the moral spectrum. Sino-Vietnamese giáo toa completes the set, following the same compositional logic — teaching turned, with a slight and telling shift, into the art of getting someone else to act.
