## Notes
- [公 (char)](../characters/公%20(char).md) "public, official" + [斤](../characters/斤.md) "kilogram" — stand-in for [[斤]], which cannot appear independently

公斤 (gōngjīn) is the standard Chinese term for kilogram. The compound uses 公 as a prefix meaning "metric" or "official/international" (as in 公里 for kilometre, 公尺 for metre, 公噸 for metric tonne) and 斤 (jīn), the traditional Chinese unit of weight approximately equal to 500 grams. The prefix 公 indicates that this is the internationally standardised version of the 斤, bringing it into the metric system at exactly 1000 grams.

The 公- prefix was adopted in Chinese during the early Republican era as a systematic way to render metric units in characters: 公 (meaning "public, official, international") was prepended to existing weight and length characters to form the metric equivalents. This produced a coherent family: 公斤 (kilogram), 公里 (kilometre), 公尺 (metre), 公升 (litre). In Japanese the term キログラム (kiro-guramu, from English kilogram) is standard; Japanese does not use 公斤 except in Chinese-language contexts. Korean similarly uses 킬로그램, though 근 (斤) remains in use for the traditional unit in markets.
