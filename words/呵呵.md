## Notes
- doubling of [呵](characters/呵.md) — stand-in for [[呵]], which cannot appear independently. Used in the chengyu <ruby>[呵呵大笑](/chengyu/呵呵大笑.md)<rt>ㄏㄚㄏㄚㄉㄚㄧㄙ⼄ㄨ</rt></ruby>.

Mandarin 呵呵 (hēhē) has undergone a striking, well-documented shift in modern internet usage: rather than genuine laughter, it now often reads as cold, dismissive, or outright sarcastic — closer to a mocking "oh, sure" than an actual laugh — a shift traceable as far back as the Ming and Qing dynasties, and sharp enough that many Chinese internet users now avoid typing it sincerely at all, reaching for 哈哈 (hāhā, unambiguous genuine laughter, kept here as an alias) instead. None of the other languages in the family picked up that same sarcastic undertone: Japanese has several distinct laugh-sound variants depending on tone — わはは (a loud, hearty laugh), あはは (a lighter, more carefree or embarrassed laugh), and ハハハ (a plain, largely tone-neutral rendering closest to Dan'a'yo's own はは) — none of which carry Mandarin 呵呵's specific mockery. Korean 하하 likewise stays a genuine, neutral laugh sound; the closest Korean equivalent to typed, ambiguous online laughter is actually ㅋㅋㅋ ("kkk"), a completely different written convention (repeated consonant letters, not a spelled-out word) that is Korea's default text-laughter marker the way 哈哈/lol function elsewhere, without 呵呵's specific sarcastic baggage.
