## Notes
- [и·і](../characters/и·і.md) "jump, leap" + [жўҒ (char)](../characters/жўҒ%20(char).md) "roof beam, rafter"

The image of и·іжўҒ is vivid and deliberately undignified: small animals вҖ” foxes, wildcats, weasels вҖ” leaping and scampering across the roof beams of a house, beyond the reach of authority and heedless of order. The phrase traces to the Zhuangzi (иҺҠеӯҗ), where such creatures serve as images of low, chaotic energy contrasted against the larger forces that govern nature. Over time, и·іжўҒ came to describe troublemakers of all kinds вҖ” people who exploit disorder to act recklessly and cause disruption, precisely because no one is stopping them.

In modern Chinese, и·іжўҒ most commonly appears as the first half of the chengyu [[и·іжўҒи·ӢжүҲ]] or in the compound и·іжўҒе°Ҹдё‘ (tiГ oliГЎng xiЗҺochЗ’u, "a clown who runs rampant") вҖ” a term for a petty, contemptible schemer who causes trouble despite having no real power or standing. In Japanese (гҒЎгӮҮгҒҶгӮҠгӮҮгҒҶ, chЕҚryЕҚ), the word appears chiefly in classical and literary contexts.
