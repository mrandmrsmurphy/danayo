## Notes
- [跳](../characters/跳.md) "jump, leap" + [梁 (char)](../characters/梁%20(char).md) "roof beam, rafter"

The image of 跳梁 is vivid and deliberately undignified: small animals — foxes, wildcats, weasels — leaping and scampering across the roof beams of a house, beyond the reach of authority and heedless of order. The phrase traces to the Zhuangzi (莊子), where such creatures serve as images of low, chaotic energy contrasted against the larger forces that govern nature. Over time, 跳梁 came to describe troublemakers of all kinds — people who exploit disorder to act recklessly and cause disruption, precisely because no one is stopping them.

In modern Chinese, 跳梁 most commonly appears as the first half of the chengyu [[跳梁跋扈]] or in the compound 跳梁小丑 (tiàoliáng xiǎochǒu, "a clown who runs rampant") — a term for a petty, contemptible schemer who causes trouble despite having no real power or standing. In Japanese (ちょうりょう, chōryō), the word appears chiefly in classical and literary contexts. Vietnamese khiêu lương survives in the exact same idiomatic frame as its Mandarin cognate — attested in phrases like 小醜跳梁 (tiểu xú khiêu lương, "a petty troublemaker running rampant") — rather than as an independent everyday word.
