## Notes
- [木 (char)](../characters/木%20(char).md) "tree; wood"

木 in Dan'a'yo is the noun for "tree; wood" and serves as the determiner (限定詞) for trees and woody plants — appearing in naming compounds to classify arboreal species: 楠木 (camphor wood), 檀木 (sandalwood), 柊木 (holly osmanthus), 檜木 (hinoki cypress), 梓木 (catalpa wood), 樺木 (birch). The character is a canonical 象形 pictograph of a tree: vertical trunk, lateral branches above, roots below — among the most visually transparent pictographic characters, its tree-shape visible from Oracle Bone Script onward. 木 appears on the Swadesh list (entry 51: "tree") and serves as Kangxi radical 75, underlying more than 400 sinographic characters related to wood, trees, and wooden objects. As a determiner, 木 contrasts with [[草]] (herbaceous plants/grass) in marking the woody-plant category. 木 also serves as the Dan'a'yo stand-in for the character [[木]].

Mandarin 木 (mù): wood; tree — 木头 (mùtou, wood; blockhead — colloquial), 木材 (mùcái, lumber; timber), 树木 (shùmù, trees and shrubs), 木工 (mùgōng, carpentry); the everyday Mandarin word for "tree" is 树 (shù), while 木 leans toward the material "wood" in compound contexts. Cantonese 木 (muk6): 木材 (muk6 coi4, timber), 木工 (muk6 gung1, carpenter); colloquially 樹 (syu6) for a tree. Japanese 木 (き/ki, native; もく/MOKU or ぼく/BOKU, Sino-Japanese): 木 (ki, a tree), 木曜日 (mokuyōbi, Thursday — lit. "wood-day"), 木材 (mokuzai, lumber), 樹木 (jumoku, trees). Korean 나무 (namu) is the native word for tree and wood; Sino-Korean 목 (木) in compounds: 목재 (木材, lumber), 목요일 (木曜日, Thursday), 수목 (樹木, trees). Vietnamese gỗ: gỗ cứng (hardwood), cây gỗ (a tree); Sino-Vietnamese mộc (木) in compounds: mộc lan (magnolia — lit. "wood orchid"), mộc nhĩ (木耳, wood ear mushroom).

kwin is true: Dan'a'yo 목 exactly matches Korean 목. 木 reads 목 (mog/ㄇㄛㄎ) in Dan'a'yo — MC 明母 (m) + 屋一韻 (uk) gives a bilabial nasal onset with the -ok coda. Both Dan'a'yo and Sino-Korean map MC 明母 to m- and MC 屋一韻 (uk) to plain -ok, producing identical 목 forms. The MC 屋一韻 is one of the most regularly preserved finals in both systems — a simple short -uk coda with no medial or diphthong — making 木 a phonologically stable kwin: true case.
