## Notes
- [麻](../characters/麻.md) "hemp" + [痺](../characters/痺.md) "numb; paralyzed"

Numbness, paralysis, or the loss of sensation and motor control in a body part or limb. The compound is unusual: 麻 means "hemp" but here contributes the sense of *tingling numbness* — the sensation of a limb that has "gone to sleep," described in Chinese as 麻 (literally: the prickling feeling of hemp fibers against skin). 痺 contributes the clinical sense of "paralysis" or "obstruction of circulation." Together 麻痺 covers the full spectrum from the temporary pins-and-needles of a sleeping limb to permanent neurological paralysis.

The hemp metaphor embedded in 麻 is precise: the sharp, prickling sensation of numbness was historically likened to the feel of rough hemp fabric or hemp rope against bare skin — a common enough texture in pre-industrial life to serve as the reference point for a neurological sensation. This poetic etymology is shared across the Sinosphere: the Japanese まひ (mahi) and Korean 마비 (mabi) preserve it without alteration.

In **medicine**, 麻痺 covers a wide range of conditions:
- **末梢神経麻痺** (peripheral nerve palsy) — compression or damage to peripheral nerves, producing local numbness and weakness
- **脳性麻痺** (nōsei mahi / 腦性麻痺, cerebral palsy) — motor disability from perinatal brain injury
- **小児麻痺** (shōni mahi / 小兒麻痺, poliomyelitis, lit. "child paralysis") — the historical Chinese/Japanese name for polio, whose eradication was one of the 20th century's greatest public health achievements
- **顔面神経麻痺** (facial nerve palsy, Bell's palsy)

In **figurative use**, 麻痺 describes any state of being frozen, unresponsive, or incapacitated — system failure, bureaucratic paralysis, emotional numbness after trauma. 都市機能が麻痺する ("city functions become paralyzed") is a standard phrase in Japanese disaster and crisis reporting. The figurative meaning extends naturally: an organization, a government, a person can all 麻痺する when overwhelmed or immobilized.
