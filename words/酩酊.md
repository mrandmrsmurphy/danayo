## Notes
- [酩](../characters/酩.md) "drunk" + 酊 "thoroughly intoxicated" — the compound for dead drunkenness; stand-in for [[酩]], which cannot appear independently; 酊 has no separate Dan'a'yo character entry — its phonology ㄉㄝㄫ is derived from its phonetic component 丁 (ㄉㄝㄫ)

**酩酊** (*mǐngdǐng*) means thoroughly, helplessly drunk — not merely 醉 (*zuì*, tipsy or intoxicated) but the state beyond it, where coherence, balance, and consciousness begin to fail. Both components carry the 酉 (wine vessel, alcohol) radical: 酩 = 酉 + 名 (phonetic *míng*), 酊 = 酉 + 丁 (phonetic *dīng*). The pairing is phonosemantic reduplication: two characters built on the same semantic radical and rhyming phonetics, joined to intensify a single meaning. Neither character appears outside this compound.

The compound almost always appears in the set phrase **酩酊大醉** (*mǐngdǐng dàzuì*, dead drunk — 大醉 being "greatly drunk"), which forms the standard four-character intensified expression for extreme intoxication in literary Chinese. The phrase appears in descriptions of banquets, laments over excess, and comic or tragic scenes of men who have drunk past the point of self-command. Its register is formal and somewhat archaic: everyday modern Chinese uses 喝醉了 or 醉了, reserving 酩酊大醉 for emphasis, irony, or literary effect.

The graphic etymology of both characters links drunkenness to nomenclature and enumeration: 名 (name, reputation) and 丁 (the fourth heavenly stem, a unit-mark) have nothing to do with alcohol — they are phonetic loans. But the effect is a word that looks like alcohol twice over, the 酉 radical doubled in the compound's visual field. 酊 specifically borrows the phonetic 丁, which gives it its Dan'a'yo syllable ㄉㄝㄫ (*deng*) — the same syllable as 丁 itself.

Japanese **めいてい** (*meitei*) is the standard Sino-Japanese word for intoxication in formal, medical, and legal registers: **酩酊状態** (*meitei jōtai*, state of intoxication) appears in traffic law, medical reports, and criminal law contexts to describe the condition of a person under the influence. The word has a slightly clinical or archaic weight in Japanese — everyday language uses **酔っ払う** (*yopparai*, to get drunk) — which makes it useful for objective description without the colloquial familiarity of simpler words. Korean **명정** (*myeongjeong*) is the Sino-Korean classical literary form for drunkenness; everyday Korean uses **만취** (滿醉, mantui, fully drunk) or **술에 취하다** (to become drunk with alcohol).
