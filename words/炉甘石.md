## Notes
- [炉](../characters/炉.md) "furnace; stove" + [甘 (char)](../characters/甘%20(char).md) "sweet" + [石 (char)](../characters/石%20(char).md) "stone" — the characters are phonosemantic rather than directly descriptive; the name is a traditional Chinese pharmacological designation

**炉甘石** (*lúgānshí*) is calamine — in Chinese traditional medicine and mineralogy, principally zinc carbonate (ZnCO₃, the mineral smithsonite) and related zinc ores including hemimorphite (Zn₄Si₂O₇(OH)₂·H₂O). The name appears in Li Shizhen's **本草綱目** (*Běncǎo Gāngmù*, "Compendium of Materia Medica", 1596 AD), the great Ming pharmacopoeia, where it is classified among the stone medicines and praised for its applications in ophthalmology and dermatology. Its etymology is uncertain: the compound does not parse transparently as 炉 "furnace" + 甘 "sweet" + 石 "stone," and the name likely reflects either a phonetic borrowing from a Persian or Arabic source term along the Silk Road, or an early Chinese pharmacological coinage that was later fixed in the tradition.

In Chinese traditional medicine, 炉甘石 was primarily used as an ophthalmic preparation — ground fine and applied to treat eye inflammation, pterygium, and corneal opacity — and topically for skin conditions including weeping sores, eczema, and burns. These applications map closely onto the modern use of calamine lotion, which consists of zinc oxide (ZnO) with a small proportion of ferric oxide (Fe₂O₃) giving its characteristic pink colour. The active principle throughout is zinc, in one form or another.

The metallurgical significance of 炉甘石 extends beyond medicine: zinc carbonate and zinc silicate ores were the historical raw material for brass manufacture. By co-smelting copper with 炉甘石, smiths in China — and simultaneously in South Asia and later Europe — produced brass long before metallic zinc was isolated as a distinct element. In this sense, 炉甘石 underlies a substantial portion of pre-modern metallurgical technology across Eurasia.

The English term *calamine* descends from Medieval Latin *calamine* → *lapis calaminaris* ("calamine stone"), itself of uncertain origin — possibly from Latinised Greek *cadmia* (another name for zinc ore, related to the mythological founder of Thebes, Cadmus). The English, Latin, and Chinese traditions converged on the same mineral from entirely independent directions. The element zinc (亜鉛, Dan'a'yo rendering) takes its name from the German *Zink*, of disputed origin, and was first recognised as a distinct metal in Europe in the 17th century AD despite the long prior use of its ores.
