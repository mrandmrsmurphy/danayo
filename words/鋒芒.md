## Notes
- [鋒](../characters/鋒.md) "spear-point, cutting edge" + [芒](../characters/芒.md) "awn, point" — the sharpest point of a blade or weapon; stand-in for [[鋒]], which cannot appear independently. 鋩 (金 + 芒, specifying the blade context) is a graphic variant of 芒 and is kept as an alias rather than a separate entry.

**鋒鋩** (*fēngmáng*) is the compound for keen edge — the combined sharpness of a blade's cutting face (鋒) and its tip (鋩). Both components name aspects of the same thing: the dangerous, penetrating point of a weapon. As a compound, 鋒鋩 has moved far from literal metallurgy into figurative use, naming the intellectual sharpness, rhetorical edge, or aggressive energy that a person projects.

The character 鋩 is a graphic variant of 芒, which originally meant the awn or beard of a grass or grain stalk — the fine, needle-sharp point at the tip of a seed head. By extension 芒 came to mean any needle-like protrusion, and in the compound 鋒芒 (the more common simplified form, 锋芒) it names the pointed tip of a blade. 鋩 adds the metal radical 金 to specify the blade context. The two forms 鋒鋩 and 鋒芒 are interchangeable in classical and modern usage; 锋芒 (simplified) and 鋒芒 (traditional) are the dominant written forms in contemporary East Asian texts.

**鋒芒畢露** (*fēngmáng bìlù*, every edge of the blade fully exposed) is the classical idiom for displaying one's full ability, ambition, or aggression without restraint. Its implied critique — the fully drawn blade is dangerous and often unwise — makes the phrase double-edged: it can describe admirable directness or reckless overexposure. The counterpart idiom **鋒芒內斂** (*fēngmáng nèiliǎn*, the edge turned inward, restrained) describes the strategic withholding of ability — talent kept in reserve, sharpness hidden until the right moment.

In Tang and Song poetry, 鋒 and 鋒鋩 appear in descriptions of brushwork: the 鋒 of a calligraphy brush is its tip, and the quality of a stroke is judged by how the 鋒 is handled — whether it enters (入鋒), exits (出鋒), or hides (藏鋒). The vocabulary of sword-edge and brush-tip overlap completely in classical aesthetics, because both demand the same mastery of the pointed entry into a surface.

Korean **봉망** (*bongmang*) preserves the compound in literary and classical contexts; modern Korean uses **날카로움** (native, sharpness) or **예리함** (銳利, yeriham) in everyday speech. Japanese **ほうぼう** (*hōbō*) is the Sino-Japanese reading; 鋒芒 appears in formal and literary registers but is not a common word in modern Japanese. Vietnamese **phong mang** (*phong* from 鋒, *mang* from 鋩/芒) is the Sino-Vietnamese compound; Vietnamese uses **sắc bén** (sharp, keen) or **tiên phong** (vanguard, cutting edge — metaphorical) in everyday contexts.
