## Notes
- [助](../characters/助.md) "assist, help, supplement" + [詞](../characters/詞.md) "word, expression" — a helping word; a word that assists other words.

Grammatical particles are one of the defining structural features of East Asian languages. Rather than using prepositions preceding their nouns (as European languages do), Japanese and Korean use postpositional particles that follow the noun and mark its grammatical role. Japanese は (topic), が (subject), を (object), に (locative/dative), で (instrumental), へ (directional), と (comitative), の (genitive) form the core particle inventory; Korean has a closely parallel system: 이/가 (subject), 을/를 (object), 은/는 (topic), 에 (locative), 와/과 (comitative).

The term 助詞 is a Meiji-era Japanese grammatical coinage — part of the vast vocabulary of Western grammatical categories translated into Sino-Japanese compounds in the 19th century and then adopted into Korean (조사). Before systematic Meiji-era grammar writing, particles had been described under various rubrics; 助詞 as a fixed technical term belongs to the project of building a European-style grammatical description of Japanese to match Western linguistic science.

In Mandarin, the equivalent category uses 助词 (zhùcí) with the same characters, but the particle inventory is thinner — Mandarin has fewer postpositional particles and relies more on word order and aspect markers. Vietnamese grammar uses 助詞 rarely; trợ từ is the native-Vietnamese equivalent term.

Dan'a'yo uses a particle system described in the grammar book (文法 chapters), making 助詞 a term with direct practical relevance within the vault.
