## Notes
- [引 (char)](../characters/引%20(char).md) "to draw, to pull" + [禍](../characters/禍.md) "calamity" — literally "to draw calamity [toward oneself]," i.e. to bring disaster upon oneself.

引禍 (yǐnhuò) pairs 引 — the same "draw, tug" root seen in [[牽引]] and [[引出]] — with 禍, "calamity, misfortune" (cf. [[禍事]], [[禍害]]). Where 招 ("to beckon") frames disaster as something carelessly summoned, 引 frames it as something actively drawn toward oneself, as if on a line. The two verbs are near-synonyms but differ slightly in connotation: 招 suggests an inadvertent or careless invitation, while 引 suggests a more direct pulling-in of consequences.

引禍 is best attested as the second half of the four-character idiom [[招災引禍]], where the chengyu page glosses it as "to bring on calamity" and pairs it with [[招災]] ("to invite disaster"). The chengyu's own note describes both halves as "established two-character compounds," used together to intensify the sense of self-inflicted ruin — a principle the chengyu's Biblical framing applies to covenant-breaking and its consequences.

The on-yomi いんか (IN + KA), Sino-Korean 인화 (inhwa), and Sino-Vietnamese dẫn họa all follow the same compositional pattern, each readable directly off the corresponding segment of [[招災引禍]]'s pronunciation fields. Note that 인화 is also the standard Korean reading for the unrelated homophone 引火 ("ignition, catching fire") — a useful homophone collision to be aware of, since both compounds use 引 with a near-identical figurative sense of "drawing on" a destructive force.

For usage in context, see [[招災引禍]].
