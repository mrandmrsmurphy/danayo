## Notes
- [邪](../characters/邪.md) "evil; deviant; crooked" + [心 (char)](../characters/心%20(char).md) "heart; mind; intention" — a wicked or self-serving disposition; stand-in for [[邪]], which cannot appear independently

**邪心** (*xiéxīn*) names the corrupt inner disposition from which wrong action flows — the heart that is crooked (邪) rather than upright. It is the moral opposite of 正心 (*zhèngxīn*, the rectified heart), one of the foundational virtues in the **大學** (*Dàxué*, Great Learning): 欲修其身者，先正其心 ("Those who wish to cultivate themselves must first rectify their hearts"). The sequence of moral cultivation in the Great Learning — investigation of things, extension of knowledge, sincerity of will, rectification of the heart, cultivation of the self — places 正心 at the centre; 邪心 is what 正心 corrects and displaces.

In the **Confucian tradition**, 邪心 encompasses several varieties of moral disorder: selfish desire (私慾) that distorts judgment, partisan bias that warps perception, resentment and anxiety that disturb composure, and the habitual tendency toward devious or manipulative conduct. The Confucian project of self-cultivation is in large part a project of identifying and rooting out 邪心 before it manifests in action — a form of inner moral hygiene.

In the **Buddhist tradition**, 邪心 (邪志, *micchā-saṅkappa* in Pali) belongs to the analysis of wrong mental factors: wrong intention, as opposed to samma-saṅkappa (right intention), the second step of the Eightfold Path. Wrong intention in the Buddhist framework covers intention toward sensual indulgence, ill will, and cruelty — all forms of 邪心 that set the practitioner moving in the direction of suffering rather than liberation.

In **modern Korean**, 사심 (*sasim*) has narrowed somewhat from the full classical range of "wicked heart" to the more specific meaning of **selfish motive or hidden agenda** — an interest pursued for personal benefit rather than the stated or common good. 사심 없이 ("without selfish motives") is a standard formula in public and professional contexts for declaring one's disinterested intentions. The phrase 사심이 있다 ("to have a selfish motive") is the accusation; 사심 없이 is the rebuttal. This narrowing — from general wickedness to the specifically self-interested variant — reflects the word's most frequent use in contemporary Korean discourse. In **Japanese**, じゃしん (*jashin*) retains the fuller sense of evil or malicious intent, and appears in literary and classical contexts; the distinct compound 邪神 (*jashin*, evil spirit/deity) shares the 邪 element but refers to a supernatural being rather than a human disposition. Vietnamese **tà tâm** is used in Buddhist and classical literary contexts for the corrupt heart that turns toward unwholesome ends.
