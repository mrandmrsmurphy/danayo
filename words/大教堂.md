## Notes
- [大 (char)](../characters/大%20(char).md) "great" + [教](../characters/教.md) "teaching, religion" + [堂](../characters/堂.md) "hall" — great religion-hall; a large teaching hall of the faith.

A cathedral is the principal church of a diocese, defined not by its size but by its possession of the cathedra (Latin: chair) — the bishop's throne. The word 大教堂 captures this through scale (大, "great") rather than office, which is why the more precise Chinese term is 主教座堂 (zhǔjiào zuòtáng, "bishop's-seat hall"). In practice, 大教堂 is the common term for any large, imposing church building, whether or not it is technically a cathedral.

The compound 教堂 is embedded within the word — [[教堂]] (jiàotáng, "teaching-hall") is the standard word for any Christian church building, and 大教堂 simply prefixes 大 to denote the grandest rank within that category. The same logic governs [[大學]] ("great study") for university versus [[學校]] for school generally.

In East Asian architectural history, the great Gothic cathedrals of Europe arrived conceptually through Jesuit missionaries in the 16th century and physically through photographs and Western architectural manuals in the 19th. The first large purpose-built cathedrals in East Asia date to the colonial period: the Myeongdong Cathedral in Seoul (1898), Sacred Heart Cathedral in Guangzhou (1888), and St. Ignatius Cathedral in Shanghai (1906, rebuilt 1910) are among the earliest examples.
