## Notes
- [奴](../characters/奴.md) "slave" + [家](../characters/家.md) "house, household" — literally "house of slaves," i.e. a household or condition of bondage.

奴家 (nújiā) has two quite distinct historical lives in Sinitic. In later vernacular Mandarin and in Ming–Qing drama, 奴家 became a fossilized first-person pronoun — a humble self-reference used almost exclusively by women, comparable to how 小弟 or 在下 function for men. In that register it means simply "I" or "me," carrying no literal sense of slavery at all.

Dan'a'yo instead fixes 奴家 to its older, compositional reading: "house of slaves" or "house of bondage." This is the sense required by the Biblical chengyu [[引出奴家]], which renders the Exodus 20:2 formula "brought thee out of the land of Egypt, out of the house of bondage" — Hebrew _mibbêṯ ʿăḇāḏîm_. The chengyu page itself flags this divergence explicitly: the vernacular drama sense of "I/me" is a later semantic narrowing that Dan'a'yo does not follow.

Cross-linguistically, the on-yomi どか (DO + KA) and Sino-Korean 노가 (noga) both preserve the literal "slave-house" reading transparently, as does Vietnamese nô gia. None of the other CJKV traditions appear to have independently developed the Chinese drama pronoun usage, which makes 奴家 in Dan'a'yo unambiguous in a way it sometimes is not in literary Mandarin.

For usage in context, see [[引出奴家]], where 引出 ("led out") governs 奴家 to render the Exodus formula in full.
