## Notes
- [冊 (char)](../characters/冊%20(char).md) "book; bound volume" + [子](../characters/子.md) nominalizer — for booklets and pamphlets, see [[小冊子]]

**冊子** (*cèzi*) is the general Dan'a'yo word for a book — a bound written or printed work of any substantial length. The 子 suffix functions as a nominalizer, concretising 冊 into a discrete physical object. For smaller, thinner publications — booklets, pamphlets, brochures — see [[小冊子]] (*xiǎo cèzi*), which confines itself to those diminutive forms.

The character 冊 is itself a pictogram of bamboo slips bound together with cord — the original medium of Chinese writing from at least the late Shang dynasty onward. Bamboo and wood slip manuscripts (簡牘, *jiǎndú*) were the dominant writing medium in China throughout the Zhou, Qin, and Han dynasties, supplanted by silk and then by paper as paper production scaled in the Eastern Han period (1st–2nd century AD). The earliest versions of the canonical texts — the Five Classics, legal codes, philosophical works — were written and transmitted on these slip books, making 冊 a foundational character in the history of Chinese literacy. It is cognate with 典 ("canon; law; classic"), which depicts slips placed on a stand, and 侖 ("orderly sequence"), depicting slips arranged in order.

In Japanese, 冊子 (*sasshi*) spans the range from thin stapled pamphlets to bound volumes; the geminate っ reflects the SATSU + SHI assimilation. Korean 책자 (*chaekja*) is a common and standard term for any kind of bound publication — books, booklets, albums, and official documents alike. Vietnamese *sách* (from 冊) is itself the standard everyday word for "book," a direct loan that has shed the 子 suffix entirely; the full Sino-Vietnamese compound *sách tử* is rarely used independently.
