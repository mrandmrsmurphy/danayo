## Notes
- [寂](../characters/寂.md) "silent, still" + [静](../characters/静.md) "quiet"

Both constituent characters share the Korean native gloss 고요할 ("quiet, still"), and both carry the sense of stillness and absence of disturbance. Their combination intensifies and refines that shared meaning: 寂静 is not merely the absence of noise but a quality of deep, settled quietness — the silence of an empty hall, a mountain dawn, or a meditative mind.

In Chinese (jìjìng), the word is both descriptive adjective and literary noun, appearing in landscape poetry, Buddhist texts, and modern prose to describe environments or mental states of profound calm. In Buddhist usage, 寂静 (and its traditional form 寂靜) denotes liberation from mental afflictions (kleśa) — the stillness of a mind no longer stirred by craving and aversion. This usage connects to the Sanskrit *śānta* (peaceful, pacified).

In Japanese (じゃくじょう, jakujō), the reading draws on the Buddhist on'yomi JAKU (寂) + JOU (静) rather than the modern SEI/SEKI readings, marking the compound as a specifically religious or literary term. The everyday Japanese equivalent is 静か (shizuka). Note that the reverse compound [[静寂]] (じゃくせい / seijaku) also exists and is common in modern Japanese, where it means the same thing but with slightly different register — 静寂 is more widely used in contemporary contexts while 寂静 retains a more classical and Buddhist flavor. In Dan'a'yo, 静寂 is the stand-in word for the character 静.
