## Notes
- [貢](../characters/貢.md) "tribute, gifts" + [品 (char)](../characters/品%20(char).md) "article, item" — stand-in for [[貢]], which cannot appear independently

貢品 refers to tribute goods — items, produce, or valuables presented by a subordinate polity, vassal, or subject to a ruler or overlord as a mark of submission and in exchange for recognition and protection. The compound combines 貢 (gòng, to offer tribute, to present upward) with 品 (pǐn, articles, goods, items), producing a precise term for the physical objects comprising tribute rather than the act of paying it.

The tribute system (貢納制度) was fundamental to East Asian statecraft. Within the Chinese imperial framework, vassal states and border peoples were expected to send periodic missions bearing local specialties — silk, horses, medicinal plants, exotic animals, precious metals — to the imperial court. The court would typically reciprocate with gifts of greater nominal value, making the system as much a mechanism of diplomatic exchange and legitimation as an extraction of resources. 貢品 thus carried enormous symbolic weight: the content and quality of tribute goods signalled the relationship between giver and receiver. In Japan, Korea, and Vietnam, analogous tribute systems operated both in relation to China and within domestic political structures.
