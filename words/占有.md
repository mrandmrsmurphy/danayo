## Notes
- [占 (char)](../characters/占%20(char).md) "to occupy; to seize; to hold" + [有 (char)](../characters/有%20(char).md) "to have; to hold; to possess"

占有 is "to possess; to occupy; to hold" — a verb (事詞) combining two complementary possession semantics. 有 alone is the basic existential and possessive verb ("to have; to exist"); 占 adds the dimension of active occupancy or claim — seizing, taking hold of, or asserting control over. Together, 占有 implies possession with an element of deliberate occupancy: not merely having something but holding it, controlling it, or maintaining a claim to it. The compound is well-suited to legal, territorial, and property contexts where the nature of the hold matters, not just the fact of having.

Mandarin 占有 (zhànyǒu) and its traditional variant 佔有 (the alias here) are established in formal written Chinese: 佔有財產 (to possess property), 佔有率 (occupancy rate; market share), 佔有欲 (possessiveness; the desire to possess). Japanese 占有 (せんゆう, sen'yū) is a standard legal term for "possession" in property law — 占有権 (sen'yū ken, possessory right) is a recognised category in Japanese civil law, referring to the legal right arising from actually holding something regardless of ownership title. Korean 점유 (jeomu) carries the same formal register: 점유권 (possessory right). Vietnamese chiếm hữu is the Sino-Vietnamese rendering of the same compound.

The alias 佔有 reflects the traditional-character variant: 佔 is the literary and traditional form of 占, retained in Taiwan and in formal legal writing across the Sinosphere. Mainland China standardises on 占. Dan'a'yo uses 占 as the primary form — consistent with the simplified-convention characters common in HSK vocabulary — with 佔有 as a cross-reference alias for traditional-script readers. The kwin: true for this entry is notable: the Dan'a'yo Hangul transcription 점유 happens to match the Sino-Korean reading of 占有 exactly, a coincidence of shared Middle Chinese inheritance.
