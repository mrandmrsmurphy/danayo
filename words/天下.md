## Notes
- [天 (char)](../characters/天%20(char).md) "sky; heaven" + [下 (char)](../characters/下%20(char).md) "below; under"

天下 is "the world; all under heaven" — 天 (sky; heaven) + 下 (below; under). Literally "that which lies beneath heaven" — the totality of the inhabited world as seen from a heavenly vantage point. The concept originates in the Sinitic cosmological model in which Heaven (天) is the supreme overarching power, and everything beneath it constitutes the realm of human affairs. 天下 was simultaneously a geographical concept (the known civilised world) and a political one: the domain legitimately governed by the Son of Heaven (天子, the emperor), who held the Mandate of Heaven (天命). To "possess 天下" meant to hold sovereign authority over all; to "lose 天下" meant the collapse of that mandate.

Mandarin 天下 (tiānxià) carries a classical weight distinct from the ordinary word for "world" (世界, shìjiè). It appears throughout the canon: 天下為公 (the realm belongs to all people), 天下第一 (the best in all the world), and the celebrated sequence from the 大學: 修身齊家治國平天下 (cultivate the self, regulate the family, govern the state, bring peace to all under heaven). Japanese 天下 (てんか, tenka) is inseparable from Sengoku-era history: 天下統一 (tenka tōitsu, unification of the realm) was the declared goal of Oda Nobunaga and Toyotomi Hideyoshi, and 天下人 (tenka-bito, "lord of the realm") is the honorific applied to those who achieved it. Korean 천하 (天下, cheonha): 천하통일 (unification of the realm), 천하제일 (the best in the world). Vietnamese thiên hạ (天下) echoes the full Sinitic political cosmology in literary and historical contexts.

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 턴하 differs from Korean 천하 in the first syllable. 天 reads 턴 (ten/ㄊㄝㄋ) in Dan'a'yo but 천 (cheon) in Sino-Korean — MC 透母 (tʰ, aspirated alveolar stop) before the 先開 final (en, a front vowel) gives Dan'a'yo ㄊ (t-, alveolar aspirate preserved without shift), while Sino-Korean palatalizes the sequence to ㅊ (ch-, palatal aspirate). Dan'a'yo does not palatalize MC alveolar stops before front vowels; Sino-Korean does. The second syllable is identical: 下 reads 하 (ha/ㄏㄚ) in both Dan'a'yo and Sino-Korean, MC 匣母 (ɣ) + 麻二開 final giving ㅎ/h in both systems.
