## Notes
- [徳 (char)](../characters/徳%20(char).md) "virtue" + [川 (char)](../characters/川%20(char).md) "river" — literally "virtue river," the surname of the clan that ruled Japan as shoguns from 1603 to 1868. The Japanese reading is とくがわ, with the second element がわ showing rendaku voicing (かわ → がわ) typical of Japanese place- and family-names ending in 川.

The Tokugawa were a branch of the Matsudaira clan; Tokugawa Ieyasu, who had used the Matsudaira name for most of his career, adopted "Tokugawa" in 1567 while claiming descent from the Minamoto clan's Seiwa Genji line, a genealogical claim that helped legitimize his eventual appointment as shogun. After consolidating power at the Battle of Sekigahara (1600) and receiving the title of shogun in 1603, Ieyasu founded the Tokugawa shogunate, governing from [[江戸]] (Edo, modern Tokyo) rather than the imperial capital of Kyoto. Fifteen Tokugawa shoguns followed in succession over the next two and a half centuries, presiding over the long peace and relative isolation of the Edo period — marked by the sakoku policy restricting foreign contact, the rigid four-tier class system, and the rise of a distinct urban merchant culture — until the fifteenth and last shogun, Tokugawa Yoshinobu, surrendered power in 1867 amid the Meiji Restoration, ending both the shogunate and Tokugawa rule.
