## Notes
- [侵](../characters/侵.md) "invade; encroach; raid" + [入](../characters/入%20(char).md) "enter" — stand-in for [[侵]], which cannot appear independently
- 侵入 names the act of entering where one does not belong — a soldier crossing a border, a thief slipping through a window, a virus breaching a cell wall, an idea creeping uninvited into a mind. The compound is a small lesson in escalation: 入 alone is neutral, the plain verb "to enter," but prefixing it with 侵 — "to encroach, to raid, to violate" — turns a simple movement into a violation, framing entry itself as trespass. That logic carries unbroken across the region: Mandarin qīnrù and Cantonese cam1 jap6 keep the literal sense of forced or unwelcome entry, Sino-Korean 침입 (chimip) and Sino-Vietnamese xâm nhập do the same, and Sino-Japanese しんにゅう (shinnyū) extends the word into computing and biology alike — "intrusion detection," viral "invasion" of host cells — wherever something crosses a boundary it has no right to cross. Few compounds make so explicit, in their very construction, the difference between simply going somewhere and going somewhere you shouldn't.
