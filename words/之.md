## Notes
- [之 (char)](../characters/之%20(char).md) "of; genitive marker"

之 is the genitive case particle — 属格, marking possession, attribution, and the relationship "X's Y; Y of X." Suffixed to a possessor noun, 之 forms a modifier standing before the head noun: 人之法 (the law of humanity), 天之道 (the way of heaven), 天下之大 (the vastness of all under heaven). In classical Chinese, 之 was originally a verb meaning "to go," but this meaning bleached away entirely as 之 became the most ubiquitous structural particle in the literary canon — appearing in virtually every classical text as the marker of the genitive relationship. Dan'a'yo inherits this function directly, using 之 as the productive genitive suffix in both spoken and written registers. 之 also serves as the Dan'a'yo stand-in for the character [[之]].

Mandarin 之 (zhī): entirely replaced by 的 (de) in spoken Mandarin for possession, but preserved in literary set phrases and compounds: 一分之一 (yī fēn zhī yī, one-third — lit. one of three parts), 之前 (before; prior to), 之間 (between), 之後 (after; subsequent to), 總之 (in sum; in short). Cantonese: the colloquial possessive is 嘅 (ge3); 之 is found only in literary compounds. Japanese: の (no) is the productive genitive particle — 日本の文化 (Japanese culture), 私の本 (my book); 之 survives only in archaic or literary contexts, and の is among the aliases of the character 之 itself. Korean: 의 (-ui/-의) is the Korean genitive marker; 지 (之) is literary only. Vietnamese chi (之): found in Sino-Vietnamese literary compounds — chi gian (之間, between), chi tiết (之節, detail; lit. of the section).

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 티 differs from Korean 지. 之 reads 티 (ti/ㄊㄧ) in Dan'a'yo — MC 章母 (t͡ɕ) + 之韻 (ɨ) gives Dan'a'yo an unaspirated alveolar onset with -i vowel — while Sino-Korean gives 지 (ji), where the MC palatal affricate is preserved in the Korean affricate ㅈ (j-). Dan'a'yo maps the MC palatal to a plain dental where Sino-Korean maps it to the affricate.
