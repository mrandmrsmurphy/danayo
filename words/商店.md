## Notes
- [商](../characters/商.md) "commerce; trade" + [店](../characters/店.md) "shop; establishment"

商店 is "shop; store" — 商 (commerce; trade; merchant) + 店 (shop; place of business). 商 derives from the name of the Shang dynasty (商朝), whose merchants became so associated with trade that the dynastic name generalised into the word for commerce itself; 商人 (merchant) and 商業 (commerce) both carry this origin. 店 (MC t + em, 端母 + 添韻) denotes a shop or stall — a physical place where goods are kept and sold. Together, 商店 names any retail shop or store. The compound is transparent and highly productive: 書商店 (bookshop), 薬商店 (pharmacy), 花商店 (flower shop).

Mandarin 商店 (shāngdiàn) is the standard written term; colloquially 店 appears freely as a suffix: 書店 (bookstore), 藥店 (pharmacy), 飯店 (hotel/restaurant). Cantonese 商店 (soeng1 dim3) is the formal term; everyday Cantonese often uses 舖頭 (pou3 tau4). Japanese 商店 (しょうてん, shōten) is the formal term — 商店街 (しょうてんがい, shōtengai, shopping street/arcade) is a fixture of Japanese urban life, naming the covered pedestrian shopping arcades that run through the centres of most Japanese towns and cities. Korean 상점 (商店, sangjeom) is the Sino-Korean term; the native word 가게 (gage) is more common in everyday speech. Vietnamese thương tiệm (Sino-Vietnamese: 商=thương, 店=tiệm) is the formal Sino-Vietnamese form; the everyday term is cửa hàng (literally "goods door").

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 샹덤 diverges from Korean 상점 in both syllables. 商 reads 샹 (syang/ㄙ⼘ㄫ) in Dan'a'yo but 상 (sang) in Korean — Dan'a'yo preserves a medial element from the MC 陽韻 (ɨɐŋ) final, reflected in the -y- of the ROmaji, while Korean drops it. 店 reads 덤 (dem/ㄉㄝㄇ) in Dan'a'yo but 점 (jeom) in Korean — MC 端 initial (t) before the 添韻 (em, a front vowel final) palatalizes to ㅈ (j) in Sino-Korean, while Dan'a'yo preserves the unpalatalized dental ㄉ (d).
