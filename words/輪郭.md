## Notes
- [輪 (char)](../characters/輪%20(char).md) "wheel, ring, circle" + [郭 (char)](../characters/郭%20(char).md) "outermost part, outer wall" (Dan'a'yo stand-in for 廓)

In Dan'a'yo, 郭 stands in for 廓; the traditional Chinese form is 輪廓, simplified 轮廓.

輪郭 denotes the defining outer edge of a shape — the line that separates a form from its background and makes it recognizable as a distinct thing. The compound draws on 輪 (wheel, ring, circular boundary) and 廓/郭 (outer enclosure, perimeter), combining two words for outer boundaries into a compound for "outline." The resulting image is of a closed curve — not the interior substance, but the edge that gives something its visible shape.

In Japanese (りんかく, rinkaku), 輪郭 is the everyday word for outline, contour, and silhouette in both concrete and abstract usage: the outline of a face (顔の輪郭), the contours of a plan (計画の輪郭), the broad shape of an argument. It is among the more common Sino-Japanese compounds in everyday writing. The character 郭 here functions as the Japanese daiyōji (代用字) replacement for 廓 — a standard simplification in postwar Japanese orthography.

In Chinese (lúnkuò), the traditional form 輪廓 is used across Taiwan and formal writing; simplified 轮廓 is dominant in mainland China. In Korean, the initial sound rule (두음법칙) changes initial 륜 to 윤, yielding 윤곽 (yun'gwak) — a common word in Korean for outline and contour.
