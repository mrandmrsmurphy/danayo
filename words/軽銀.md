## Notes
- [軽 (char)](../characters/軽%20(char).md) "light; not heavy" + [銀 (char)](../characters/銀%20(char).md) "silver"

軽銀 is "aluminium" — 軽 (light; not heavy) + 銀 (silver). The compound is a Dan'a'yo colloquial coinage naming aluminium by its two most immediately apparent properties: its silvery metallic lustre and its strikingly low density (2.7 g/cm³, roughly a third that of iron). The analogy to "tin foil" is instructive — just as aluminium foil is universally called "tin foil" in English despite containing no tin, 軽銀 gives aluminium a transparent descriptive name rather than a transliteration or specially coined character. Dan'a'yo explicitly does not use the Mandarin-coined character 鋁/铝 (lǚ): it is a novel character with no traditional CJKV pedigree and is easily confused with 鑢 (a metal file) and 礪/砺 (a whetstone). For the Dan'a'yo periodic table entry for aluminium, see [[礬]]. The alias 鋼精 (gāngjīng) is a historical Mandarin informal coinage for aluminium; 輕銀/轻银 are the traditional and simplified character spellings of the same 軽銀 compound.

Aluminium is the most abundant metal in Earth's crust and the third most abundant element overall. The name 軽銀 is well-motivated: aluminium is not only lighter than most structural metals but was historically more expensive than silver or even gold before the Hall–Héroult electrolytic smelting process (1886) made mass production possible; Napoleon III famously reserved aluminium cutlery for his most honoured guests while lesser guests used silver. In modern CJKV usage: Mandarin 铝/鋁 (lǚ, the standard element character); Cantonese 鋁 (leoi5); Japanese アルミニウム (aruminiumu, the standard scientific transliteration) or informally アルミ (arumi); Korean 알루미늄 (alruminyum) or 경은 (輕銀, the Sino-Korean reading of the compound). Vietnamese nhôm is the standard Vietnamese word for aluminium, borrowed from French aluminium; khinh ngân is the Sino-Vietnamese reading of the characters.

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 킹인 differs from Korean 경은 in both syllables. 軽 reads 킹 (king/ㄎㄧㄫ) in Dan'a'yo but 경 (gyeong) in Sino-Korean — MC 溪母 (kʰ) + 清開韻 (iᴇŋ) gives Dan'a'yo ㄎ (k-aspirated) with a plain -ing ending, while Sino-Korean palatalises the sequence to 경 (gyeong). 銀 reads 인 ('in/ㄧㄋ) in Dan'a'yo but 은 (eun) in Sino-Korean — MC 疑母 (ŋ) + 眞B開韻 (ɣiɪn) gives a vowel-initial syllable in both, but with different vowel quality: Dan'a'yo preserves the i-vowel of the MC front vowel (인), while Sino-Korean shifts to the mid-central eu-vowel (은).
