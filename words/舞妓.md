## Notes
- [舞](../characters/舞.md) "dance" + [妓](../characters/妓.md) "female entertainer"

舞妓 names a "dancing entertainer" — most familiar in its Japanese reading まいこ (maiko), the apprentice geisha of Kyoto's hanamachi (花街, "flower towns") who specializes in traditional dance, distinguished from the senior 芸妓 (geiko/geisha) she trains to become. 舞, a 象形-classified character whose modern form derives from a depiction of a dancer with flowing sleeves (its graphemic root shared with 無, "without," via a complex history of phonetic borrowing), is the basic word for "to dance" across all five languages, with its stand_in compound 跳舞 ("to dance") the everyday verb in Mandarin.

舞妓 is the second of this batch's 妓-compounds, forming a deliberate contrast with [[妓女]] (the previous word): both combine 妓 with another character, but where 妓女 leans into 妓's "sex worker" sense, 舞妓 leans into its older "skilled performer" sense, foregrounding 舞 (dance) as the entertainer's defining art. 舞妓 also happens to be one of the few words in this entire project where the Korean reading (무기) coincides exactly with the compositional reading derived from its parts (舞 무 + 妓 기) — reflected in `kwin: true` — though in practice 무기 is far more often encountered in Korean as the homophonous (and unrelated) word for "weapon" (武器).
