## Notes
- [魔](../characters/魔.md) "demon" + [女 (char)](../characters/女%20(char).md) "woman"

A witch or sorceress — a woman who wields magical or demonic power. The compound is transparent: 魔 (demon, supernatural force) + 女 (woman). It covers both the malevolent witch of folklore and the morally positive or neutral witch figure that dominates modern East Asian popular culture.

The concept behind 魔女 in East Asian tradition has several roots. Classical Chinese demonology had the 妖女 (yāonǚ, fox-spirit woman) and 女巫 (nǚwū, female shaman-sorceress) — figures of dangerous beauty and supernatural transformation. Buddhist demonology contributed another sense: the daughters and servants of [[魔羅]] (Mara), described as 魔女 in texts like the Pali Canon — women embodying temptation sent to distract the meditating Bodhisattva. Through these streams, 魔女 developed as the general term for a supernaturally empowered woman, usually with a threatening or transgressive edge.

In Japan, **まじょ** (majo, 魔女) lives a rich double life. One strand leads toward the dark folkloric witch — the yamamba (山姥, mountain witch), the old woman of the forest, the uncanny female figure at the edge of civilization who terrifies and sometimes devours. This archetype was reinforced in the Meiji and Taisho periods by translations of Grimm's fairy tales and European witch-trial literature, which gave Japanese readers a new, specifically European witch archetype that blended with native figures.

The other strand is the dominant modern one: the **魔法少女** (mahō shōjo, "magical girl") tradition, in which 魔女 describes a young woman whose magical powers are a source of wonder, adventure, and identity rather than menace. Studio Ghibli's *Kiki's Delivery Service* (魔女の宅急便, 1989) — in which a thirteen-year-old witch must find her independence by working as a broomstick delivery girl — is the cultural touchstone for this warm portrayal, and one of the most beloved Japanese films. The genre runs from this gentle register through to the darker *Puella Magi Madoka Magica* (魔法少女まどか☆マギカ, 2011), which deconstructs the magical-girl tradition by revealing the tragic cost of magical power. In these contexts, 魔女 has entirely shed its demonic connotation and become a symbol of female agency and supernatural capability.
