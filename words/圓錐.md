## Notes
- [圓 (char)](../characters/圓%20(char).md) "circle; round" + [錐](../characters/錐.md) "awl; pointed tool" — the "round-awl" solid; a cone tapers to a point as an awl does, from a circular base

**圓錐** (*yuánzhuī*) is the standard Sinographic term for a cone: the geometric solid with a circular base that tapers uniformly to an apex. The compound is transparently descriptive — 圓 (round, circular) specifying the base, 錐 (awl) capturing the tapering-to-a-point form. It covers both the right circular cone (正圓錐, in which the apex lies directly above the centre of the base) and, in extended use, oblique cones and cone-like shapes in general.

The deeper mathematical significance of 圓錐 lies in the **圓錐曲線** (*yuánzhuī qūxiàn*, conic sections): the curves obtained by intersecting a plane with a double cone at various angles. Depending on the angle, the intersection yields a circle, an ellipse, a parabola, or a hyperbola. These four curves were systematically classified by **Apollonius of Perga** (阿波羅尼烏斯, c. 262–190 BC) in his eight-book *Conics* (《圓錐曲線論》), one of the supreme achievements of Greek mathematics. The conic sections later proved foundational to early modern physics: Kepler's first law (planetary orbits are ellipses, 1609 AD), Galileo's analysis of projectile trajectories as parabolas, and Newton's demonstration that inverse-square gravity produces conic-section orbits all depend on Apollonius's framework. The word 圓錐, when it appears in physics or astronomy contexts, often carries this resonance.

For volume, the cone satisfies V = ⅓πr²h — one-third the volume of the cylinder of equal base and height, a result known to Archimedes and proved rigorously in the *Elements* tradition. The surface area of a right circular cone (excluding the base) is πrl, where l is the slant height.

In everyday life, the cone shape appears in traffic management (交通錐, orange traffic cones), confectionery (ice cream cones), headgear (the paper dunce cap, the traditional conical hats of East and Southeast Asia), military and religious contexts (the bishop's mitre, the pointed helmet), and landscape (volcanic peaks approximate a cone, hence 火山 literally "fire mountain" but visually cone-shaped).

Japanese **円錐** (*ensui*) is the standard mathematical term in education and reference, using the shinjitai 円 for 圓. Korean has two parallel terms: **원추** (*wonchu*), the Sino-Korean compound directly corresponding to 圓錐, used in formal and mathematical registers; and **원뿔** (*wonppul*), the native Korean compound — 원 (circle) + 뿔 (horn, antler) — which dominates in everyday speech and school mathematics. The two coinages independently reach the same conceptual image: a circular thing that comes to a point, like a horn. Vietnamese **hình nón** ("nón shape") is the standard everyday term, *nón* being the traditional Vietnamese conical hat (the *nón lá*, leaf hat) — making the Vietnamese word not a geometric abstraction but a direct reference to a familiar cultural object; the Sino-Vietnamese compound *viên chùy* exists but is largely restricted to classical or technical writing.
