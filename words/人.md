## Notes
- [人 (char)](../characters/人%20(char).md) "person"

人 is "person; humanity" — in Dan'a'yo, 人 functions as a noun/pronoun for a person and as a classifier for counting people (一人, two人, etc.) with neutral register. As a correlative row heading, 人 generates 此人 (this person), 其人 (that person), 何人 (which person; who — though 誰 is the normal interrogative for persons, 何人 being rude and 何名 very polite), 皆人 (everyone). The character 人 is among the most ancient and frequent in the CJKV sphere — a pictogram of a standing human figure, side view — and serves as the human radical (部首 009) in hundreds of derived characters including 仁, 今, 以, 令, 倫. 人 also serves as the Dan'a'yo stand-in for the character [[人]].

Mandarin 人 (rén): 人口 (rénkǒu, population), 人類 (rénlèi, humankind), 人工 (réngōng, artificial; man-made), 個人 (gèrén, individual), 大人 (dàrén, adult; Your Excellency). Among the ten most frequent characters in Mandarin and classical Chinese. Cantonese 人 (jan4): 人口 (jan4 hau2, population), 人哋 (jan4 dei6, other people; someone else). Japanese 人 (にん, nin / じん, jin; native ひと, hito): にん is used in compounds denoting countable people (三人, three people), じん in nationality and role compounds (日本人, Japanese person; 軍人, soldier); the native ひと covers the general sense of "person." Korean 인 (人) in Sino-Korean: 인구 (人口, population), 인류 (人類, humankind), 개인 (個人, individual); native 사람 (saram, person). Vietnamese nhân (人): nhân loại (human race), nhân khẩu (population); the variant nhơn is archaic Southern Vietnamese.

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 닌 differs from Korean 인. 人 reads 닌 (nin/ㄋㄧㄋ) in Dan'a'yo — MC 日母 (ȵ) + 眞A開韻 (iɪn) gives Dan'a'yo an initial nasal consonant ㄋ (n-) with the -in vowel, producing 닌 — while Sino-Korean gives 인 (in), a vowel-initial syllable with no initial consonant. Dan'a'yo preserves the MC palatal nasal initial ȵ as an overt n- onset where Sino-Korean treats it as null.
