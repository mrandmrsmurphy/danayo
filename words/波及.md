## Notes
- [波](../characters/波.md) "wave" + [及 (char)](../characters/及%20(char).md) "reach out to"

To spread outward and reach; to ripple out to affect other things. The metaphor is exact and still fully alive: 波 (wave) + 及 (to reach) = "the waves reach" — the way effects propagate outward from their source like ripples on water, touching things progressively further from the center.

波及 is one of those compounds where the original image has not faded to abstraction. When a financial crisis 波及する to neighboring economies, when a scandal 波及する to implicate associates, or when a policy change 波及する to downstream industries, the wave imagery remains present: an initial disturbance, propagating outward in rings, arriving at progressively more distant targets. No single English word captures this as cleanly; "spread," "spill over," "ripple out," and "affect" all require different prepositions and lose the wave sense.

**波及効果** (hakyū kōka, "ripple effects; spillover effects") is standard vocabulary in economics and policy analysis — describing second-order consequences of an intervention that extend beyond the directly targeted area. **波及力** (hakyūryoku, "propagating power; reach") describes a phenomenon's capacity to spread. **全世界に波及した** ("it spread throughout the world") demonstrates the word's use for global-scale diffusion.

A notable semantic contrast with [[普及]]: 波及 describes the spread of effects, consequences, and disruptions — often unwanted or at least neutral in tone, something that happens rather than something engineered. 普及 describes the deliberate or welcome spread of knowledge, technology, or practice. A disease 波及する; literacy 普及する. An economic shock 波及する; smartphones 普及する. The wave metaphor in 波及 implies that the spread is somewhat natural and unstoppable — the consequences arriving whether one wills them to or not.
