## Notes
- [鉦](../characters/鉦.md) "marching gong" + [鼓 (char)](../characters/鼓%20(char).md) "drum" — the paired percussion instruments of military command; stand-in for [[鉦]], which cannot appear independently

**鉦鼓** (*zhēnggǔ*) is the paired gong-and-drum of military and ceremonial percussion — the two instruments that together governed the movement of troops in classical East Asian warfare. Their pairing is both physical (they appear together in the battle array) and semantic: as a compound, 鉦鼓 names the ensemble and, by extension, the use of percussion to command and signal.

The strategic logic of 鉦 and 鼓 was fixed in classical military doctrine. **擊鼓進軍** (beat the drum, advance) — the drum's sharp, forward-driving rhythm signalled troops to advance. **鳴金收兵** (sound the metal, withdraw the troops) — the resonant, sustaining tone of the bronze gong (金, here synonymous with 鉦) signalled halt, withdrawal, or assembly. This convention is codified in early military texts and appears throughout classical Chinese literature as a metonym for battle: 金鼓 (gong and drum) became the standard compound for the sounds of war itself. The reversal of the pairing — 鉦鼓 rather than the more common 金鼓 or 鼓金 — emphasises the gong first, which may reflect a context of halting and control as much as advance.

The 鉦 is a bronze percussion instrument of shallow, flat form — a suspended or handheld gong struck with a mallet, producing a clear, penetrating tone that carries across a battlefield. It is distinct from the larger, deeper 鐘 (bell, hung and struck) and the 磬 (stone chime). In Zhou-period ritual music, the 鉦 appeared in the percussion ensemble alongside the 鐘磬 (bronze bells and stone chimes) and the various drums of the 雅樂 (elegant music). In Japanese **雅楽** (*gagaku*), the equivalent instrument しょうこ (*shōko*) — a small suspended bronze gong — is one of the three percussion instruments of the classical court orchestra alongside the 太鼓 (*taiko*, large drum) and 鞨鼓 (*kakko*, barrel drum).

Korean **정고** (*jeonggо*) follows the same paired signalling logic and appears in descriptions of traditional Korean military formations and ceremony. Vietnamese **chinh cổ** (*chinh* from 鉦, *cổ* from 鼓) is the Sino-Vietnamese compound used in historical and literary contexts for the military percussion of classical East Asian armies.
