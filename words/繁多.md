## Notes
- [繁](../characters/繁.md) "lush; abundant; numerous" + [多 (char)](../characters/多%20(char).md) "many"

繁多 is "varied; numerous; manifold" — 繁 (lush; abundant; numerous in kind) + 多 (many; much). The two characters are near-synonyms reinforcing each other: 繁 emphasises the density and variety of things proliferating together (lush growth, complex multiplicity), while 多 is the plain quantitative word for "many." Together 繁多 names a state of abundant variety — things that are many not just in number but in type and kind. It is typically used attributively: 種類繁多 (of many varieties), 事務繁多 (numerous matters; many affairs demanding attention).

Mandarin 繁多 (fánduō) is a common written and formal word: 品種繁多 (the varieties are manifold — used of product ranges, species, or options), 事務繁多 (many matters; used to describe a busy workload). Cantonese 繁多 (faan4 do1). Japanese 繁多 (はんた, hanta) is used in a somewhat different direction: primarily "busy; numerous affairs" rather than "varied in kind" — 繁多な業務 (numerous duties), 繁多な日常 (a busy daily life). The Japanese usage tends toward "numerous and pressing" where Mandarin tends toward "many in variety." Korean 번다 (繁多, beonda): 번다한 업무 (numerous duties; heavy workload). Vietnamese phồn đa (Sino-Vietnamese: 繁=phồn, 多=đa).

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 판드 differs from Korean 번다 in both syllables. 繁 reads 판 (pan/ㄆㄚㄋ) in Dan'a'yo but 번 (beon) in Sino-Korean — MC 奉母 (v, voiced labiodental) + 桓韻 (uɑn) gives Dan'a'yo aspirated ㄆ (p-) with an -an vowel, while Sino-Korean gives unaspirated ㅂ (b-) with the -eon vowel. 多 reads 드 (dǝ/ㄉㄜ) in Dan'a'yo but 다 (da) in Sino-Korean — MC 端母 (t) + 歌韻 (ɑ) gives Dan'a'yo the schwa vowel (-ǝ, ㄜ), while Sino-Korean gives the plain -a vowel. Note that the Dan'a'yo form pandǝ is a near-homophone of the English and Korean word for the giant panda (판다, panda) — a coincidence with no linguistic significance.
