## Notes
- [陸](../characters/陸.md) "land; dry land; mainland" + [地 (char)](../characters/地%20(char).md) "ground; earth; terrain; place"

陸地 is "land; dry land; landmass" — as opposed to water or sea. 陸 (land, dry land, mainland — the terra firma of geography) + 地 (ground, earth, terrain). The compound covers the range from coastal shoreline (land as opposed to sea) to continental landmass (land as a geographic feature): 陸地面積 (land area), 陸地生態系 (terrestrial ecosystem), 上陸 (making landfall). In navigational and geographic contexts, 陸地 marks the transition from open water to land.

Mandarin 陸地 (lùdì) is the standard term for land as opposed to sea: 陸地面積 (land area), 陸地動物 (terrestrial animals), 在陸地上 (on land). Cantonese 陸地 (luk6 dei6) follows the same usage. Japanese 陸地 (りくち, rikuchi) covers the same range — land as opposed to water, the terrain of a coastline or landmass: 陸地に上がる (to come ashore). Korean 육지 (yukji) is the standard everyday word: 육지로 올라오다 (to come ashore), 육지 동물 (land animal). Vietnamese lục địa (Sino-Vietnamese, 陸地) is the standard term, often extended to mean "continent."

The Dan'a'yo Hangul transcription 룩듸 diverges from Sino-Korean 육지 in both syllables: 陸 reads 룩 (lug/ㄌㄨㄎ) vs Sino-Korean 육 (yuk); 地 reads 듸 (diǝ/ㄉㄧㄜ) vs Sino-Korean 지 (ji). The 陸 divergence preserves the lateral initial consonant (l-/ㄌ) in Dan'a'yo, whereas Sino-Korean merged l- with vowel-initial position before certain front vowels. The 地 divergence reflects different vowel inheritance from Middle Chinese. Both are characteristic features of how Dan'a'yo phonology systematically differs from Sino-Korean.
