## Notes
- [何 (char)](../characters/何%20(char).md) "what; how"

何 is "what" — the interrogative in Dan'a'yo's correlative chart, generating the question forms 何事 (what matter; what happened?), 何物 (what thing; what is it?), 何処 (where?, lit. what place), 何時 (when?, lit. what time), 何人 (which person; though 誰 is the usual form for persons), 何名 (what name; what is it called — very polite). The character combines 人 (person) as semantic component with a phonetic element; in classical Chinese 何 means "what; which; how; why" and is one of the most versatile interrogative particles in the literary canon. Dan'a'yo restricts it to the dedicated interrogative column of the correlative system. 何 also serves as the Dan'a'yo stand-in for the character [[何]].

Mandarin 何 (hé) is primarily literary: 如何 (rúhé, how; what to do), 何時 (héshí, when), 何故 (hégù, why; what reason), 任何 (rènhé, any). Modern Mandarin colloquially prefers 什么 (shénme, what) and 哪 (nǎ, which). Cantonese 何 (ho4): 為何 (wai6 ho4, why), 何況 (ho4 fong3, let alone; moreover). Japanese 何 (なに/なん, nani/nan) is the everyday interrogative: 何ですか (what is it?), 何時 (なんじ, what time; いつ, when), 何処 (どこ, where). Korean 하 (何) in Sino-Korean; native 무엇 (what), 어디 (where), 언제 (when). Vietnamese hà (何): Sino-Vietnamese literary; modern uses gì (what), đâu (where).

kwin is true: Dan'a'yo 하 exactly matches Korean 하. Both read ha/하 — MC 匣母 (ɣ) + 歌韻 (ɑ) gives a plain open-vowel syllable with a fricative onset in both Dan'a'yo and Sino-Korean, producing identical forms. 何 is one of the cleaner kwin: true cases, with no phonological divergence in either the initial or the rhyme.
