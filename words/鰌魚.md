## Notes
- [鰌](../characters/鰌.md) "loach" + [魚 (char)](../characters/魚%20(char).md) "fish" — the loach (*Misgurnus anguillicaudatus* and related species); 魚 functions as the taxon classifier making the fish identification explicit; stand-in for [[鰌]], which cannot appear independently

**鰌魚** (*qiūyú*) is the loach — a small, elongated freshwater fish of the family Cobitidae, found throughout East Asia in rice paddies, slow rivers, streams, and muddy shallow waters. The most widespread species is *Misgurnus anguillicaudatus* (泥鰌, *mud loach* or *Oriental weatherloach*), distinguished by its whisker-like barbels, slippery mucus-covered body, and tolerance for low-oxygen, turbid conditions — it can breathe atmospheric air through its intestine and survive in mud when water sources dry up.

The name **weatherloach** reflects one of the loach's most noted characteristics: its sensitivity to changes in atmospheric pressure. Before storms, barometric pressure drops and dissolved oxygen levels in water fluctuate, causing the loach to become hyperactive — swimming rapidly to the surface, writhing at the bottom, or attempting to leave the water entirely. This behaviour was widely observed and used as a folk weather predictor across East Asia; 泥鰌が騒いでいる ("the loach is stirring") was a traditional sign of coming rain.

In **Japanese cuisine**, the loach (どじょう *dojō*) is the central ingredient of **柳川鍋** (*yanagawa-nabe*), a dish in which whole loach are simmered in a dashi-and-soy broth with burdock root and finished with egg — a specialty of old Edo (Tokyo), associated with the Yanagawa-style earthenware pot (*yanagawa nabe*). The dish was a staple of the working-class neighbourhoods along the Sumida River in the Edo period, where loach were abundant in the irrigation ditches and rice paddies of the Kantō plain. どじょう restaurants (*dojō-ya*) have operated continuously in the Asakusa district of Tokyo since the Edo period; Komakata Dōjō, established around 1801 AD, is one of the oldest continuously operating restaurants in Japan.

In **Korean cuisine**, 추어탕 (*chueo-tang*, loach soup) is a hearty broth made by simmering whole loach until they dissolve into the liquid, producing a rich, thick soup seasoned with doenjang (soybean paste), perilla, and chili. The dish is particularly associated with the Jeonju and Jeolla region of southwestern Korea, where loach were abundant in the rice paddy irrigation systems, and is considered autumn fare — warming and nourishing for the harvest season. Korean 추어 (鰌魚) is the Sino-Korean compound; the native word is 미꾸라지 (*mikkuraji*).

Japanese **どじょう** (*dojō*) is the native word; the Sino-Japanese 鰌魚 (*shūgyo*) appears only in formal or historical contexts. Vietnamese **cá chạch** is the native term; the Sino-Vietnamese *tưu ngư* exists in classical texts but is not in everyday use.
