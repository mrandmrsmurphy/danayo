## Notes
- [攘](../characters/攘.md) "expel; repel; push aside" + [夷](../characters/夷.md) "barbarians; the Yi; non-Chinese peoples"

攘夷 (rǎng yí) is the political-military doctrine of repelling or expelling non-Chinese peoples (夷, "barbarians; the Yi") from the realm. As a policy concept it originates in the Spring and Autumn period, where it described the duty of hegemonic lords to defend the civilized states from Rong and Di incursions. Confucius's endorsement of Guan Zhong's success in this task (《論語·憲問》: 微管仲，吾其被髮左衽矣 — "Without Guan Zhong we would be wearing our hair unbound and our robes fastened on the left") gave 攘夷 its moral and philosophical authority.

In modern usage 攘夷 is primarily known through its role in the compound 尊王攘夷 and through Japanese history, where じょうい (jōi) was one of the two poles of the Bakumatsu political slogan そんのうじょうい (sonnō jōi, 尊王攘夷). In that context, 攘夷 meant specifically the expulsion of Western powers and the abrogation of the unequal treaties. The irony that the movement ended with Japan embracing rapid Westernization rather than expelling the West gives 攘夷 its particular historical resonance in Japanese political discourse.
