## Notes
- [感](../characters/感.md) "feel; sense" + [謝](../characters/謝.md) "thank" — `characters/謝.md`'s own `stand_in` points here, meaning 謝 does not stand independently outside this compound. `kwin: false` per the AND-rule: `characters/感.md` is `kwin: true`, `characters/謝.md` is `kwin: false` → compound `false`.
- The four languages diverge on register rather than core meaning. Mandarin 感谢 gǎnxiè and Korean 감사 gamsa are the everyday, neutral words for "thank you" (감사합니다 is the standard formal Korean thanks). Japanese 感謝 kansha, by contrast, expresses deeper or more formal gratitude (e.g. 感謝します) and sits above the far more common casual ありがとう in register — the two aren't interchangeable the way an English learner might assume. Vietnamese cảm tạ is likewise the formal/literary register; everyday spoken Vietnamese instead reaches for cảm ơn (感 + a native particle), reserving cảm tạ for writing, ceremony, or emphasis.
