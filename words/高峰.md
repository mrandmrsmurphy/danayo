## Notes
- [高 (char)](../characters/高%20(char).md) "high, tall, elevated" + [峰](../characters/峰.md) "mountain peak" — the highest point; stand-in for [[峰]], which cannot appear independently

**高峰** (*gāofēng*) is peak or summit — both the literal mountain top and the figurative apex of any trajectory. 高 (high) + 峰 (peak) is a transparent intensifying compound: the peak at its highest, the summit identified as such by the elevation it commands. As a geographic term it describes the highest point of a mountain range or a specific named peak; as a metaphorical term it covers the apex of a career, the peak of a historical period, the crest of a wave of activity.

The compound is highly productive in Chinese figurative and statistical language. **高峰期** (*gāofēngqī*, peak period) is the standard term for rush hour, peak season, or any period of maximum activity: 上班高峰期 (morning rush), 旅遊高峰期 (peak tourist season). **高峰會議** (*gāofēng huìyì*, summit meeting) — borrowed into diplomatic English as "summit" — preserves the mountaineering metaphor: world leaders meeting at the figurative apex. The English word *summit* in its diplomatic sense and the Chinese 高峰會議 developed their shared metaphor independently but converged in postwar international usage.

峰 in isolation names any mountain peak or crest-shaped form — including the crest of a waveform in physics and the peaks of a histogram — but it cannot appear as a Dan'a'yo word without a companion. 高峰 is its legitimising compound, carrying the character's core meaning of elevation and culmination into usable form. The character 峰 compounds with directional and comparative modifiers: 山峰 (shānfēng, mountain peak), 絕峰 (juéfēng, unparalleled peak), 雙峰 (shuāngfēng, twin peaks).

Korean **고봉** (*gobong*) is used in geographic naming and formal contexts; everyday Korean uses the native word **봉우리** (bong-uli, mountain peak) or **정상** (頂上, jeongsan, summit/top) in overlapping registers. Japanese **こうほう** (*kōhō*) is the Sino-Japanese compound; native Japanese uses **みね** (*mine*, mountain peak) or **いただき** (*itadaki*, summit, top) for the same terrain. Vietnamese **cao phong** (*cao* from 高, *phong* from 峰) is the Sino-Vietnamese literary compound; everyday Vietnamese uses **đỉnh núi** (mountain summit) or **đỉnh cao** (highest point) for both literal and figurative peaks.
