## Notes
- [哇 (char)](../characters/哇%20(char).md) "wow"

哇 is a 形聲字 (phono-semantic compound): the semantic component 口 (mouth) signals that the character relates to oral sounds, while 呙 serves as the phonetic. The graphic origin thus ties the character to any sound produced by the mouth — which explains its range of uses across onomatopoeia (crying, vomiting sounds) and pure exclamation.

In Modern Standard Mandarin, 哇 (wā) is the unmarked exclamation of surprise or admiration, equivalent to English *wow* or *oh!* It is also pressed into service as a sentence-final particle: Chinese phonology requires that the neutral particle 啊 assimilate to the final consonant of the preceding syllable, and after syllables ending in -u or -ao the result is 哇 rather than 啊 (e.g. 好啊 → 好哇 in the appropriate phonological context).

Cantonese expresses the same exclamatory function with its own particles (notably 喎 and 囉), so 哇 appears in Cantonese primarily as a borrowing or for emphasis in written media. In Korean and Vietnamese the character carries no living interjection use; the readings 와 and *oa/oà* reflect the historical Sino-xenic pronunciation rather than any modern exclamatory word. Japanese likewise has no active interjection use; the reading わ is the Kan-on reflex of the Middle Chinese initial.

In Dan'a'yo, 哇 stands as the 感詞 for the syllable ⺢ㄨ, serving as the vault's primary vocabulary word for expressions of wonder or surprise.
