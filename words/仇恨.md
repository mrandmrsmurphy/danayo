## Notes
- [仇](../characters/仇.md) "enemy, foe" + [恨 (char)](../characters/恨%20(char).md) "hate, grudge" — the abstract feeling of enmity itself, rather than the person who provokes it. This word is the viable form of [仇](../characters/仇.md): 仇 alone is too bound to stand as a Dan'a'yo word, and 仇恨 is its `stand_in` compound, deliberately chosen to keep 仇's sense the emotional/relational grudge, in contrast to [[敵人]] (the concrete adversary 敵 stands in for) — splitting apart what the collapsed compound [[仇敵]] had blurred into one generic "enemy."

Mandarin chóuhèn and Cantonese sau4han6 are both standard, everyday words for "hatred, enmity" — 仇恨心理 (a mentality of hatred), 化解仇恨 (to dissolve/resolve enmity). Neither Japanese nor Korean actually uses this exact compound as live vocabulary, though both readings above are the regular, mechanically-derived on'yomi/Sino-Korean transcriptions: Japanese reaches instead for native 恨み (urami, from the verb 恨む) or the more formal 憎悪 (zōo) to cover the same ground, and Korean prefers 怨恨 (원한, using 怨 "resent" rather than 仇) as its standard word for "grudge, resentment." So 仇恨 is best understood as a Mandarin/Cantonese-centered formation — the two Sinitic branches that inherited 仇 as a live, everyday character — while Japanese and Korean built the same concept from different roots entirely.
