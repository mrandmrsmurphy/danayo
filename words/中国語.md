## Notes
- [中 (char)](../characters/中%20(char).md) "middle; China" + [国](../characters/国.md) "nation" + [語](../characters/語.md) "language"

中国語 is "the Chinese language; Mandarin Chinese" — 中国 (China) + 語 (language). The compound follows the productive CJKV pattern for language names: country + 語 (as in 英語, Japanese: English; 韓国語, Japanese: Korean). 中国語 is primarily a Japanese and Korean coinage for naming the Chinese language — in Mandarin itself, 中文 or 普通話 are the common terms. The name typically refers to Mandarin (standard Chinese) in modern usage, though 中国語 encompasses all varieties of Chinese as a language family.

Japanese 中国語 (ちゅうごくご, chūgokugo) is the standard Japanese term for the Chinese language, typically meaning Mandarin. Korean 중국어 (中國語, junggugeo) is the standard Korean term. In Mandarin, 中文 (zhōngwén) is the everyday language name; 普通話 (pǔtōnghuà) specifies standard Mandarin; 漢語 (hànyǔ) is the formal linguistic term. Vietnamese Tiếng Trung Quốc (tiếng = language + Trung Quốc = China) is the standard Vietnamese term. The alias 中文 points to the overlapping Dan'a'yo word [[中文]], which emphasises the written/cultural dimension of Chinese; 中国語 emphasises language as a system of speech and communication.

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 중곡요 diverges from Korean 중국어 in both the second and third syllables. 国 reads 곡 (gog/ㄍㄛㄎ) vs Sino-Korean 국; 語 reads 요 ('yo/⼄) in Dan'a'yo vs 어 (eo) in Sino-Korean. The 語 divergence: MC initial ŋ (疑 initial) + final ɨʌ gives Dan'a'yo a vowel-quality ⼄ (romanised 'yo, written 요 in Hangul), while Sino-Korean gives 어. Both the 곡/국 and 요/어 correspondences are regular features of Dan'a'yo phonology vs Sino-Korean.
