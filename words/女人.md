## Notes
- [女 (char)](../characters/女%20(char).md) "woman" + [人 (char)](../characters/人%20(char).md) "person"

女人 is "woman" — a compound reinforcing 女 "woman" with the general noun 人 "person," distinct in register and connotation from language to language: in some it is a plain everyday word, in others a specifically literary or ritual one.

Mandarin 女人 (nǚrén) is everyday and neutral, the ordinary word for "woman" alongside 女性 (nǚxìng, more formal/clinical "female"). Cantonese 女人 (neoi5jan4, colloquially often with a changed tone jan2) patterns the same way. Japanese 女人 (にょにん, nyonin) is markedly more archaic and ritually loaded than the everyday 女 (onna) — it survives mainly in historical/religious vocabulary, most famously 女人禁制 (nyonin kinsei, "forbidden to women"), the traditional prohibition barring women from many sacred mountains and temple precincts in Japan (lifted by Meiji-era edict on most sites in 1872, though a few, including parts of Ōminesan, still observe it). Korean 녀인 (North Korean 문화어 form; South Korean 두음법칙 gives 여인) is a literary word for "woman" with no male counterpart, common in poetry and song lyrics rather than ordinary conversation — closer in register to Japanese 女人 than to Mandarin's everyday 女人. Vietnamese nữ nhân shares the same literary/formal register, with phụ nữ (built from different characters, 婦女) serving as the everyday equivalent instead.
