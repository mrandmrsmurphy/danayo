## Notes
- [四 (char)](../characters/四%20(char).md) "four"

四 is the numeral "four." The character's own page traces an unusual history: originally a pictograph of exhaling breath (the original form of 呬), later borrowed purely for its sound to write the unrelated numeral "four" — the character's shape drifted over time from four simple lines to the modern 口+八 form. Before the Western Zhou it was written as four stacked strokes (亖), later standardized to 四 specifically to avoid confusion with 二/三 in vertical writing.

Mandarin 四 (sì). Cantonese 四 (sei3). Japanese shows a real, well-known avoidance pattern: the on'yomi し (shi) is a near-homophone of 死 (shi, "death"), so the native-origin よん (yon) is often preferred instead in contexts where the ambiguity would be unwelcome — hospital rooms, hotel floors, and gift quantities frequently skip 四 for exactly this reason, alongside the parallel avoidance of 九 (ku, homophonous with 苦 "suffering"). Korean's own Sino-Korean reading is 사 (sa), which carries an identical death-taboo collision with 死 (also sa) — and per the character page's own note, **the Dan'a'yo reading was deliberately derived away from a literal 사-sounding form specifically to sidestep this taboo**, landing on 싀 (siǝ) instead of a more mechanical rendering. Vietnamese tứ is the standard Sino-Vietnamese reading, used in compounds and formal counting (tứ phương, "the four directions"), with everyday counting instead using the native bốn.

四 shares its exact Dan'a'yo reading (ㄙㄧㄜ/siǝ/싀) with two homophones: [[矢]] "arrow" and [[視]] "to look at, inspect" — three otherwise-unrelated words converging on the same Middle Chinese syllable.
