## Notes
- [室](../characters/室.md) "room" + [町](../characters/町.md) "town, district" — literally "storeroom-town," a native Japanese place name (むろ + まち) read with its own kun'yomi rather than borrowed Sino-Japanese values, the same pattern as [[鎌倉]] and [[奈良]]. It names a street and district in Kyoto where the third Ashikaga shogun, Yoshimitsu, built his residence, the Flower Palace (花の御所), in 1378.

The Muromachi period (1336–1573) takes its name from this district because the Ashikaga shogunate governed from it for most of the period's length. Ashikaga Takauji founded the shogunate in 1336 after breaking with Emperor Go-Daigo's brief restoration of imperial rule, ending the [[鎌倉]] shogunate's successor struggle known as the Kenmu Restoration; the period that followed is divided into the Nanboku-chō era (1336–1392), during which rival Northern and Southern imperial courts each claimed legitimacy, and the subsequent unified Muromachi era proper. Ashikaga rule reached its cultural and political peak under Yoshimitsu, whose patronage shaped the Kitayama culture (北山文化) that produced Kyoto's Golden Pavilion (金閣寺), and again later under Yoshimasa, whose Higashiyama culture (東山文化) gave rise to the Silver Pavilion (銀閣寺), the tea ceremony, and Noh theater in something close to their lasting classical forms.

Ashikaga authority, however, was never as centralized as Kamakura's, and from the mid-15th century it unraveled: the Ōnin War (1467–1477) — a succession dispute that devastated Kyoto — marked the effective collapse of shogunal control over the provinces and the start of the Sengoku ("Warring States") period, a century-plus of decentralized military conflict among rival daimyo that the Muromachi shogunate survived only nominally until Oda Nobunaga deposed the last shogun, Ashikaga Yoshiaki, in 1573.

Because むろまち is a native Japanese toponym rather than a Sino-Japanese learned term, Mandarin Shìtǐng, Cantonese sat1 ting2, Korean 무로마치, and Vietnamese Thất Đinh diverge from one another in the same way seen for [[新羅]]: Korean simply transliterates the Japanese sound (무로마치, exactly as 가마쿠라 transliterates Kamakura), while Mandarin, Cantonese, and Vietnamese all read the two characters with their own native Sino-readings of 室 and 町.
