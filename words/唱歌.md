## Notes
- [唱](../characters/唱.md) "chant, sing" + [歌](../characters/歌.md) "song" — stand-in for [[唱]], which cannot appear independently

Mandarin chànggē, Cantonese coeng3 go1, Japanese しょうか (shōka), and Korean 창가 (changga) are all real words for "singing," though 唱歌 has drifted to a narrower institutional sense in Japanese and Korean specifically — 唱歌/しょうか and 창가 both name a genre of pre-war school songs (Meiji-era Japan, and the Korean genre it directly inspired under Japanese cultural influence) rather than "singing" as a general everyday activity, for which both languages instead use native verbs (歌う, うたう; 노래하다). Vietnamese xướng ca is a real, richly attested Sino-Vietnamese compound with its own striking cultural history: the old feudal phrase xướng ca vô loài ("performers rank with no social class") reflected a traditional Confucian view that looked down on professional singers/entertainers — a view now considered outdated, with modern Vietnamese respecting performing artists like any other profession.
