## Notes
- [何 (char)](../characters/何%20(char).md) "what; which; interrogative" + [人 (char)](../characters/人%20(char).md) "person; people"

何人 is the direct-register interrogative person correlative — "who?" in its plainest, most confrontational form. In the Dan'a'yo correlative table, the 何-column slot of the 人 row is filled by 誰 (*syei*), the neutral and socially unmarked "who?" — but 何人 exists as the explicit compositional form, its literal "what person?" carrying an edge that 誰 lacks. The three-level person-interrogative system is the only register-differentiated paradigm in the correlative table: 何人 (rude/direct) / 誰 (neutral, the default) / 何名 (honorific-polite). A security challenge, an angry confrontation, a dramatic narrative moment — these are the contexts where 何人 belongs over 誰.

The CJKV source languages each have their own person-interrogative register gradients. Japanese 誰 (dare) in unmodified plain speech — 誰だ? ("who is it?"), お前は誰だ ("who are you?" — confrontational) — approximates the 何人 register; どなた (donata) or どなた様 map to 何名. Korean 누구 (nugu) in the plain/blunt form — 누구야? ("who is it?" — blunt) — parallels 何人; 어느 분 ("which honorable person?" — 분 = polite person classifier) parallels 何名. Cantonese 邊個 (bin1 go3, "which one? who?") is the direct everyday form. Mandarin does not stratify 誰 (shéi) by register as sharply, though 哪位 (nǎ wèi, "which esteemed person?" — 位 = honorific classifier) functions as the polite equivalent mapping to 何名.

The existence of both 誰 and 何人 illustrates a deliberate design choice: high-frequency words get phonologically compact forms for fluency, while compositionally transparent alternatives serve register or stylistic functions. 誰 is shorter and less marked; 何人 is longer, more literal, and socially sharper. The parallel with Chinese 何人 (hé rén, classical "who; what person") means 何人 is not purely a Dan'a'yo neologism — it is an attested classical CJKV compound repurposed to fill the rude end of the interrogative-person register scale.
