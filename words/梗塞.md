## Notes
- [梗](../characters/梗.md) "branch, stem; to obstruct" + [塞](../characters/塞.md) "stop up, block"

Literally "stemmed and blocked," 梗塞 describes the obstruction of a passage — metaphorically or physically. In modern medicine, it is the standard East Asian clinical term for infarction: 心肌梗塞 (xīnjī gěngsè, myocardial infarction) in Chinese, 脳梗塞 (nōkōsoku, cerebral infarction) in Japanese. The medical usage dominates across the CJKV sphere.

The Dan'a'yo phonology preserves an interesting split in the character 塞: Classical Chinese reads 塞 as sè (心+咍 → ㄙㄚㄧ in Dan'a'yo), while the colloquial Mandarin has sāi (also used in fixed expressions like 阻塞 "to obstruct"). Both readings derive from the same Middle Chinese source; Dan'a'yo uses ㄙㄚㄧ, the literary form, giving 梗塞 its reading ㄍㄚㄫㄙㄚㄧ.

In Japanese, こうそく (kōsoku) shows the regular on'yomi treatment: 梗 (KOU, 見+庚 → こう) + 塞 (SOKU, 心+沃 → そく), where the Japanese reading of 塞 draws on the entering-tone MC final. Korean 경색 (gyeongsaek) similarly preserves the stop consonant: 梗 = 경 and 塞 = 색 (from MC *sɐk with the -k coda retained in Korean).
