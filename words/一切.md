## Notes
- [一 (char)](../characters/一%20(char).md) "one" + [切 (char)](../characters/切%20(char).md) "cut"

一切 is "everything, the whole, entirety" — literally "one cut," extended to mean the totality of something, all of it without remainder. The word is closely associated with Buddhist scripture translation: it is the standard Chinese rendering of Sanskrit *sarva* ("all"), appearing constantly in Buddhist texts (一切眾生 "all sentient beings," 一切法 "all dharmas"), which spread its everyday "everything, all" sense across the wider Sinosphere. It functions as a near-synonym of [[全体]] and [[全部]], though 一切 leans more abstract/inclusive ("everything, without exception") where those two more often describe a concrete whole made of counted parts.

Mandarin 一切 (yīqiè, "everything, all"). Cantonese 一切 (jat1cai3). Japanese いっさい (issai) carries the same "everything, all" sense, but is also very commonly used adverbially with a following negative to mean "not at all, none whatsoever" (一切ない issai nai, 一切禁止 issai kinshi "strictly forbidden"). Korean shows a real semantic fork from the same root: 일체 (ilche) keeps the Chinese "everything, the whole" sense in formal/written register, while a doublet reading of the same characters, 일절 (iljeol), narrowed to an adverb used almost exclusively in negative constructions ("not at all," 일절 없다 "there is none whatsoever") — the same two-character string, two different Sino-Korean readings, two different grammatical roles. Everyday spoken Korean more often reaches for the native words 모두 or 죄다 ("all, every bit") instead of either Sino-Korean form. Vietnamese shows the most striking drift of all: nhất thiết does not mean "everything" — it has narrowed to mean "necessarily, absolutely (must)," an obligation/emphasis adverb (Giàu có không nhất thiết đồng nghĩa với hạnh phúc, "wealth doesn't necessarily mean happiness"), a long way from the Buddhist "all dharmas" sense the compound started from.
