## Notes
- [天 (char)](../characters/天%20(char).md) "sky; heaven" + [竺](../characters/竺.md) "India; bamboo" + [鼠](../characters/鼠.md) "rat; mouse"

The guinea pig (*Cavia porcellus*) — a domesticated South American rodent. The name literally means "India-rat": 天竺 (tiānzhú) was the classical Chinese name for the Indian subcontinent, derived ultimately from Sanskrit *Sindhu* (the Indus River). The geographic attribution is wrong — guinea pigs come from the Andes, not India — but this kind of exotic-place misnaming for newly arrived foreign animals was common in both East Asian and European traditions. The English name is equally inaccurate: guinea pigs are neither from Guinea nor pigs.

Guinea pigs were domesticated by Andean peoples of South America (modern Peru, Bolivia, Ecuador) around 5000 BCE, primarily as a food animal. They remain an important protein source in Andean cuisine today, where they are called **cuy**. Spanish colonizers introduced them to Europe in the 16th century, where they quickly became fashionable pets among wealthy households and indispensable subjects in early scientific experimentation.

In 19th and 20th century biomedical research, guinea pigs played critical roles: Axel Holst and Theodor Frølich used them to discover Vitamin C (1907); Robert Koch used them to develop tuberculosis diagnostics; and they were standard subjects in immunology, allergy research, and drug safety testing throughout the early modern period. From this usage comes the English idiom "guinea pig" — a person used as a test subject — though Chinese uses 小白鼠 (white mouse) for the same metaphor.

Japanese: **てんじくねずみ** (tenjiku nezumi), same kanji; also テンジクネズミ in katakana in biological contexts. A related compound: 鬼天竺鼠 (oni tenjiku nezumi, "demon guinea pig") is the Japanese name for the capybara — the world's largest rodent, which looks somewhat like a giant guinea pig.
