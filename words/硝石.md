## Notes
- [硝](../characters/硝.md) "saltpetre; nitrate" + [石 (char)](../characters/石%20(char).md) "rock; stone"

硝石 is "saltpetre; potassium nitrate" — 硝 (saltpetre; nitrate) + 石 (rock; stone). Saltpetre (KNO₃, potassium nitrate) forms naturally as a white crystalline efflorescence on cave walls, dry soils, and masonry — a mineral that grows from the earth like a slow stone, hence the "stone" component in both the Sinitic name and the European etymology (Medieval Latin salpetra, salt of stone; cf. English "peter" from Greek petros, rock). It is one of the three ingredients of gunpowder alongside charcoal and sulphur; its discovery and application in East Asia, documented from at least the 9th century Tang dynasty, predates European knowledge by several centuries. 硝石 also serves as the Dan'a'yo stand-in for [[硝]], the bound character for "saltpetre; nitrate."

Mandarin 硝石 (xiāoshí) is the historical and mineralogical term for saltpetre; modern chemical usage prefers 硝酸鉀 (xiāosuānjiǎ, potassium nitrate). Cantonese 硝石 (siu1 sek6). Japanese 硝石 (しょうせき, shōseki) is the standard historical term — essential vocabulary in Japanese military history and the history of chemistry: 硝石の産地 (saltpetre-producing regions), 硝石の精製 (refining of saltpetre). Korean 초석 (硝石, choseok): 초석을 캐다 (to mine saltpetre). Vietnamese tiêu thạch (Sino-Vietnamese: 硝=tiêu, 石=thạch). Saltpetre was a strategic material of the first order in pre-industrial warfare — states that controlled its supply held a decisive military advantage. India (under the Mughal empire) and later Chile (as sodium nitrate) were the dominant global sources, and the saltpetre trade shaped early modern European colonial expansion.

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 숏석 differs from Korean 초석 in the first syllable. 硝 reads 숏 (syou/ㄙ⼄ㄨ) in Dan'a'yo but 초 (cho) in Sino-Korean — MC 心母 (s) + 宵A韻 (iᴇu) gives Dan'a'yo ㄙ⼄ㄨ (syou), preserving both the y-medial and the -ou diphthong of the MC rhyme, while Sino-Korean palatalises the sequence to 초 (cho). The second syllable is identical: 石 reads 석 (seg/ㄙㄝㄎ) in both Dan'a'yo and Sino-Korean — MC 船母 (ʑ) + 昔開韻 (iᴇk) giving seg/석 in both systems.
