## Notes
- [被 (char)](../characters/被%20(char).md) "passive marker; -ee"

被 is the passive agent marker in Dan'a'yo — passive prefix/suffix marking the patient of an action: 被教 (be taught; the one taught), 被打 (be hit; the one struck), 被書 (the thing written). In the topic-retaining particle system, 被 marks the passive agent — the entity that undergoes the action rather than performs it — complementing [[者]] (the active agent suffix). As a prefix it voices the passive of a verb; as a suffix it derives the patient noun ("one who is -ed; -ee"). The character is a 形声 compound: semantic 衣 (clothing) + phonetic 皮 (OC *brai, skin; hide), originally meaning "a coverlet; to cover; to drape over" — the sense of "being covered; being acted upon" extended naturally to the grammatical passive. 被 also serves as the Dan'a'yo stand-in for the character [[被]].

The comparison chart: 被 parallels Mandarin 被 (bèi, passive marker), Cantonese 被 (bei6) or colloq. 畀 (bei2), Japanese れる/られる (-reru/-rareru, passive suffix), Korean 피동 (被動) expressed through morphological verb suffixes (-이/히/리/기-), Vietnamese bị (adverse passive) and được (beneficial passive). Mandarin 被 (bèi): 他被打了 (he was hit — adversative sense), 书被读完了 (the book was read through); 被 in Mandarin carries a slightly adverse/unwanted connotation. Japanese passive suffix れる/られる: 本が読まれる (the book is read), 先生に教えられる (to be taught by the teacher) — semantically neutral and highly productive. Korean passive: derivational suffixes 이/히/리/기 on verb stems — 읽다 (read) → 읽히다 (be read); Sino-Korean 피- (被) prefix in compounds: 피해 (被害, damage suffered), 피의자 (被疑者, suspect — lit. "the suspected one"), 피해자 (被害者, victim). Vietnamese bị: Anh ấy bị đánh (he was hit — bị for negative outcomes), Anh ấy được khen (he was praised — được for positive outcomes).

kwin is true: Dan'a'yo 피 exactly matches Korean 피. 被 reads 피 (pi/ㄆㄧ) in Dan'a'yo — MC 並母 (b) + 支B三開韻 (ɣiᴇ) gives a voiced bilabial stop that shifts to aspirated ㄆ (ph-) in Dan'a'yo, with the plain -i vowel. Sino-Korean 피 (pi) maps the same MC voiced stop to aspirated ㅍ (ph-) as well — both systems aspirate MC 並母 — and the same MC final to -i. The MC 支B三開韻 produced plain -i in both Dan'a'yo and Korean, and MC 並母 aspirated to ph- in both, producing identical 피 forms. This shared aspiration of MC voiced stops is a feature of both Dan'a'yo and Sino-Korean phonological development.
