## Notes
- [九 (char)](../characters/九%20(char).md) "nine" + [卿 (char)](../characters/卿%20(char).md) "noble; high officer"

A collective term for the nine highest-ranking executive officials below the Three Excellencies (三公) in imperial Chinese government. The Nine Ministers constituted the second tier of central administration, each heading a major bureau of the imperial bureaucracy. The exact composition of the nine changed across dynasties:

- In the **Zhou dynasty**, the Nine Ministers (九卿) included the Minister of Education (大司徒), Minister of War (大司馬), Minister of Works (大司空), and six others administering ritual, music, and justice.
- In the **Eastern Han**, the nine posts were formalized around functional ministries including the Superintendent of the Imperial Household (光禄勋), the Commandant of the Guards (卫尉), and the Minister of Ceremonies (太常), among others.
- Later dynasties (Sui, Tang, Ming, Qing) each defined their own grouping of nine, but the term 九卿 persisted as a prestigious shorthand for the top tier of the imperial civil service below the Chancellor.

The phrase appears in classical texts whenever referring to the assembled highest ministers of state.
