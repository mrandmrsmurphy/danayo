## Notes
- [於 (char)](../characters/於%20(char).md) "in; at; during"

於 is the locative case particle — 処格, marking the place or time frame in which an action occurs: "in; at; during." It attaches to a location or temporal noun to form the frame of an event: 學於此 (study here), 坐於椅 (sit on the chair), 生於春 (born in spring). In the comparison chart, 於 parallels Mandarin 在 (zài, at; in), Cantonese 喺 (hai2), Japanese に (ni) or で (de) for location, Korean 에/에서 (e/eseo, at; in), Vietnamese ở (at; in; to reside). Dan'a'yo distinguishes 於 (static location or temporal frame) from 由 (source, ablative) and 向 (direction, allative). 於 also serves as the Dan'a'yo stand-in for the character [[於]].

Mandarin 於 (yú): the classical locative preposition, now largely replaced by 在 (zài) in spoken Mandarin, but preserved in literary set phrases: 於是 (thereupon; and so), 至於 (as for; to the point of), 關於 (regarding; about), 對於 (toward; with regard to), 由於 (due to; because of). Cantonese 喺 (hai2) is the colloquial locative: 喺屋企 (at home), 喺度 (here; present). Japanese: の where there are two locative particles — に (ni) for existence and goals (家にいる, be at home; 学校に行く, go to school), で (de) for the location of an activity (図書館で勉強する, study in the library). Korean 에 (e) marks locations of existence and temporal frames (집에 있다, be at home; 월요일에, on Monday), while 에서 (eseo) marks the location of an activity (도서관에서 공부하다, study at the library). Vietnamese ở (to be at; to live in): ở đây (here), ở Hà Nội (in Hanoi).

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 오 differs from Korean 어. 於 reads 오 ('o/ㄛ) in Dan'a'yo — MC 影母 (ʔ) + 魚韻 (ɨʌ) gives Dan'a'yo a plain back -o vowel — while Sino-Korean gives 어 (eo), the mid-central unrounded vowel. Note that 於 (ㄛ/'o/오) is a Dan'a'yo homophone of [[五]] (ㄛ/'o/오, "five").
