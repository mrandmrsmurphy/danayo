## Notes
- [斬 (char)](../characters/斬%20(char).md) as 代用字 for 塹 "sunken trench" + 壕 "moat; protective ditch"
- 斬 carries 塹 as an alias, authorising the Dan'a'yo spelling 斬壕 in place of 塹壕. The simplified Chinese alias 堑壕 uses 堑 (simplified 塹) and the same 壕.
- Note: 壕 does not yet have its own character file; its Dan'a'yo phonology (ㄏㄚㄨ, *hau*) is derived from its Middle Chinese reconstruction /ɦɑu/ (phonetic 豪).

A 塹壕 is a long, narrow cut in the ground used as a defensive military position — soldiers fight from within it rather than in the open. The compound pairs two near-synonymous earthwork terms: 塹/斬 (a cut or sunken ditch, literally the action of cutting into the earth) and 壕 (a moat or deep protective channel). Together they name the defining military structure of the twentieth century's first industrial war.

Trench warfare entered the CJKV vocabulary primarily through the First World War and the military vocabulary that spread across East Asia during the early twentieth century. The term is standard across Chinese, Japanese (ざんごう), and Korean (참호, 塹壕), all using the same compound with identical meaning.
