## Notes
- [苹](../characters/苹.md) "apple; duckweed" + [果](../characters/果.md) "fruit" — apple-fruit.

There is no consensus on "apple" across the Sinosphere, and the situation is a lexical case study in how a Central Asian fruit diffused eastward with no common name.

In Mandarin, 苹果 (píngguǒ) is the mainland standard. The traditional-character form 蘋果 (蘋 = duckweed, borrowed for its sound) is used in Taiwan and classical writing. The character 蘋/苹 was borrowed purely phonetically — an approximation of the fruit's Sogdian or Persian name as it arrived from Central Asia along the Silk Road. The "duckweed" meaning is irrelevant; the character was requisitioned for its sound.

Japanese uses りんご (林檎, ringo) — itself a Japanisation of a Chinese term 林檎 meaning "forest quince," applied to the apple by association. The Sino-Japanese reading (ひんきん or similar) has been completely displaced; りんご is so thoroughly naturalized that it is typically written in kana alone.

Korean uses 사과 (沙果, sagwa), literally "sandy fruit" — a reference to the slightly granular texture of certain apple varieties. This form is entirely Korean in origin and bears no relation to any Chinese or Japanese term.

Vietnamese quả táo uses táo, a native word that originally referred to jujubes (棗, zǎo) before being extended to apples as the latter became common. This creates a residual ambiguity: táo can mean either apple or jujube, resolved only by context or the classifier quả.

Dan'a'yo uses 苹果 (the simplified mainland form) as the most widely circulated written representation, with the aliases listing the significant orthographic alternates across the Sinosphere.
