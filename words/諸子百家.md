## Notes
- Built from two existing words in sequence: [[諸子]] ("the various masters," the philosophers themselves) + [[百家]] ("the hundred schools," the traditions they founded) — "the various masters of the hundred schools."

諸子百家 is the standard collective name for the explosion of independent philosophical and political thought in China during the Spring and Autumn and Warring States periods (c. 770–221 BC), when the weakening of Zhou royal authority let regional courts and travelling scholars compete openly for influence. "Hundred" is hyperbolic — traditional bibliographers such as the Han-dynasty *Book of Han* (漢書·藝文志) actually enumerate ten major lineages — but the schools that mattered most for later history were Confucianism (儒家, Confucius, Mencius), Daoism (道家, Laozi, Zhuangzi), Legalism ([[法家]], Han Feizi, Li Si, Shang Yang), Mohism (墨家, Mozi), and the School of Names (名家). Their open contention for the ear of rulers and students was itself given a name, 百家爭鳴 ("the hundred schools contend"), now used generally for any period or environment of vigorous, pluralistic intellectual debate.

The end of this pluralism came abruptly: after unifying China in 221 BC, the Qin state under Legalist influence moved to suppress rival schools, culminating in the (likely partly legendary, but symbolically decisive) burning of books and burying of scholars traditionally dated to 213–212 BC. Confucianism's later revival and eventual establishment as state orthodoxy under the Han dynasty did not erase the other schools' influence — Legalist administrative technique and Daoist cosmology, among others, persisted as undercurrents within nominally Confucian statecraft for the rest of imperial Chinese history.

Vietnamese conventionally reverses the word order to Bách Gia Chư Tử (百家諸子, "the hundred schools' various masters") rather than calque the Chư Tử Bách Gia order used here to match the Dan'a'yo characters; both orders are attested, but the reversed form is by far the more common in Vietnamese-language reference works.
