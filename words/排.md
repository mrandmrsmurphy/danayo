## Notes
- [排 (char)](../characters/排%20(char).md) "row; rank; line; to arrange in a row"

排 is "row; rank; line" — a linear arrangement of people or things placed in sequence. The character is 形声 (OC \*brɯːl): 扌 (hand) + phonetic 非 (OC \*pɯl). The hand radical grounds the word in the physical act of arranging — pushing and spreading things into line. In Dan'a'yo as a 名詞, 排 names the row or rank itself: the ordered sequence as an entity. The word also operates freely as a verbal morpheme in compounds: 排列 (to arrange in sequence), 排斥 (to reject; exclude), 排除 (to remove; eliminate), 排球 (volleyball — literally "net-row ball," named for the players lined up across the net).

Mandarin 排 (pái) is widely used as both noun (一排 yī pái, a row of) and verb (排列 to arrange, 排斥 to reject, 排放 to emit/discharge). Cantonese 排 (paai4): 一排 (jat1 paai4, a row of); 排骨 (paai4 gwat1, pork ribs — the meat arranged along the rib rack). Japanese 排 (ハイ, hai) appears primarily as a bound morpheme in formal compounds: 排除 (はいじょ, haijo — exclusion), 排斥 (はいせき, haiseki — rejection), 排泄 (はいせつ, haisetsu — excretion). Korean 배 (排, bae): 배열 (排列, arrangement), 배제 (排除, exclusion), 배구 (排球, volleyball). Vietnamese bài is the standard Sino-Vietnamese reading, appearing in compound terms borrowed from Chinese.

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 패 (pai/ㄆㄚㄧ) differs from Korean 배 in the initial consonant. MC 並 (voiced labial stop, b-) gives Dan'a'yo aspirated ㄆ (p-), yielding 패, while Sino-Korean gives unaspirated ㅂ (b-), yielding 배. This p-/b- correspondence from MC 並 reflects a systematic divergence: Dan'a'yo aspirates MC voiced stops (following the same development as Mandarin, where 並 → p-), while Sino-Korean preserves them as lax unaspirated stops (ㅂ/ㄷ/ㄱ). The same pattern appears in other MC 並-initial characters across the lexicon.
