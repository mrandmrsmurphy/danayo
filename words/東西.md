## Notes
- [東](../characters/東.md) "east" + [西](../characters/西.md) "west" — neither character's own `stand_in` points here (東's own points to `東方`, 西's own to `西方`); 東西 is an independent compound. `kwin: false` per the AND-rule: `characters/東.md` is `kwin: true`, `characters/西.md` is `kwin: false` → compound `false`.
- **A genuine and important tone-based heteronym, documented rather than forced into one gloss**: in Mandarin, the neutral-tone reading dōngxi (as stored here) has grammaticalized into one of the most common everyday nouns in the language, meaning "thing, object, stuff" (买东西 "to go shopping," 什么东西 "what thing/what is this"). The full-tone reading dōngxī keeps the literal, compositional sense "east and west" (东西方 "East and West/Occident and Orient"). Japanese とうざい, Korean 동서, and Cantonese dung1 sai1 all preserve only the literal "east and west" sense — none of them extended it to mean "thing" the way colloquial Mandarin did (Japanese uses 物/もの, Korean 것, and Cantonese 嘢/je5 for "thing" instead).
