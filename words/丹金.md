## Notes
**Form.** 丹金 is 丹 + 金 “metal,” following the rule for transition elements.

**Etymological chain.** The international name _hafnium_ derives from Latin _Hafnia_ “Copenhagen.” The city name itself is opaque in CJKV transcription (哥本哈根), so a direct one-character reduction is not viable. Our solution moves one level up:

- Copenhagen → Denmark → 丹麥
- 丹 (retained as symbol) + 金

This is a **two-step toponymic reduction**, analogous to other place-based names but with an intermediate substitution.

**Motivation.**

- 丹 is the conventional Sinitic abbreviation for Denmark (丹麥).
- This preserves the **national association** of the naming (the element was discovered in Copenhagen, Denmark).
- It avoids the unusable multi-character transcription 哥本哈根.

**Type of formation.**  
A **secondary toponymic abbreviation**:

- original referent: city (Copenhagen)
- mapped to: country (Denmark)
- reduced to: 丹
- suffixed: 金

This is slightly indirect but still faithful to the historical naming context.

**Fit within the system.**

1. Maintains meaning-based naming without phonetic borrowing.
2. Preserves a clear geographic association.
3. Provides a simple, high-frequency, unique graph for symbol use.
4. Uses 金 appropriately for a d-block element.

**Comparative CJKV forms.**

- Modern Chinese: 铪 (phonetic)
- Japanese: ハフニウム (phonetic)
- Korean: 하프늄 (phonetic)
- Vietnamese: hafni (phonetic)

Modern forms are phonetic; 丹金 restores a graph-based geographic reference.

**Clarity and issues.**

- 丹 commonly means “cinnabar; red,” which is unrelated semantically to the element.
- However, as with 法 (France) or 波 (Poland), the toponymic usage is conventional and acceptable once fixed.
- The two-step mapping is slightly less transparent but still systematic.

**Conclusion.**  
丹金 is a pragmatic and internally consistent solution to an otherwise unusable place-name etymology. The shift from Copenhagen to Denmark is justified by the need for a one-character symbol and remains faithful to the historical naming context.
