## Notes
- [高 (char)](../characters/高%20(char).md) "high" + [句 (char)](../characters/句%20(char).md) "phrase" + [麗](../characters/麗.md) "beautiful" — characters chosen for sound rather than sense, in the standard manner of transcribing a non-Chinese place or polity name; Goguryeo's own name almost certainly predates this Sinitic spelling.

Goguryeo was the northernmost of the [[三国|Three Kingdoms]] of Korea, traditionally founded in 37 BC in the Yalu River basin and lasting until its conquest in 668 AD. At its height in the 5th–6th centuries it controlled not only the northern half of the Korean peninsula but also large stretches of Manchuria, making it as much a Manchurian power as a Korean one — a fact that underlies a long-running, politically charged dispute between Korean and Chinese historiography over whether Goguryeo belongs to Korean or Chinese national history. Its relationship with the Chinese mainland was defined by recurring conflict: Goguryeo absorbed and eventually extinguished the Han-era Chinese commandery of Lelang (樂浪郡) in the early 4th century, and several centuries later repelled massive invasions by the Sui dynasty — the Goguryeo–Sui War (598–614) is often cited among the contributing causes of the Sui dynasty's collapse, given the catastrophic losses inflicted on Sui armies at battles such as Salsu.

To its south, Goguryeo contested the peninsula with [[百済]] (Baekje) and [[新羅]] (Silla), the other two of the Three Kingdoms. This balance collapsed in the 7th century when Silla allied with Tang China against its two rivals: Baekje fell in 660, and Goguryeo — already weakened by decades of war with the Tang following the Sui debacle — fell in 668, its territory divided between Tang military administration and the new Tang vassal state of Lesser Goguryeo, with the bulk of the old Goguryeo elite eventually contributing to the rise of [[渤海]] (Balhae) to the north. Silla's victory marked the beginning of Unified Silla (統一新羅, [[統一]] + [[新羅]]) in the south.

The mandarin reading of this name is irregular: 句 is read jù in ordinary vocabulary but gōu specifically in 高句麗 and related historical terms, an exceptional reading preserved only in this proper noun. The Dan'a'yo pronunciation gaugule does not follow this exception — it is built by straightforward concatenation of each character's ordinary Dan'a'yo bound-form reading, per the convention used for all compound words.
