## Notes
**魔銅** is a binomial neologism for the chemical element **Nickel** (Atomic Number: 28). It serves as a specialized semantic calque of the German term **[Kupfernickel](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/nickel#Etymology)**, distinguishing the "demon" of the copper mines from the "goblin" of the silver mines.

### 1. The Etymological Distinction: Kobold vs. Nick

While both Cobalt and Nickel derive from malevolent spirits, the specific entities in the German mining tradition are distinct. The choice of **魔** (Mó) for Nickel creates a necessary linguistic separation:

- **The "Nick":** The name **[Nickel](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/nickel#Etymology)** was a diminutive for **Nikolaus**, often used to refer to a [nix, water-spirit, or a specific type of malicious demon](https://www.google.com/search?q=https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nickel_\(folklore\)) that haunted the mines.
- **The "魔" Logic:** By using **魔** (Magic/Demon/Mara), we assign Nickel a "heavier" or more "occult" descriptor than the **鬼** (Ghost/Goblin) used for Cobalt. In many CJKV contexts, **魔** implies a higher level of deceptive power or enchantment, fitting for a metal that so convincingly mimicked copper.

### 2. CJKV Linguistic Resonance
- **Phonetic Echo:** The character **魔** (Mandarin: _mó_; Japanese: _ma_; Korean: _ma_; Vietnamese: _ma_) provides a soft "M" sound. While this doesn't mimic the "N" of Nickel, it provides a strong, memorable "M" for **M**etal or **M**agic.
- **Semantic Depth:** **魔** is universally understood across the Sinosphere as something supernatural and transformative. It captures the "trickster" nature of the [Kupfernickel](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/nickel#Etymology) ore—the copper that wasn't copper.
