## Notes
- [若 (char)](../characters/若%20(char).md) "if; supposing; like; as if"

若 is the conditional linker in Dan'a'yo — "if; supposing that; in the event that" — introducing the protasis of a conditional: 若A，則B ("if A, then B"). Unlike [[雖]] (concessive: "even though A, still B"), 若 signals that the outcome in B depends on A obtaining; the consequent is contingent rather than guaranteed despite. In Classical Chinese 若 is the canonical conditional particle, appearing throughout the canon alongside 如 (rú, "if; like") and 設 (shè, "suppose that"). 若 also functions as a similative ("like; as if") in some classical constructions — conditional and similative senses share a common root in hypothetical identification: "if X were the case; as if X." The natural Vietnamese equivalent nhược (若) — used in nhược bằng ("if; in case") and nhược điểm ("weakness; shortcoming," lit. "if-point") — shows 若 surviving in Sino-Vietnamese compounds.

若 (ㄋ⼘/nya/냐 — Mandarin ruò, Cantonese joek6, Korean 야, Japanese JAKU/じゃく, Vietnamese nhược): "if; like; as if" — MC 日母 (ȵ) + 藥韻 (ia). The character 若 depicts a kneeling figure with flowing hair, phonetically borrowed for the conditional/similative particle with no inherent connection to the graphic components. MC 日母 (ȵ, palatal nasal) is preserved as ㄋ (n-) in Dan'a'yo — the same retention as [[而]] (ㄋㄧ), [[汝]] (ㄋㄛㄧ) — with MC 藥韻 (ia) giving the medial -ya- (ㄋ⼘ = n-ya). Mandarin ruò reflects 日母 → r- (retroflex) and 藥韻 → -uo. Cantonese joek6 drops the initial and preserves the entering-tone -k coda. Japanese じゃく (JAKU) reflects 日母 → j- with 藥韻 → -aku; the native equivalent もし (moshi) is the everyday conditional. Korean 야 drops the initial entirely (null onset) with -ya from the rhyme. Vietnamese nhược preserves the palatal nasal as nh- and gives -ược from the rhyme.

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 냐 does not match Korean 야. The initial diverges: Dan'a'yo retains MC 日母 as ㄋ (n-냐), while Korean drops it entirely (야, null onset). The vowel partially aligns — both show -ya- from the 藥韻 — but the initial consonant split is decisive. This is the same 日母 retention pattern seen across [[而]] (Dan'a'yo 니 vs Korean 이), [[汝]] (Dan'a'yo 뇌 vs Korean 여), and [[故而]] (Dan'a'yo 고니 vs Korean 고이): Dan'a'yo consistently preserves MC ȵ as ㄋ, Korean consistently drops it.
