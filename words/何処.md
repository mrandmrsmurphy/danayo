## Notes
- [何 (char)](../characters/何%20(char).md) "what; which; interrogative" + [処 (char)](../characters/処%20(char).md) "place; location"

何処 is the interrogative place correlative — "where?" — and is one of the most cross-linguistically grounded entries in the entire correlative table. In Japanese, 何処 is written with these exact two characters and read どこ (doko) — "where" in ordinary everyday speech. The Dan'a'yo reading *haco* differs from the Japanese reading, but the grapheme is identical, making 何処 one of the few correlatives that is not purely a Dan'a'yo coinage but overlaps directly with an existing Japanese orthographic compound.

Mandarin 哪裡 (nǎlǐ, "which-interior → where?") and the Beijing colloquial 哪兒 (nǎr, with r-suffix — listed as an alias) are the standard equivalents. Cantonese uses 邊度 (bin1 dou6, "which place?") colloquially. Korean 어디 (eodi, "where") is the standard interrogative place word; Vietnamese đâu (where?) is monosyllabic and highly frequent. All five languages have a dedicated "where" pronoun, confirming 何処's Swadesh list membership (entry 13) and its status as universal basic vocabulary.

Unlike most 何X correlatives (which are Dan'a'yo neologisms or classical Chinese compounds), 何処/どこ (doko) is in active everyday use in modern Japanese — not a literary or formal form but the standard spoken word. This means Japanese speakers encounter 何処 as an entirely familiar compound with a different reading (*haco* in Dan'a'yo vs *doko* in Japanese), analogous to the way ateji readings work: same characters, different phonology, same meaning. The alias 哪兒 marks the Beijing r-suffix colloquial form that is less formal than 哪裡 but equally standard in northern Mandarin.
