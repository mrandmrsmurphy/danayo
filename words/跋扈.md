## Notes
- 跋 "to trample, tread recklessly" + [扈](../characters/扈.md) "retinue, escort; insolently follow"

跋扈 describes power exercised without restraint or deference — the conduct of someone who acts as though no authority stands above them. The etymology is physical: 跋 (to trample, stride over) combined with 扈 (to follow in retinue, but here with the sense of overwhelming presence) produces an image of someone who barges through, trampling everything underfoot.

The phrase is associated with its most famous historical instance: the regent Liang Ji (梁冀) of the Later Han, described in the 後漢書 as 跋扈將軍 ("the domineering general"). Liang Ji dominated the Han court for over two decades, poisoning one emperor who displeased him and installing puppet rulers. His eventual downfall in 159 AD remains one of the most dramatic falls from power in Han history, and 跋扈 has ever since carried his shadow — the image of court power grown cancerous, unchecked by loyalty or law.

In modern Japanese (ばっこ, bakko), 跋扈 is a living literary word used in journalism and historical writing to describe villains, warlords, or interests that run amok within an institution. It appears frequently in the phrase 悪が跋扈する ("evil runs rampant").
