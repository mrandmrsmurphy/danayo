## Notes
- [白 (char)](../characters/白%20(char).md) "white" + [亜](../characters/亜.md) "chalk" (Dan'a'yo stand-in for 堊)

In Dan'a'yo, 亜 serves as the graphemic and phonetic stand-in for 堊 (chalk, white earth), which shares the same MC initial and final; the alias 白堊 covers the traditional Chinese spelling. The Mandarin reading báiè and the Korean 백악 use 堊's readings rather than 亜's (yà / 아), reflecting the concept rather than the substitute character.

白堊 / 白亜 is the East Asian geological and mineralogical term for chalk — the soft white sedimentary rock composed primarily of calcium carbonate (CaCO₃), formed from the compacted remains of marine microorganisms. The English word "chalk" and the name of the Cretaceous Period (from Latin *creta*, chalk) both refer to the chalky limestone deposits that characterize that era's rock record in Europe, particularly the famous White Cliffs of Dover (白亜の断崖 in Japanese). In Japanese (はくあ, hakua), the compound is used in both mineralogical and everyday contexts (chalk for blackboards being 白墨 or チョーク in modern Japanese, while 白亜 retains the geological and poetic sense). In Korean (백악), 백악 also appears as a place name: 백악산 (白岳山) is the mountain behind Gyeongbokgung Palace in Seoul, named for its white rock outcroppings.
