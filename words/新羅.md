## Notes
- [新 (char)](../characters/新%20(char).md) "new" + [羅](../characters/羅.md) "Rome; net" — a Sinitic transcription chosen for sound, not sense; the name itself originates as a transcription of the kingdom's native Korean name. Silla's own historical records describe several earlier names — including 斯盧 (Saro) and 斯羅 (Sara) — before 新羅 was fixed as the official name in 503 AD under King Jijeung.

Silla was the southeasternmost of the [[三国|Three Kingdoms]] of Korea, traditionally founded in 57 BC around the Gyeongju basin and the last of the three to fall under unified rule, surviving until 935 AD. For most of the Three Kingdoms period Silla was the weakest of the three, frequently pressured by both [[高句麗]] (Goguryeo) to the north and 百済 (Baekje) to the west, and it leaned on alliances with both Goguryeo and, later, Tang China for protection. That Tang alliance proved decisive: in the 660s, Silla and Tang forces conquered Baekje (660) and then Goguryeo (668), after which Silla turned on its erstwhile ally and expelled Tang forces from the peninsula by 676, unifying the Three Kingdoms under Silla rule as [[統一新羅]] (Unified Silla).

Silla's capital at Gyeongju (慶州) — known in the period as Geumseong (金城) — became one of the great cities of East Asia under unification, and the kingdom's adoption of Tang-style bureaucratic institutions, Buddhism, and the bone-rank (骨品制) aristocratic system shaped Korean political culture well beyond Silla's own collapse in 935, when the last king ceded the throne to [[高麗]] (Goryeo).

The Japanese reading しらぎ (shiragi) is the conventional historical reading used in Japanese historiography and education, distinct from the regular Sino-Japanese on'yomi concatenation しんら (shinra, しん + ら) that the characters would otherwise produce — a divergence of the same kind seen in [[高句麗]]'s irregular Mandarin reading, where a name's established historical pronunciation departs from the mechanical reading of its component characters.
