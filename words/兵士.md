## Notes
- [兵](../characters/兵.md) "soldier; weapon; military" + [士 (char)](../characters/士%20(char).md) "man of standing; warrior; professional"

兵士 is "soldier; military personnel" — a compound fusing two classical terms for fighters. 兵 covers the material and institutional dimension: troops as a mass, armies, weapons, the machinery of war. 士 covers the individual person's standing: a trained, ranked, or professional fighter — the warrior-scholar who has earned a place in a hierarchy. Together, 兵士 refers to a soldier as an individual person rather than a unit or a force, with a slight formality and dignity conferred by 士.

Mandarin 兵士 (bīngshì) is a slightly literary "soldier" — more common in formal or historical prose than the everyday 士兵 (shìbīng, the same compound reversed) or 軍人 (jūnrén, military person, civilian-facing). Japanese 兵士 (へいし, heishi) is the standard word for individual soldier in both historical and modern contexts, distinct from 軍人 (gunjin, serviceman) or 兵隊 (heitai, troops/soldiers as a collective). Korean 병사 (byeongsa) is the standard word for enlisted soldier. Vietnamese binh sĩ is the Sino-Vietnamese compound, used in military contexts.

The Dan'a'yo 諺文 transcription 병새 concatenates the character-level readings 兵=병 (byeng) and 士=새 (sai), giving 병새 — which differs from the Sino-Korean reading of 兵士 as 병사 (byeongsa). This is a routine feature of Dan'a'yo: the Hangul transcription reflects Dan'a'yo phonology (built from individual character readings), not the Sino-Korean readings of the same characters, since both systems draw from Middle Chinese through divergent phonological paths. The kwin is therefore false even though the characters are the same.
