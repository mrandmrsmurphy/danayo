## Notes
- [梓](../characters/梓.md) "catalpa tree" + [木 (char)](../characters/木%20(char).md) "wood; tree" — stand-in for [[梓]], which cannot appear independently
- 梓木 names the timber of the catalpa (キササゲ, Catalpa ovata), one of the most culturally esteemed trees of premodern East Asia: it was called 木王, "king of the woods," and its even, warp-resistant grain made it the wood of choice for printing blocks, so that idioms like 上梓 and 梓行 came to mean "to publish" by metonymy. Paired with 桑 (mulberry) in the old phrase 桑梓, the catalpa also stood for one's native village, since both trees were traditionally planted around the family home. The word is read しぼく (shiboku) in Japanese and 재목 (jaemok) in Sino-Korean — the latter a curious homophone of the everyday word 材木 "lumber," though written with an entirely different first character.
