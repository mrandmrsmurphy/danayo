## Notes
- [親](../characters/親.md) "parent" + [子](../characters/子.md) "child" + [井](../characters/井.md) "bowl (via 丼)"

The name is a deliberate culinary pun: 親 ("parent") and 子 ("child") appear together in the bowl in the form of chicken meat — the parent bird — and egg — the child. This play on biological kinship is what makes 親子井 one of the most memorable compound names in Japanese food vocabulary.

The dish was first served at the Tamahide (玉ひで) restaurant in Ningyōchō, Tokyo, in 1891, and that establishment still trades today. In the standard recipe, sliced chicken thigh and onion are simmered in a dashi-soy broth, then partially set egg is poured over the top and served over steamed rice.

The Japanese reading おやこどん is entirely native: 親 takes its kun-yomi おや ("parent"), 子 takes its kun-yomi こ ("child"), and 丼/井 is read as どん, the contracted form of どんぶり. None of the three components use their Sino-Japanese on-yomi. This is not the regular concatenation of Dan'a'yo component readings, which would give *cin + jǝ + jing*.

In simplified Chinese orthography, 親 is written 亲, giving the alias 亲子丼.
