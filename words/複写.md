## Notes
- [複](../characters/複.md) "duplicate; repeat" + [写](../characters/写.md) "copy; reproduce" — a reproduced copy of a document or image; stand-in for [[写]], which cannot appear independently

**複写** (*fùxiě*) is the compound for a reproduced copy — any document or image duplicated from an original. The term spans two technological eras: the pre-electronic age of **carbon paper** (複写紙, *fukushashi*), in which pressure on the top sheet transferred ink to sheets beneath, producing simultaneous copies; and the modern era of **photocopying** (電子複写), in which optical and electrostatic processes reproduce documents without mechanical contact.

Carbon paper copying was the dominant form of office duplication from the late 19th century through the mid-20th. The word 複写 in older Japanese and Chinese business contexts almost invariably refers to this carbon-copy process — the triplicate form, the receipt book with its flimsy interleaved sheets, the carbon smudge on the clerk's fingers. Chester Carlson invented xerography (乾式複写) in 1938 AD, and the Haloid Company (later Xerox) commercialised the first plain-paper photocopier in 1959 AD, gradually displacing the carbon copy across the following decade.

In modern Japanese, **複写** (*fukusha*) coexists with the loanword コピー (*kopī*, from English "copy") for photocopying. 複写 is the more formal and technical term — used in legal documents, library contexts, and technical specifications — while コピー dominates everyday speech. 複写機 (photocopier) and 複写物 (a reproduced copy, a duplicate) are standard compounds. The distinction from **複製** (*fukusei*, "reproduction, replica") is one of scope: 複写 applies to document duplication; 複製 extends to reproductions of artworks, recordings, and software.

In **Korean**, 복사 (*boksa*) is the standard everyday term for both "photocopy" (noun) and "to photocopy" (verb), and is also used for the computing sense of "copy" (파일 복사, file copy). **Vietnamese** *phức tả* is the Sino-Vietnamese compound; in practice, Vietnamese uses *bản sao* ("copy, duplicate") or the loanword *photocopy* in everyday contexts.
