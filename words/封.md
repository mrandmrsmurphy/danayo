## Notes
- [封 (char)](../characters/封%20(char).md) "seal, close; enfeoff"

封 carries two principal senses that are etymologically connected. The older sense is feudal: to enfeoff — to grant a piece of land together with authority over it, formally investing someone as a lord over a territory. The Zhou dynasty built its political order on this system of 封建 (fēngjiàn, "enfeoffment and establishment"), from which the modern word 封建制度 (feudalism) derives. The newer but now more common sense is physical: to seal or close — to close a letter, to seal a container, to block a passage. Both senses share the image of a boundary established by authority: territory bounded by royal grant, an envelope bounded by wax or glue.

In Chinese, 封 also functions as a counter (量詞) for letters: 一封信 (yī fēng xìn, "one letter"). In Japanese (ふう, fū), the sealing sense is dominant: 封をする (to seal), 封筒 (fūtō, envelope), 開封 (kaifū, to unseal/open). In Vietnamese, phong covers both the feudal grant sense (phong tước, to confer a title) and the sealing sense.
