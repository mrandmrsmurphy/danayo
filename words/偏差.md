## Notes
- [偏 (char)](../characters/偏%20(char).md) "slanted, inclined, biased" + [差](../characters/差.md) "difference, discrepancy, error" — a biased difference; a gap skewed to one side.

偏差 covers two related senses: statistical deviation (the distance of a data point from a reference value) and systematic bias (a consistent skew in measurement or judgement). The two senses are logically related — bias is a systematic non-random deviation — but in usage they pull in different directions: statistical 偏差 is morally neutral, a fact about distributions; cognitive or political 偏差 carries a critical charge, implying error or distortion.

The compound's most culturally embedded use in East Asia is the Japanese 偏差値 (hensachi, "deviation value") — the score used in high school and university entrance exam ranking. 偏差値 is a standardized measure: a score of 50 means exactly average; each 10 points represents one standard deviation above or below the mean. Japanese schools and universities are routinely described by their 偏差値 (a top university might have 偏差値 70; a standard school, 50). The system is deeply woven into Japanese educational culture — students speak casually of their own 偏差値 as a proxy for academic standing, and cram schools (予備校) are organised around raising it. The word has no precise equivalent in other languages: it is a Japanese-specific institutional usage of an otherwise standard statistical term.

Korean 편차 (偏差) is used in statistics and in the phrase 표준편차 (標準偏差, standard deviation), the direct cognate of 標準偏差 (hyōjun hensa) in Japanese.
