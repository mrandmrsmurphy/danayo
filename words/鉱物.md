## Notes
- [鉱](../characters/鉱.md) "ore; mineral deposit; mining" + [物 (char)](../characters/物%20(char).md) "thing; substance; object"

鉱物 is "mineral" — naturally occurring inorganic substances with a defined chemical composition and crystalline structure. The compound is geological: 鉱 (ore, mineral deposit, the substance extracted by mining) + 物 (thing, substance, material). Together they denote "ore-substance" or "mineral material" — covering the full spectrum of mineralogy from gemstones and metals to silicates, sulfides, and carbonates. In scientific and geological contexts, 鉱物 is the standard technical term.

Mandarin 礦物 (kuàngwù; traditional 礦, simplified 矿) is the scientific standard: 礦物學 (mineralogy), 礦物資源 (mineral resources), 礦物質 (mineral content/nutrients). Cantonese 礦物 (kwong3 mat6) follows the same form. Japanese 鉱物 (こうぶつ, kōbutsu) is the standard scientific term: 鉱物学 (mineralogy), 鉱物資源 (mineral resources), 鉱物油 (mineral oil). Korean 광물 (gwangmul) is the standard: 광물 자원 (mineral resources), 광물학 (mineralogy). Vietnamese khoáng vật is the Sino-Vietnamese equivalent.

A correction from the original file: kwin is false for 鉱物, not true. The Dan'a'yo Hangul 광묻 differs from Sino-Korean 광물 in the second syllable: 物 in Dan'a'yo reads 묻 (mud, ㄇㄨㄊ), while the Sino-Korean reading of 物 is 물 (mul). Dan'a'yo preserves the Middle Chinese final stop consonant *-t (reflected in the ㄊ of ㄇㄨㄊ) that Sino-Korean has dropped, giving 묻 rather than 물. This 묻/물 divergence is systematic across all words containing 物 in Dan'a'yo — see the 物-row correlatives (此物, 其物, 彼物, etc.) where the same phonological split appears.
