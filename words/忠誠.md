## Notes
- [忠](../characters/忠.md) "loyalty; devotion; fidelity" + [誠](../characters/誠.md) "sincerity; honesty"

忠誠 pairs the two cardinal Confucian virtues of interpersonal reliability. 忠 — graphemically 中 over 心, "the centre of the heart" — names the wholehearted devotion that a subject or minister owes to a ruler, and that any person owes to those in whose care they have placed themselves. 誠 — phonetic 成 over 言, "speech that fulfils what it promises" — is sincerity, the virtue of being genuinely what one represents oneself to be. Together 忠誠 describes loyalty that is not merely performative but grounded in authentic feeling: the opposite of flattery or calculated compliance.

The compound is central to East Asian political ethics. In the Analects, Zhengzi lists 忠 among the virtues he daily examines in himself; Mencius develops 誠 as the virtue linking heaven and humanity. In premodern Korea, 忠誠 carried particularly high political salience: 충성 (chungseong) was the defining virtue of the yangban official and the retainer, and its violation — disloyalty — was among the gravest social failures. In the modern South Korean military, 충성! functions as a salute and acknowledgment — spoken in response to commands — making it one of the few Sino-Korean compounds in everyday military idiom. In Japanese, ちゅうせい (chūsei) appears in formal and literary contexts but is less embedded in contemporary military culture than in Korea.

The kwin=false marking reflects the divergence between Dan'a'yo 퉁싱 (tung + sing, concatenated from the sinographic readings) and Korean 충성 (chungseong), where 忠 maps to 충 and 誠 maps to 성 — both vowels differ systematically from the Dan'a'yo forms.
