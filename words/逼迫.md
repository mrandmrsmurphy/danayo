## Notes
- [逼](../characters/逼.md) "press; close in; threaten" + [迫](../characters/迫.md) "force; compel; urgency" — stand-in for [[逼]], which cannot appear independently in Dan'a'yo.

逼迫 combines two near-synonymous characters of pressure and compulsion into a single, intensified sense: to force or coerce someone, typically through sustained pressure, threat, or urgency rather than outright physical violence. 逼 contributes the imagery of closing in or hemming in — pressing toward someone until they have no room to refuse — while 迫 contributes urgency and compulsion, the sense of something that cannot be delayed or avoided. Together they describe coercion that operates through pressure rather than force in the narrowly physical sense.

The compound is attested across the region with related but distinct shades of meaning. In Mandarin (bīpò) and Cantonese (bik1 baak1), it means straightforwardly "to force, coerce, compel." In Korean, 핍박 (pibak) has narrowed toward "persecution, oppression" — particularly of a group over time (종교 핍박, "religious persecution") — rather than a single act of coercion. Japanese 逼迫 (ひっぱく) has drifted further still, from coercion of a person to the *state* of being under pressure, especially financial or situational strain (財政が逼迫する, "finances become strained"). Vietnamese bức bách preserves the original coercive sense, "pressing, urgent, oppressive." This semantic spread across Mandarin/Cantonese/Vietnamese (coercion of a person), Korean (persecution), and Japanese (a state of strain) reflects a single etymon diverging along different metaphorical paths in each daughter tradition, while Dan'a'yo's own sense follows the more conservative Mandarin/Cantonese/Vietnamese core: to force or compel through pressure.
