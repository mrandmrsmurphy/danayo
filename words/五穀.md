## Notes
- [五 (char)](../characters/五%20(char).md) "five" + [穀](../characters/穀.md) "cereal; grain"

The five staple food crops that formed the agricultural foundation of traditional East Asian civilization. The precise identity of the five grains was disputed even in antiquity — different classical texts give different lists, reflecting regional cultivation patterns — but two groupings are most common:

**Northern tradition** (emphasizing dry crops):
稷 broomcorn millet · 黍 panicled millet · 麥 wheat/barley · 菽 soybeans · 麻 hemp (seeds)

**Southern tradition** (including rice):
稻 rice · 稷 broomcorn millet · 黍 panicled millet · 麥 wheat/barley · 菽 soybeans

The concept of the Five Grains appears in the *Rites of Zhou* (周禮) and the *Book of Documents* (尚書) and was central to imperial ritual agriculture. The Emperor was required to perform the **Plowing Ceremony** (籍田禮) each spring — personally driving a plow through a ceremonial field to symbolize imperial responsibility for the harvest and divine mandate over agricultural prosperity. Failure of the harvest could be read as a sign that the Emperor had lost the Mandate of Heaven (天命).

The phrase **五穀豐登** (wǔgǔ fēngdēng, "the Five Grains ripen abundantly") is among the most common auspicious expressions in Chinese culture, appearing on New Year scrolls, ceremonial objects, and as a standard blessing for farmers and households. 五穀 remains the standard modern Chinese term for "grains and cereals" in compound words like 五穀雜糧 (multigrain foods), now widely used in nutrition marketing. In Korean, 오곡밥 (five-grain rice) is a traditional dish eaten on the first full moon of the lunar new year.
