## Notes
- [七 (char)](../characters/七%20(char).md) "seven"

七 is the numeral "seven." The character is 象形 (pictographic): a vertical stroke bisected by a horizontal stroke — the original form later gave rise to [[切]] ("to cut," Old Chinese *sn̥ʰiːd), since 七's shape was reused as the phonetic component once 七 itself was fully given over to the numeral sense. Like the other single-digit numerals, 七 is far from confined to standalone counting — it heads a large family of compounds in Dan'a'yo, including [[七十]] (seventy), [[七月]] (July), [[七夕]] (Qixi, the "Chinese Valentine's Day"), and [[七対子]] (Seven Pairs, a mahjong hand), among many others.

Mandarin 七 (qī, seven). Cantonese 七 (cat1). Japanese 七 has the on'yomi しち (shichi), used for formal counting and in most compounds, alongside a native reading なな (nana) that is often preferred in casual speech specifically because しち can be confused with 一 (いち, ichi) in noisy or spoken contexts — a practical disambiguation habit, not a meaning difference. Korean 칠 (chil) is the Sino-Korean reading; the native Korean numeral is 일곱 (ilgop). Vietnamese thất is the Sino-Vietnamese reading, reserved for set compounds and classical contexts, while everyday counting uses the native bảy.

Like [[一]] and its formal variant [[壱]], 七 has a dedicated anti-forgery/financial variant that shares its exact Dan'a'yo reading ㄑㄧㄊ/cid/칟: [[漆]] ("lacquer, varnish" as a character, but used as the complex numeral form on financial documents precisely because its many strokes are much harder to alter than 七's two).

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 칟 (chit) and Korean 칠 (chil) both come from the same Middle Chinese source (t͡sʰ + iɪt, an entering-tone syllable with a -t coda) but diverge in the coda itself — Dan'a'yo keeps the -t coda as -d/-t (칟 = chi + ᆮ), while Sino-Korean shows the same regular MC -t→-l shift already seen on [[一]] (일, not the expected 읻) and on 日/八. The two systems start from an identical MC syllable and diverge only because Korean underwent that later coda-lenition change.
