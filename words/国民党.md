## Notes
- [国](../characters/国.md) "nation" + [民](../characters/民.md) "people" + [党](../characters/党.md) "party" — Nationalist Party; most prominently the Kuomintang (KMT) of China and Taiwan

The Chinese Kuomintang (中国国民党) traces its origins to the Tongmenghui (中国同盟会), the revolutionary coalition formed by Sun Yat-sen (孫文) in 1905. After the 1911 Revolution overthrew the Qing dynasty, Song Jiaoren (宋教仁) reorganized it as the 国民党 in 1912. Sun reconstituted it as the 中国国民党 in 1919 on Leninist party lines, briefly allying with the Soviet Union and the Chinese Communist Party. Chiang Kai-shek (蒋介石) took control after Sun's death in 1925 and broke the Communist alliance in the Shanghai Massacre of 1927. The KMT government — the Republic of China (中華民国) — ruled mainland China from Nanjing until losing the civil war to the CCP; in 1949 it retreated to Taiwan, where it remains a major political party today.

In Japan, 国民党 (こくみんとう) has been the name of several distinct parties across the modern period. The compound was natural in Meiji-era political vocabulary: 国民 (the nation's people, citizens as a collective body) was a key term in constitutional debates, and 党 designated a formal political organization rather than a factional clique.

The compound reads transparently across all five languages of Dan'a'yo's sphere — Mandarin guómíndǎng, Cantonese gwok3 man4 dong2, Japanese こくみんとう, Korean 국민당, Vietnamese Quốc Dân Đảng — a rare case where the Sinitic compound functions identically with no phonetic adaptation needed in any branch.
