## Notes
- [元](../characters/元.md) "primary; first; source; fundamental" + [素](../characters/素.md) "plain substance; basic material; elemental ingredient"

元素 is "element" — in chemistry, a pure substance that cannot be decomposed into simpler substances by chemical means; more broadly, any irreducible fundamental constituent. Both characters independently carry senses of primacy and basicness: 元 = the originary, the foundational (元旦, New Year's Day; 元首, head of state — always the first or fundamental thing); 素 = the unadorned, the basic ingredient (素材, raw material; 素食, plain vegetarian food). Their compound doubles down: element as the irreducibly primary substance.

Mandarin 元素 (yuánsù) is the scientific standard for chemical element, used across chemistry, physics, and philosophy — 元素周期表 (the periodic table of elements). Japanese 元素 (げんそ, genso) is the scientific standard: 元素周期表 (gensoshūkihyō, periodic table), 放射性元素 (radioactive elements). Korean 원소 (wonso) is the standard term. Vietnamese nguyên tố is the Sino-Vietnamese equivalent, with 素 → tố. The compound is one of the 19th-century wasei-kango (和製漢語) — scientific neologisms coined in Japan for Western chemical concepts, then adopted across the Sinosphere.

The kwin: true for this entry is notable: the Dan'a'yo Hangul transcription 원소 happens to match the Sino-Korean 원소 exactly. This occurs because both Dan'a'yo and Korean inherit the phonological shapes of 元 (wen → won/원) and 素 (so → 소) from closely related Middle Chinese paths, producing convergent readings despite independent development. 元素 is one of the scientific vocabulary items where Japanese coinage, Chinese adoption, Korean transcription, and Dan'a'yo phonology all align cleanly.
