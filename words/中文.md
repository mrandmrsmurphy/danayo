## Notes
- [中 (char)](../characters/中%20(char).md) "middle; China" + [文](../characters/文.md) "culture; writing; text; literature"

中文 is "Chinese; Chinese language; Chinese writing; Chinese culture" — 中 (middle; China) + 文 (culture, writing, text, literature). The compound spans an unusually wide semantic range: 中文 can refer to the Chinese written language (as a writing system), the Chinese language itself (as a medium of communication), or Chinese culture broadly — the body captures this as 中国之文化, a full four-character expansion of the compound. This breadth reflects the classical Chinese integration of language, writing, and civilisation into a single concept: 文 encompasses all three, rooted in the pictogram of intersecting lines (a decorative pattern → writing → culture → civilisation).

Mandarin 中文 (zhōngwén) is the primary everyday term for "the Chinese language" and "Chinese": 你会中文吗？(Can you speak/read Chinese?), 中文名字 (Chinese name), 中文教材 (Chinese language materials). It is more common in daily speech than 中国語 or 普通話. Cantonese 中文 (zung1 man4) is likewise the everyday term in Hong Kong for the Chinese language and Chinese writing. Japanese ちゅうぶん (chūbun) uses the compound in the literary/textual sense (Chinese-language text). Korean 중문 (中文, jungmun) appears in academic contexts: 중문학과 (Department of Chinese Language and Literature), 중문학 (Chinese literature studies). Vietnamese Trung văn is the Sino-Vietnamese equivalent (Trung = 中, văn = 文).

kwin: true for 中文 — Dan'a'yo 중문 exactly matches Korean 중문. The compound's interpretation as a compression of 中国之文化 is an etymological gloss; in practice, 中文 functions primarily as a language name (cf. [[中国語]], the more technical language-as-system compound). The two Dan'a'yo words partially overlap: 中文 emphasises the cultural-written dimension of Chinese, while 中国語 emphasises language as a system of speech and communication; in Mandarin, 中文 is the everyday term for both.
