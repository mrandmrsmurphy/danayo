## Notes
- [閏 (char)](../characters/閏%20(char).md) "intercalary" + [秒 (char)](../characters/秒%20(char).md) "second (time)"

A one-second adjustment inserted into Coordinated Universal Time (UTC) to compensate for the gradual and irregular slowing of Earth's rotation. Leap seconds are administered by the **International Earth Rotation and Reference Systems Service** (IERS) and announced approximately six months in advance. When a leap second is inserted, the last minute of June 30 or December 31 becomes 61 seconds long, with the sequence 23:59:58 → 23:59:59 → **23:59:60** → 00:00:00.

The problem the leap second solves is a fundamental one: there are two different ways to define a "second."

The **SI second** (the basis of International Atomic Time, TAI) is defined as exactly 9,192,631,770 oscillations of the cesium-133 atom at ground state — a definition of extraordinary precision and stability, anchored to quantum physics rather than astronomy.

**Astronomical time** is defined by Earth's rotation: one day = the time for Earth to rotate once relative to the Sun. But Earth's rotation is neither constant nor perfectly predictable. Tidal friction caused by the Moon's gravitational pull on Earth's oceans gradually dissipates rotational energy, lengthening the day by roughly 1.7 milliseconds per century. Irregular movements of Earth's molten core, atmospheric pressure changes, and post-glacial rebound all cause additional variation.

If UTC tracked only atomic time with no correction, solar noon would drift later and later relative to the clock over centuries. Since 1972, **27 leap seconds** have been inserted (as of 2024) to keep UTC within 0.9 seconds of astronomical time — the agreement that defines the relationship between UTC and TAI as UTC = TAI − 37 seconds.

Unlike [[閏日]] (leap days, inserted on a predictable arithmetic schedule) and [[閏月]] (leap months, placed by astronomical rule), leap seconds are **irregular**: their timing depends on unpredictable geophysical variation and cannot be calculated more than a few months in advance. This irregularity makes them uniquely difficult for digital infrastructure.

Leap seconds have caused repeated problems for networked computing. The 61-second minute breaks assumptions embedded in software, operating systems, databases, and network protocols. Major outages caused or contributed to by leap seconds include crashes at Cloudflare, Reddit, LinkedIn, and Mozilla. After decades of debate, a **2022 ITU vote decided to abolish the leap second by 2035**, allowing UTC to drift from solar time by up to one minute before a large correction — or possibly permanently decoupling atomic and astronomical time.
