## Notes
- [老 (char)](../characters/老%20(char).md) "old; prefix" + [鼠](../characters/鼠.md) "rat"

In Mandarin, 老 functions as a colloquializing prefix before a number of animal names — 老虎 (tiger), 老鷹 (eagle), 老鼠 (rat) — without carrying a literal "old" meaning. The prefix lends a familiar, everyday register; the animals so named tend to be common creatures of the human environment. 老鼠 is the standard modern Mandarin word for rat or mouse (the same word covers both), and the bare 鼠 rarely appears in spoken usage.

Classically, 老鼠 carried an additional figurative sense: a cunning or deceitful person, one who scurries in the shadows and cannot be trusted. This usage survives in set phrases and in the compound's extension into slang (see [[老鼠人]]).

The Japanese on-yomi reading ろうそ is attested but uncommon; Japanese uses ねずみ (nezumi) for rat and mouse in everyday speech. In Cantonese, 老鼠 (lou5 syu2) is the ordinary colloquial word for rat.
