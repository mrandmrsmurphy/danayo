## Notes
- [芥](../characters/芥.md) "mustard plant" + [子](../characters/子.md) "seed, child" — stand-in for [[芥]], which cannot appear independently

芥子 (からし in Japanese; jièzǐ in Mandarin) means mustard seed or mustard — the tiny seed of the mustard plant (Sinapis or Brassica species), ground to make the condiment. In Chinese and Buddhist literary usage 芥子 is proverbially the smallest thing imaginable: the phrase 芥子纳須彌 (jièzǐ nà Xūmí, "fitting Mount Sumeru into a mustard seed") from the Vimalakirti Sutra is a classic expression of the paradox of boundless space contained in a tiny vessel. The mustard seed thus became a symbol of the infinite contained within the minuscule.

In Japanese the same characters have two entirely separate readings depending on context: からし (karashi) means mustard the condiment, while けし (keshi) — the same two characters read differently — refers to the opium poppy (Papaver somniferum). This reading split is a celebrated example of Japanese jukujikun (熟字訓), where the meaning rather than the individual character sounds determines the reading. からし mustard is used extensively in Japanese cuisine as a condiment with natto, shumai, and oden.
