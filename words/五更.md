## Notes
- [五 (char)](../characters/五%20(char).md) "five" + [更 (char)](../characters/更%20(char).md) "more; again; a watch (of the night)"

The traditional East Asian division of the night into five equal periods. Before mechanical clocks, timekeeping at night relied on water clocks (刻漏, kèlòu) and the audible signals of watchmen beating drums or striking gongs as they patrolled city walls and streets. Each of the five watches (更, gēng) lasted approximately two hours:

| Watch | Approximate time | Traditional name | Meaning |
|-------|-----------------|-----------------|---------|
| 初更 (First Watch) | 7–9 pm | 黄昏 | Dusk |
| 二更 (Second Watch) | 9–11 pm | 人定 | People settle |
| 三更 (Third Watch) | 11 pm–1 am | 夜半 | Dead of night |
| 四更 (Fourth Watch) | 1–3 am | 雞鳴 | Cockcrow |
| **五更** (Fifth Watch) | **3–5 am** | **平旦** | **Daybreak** |

**三更** (the third watch, midnight) is the most culturally significant in Chinese literature: it is the hour of ghosts, secret lovers, and clandestine meetings. "三更半夜" ("in the middle of the third watch") remains a living idiom for "in the dead of night." Classical poetry is saturated with images of lying awake at 三更, listening to rain or distant drums.

**五更** — the fifth watch, just before dawn — was the hour when imperial officials had to be already dressed and en route to the palace for morning court. The court bell struck at 五更, and any official who arrived late faced punishment. The desperate pre-dawn rush of civil servants is a recurring subject of Tang and Song dynasty poetry; Du Fu (杜甫) and others wrote movingly of leaving home in winter darkness while their families still slept.

In Vietnam, the night watches (canh) were used analogously: 五更 = canh năm, the fifth watch, still used poetically to mean "just before dawn."
