## Notes
- [逮](../characters/逮.md) "to reach; to catch up to; to apprehend" + [捕 (char)](../characters/捕%20(char).md) "to capture; to seize"

逮捕 combines 逮 — originally meaning "to reach, to catch up with, to attain" (from 辵 "movement" + 隶, a hand seizing something) — with 捕 — "to capture, to seize physically" (from 扌 "hand" + 甫, phonetic). Together they form the legal compound for arrest: an officer of the law reaches and physically detains a suspect. The compound appears in legal codes from the Tang dynasty onward and has remained the standard formal term for arrest across all four writing-using CJKV languages.

In legal contexts 逮捕 (Chinese dàibǔ, Japanese taiho, Korean 체포 chepo — note the different first character in Korean) is distinguished from related terms of varying coercive force. In Japan, 逮捕 (taiho) is the standard criminal procedure term for arrest, triggering specific rights (the right to be informed of the charges, the right to counsel), and 現行犯逮捕 (arrest in flagrante delicto, without warrant) is distinguished from 令状逮捕 (warrant arrest). 逮捕令状 is the arrest warrant. The compound appears in news headlines constantly — 逮捕された (was arrested) is one of the most common constructions in Japanese crime reporting.

Korean 체포 uses a different first character: 逮 maps to 체 in Korean (not 대 as in Dan'a'yo), reflecting a different phonological development for this character class — making this one of the clearer illustrations of why kwin=false. Vietnamese bắt giữ uses native terms rather than the sinographic compound for arrest.
