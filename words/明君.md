## Notes
- [明 (char)](../characters/明%20(char).md) "bright, clear" + [君 (char)](../characters/君%20(char).md) "ruler, lord"

明君 is the East Asian ideal of the enlightened ruler — a monarch whose governance is characterized by clarity of judgment, virtue, and a genuine concern for the welfare of the people. The compound brings together 明 (brightness, clarity, perspicacity) and 君 (ruler, lord), framing good rulership as fundamentally a matter of clear-sightedness: the 明君 sees truly, hears honestly, and acts without the distortions of flattery, faction, or self-interest.

The concept is central to Confucian political philosophy, where the quality of government was understood to flow directly from the moral and intellectual character of the ruler. Classical texts such as the *Shang Shu* (尚書), Mencius (孟子), and the *Zhen Guan Zheng Yao* (貞觀政要, records of Tang Taizong's reign) elaborate the qualities of the 明君 in contrast to the 昏君 (hūnjūn, the dim or dissolute ruler). Tang Taizong (唐太宗, r. 626–649 AD) is often cited as the historical exemplar — a ruler who actively solicited criticism, maintained the *jianyi* (諫議, remonstrance) system, and is credited with overseeing one of China's golden ages.

In Japanese (めいくん, meikun), the term appears in historical writing and political philosophy. In Vietnamese (minh quân), it is part of the Confucian political vocabulary that shaped Vietnamese imperial thought, particularly in the writings of Nguyễn Trãi (阮廌, 1380–1442), who wrote extensively on the obligations of enlightened rulership to the people.
