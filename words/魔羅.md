## Notes
- [魔](../characters/魔.md) "demon" + [羅](../characters/羅.md) "Rome; net; spread"

A phonetic transcription of Sanskrit **मार** (māra) — "killing," "death," "destroyer." In Buddhist cosmology, Māra is the great demon who personifies the forces that obstruct spiritual liberation: craving, attachment, passion, delusion, and the terror of death. He is the lord of the **欲界** (yùjiè, realm of sensuous desire), the lowest of the three realms of existence, and his domain encompasses everything that binds beings to the cycle of rebirth ([[輪廻]], saṃsāra). The characters 魔羅 are phonetic, not semantic — 羅 here represents the Sanskrit syllable *rā*, not its meaning of "net" or "Rome."

The most celebrated episode involving Māra is his assault on Siddhārtha Gautama on the night of the Buddha's enlightenment under the Bodhi tree at Bodh Gaya. As the meditating Bodhisattva approached awakening, Māra sent three daughters — **Taṇhā** (Craving), **Arati** (Discontent/Aversion), and **Rāgā** (Passion) — to seduce him away from meditation. When seduction failed, Māra marshalled his demonic armies to attack with fear, doubt, and violence. The Buddha responded with the **earth-touching gesture** (bhūmisparśa mudrā, 觸地印) — touching the ground with his right hand, calling the earth itself to bear witness to his accumulated merit through countless lifetimes. The earth trembled in affirmation; Māra's armies fled; and the Buddha passed into full enlightenment. This scene is among the most depicted subjects in all of Buddhist art across the entire Sinosphere, from Gandharan sculpture to Japanese painting to Thai temple murals.

In Japanese, **まら** (mara) acquired a second, frankly anatomical meaning dating to the early Heian period (attested from the early 800s CE). Buddhist monks in monastic communities used 魔羅 as slang for the penis — the physical seat of the sexual desire that Mara embodies. The semantic shift from "demon of desire" to "the body part most associated with desire" is a piece of wry monastic wordplay: to say まら is to invoke Mara's power while naming its instrument. This secondary meaning is now the more common usage in informal Japanese speech, appearing in literature, comedy, and bawdy contexts while the Buddhist sense survives in religious and academic writing. The Ryukyuan languages independently preserve the same anatomical meaning across multiple dialects (Okinawan, Miyako, Yaeyama, and others), evidence that the monastic slang spread widely before the standard Japanese dialects diverged.

The alternate writing **摩羅** (using 摩 "to rub; to grind" in place of 魔 "demon") is found in older Buddhist texts — 摩 was sometimes used as a phonetic transcription of the Sanskrit *mā* before 魔 became standardized. Both forms refer to the same Sanskrit original.
