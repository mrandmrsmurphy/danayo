## Notes
- [皆 (char)](../characters/皆%20(char).md) "all; every; universal" + [名 (char)](../characters/名%20(char).md) "name; honorific person classifier"

皆名 is the universal honorific correlative — "everyone (polite); all distinguished persons" — the respectful counterpart to 皆人. It addresses or refers to all members of a respected group collectively, at the highest register of the person column. The Chinese equivalent 各位 (gèwèi, lit. "each [honorific classifier]") is the standard formal address form: 各位嘉賓 ("distinguished guests"), 各位同仁 ("esteemed colleagues"), 各位代表 ("honoured delegates") — the opening address of any formal Chinese public speech or document.

Japanese 皆様 (mina-sama, "everyone-[most honorific suffix]") is the direct polite equivalent and worth a specific note: 皆様 is also a Dan'a'yo correlative word in the 様 row, meaning "in the manner of all." This means Japanese 皆様 maps to two different Dan'a'yo forms — 皆名 (all distinguished persons, as a noun) and 皆様 (in every manner, as an adverbial correlative) — a coincidence arising from Japanese's use of 様 (sama) as both an honorific suffix and a manner word. Korean 여러분 (yeoreobun, "all of you" — the standard polite public address) is the functional equivalent; 분 (分/분) here parallels Dan'a'yo 名 and Chinese 位 as the honorific person classifier.

The 名 row as a whole illustrates how Dan'a'yo systematises register into the correlative table. The 人/名 contrast (neutral/polite) makes politeness a compositional parameter: any 人-row correlative can be shifted to polite register simply by substituting 名 for 人. The pattern is a direct analog of how Chinese 人 vs 位, Japanese 人 vs 方/様, and Korean 사람 vs 분 mark register in person reference — but in Dan'a'yo it is regular, systematic, and part of the base grammar rather than a patchwork of lexical choices.
