## Notes
- [六 (char)](../characters/六%20(char).md) "six; 6"

六 is "six" — the integer 6. The character is classified as 指事 (indicative/abstract symbol): the numeral six is represented by an abstract graphic form rather than a depicted object. One theory (Liu Xinglong) holds that the form was phonetically borrowed from the similar-sounding 廬 (OC *ra, "shed; cabin") to represent the homophonous numeral in ancient Chinese — a case of rebus borrowing rather than semantic pictography. The 76th most-used character in Classical Chinese.

Mandarin 六 (liù). Cantonese 六 (luk6). Japanese 六 (ろく, roku; also りく, riku in some formal compounds; native むっ, mutt-, in compounds like 六日 むいか). Korean: Sino-Korean 륙 (ryuk) appears in formal compounds; the native Korean word for six is 여섯 (yeoseot). Vietnamese lục is the standard Sino-Vietnamese reading, used in formal and compound contexts (cf. native sáu for everyday counting). The character appears in Dan'a'yo compounds including [[六十]] (sixty), [[六月]] (June), and [[六書]] (the six categories of Chinese characters).

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 룩 (lug/ㄌㄨㄎ) differs from Sino-Korean 륙 (ryuk) in the vowel — Dan'a'yo gives ㅜ (u) while Sino-Korean gives ㅠ (yu, a palatalised diphthong). Both derive from MC l + ɨuk (來 initial + 屋三 final), but through different vowel-development paths: Dan'a'yo preserves the plain u-vowel, while Sino-Korean shows the yu-diphthong from the same source. The original word file had ROmaji "luk" (with final k), corrected here to "lug" — final ㄎ consistently maps to "g" in Dan'a'yo ROmaji (cf. 国=gog, 学=hag, 玉='yog).
