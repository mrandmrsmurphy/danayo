## Notes
- [指](../characters/指.md) "finger" + [事 (char)](../characters/事%20(char).md) "thing, action" — one of the [[六書]] (liùshū), the traditional six-category classification of Chinese characters.

**指事** (*zhǐshì*) characters are those that "point to" or "indicate" an abstract idea or relationship through a non-pictorial, non-arbitrary diagram — typically a simple geometric mark added to or layered onto another graph. The textbook examples are 上 ("above") and 下 ("below"), each built from a baseline with a mark indicating relative position, and 一/二/三 for the small numerals, whose strokes directly count rather than depict.

The category sits between 象形 (pictograms, which depict concrete objects) and 会意 (compound ideographs, which combine multiple meaningful elements). 指事 characters are comparatively few in number — traditional accounts put them at well under a hundred — but several are among the most ancient and frequently used graphs in the script, including the basic numerals and locational terms like 中 ("middle") and 本 ("root, origin").

In Japanese pedagogy the term 指事文字 (*shiji moji*) is used in the same sense, taught alongside 象形文字, 会意文字, and 形声文字 as the four "structural" categories (the remaining two of the traditional six, 転注 and 仮借, describe usage rather than structure and are largely set aside in modern teaching). Korean and Vietnamese scholarly traditions preserve the same 六書 framework and terminology nearly unchanged, reflecting its origin in the Han-dynasty *Shuowen Jiezi*.
