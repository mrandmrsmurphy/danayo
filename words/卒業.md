## Notes
- [卒](../characters/卒.md) "soldier; finish; die" + [業](../characters/業%20(char).md) "profession, studies, karma" — completion of studies.

The pairing of 卒 and 業 is etymologically compact: 卒 means both "soldier" and "to finish or die" — the semantic thread being the end of a stage of service or life. 業 in the academic sense derives from its Confucian and Buddhist usage for the accumulated work or karma one undertakes. 卒業 thus carries the sense of having discharged one's scholarly obligation — completing what one was set to do.

In Japanese, 卒業 (そつぎょう, sotsugyō) is the standard word for completing any level of formal education and anchors a large family of compounds: 卒業式 (graduation ceremony), 卒業生 (graduate, alumnus), 卒業論文 (graduation thesis). In modern colloquial Japanese, 卒業する has been extended metaphorically to mean "growing out of" or "moving on from" something non-academic — a fan can 卒業する from a music group, or a person from a habit. The extended use treats all stages of life as having their own curriculum to complete.

Korean 졸업 (卒業, joreop) functions identically to the Japanese form, as the universal term for graduation at any educational level. The structural parallel reflects the depth of Meiji-era Japanese influence on Korean institutional vocabulary during the colonial period.

In Mandarin, 卒业 (zúyè) exists but is far less common than 毕业 (bìyè), which dominates modern usage. Dan'a'yo follows the Sino-Japanese form, consistent with its general preference for forms that have wide East Asian currency.
