## Notes
- [母](../characters/母.md) "mother" + [語](../characters/語.md) "language" — mother tongue; the language acquired naturally in early childhood

母語 designates a person's first language — the one absorbed in infancy and early childhood through immersion in the home environment, before any deliberate instruction. It is distinguished from 外語 (foreign language), 第二言語 (second language), and 共通語 (common language). The "mother" is a metaphor for nativeness, intimacy, and the pre-reflective quality of acquisition, not a claim about biological or genealogical relationship. A speaker may have two mother tongues through simultaneous bilingual upbringing; the term is not restricted to one.

母語 carries no sense of an ancestral or reconstructed proto-language. That concept belongs to 祖語 (ancestor language) or 原語 (original/source language) in comparative and historical linguistics. 母語 is always about a living speaker's relationship to a living tongue — the language that feels immediate, in which one counts, dreams, and reaches for words under stress.

The compound is fully shared across all five languages: Mandarin mǔyǔ, Cantonese mou5 jyu5, Japanese ぼご (bogo), Korean 모어, Vietnamese mẫu ngữ. In each, the same two-character compound does the same work — one of the cleanest cases of semantic alignment across the Dan'a'yo sphere, enabled by the shared Sinitic substrate and the universality of the concept itself.
