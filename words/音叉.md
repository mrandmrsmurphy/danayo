## Notes
- [音](../characters/音.md) "sound" + [叉 (char)](../characters/叉%20(char).md) "fork; prong"

A tuning fork — a two-pronged metal instrument that vibrates at a precise, fixed frequency when struck, producing a pure tone used to tune musical instruments. The name is transparent in both Chinese (音叉, "sound fork") and Japanese (おんさ, onsa, same characters); the English name "tuning fork" emphasizes function while the East Asian name emphasizes shape.

The tuning fork was invented in 1711 by John Shore, sergeant-trumpeter to the English royal court. Unlike a bell or a plucked string, a struck tuning fork produces a nearly pure sine wave with minimal overtones — making it exceptionally stable as a pitch reference. The international standard concert pitch **A4 = 440 Hz**, adopted by the ISO in 1939, is most commonly embodied in the 440 Hz tuning fork. (Historically, pitch standards varied widely: Baroque tuning used A ≈ 415 Hz, roughly a semitone lower; some ensembles still observe this for period performance.)

Beyond music, the tuning fork has a significant place in **clinical audiology**. The Rinne test and Weber test use vibrating forks (typically 256 Hz or 512 Hz) placed on the skull or beside the ear to distinguish conductive hearing loss (a problem in the outer or middle ear) from sensorineural hearing loss (a problem in the cochlea or auditory nerve). These tests require no electronics and remain standard in clinical medicine, particularly in settings with limited diagnostic equipment.

The 音叉 is also the physical basis of the quartz crystal oscillator — modern clocks and digital electronics use a tiny quartz crystal that vibrates like a tuning fork at 32,768 Hz to keep time.
