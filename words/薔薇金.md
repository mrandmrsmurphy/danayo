## Notes
### Form
薔薇金 is [[薔薇]] “rose” + [金 (char)](characters/金%20(char).md) “metal.” This is a tri-character formation, parallel to other necessary expansions (e.g., 苦土素).

### Motivation
The international name _rhodium_ derives from Greek ῥόδον (_rhodon_) “rose,” referring to the rose-colored salts of the element. 薔薇 is the standard Sinitic term for “rose,” making this a **direct semantic calque**.

### Why a two-character base (薔薇)
- There is no widely accepted single-character graph that unambiguously means “rose” in Classical Chinese.
- Alternatives like 紅 (“red”) or 丹 (“cinnabar”) would shift the meaning from “rose” to generic color, losing the specific botanical imagery present in the original Greek.
- [薔](characters/薔.md) or [薇](characters/薇.md) alone are incomplete: each refers to a plant type, but only together do they reliably denote “rose.”

Thus the three-character structure is justified to preserve semantic precision.

### Type of formation
A **faithful semantic calque** of the Greek root, expanded to two graphs for lexical completeness, then suffixed with 金.

### Fit within the system
1. Preserves original etymology more precisely than a color-based shortcut.
2. Uses 金 appropriately for a transition metal.
3. Accepts tri-character expansion where a single graph is insufficient.

### Comparative CJKV forms
- Modern Chinese: 铑 (phonetic)
- Japanese: ロジウム (phonetic)
- Korean: 로듐 (phonetic)
- Vietnamese: rhodi (phonetic)

Modern forms are phonetic; 薔薇金 restores the original semantic imagery.

### Clarity and issues
- The term is longer than usual but semantically exact.
- Requires users to recognize a relatively literary plant term.
- Symbol selection must be fixed to avoid ambiguity.

### Conclusion
薔薇金 is a precise and etymologically faithful rendering of _rhodium_. The tri-character form is justified, and selecting 薇 as the symbol maintains phonological distinctiveness.
