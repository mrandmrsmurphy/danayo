## Notes
- [法 (char)](../characters/法%20(char).md) "rule, law" + [律](../characters/律.md) "statute, principle, regulation, discipline" — the standard compound for "law" across the whole family; stand-in for [[律]], which cannot appear independently.

法律/법률/pháp luật all trace to the same "rule + regulation" image, but the four languages don't agree on how *technical* the compound is. Mandarin fǎlǜ is the ordinary, all-purpose word for "law" — it covers the legal system generally and any individual statute without any register distinction from plainer 法. Japanese and Korean, by contrast, use 法律/법률 as a genuine term of art: in Japan's legal hierarchy, 法律 (hōritsu) names specifically the statutes passed by the Diet, sitting below the Constitution (憲法) and above Cabinet orders (政令) and ministerial ordinances — a cabinet order or municipal ordinance is never called 法律 even though it is still "law" in the loose sense. Korean 법률 works the same way in the Constitution and legislation theory: it denotes an act of the National Assembly specifically, distinct from the broader 법령 (which also folds in presidential/prime-ministerial/ministerial decrees) and from municipal 조례. Vietnamese splits the difference differently again — pháp luật is the broad, systemic term (the entire body of law and legal norms of a state), while an individual enacted law is usually just luật (e.g. Luật Hình sự, "Penal Code"); pháp luật is essentially never used the way hōritsu/beomnyul are, to single out one specific enactment.
