## Notes
- [勅](../characters/勅.md) "imperial decree" + [令](../characters/令%20(char).md) "command; order" — stand-in for [[勅]], which cannot appear independently
- 勅令 names the highest possible register of command — not an ordinary order but a word issued by the throne itself, where 勅 (and its alternate form 敕, recorded as a vault alias) marks the document or pronouncement as specifically imperial, and 令 supplies the basic idea of an order or directive. The compound kept this exact, formal weight everywhere it traveled: Mandarin chìlìng and Cantonese cik1 ling6 name the historical imperial edict, Sino-Korean 칙령 (chingnyeong) names the Joseon-era royal decree (and, after 1894, the highest form of Korean imperial law during the Korean Empire period), Sino-Japanese ちょくれい (chokurei) names the Meiji- and Taishō-era cabinet orders issued in the emperor's name — a real constitutional category of Japanese law before the postwar constitution replaced it with 政令 — and Sino-Vietnamese sắc lệnh names the decree-orders of the Nguyễn court and, later, of Vietnam's modern presidents and prime ministers. A word for command at its most absolute, it followed the institution of monarchy itself across the region, and outlived more than one of those monarchies as a legal term.
