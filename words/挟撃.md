## Notes
- [挟 (char)](../characters/挟%20(char).md) "clasp, hold between" + [撃 (char)](../characters/撃%20(char).md) "strike, hit"

挟撃 denotes the act of striking an enemy from two sides simultaneously — a converging attack that closes like a vice. Where [[挟攻]] describes the tactical posture (attacking from a flanking or encircling position), 挟撃 emphasizes the strike itself: the moment of impact from converging directions.

The compound is borrowed from Chinese 夾擊/夹击 (jiājī), where 夾 ("to sandwich, to clamp") conveys the same spatial image. In Japanese (きょうげき, kyōgeki), 挟 replaced 夾 as the standard writing; the traditional kyūjitai spelling uses 挾擊. The compound is standard military vocabulary in Japanese historical writing, appearing in accounts of battles from the Sengoku period through the Pacific War. In Korean (협격), it appears in military history and strategic contexts.

The full range of orthographic variants across traditional/simplified Chinese and Japanese scripts is captured in the aliases: 挾撃 (kyūjitai Japanese), 挟擊 / 挾擊 (traditional Chinese 撃→擊), 挟击 / 挾击 (simplified Chinese 撃→击).
