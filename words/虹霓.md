## Notes
- [虹](../characters/虹.md) "rainbow; primary arc" + [霓](../characters/霓.md) "secondary rainbow" — 霓's stand-in word

**虹霓** (*hóngnì*) classically denotes the rainbow in its two-arc form: 虹 (*hóng*) is the primary rainbow — the brighter inner arc — and 霓 (*ní*) is the secondary rainbow, a dimmer outer arc whose colour sequence is reversed. Together the compound is a dvandva pairing both arcs, appearing in Classical Chinese poetry and cosmological writing as a stock image of celestial splendour and transience.

In Dan'a'yo, 虹霓 is repurposed as the word for **neon**: the luminous discharge phenomenon that gives neon signs and neon lighting their vivid, saturated, arc-bright colour. The semantic extension is motivated — neon light produces exactly the jewel-coloured spectral brilliance that 虹霓 evokes, and the extension from "rainbow light" to "neon light" is transparent. This repurposing follows a long East Asian tradition of redirecting classically-charged compounds toward modern scientific and commercial coinages.

The chemical element neon (atomic number 10, noble gas) is named [[虹素]] (*hongso*, ㄏㄛㄫㄙㄛ) in Dan'a'yo — a 素-suffix coinage back-formed from this word, parallel to 養素 (oxygen), 炭素 (carbon), and 窒素 (nitrogen). Standard Chinese names the element 氖 (*nǎi*), a novel character coined specifically for transliteration; Dan'a'yo prefers the compound approach, deriving the element name from the word for the phenomenon it famously produces.
