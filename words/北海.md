## Notes
- [北](../characters/北.md) "north" + [海](../characters/海.md) "sea" — the Northern Sea; both the northernmost of the classical [[四海]] and the modern North Sea of Europe

Of the 四海, the Northern Sea has the richest classical literary life — not because it was better known geographically, but because its remoteness made it the perfect setting for myth and exile. The *Zhuangzi* (莊子) opens in the 北冥 (Northern Darkness, a near-synonym for 北海): "北冥有魚，其名為鯤" — "In the northern darkness there is a fish, and its name is Kūn." This fish is so vast it fills the sea; it transforms into a bird called Péng (鵬) that rises ninety thousand li into the sky and flies to the Southern Darkness. The passage is one of the most celebrated in classical Chinese literature, and it gives 北海 a mythological scale that no actual body of water could match.

The Han dynasty gave 北海 a more specific geography when the diplomat Su Wu (蘇武) was sent as an envoy to the Xiongnu in 100 BC and held captive for nineteen years. The Xiongnu exiled him to the 北海 — identified by later historians as the region of Lake Baikal — to tend a flock of rams, promising he could return to Han when the rams gave milk. 蘇武牧羊 (Su Wu tending his flock) became a canonical image of loyal endurance under impossible conditions, and 北海 acquired its permanent association with remote, cold isolation.

In modern usage, 北海 has migrated westward to name the North Sea between Britain and continental Europe — a recent application with none of the classical weight. The two senses coexist without confusion because context separates them: the classical 北海 belongs to cosmology and literary allusion; the modern 北海 belongs to geography and petroleum policy. Compare [[西海]], whose referent also shifted over time but never acquired an equally settled modern counterpart.
