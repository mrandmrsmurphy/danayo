## Notes
- [蜀](../characters/蜀.md) "Shu (kingdom)" + [国](../characters/国.md) "kingdom, country"

蜀 names the region centered on the Sichuan basin, walled off from the rest of China by mountains on every side — a geographic isolation that runs through its whole political history. The character itself is a pictograph of a silkworm or caterpillar (its English gloss here, "Shu kingdom," obscures this older meaning), reflecting the region's ancient association with sericulture; 蜀 still appears as a component in 濁 ("muddy"), cross-linked from [[蜀]]'s own Notes.

蜀国 is the generic term for "the kingdom of Shu" across Chinese history — applicable to the semi-legendary Kaiming dynasty conquered by Qin in 316 BC, the independent kingdom of Shu-Han during the Three Kingdoms period (蜀漢, 221–263 AD, ruled by Liu Bei and later Zhuge Liang), and the Five Dynasties-era kingdoms of Former and Later Shu. 蜀漢 (Shu Han) is the more precise term for the Three Kingdoms state specifically — the one most familiar from the *Romance of the Three Kingdoms* — while 蜀国 is the broader, less period-specific name used here as the Dan'a'yo headword, parallel to how 宋朝 names a dynasty rather than a single reign.
