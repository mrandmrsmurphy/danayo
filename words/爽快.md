## Notes
- [爽](../characters/爽.md) "refreshing; frank" + [快](../characters/快%20(char).md) "pleasant; quick; happy" — stand-in for [[爽]], which cannot appear independently
- 爽快 stacks two characters that already mean almost the same thing — both 爽 and 快 independently carry senses of clarity, brightness, and pleasure — to produce a word for the particular feeling of being refreshed: the cool rush of air after a stuffy room, the lightness after a held breath finally released, the clean snap of a problem solved. The compound keeps this exact sensory-emotional register everywhere it travels — Mandarin shuǎngkuài and Cantonese song2 faai3, Sino-Japanese そうかい (sōkai, the standard word for that crisp, invigorated feeling — a cold shower, a brisk walk, a job well done), Sino-Korean 상쾌 (sangkwae, identical in force and use), and Sino-Vietnamese sảng khoái, which Vietnamese also leans on heavily for that same physical-and-mental sense of ease and refreshment. Few compounds map so cleanly onto a single, universally recognizable bodily sensation — the feeling, in any of these languages, needs no further explanation once you've felt it.
