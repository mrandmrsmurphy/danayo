## Notes
- [茜](../characters/茜.md) "madder" + [草](../characters/草%20(char).md) "grass; plant" — stand-in for [[茜]], which cannot appear independently
- 茜草 names *Rubia cordifolia*, the climbing perennial whose root has dyed cloth a deep madder-red across East Asia for millennia — the same root that gives Japanese its word for that very color, あかね色 (akane-iro). The plant-name compound stays close to its pharmacological roots in every language: Mandarin qiàncǎo and Cantonese sin3 cou2 name the plant plainly as "madder-grass," while Sino-Japanese せんそう (sensō) and Sino-Korean 천초 (cheoncho) both survive chiefly as the formal, herbal-medicine names for the dried root — 茜草／茜根 in Japanese kampo, 천초／천초근 in Korean hanbang — used historically (with later-discovered caution, as the root is now known to carry kidney-damaging compounds) to cool the blood, stop bleeding, and ease gynecological complaints. Sino-Vietnamese thiến thảo completes the set, following the same plain "madder-grass" logic as its Chinese cousins — a humble dye-plant whose name, in its more learned forms, doubles as a small index of how far East Asian herbal medicine traveled on shared vocabulary.
