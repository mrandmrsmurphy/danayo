## Notes
- [干](../characters/干.md) "dry; desiccate" + [芻](../characters/芻.md) "grass; hay; fodder" — dried grass cut for animal feed; hay; stand-in for [[芻]], which cannot appear independently

**干芻** (*gān chú*) is dried grass or dried fodder — hay, cut and cured for storage and winter feeding of livestock. The compound is compositionally direct: 干 (dry, to dry out) specifying the state, 芻 (cut grass, fodder) the material. 芻 alone denotes fresh-cut grass and by extension any animal fodder; 干芻 is the preserved, dried form.

The character 芻 is a 會意 (compound ideograph) showing a hand grasping a bundle of grass — the original image of the agricultural labourer cutting fodder. In classical Chinese, 芻 appears in several important compounds. **芻狗** (*chúgǒu*, straw dogs) are the ritual objects of woven straw used in sacrificial ceremony and then discarded — the image invoked in the 道德經's 天地不仁 passage: 天地不仁，以萬物為[[芻狗]] (see [[天地不仁]]). **芻言** (*chúyán*, "grass words") is a conventional self-deprecatory formula for one's own humble speech — "my modest remarks," offered as raw fodder rather than refined argument. **芻議** (*chúyì*, "hay opinion") similarly frames one's own views as rough material for others to refine.

Hay production was fundamental to pre-modern animal husbandry across East Asia, as in every agricultural civilisation that kept horses, cattle, and sheep through winter. The quality and abundance of 干芻 was a strategic consideration in military logistics — a cavalry force's operational range was limited by available fodder as much as by water and grain. The *Art of War* tradition addresses fodder supply (芻糧 *chúliáng*, "fodder and provisions") as a component of military preparation.

In modern usage, 干芻 is largely supplanted by 干草 (*gāncǎo*, dried grass/hay) in everyday Chinese, which is more transparent to modern readers unfamiliar with the classical 芻. The compound 干芻 is preserved in agricultural history, classical texts, and technical veterinary contexts. Korean **간추** (*ganchu*) and Vietnamese **can sô** are the Sino-Vietnamese and Sino-Korean forms, both restricted to classical or specialised registers; everyday terms for hay in both languages draw on native vocabulary.
