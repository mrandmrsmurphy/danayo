## Notes
- [羽](../characters/羽.md) "feather; plume" + [翼](../characters/翼.md) "wing; fin" — stand-in for [[翼]], which cannot appear independently

羽翼 names a bird's wing literally, and by extension — the wing as what lifts and supports flight — "support, assistance," or the person providing it (a right-hand man); the historical chengyu 羽翼已成/已豐 ("the wings have already grown/filled out") describes someone who has become fully capable and independent.

**A genuine, well-documented homophone collision, attested independently in both Japanese and Korean**: this compound's real reading — Japanese うよく (uyoku), Korean 우익 (corrected from a wrongly-substituted native `날개`, itself real and attested with this exact "wings/assistance" meaning, verified via search) — is an exact homophone of the unrelated, far more common word 右翼 ("right wing," politically or militarily; also うよく/우익), since 羽 and 右 happen to share the same Sino-Japanese and Sino-Korean readings. Both language communities preserve this as a real, live homophone pair rather than one crowding out the other. **`kwin` corrected `false` → `true`**: both `characters/羽.md` and `characters/翼.md` are individually `kwin: true`, and — unlike the ambiguous, unattested cases on [[長牙]] and [[指甲]] earlier this sweep — this compound's Korean reading is a real, independently-verified word (not merely compositional), so the AND-rule result is directly confirmed rather than just mechanically assumed. Mandarin yǔyì and Vietnamese vũ dực are likewise real, attested compounds carrying the same literal-and-figurative double meaning across the sphere.
