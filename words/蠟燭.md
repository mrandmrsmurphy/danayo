## Notes
- [蠟](../characters/蠟.md) "wax" + [燭](../characters/燭.md) "candle; taper" — stand-in for both [[蠟]] and [[燭]], neither of which can appear independently
- 蠟燭 is the plain, everyday word for "candle" across the Sinosphere — a stick of wax (蠟) built around a wick (燭) and burned for light. Mandarin làzhú, Cantonese laap6 zuk1, and Sino-Korean 납촉 (napchok) keep the compound transparent and literal, while Japanese opted for a slightly different framing: ろうそく (rōsoku) writes the second half with 燭 but reads it as そく — itself an old reading tied to candlelight and lamplight imagery, appearing also in words like 燭台 "candlestick." Candles entered East Asia by very different historical routes — beeswax tapers from the West through Buddhist trade networks, and tallow- or wax-tree candles developed independently in China and Japan — but the compound itself, naming the simple union of fuel and flame, proved durable enough to become the region's shared, ordinary word for the object on every dinner table and shrine altar alike.
