## Notes
- [江](../characters/江.md) "cove, inlet" + [戸](../characters/戸.md) "door, entrance" — a native Japanese place name (え + ど) read with native readings rather than borrowed Sino-Japanese values, the same pattern as [[安土]], [[桃山]], and [[新羅|新羅's しらぎ]]. The name is first attested in the Azuma Kagami, a chronicle begun in 1180, and likely describes the original geography of the site — an inlet where the Hirakawa river met Edo Bay.

Edo grew from a minor castle town into the de facto capital of Japan after [[徳川]] Ieyasu made it his base in 1590 and then the seat of his shogunate from 1603. Under Tokugawa rule, Edo became one of the largest cities in the world by the 18th century, sustained by the sankin-kōtai (参勤交代) system that required regional daimyo to maintain residences and alternate periods of attendance there, ringing the shogun's court with a permanently resident provincial aristocracy and the merchant and artisan classes that served them. This urban growth fed a flourishing popular culture — ukiyo-e woodblock prints, kabuki theater, and a vigorous publishing trade — distinct from the more aristocratic culture of Kyoto. The Edo period (江戸時代) takes its name from the city and lasted until the Meiji Restoration of 1868, when the shogunate fell, the Tokugawa surrendered the city, and it was renamed Tokyo (東京, "eastern capital") to mark its new role as the seat of the restored imperial government.

The Korean reading 에도 is a modern phonetic transliteration of the Japanese name and is the standard form in contemporary Korean historical writing; an older Sino-Korean reading 강호 (using the ordinary Korean values of the two characters) also exists but is now treated as a secondary, classical form — the same え/ど → 에도 versus 강호 tension echoes the しらぎ/しんら divergence noted at [[新羅]].
