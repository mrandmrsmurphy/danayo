## Notes
- [祖](../characters/祖.md) "ancestor; founder" + [先](../characters/先.md) "before; prior; predecessor"

祖先 pairs 祖 (ancestor — graphemically 示, "spirit altar" + 且, "ancestral tablet") with 先 (that which comes before, the prior generation) to form the standard literary word for ancestors and forebears. The compound is semantically layered: 祖 grounds ancestry in ritual observance — the tablets placed on family altars that make ancestors present to the living — while 先 frames them temporally as predecessors whose prior existence conditions the present. Together 祖先 names both the people and the obligation they represent.

Ancestor veneration structured social, religious, and political life across the CJKV sphere. Confucian filial piety (孝) extended backward to ancestors as well as to living parents; the 祭祖 (ancestral memorial rites) performed at New Year, Qingming, and other occasions were the primary ritual expression of this obligation. In Korean society, 제사 (祭祀) ancestor memorial rites remain widely practiced; the term 조상 (祖上, forebears) is also common alongside 조선 as the Sino-Korean reading of 祖先. Vietnamese tổ tiên combines the Sino-Vietnamese forms and is the standard everyday term. Japanese そせん (sosen) is used in genealogical, biological, and cultural history contexts.

A notable homonym arises in Korean: 조선 (joseon) is simultaneously the Sino-Korean reading of 祖先 (ancestors) and the name of the Joseon dynasty (朝鮮, 1392–1897), the last Korean dynastic state — entirely different characters but phonologically identical. The kwin=false marking reflects the divergence between Dan'a'yo 즈선 (jǝ + sen, with 祖 taking its reduced-vowel Dan'a'yo reading) and Korean 조선.
