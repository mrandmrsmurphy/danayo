## Notes
- [凶 (char)](../characters/凶%20(char).md) "unlucky; sinister" + [手 (char)](../characters/手%20(char).md) "hand"

A murderer; the person who killed. Literally "the sinister hand" — the hand that dealt the fatal blow. In Chinese, 凶手 is the default word for **the perpetrator of a killing whose identity is being sought or established**: the unknown killer in a mystery, the suspect in a murder investigation, the convicted killer in a sentencing report.

The term occupies a specific register in Chinese crime vocabulary, distinct from related words:
- **殺人犯** (shārénfàn) — a convicted murderer; emphasizes legal status after judgment
- **刺客** (cìkè) — an assassin; emphasizes professional, contracted killing
- **兇手 / 凶手** — the killer as such; neutral as to motive, method, or legal status; the person who did it

In Chinese crime fiction and the enormously popular **crime drama genre** — both in Taiwan's classic whodunit tradition and mainland China's contemporary detective serials — 凶手是谁？ ("Who is the killer?") is the central question of the narrative. 真凶 (zhēnxiōng, "the real killer") is the corresponding phrase when an innocent person has been wrongly accused and the true perpetrator remains at large. These phrases are as culturally embedded in Chinese crime narrative as "whodunit" is in English.

In Japanese, **凶手** (kyōshu) is less common than in Chinese, with Japanese preferring **犯人** (hannin, "the criminal; the perpetrator") for everyday use, and **殺人者** (satsujinsha) in legal and formal contexts. 凶手 appears mainly in literary writing, historical fiction, and translations of Chinese texts.
