## Notes
- [何 (char)](../characters/何%20(char).md) "what; which; interrogative" + [名 (char)](../characters/名%20(char).md) "name; honorific person classifier"

何名 is the interrogative honorific correlative — "who? (very polite)" — the most deferential rung of Dan'a'yo's three-level who-ladder. The grammar notes this ladder explicitly: 何人 (*hanin*) is rude, 誰 is the neutral standard, and 何名 is reserved for the most polite register. Asking 何名 signals maximal deference both toward the person being asked about and toward the person being asked, using 名 (the honorific person classifier) to frame the inquiry as one involving a respected individual.

Japanese どなた (donata) is the natural gloss and a genuine cross-linguistic alignment: どなた is the everyday polite word for "who" in Japanese, used in formal speech and with strangers (どなたですか "May I ask who you are?"; どなたにお取り次ぎしますか "To whom shall I connect you?"). This is not a borrowing or direct model — Dan'a'yo's 何名 is derived compositionally from 何 + 名, and the convergence with Japanese どなた simply reflects that both systems grammaticalised the same pragmatic need. Chinese 哪位 (nǎ wèi, "which [位]?") is the direct parallel: 請問您是哪位？ ("May I ask who you are? — polite"). Korean 어느 분 (eo-neu bun, "which honoured person?") functions identically.

The three-way who-system (何人/誰/何名) is one of Dan'a'yo's most explicitly register-sensitive features, built directly into the correlative table rather than handled by separate honorific vocabulary. Korean parallels this most closely: 누구 (nugu, neutral) / 어느 분 (eo-neu bun, polite) form a two-level system, with rude pressure expressed through sentence-final particles rather than a dedicated rude pronoun. Japanese has だれ (dare, neutral) / どなた (donata, polite), with rudeness expressed prosodically (だれだ! "Who is it?!"). Dan'a'yo's lexically explicit three-level system (rude/neutral/polite) gives the register distinction full grammatical weight.
