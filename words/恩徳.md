## Notes
- [恩](../characters/恩.md) "kindness" + [徳 (char)](../characters/徳%20(char).md) "virtue"
- 恩徳 (Mandarin 恩德 ēndé) pairs 恩 ("kindness, favor bestowed from above") with 徳 ("virtue, moral excellence") to denote benevolence shown from a position of superiority — the gracious conduct of a ruler, deity, parent, or benefactor toward those beneath them, rather than kindness between equals. The compound is attested across the Sinosphere with this same asymmetric flavor: Japanese 恩徳 (おんとく, ontoku) appears in Buddhist and Confucian contexts describing the compassionate grace of a buddha or teacher, Korean 은덕 (eundeok) carries the same sense of grace or favor received, and Sino-Vietnamese ân đức preserves the pairing intact (ân "favor, grace" + đức "virtue").
- Semantically, 恩徳 sits beside its near-synonym [[恩恵]] (the stand-in word for 恩) and [[恩情]] ("kindness, affection") in the family of compounds built on 恩, but is distinguished by foregrounding 徳's connotation of virtue as the source of the grace — the benevolence is framed as a moral quality of the giver, not merely an act or feeling.
