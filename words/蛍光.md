## Notes
- [蛍 (char)](../characters/蛍%20(char).md) "firefly" + [光 (char)](../characters/光%20(char).md) "light"

Two related meanings — classical and scientific — unified by the image of cold, glowing light.

**1. The light of a firefly** (classical meaning). In poetry and traditional writing, 蛍光 evokes the faint, pulsing luminescence of fireflies over summer water — gentle, transient, and cool. It sits at the lower end of a scale of natural lights: 日光 (sunlight), 月光 (moonlight), 星光 (starlight), 蛍光 (firefly-light). The image is associated with summer evenings, rural peace, and the brevity of beautiful things. See also [[蛍火虫]] for the broader cultural context of the firefly in East Asian literature.

**2. Fluorescence** (scientific meaning). The word was extended to describe the phenomenon in physics where a substance absorbs light of one wavelength and re-emits it at a longer wavelength — producing the characteristic glow of fluorescent materials under ultraviolet light. The naming logic is exact: fluorescent light is cold (no heat), spontaneous, and luminous without combustion — like a firefly. The word 蛍光 was coined to capture this quality by analogy.

In modern Japanese, 蛍光 anchors a family of compounds that dominate everyday life:

- **蛍光灯** (keikoōtō, "fluorescent lamp") — the long tubular fluorescent tube that lit virtually every office, school, and home in postwar Japan. Now being phased out in favor of LED lighting, the 蛍光灯 is already becoming a nostalgic object, its distinctive flicker and cold white light an involuntary memory of 20th-century institutional spaces.
- **蛍光ペン** (keikoōpen, "highlighter marker") — the translucent ink marker used to mark text, exploiting fluorescent dyes that glow under both natural and artificial light.
- **蛍光タンパク質** — **Green Fluorescent Protein (GFP)**, a protein derived from the jellyfish *Aequorea victoria* that fluoresces bright green under blue or UV light. The cloning of GFP and its development as a molecular tag revolutionized cell biology; researchers Shimomura, Chalfie, and Tsien were awarded the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 2008. GFP is now one of the most widely used tools in biomedical research, used to make genes, proteins, and entire cells visibly glow so they can be tracked in living organisms.

蛍光 thus traverses from a firefly's glimmer over a summer river to the cutting edge of Nobel Prize science — the same compound covering poetic transience and molecular imaging.
