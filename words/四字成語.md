## Notes
- [四 (char)](../characters/四%20(char).md) "four" + [字 (char)](../characters/字%20(char).md) "character; glyph" + [成 (char)](../characters/成%20(char).md) "to become; fixed; established" + [語](../characters/語.md) "expression; language"

四字成語 is "four-character fixed expression" — a metalinguistic term for the culturally marked category of set phrases four characters in length across the CJKV literary sphere. Literally: "four-character (四字) formed/fixed (成) expression (語)." Once a phrase stabilises at four characters, it carries an implicit cultural frame: rhythmically balanced, semantically dense, and elevated in register. While many four-character sequences are ordinary compounds, 四字成語 names the expectation that such forms carry intertextual depth or aphoristic weight beyond their surface meaning — the four-character length functions as a threshold at which rhythm, memorability, and literary seriousness coalesce. The term is descriptive rather than prescriptive, naming a shared cultural intuition rather than a strict formal category.

Mandarin 四字成語 (sì zì chéng yǔ) is the standard scholarly and educational term; in everyday use, 成語 alone often suffices. The Japanese alias 四字熟語 (よじじゅくご, yoji jukugo) is the preferred Japanese term — 熟語 (jukugo, "ripened/established compound") rather than 成語 (seigo, "formed expression"); Japanese reference works like the 四字熟語辞典 classify and explain thousands of such forms. Korean 사자성어 (四字成語, sa-ja-seong-eo) is widely taught in schools as a distinct category of classical idiom, with dedicated textbooks and entrance-exam components. Vietnamese tứ tự thành ngữ (Sino-Vietnamese: 四=tứ, 字=tự, 成=thành, 語=ngữ) is the formal equivalent.

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 싀지싱요 diverges from Korean 사자성어 across all four syllables. The most notable divergence is 四: Dan'a'yo reads 싀 (siǝ/ㄙㄧㄜ) rather than adopting the Sino-Korean 사 (sa). The 四 character file records the reason explicitly: "In order to keep the ancient Far Eastern symbolism of 'four' being homophonous with 'death', the Korean pronunciation 사 was *not* adopted" — in modern Sino-Korean, 四 (사) is homophonous with 死 (사, death), the source of widespread tetraphobia; Dan'a'yo avoids this by using an older phonological form derived from MC siǝ. The alias 四字熟語 is the Japanese form, differing only in 成→熟 — same concept, different metaphor for the fixedness of the phrase.
