## Notes
- [螟](../characters/螟.md) "stem borer, rice moth larva" + 蛉 "dragonfly larva, small flying insect" — an insect compound that became the classical term for an adopted son; stand-in for [[螟]], which cannot appear independently; 蛉 has no separate Dan'a'yo character entry — its phonology ㄌㄝㄫ is derived from the same rhyme class as 鈴 and 零 (all MC 來母 青韻)

**螟蛉** (*mínglíng*) is at once a zoological term and one of classical Chinese literature's most enduring metaphors for adoption and the transforming power of instruction. Literally it names a larva — specifically the caterpillar of certain moths (螟) preyed upon by the sand wasp (蜾蠃, *guǒluǒ*) — but through a famous misreading of ancient natural history, it became the standard classical expression for an adopted son taken in and raised as one's own.

The source is the 詩經 小雅 小宛 (*Shī jīng*, Ode 196): **螟蛉有子，蜾蠃負之** (*mínglíng yǒu zǐ, guǒluǒ fù zhī*) — "The 螟蛉 has a child; the 蜾蠃 carries it." Ancient commentators interpreted this to mean that the sand wasp, being unable to reproduce, captured 螟蛉 caterpillars, carried them to its nest, and transformed them into wasps through sustained incantation and nurture — singing *類我，類我* ("be like me, be like me") until the caterpillar was remade in the wasp's image. The actual biology is entirely different: the sand wasp paralyses caterpillars and deposits them as food for its own larvae. But the misreading was authoritative for two millennia and generated the compound **螟蛉子** (*mínglíng zǐ*, "螟蛉 child") as the classical term for an adopted son — one taken from outside and transformed by instruction into a member of the family.

The metaphysical implication was significant. Where biological inheritance was the dominant model of character transmission, the 螟蛉 story offered a rival model: character is made by instruction and environment, not born from blood. Confucian thinkers found it congenial — it aligned with 教化 (*jiàohuà*, civilising instruction) as the primary mechanism of human cultivation. The story was also a cautionary one: not all 螟蛉 are successfully transformed, and adopted sons of uncertain origin carried social and legal ambiguities through much of Chinese legal history.

Korean **명령** (*myeongryeong*) is the Sino-Korean reading of 螟蛉, but is entirely overshadowed by the identical pronunciation of 命令 (*myeongryeong*, order, command) — making 螟蛉 and 命令 exact homophones in Korean. The classical 螟蛉子 sense (adopted son) survives in literary usage. Japanese **めいれい** (*meirei*) is the Sino-Japanese reading; **螟蛉子** (*meireishi* or *meirei no ko*) appears in classical literary and philological contexts for adopted sons. Vietnamese **minh linh** (*minh* from 螟, *linh* from 蛉) is the Sino-Vietnamese compound; the adoption metaphor does not appear to be widely current in Vietnamese literary tradition, where **con nuôi** (foster/adopted child) is the standard term.
