## Notes
- [安](../characters/安.md) "peaceful" + [土 (char)](../characters/土%20(char).md) "earth" — literally "peaceful earth," a native Japanese place name (あづち) read with native readings rather than borrowed Sino-Japanese values, the same pattern as [[鎌倉]] and [[奈良]]. It names a hill on the eastern shore of Lake Biwa in present-day Shiga Prefecture.

Azuchi is famous for Azuchi Castle (安土城), which Oda Nobunaga began building there in 1576 as the seat from which he intended to govern a unified Japan. The castle was extraordinary for its time: a seven-story tenshu (castle keep) decorated internally with gold leaf and paintings by Kanō Eitoku, combining Japanese castle architecture with European-influenced elements Nobunaga had encountered through contact with Portuguese traders and Jesuit missionaries. It stood for barely six years — Nobunaga was killed in the Honnō-ji Incident in 1582, and Azuchi Castle burned down shortly afterward under contested circumstances, never to be rebuilt. Despite this brief existence, Azuchi gives its name, paired with [[桃山]] (Momoyama), to the Azuchi–Momoyama period (1568–1600/1603) spanning Nobunaga's and then Toyotomi Hideyoshi's drives toward national unification, which followed the collapse of Ashikaga shogunal authority during the [[室町|Muromachi]] period's Sengoku era.
