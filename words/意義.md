## Notes
- [意](../characters/意.md) "meaning, intent" + [義](../characters/義.md) "meaning, principle" — stand-in for [[義]], which cannot appear independently.

Korean and Japanese both draw a sharp register/sense line between this compound and its sibling 意味/意味 (imi/의미), which is not shared with the same clarity by Mandarin. 뜻 (the plain native word) and 意味/의미 cover ordinary "meaning" — the meaning of a word, a sentence, a gesture — while 意義/의의 leans specifically toward "significance, importance, value": "이번 회의의 의의" (the significance of this meeting), 意義深い (significant, meaningful in a weighty sense), rather than a word's dictionary definition. Vietnamese ý nghĩa doesn't split the same way — it covers both "meaning" and "significance" without a separate everyday word doing the plainer job, closer to how English "meaning" itself stretches to cover both senses.
