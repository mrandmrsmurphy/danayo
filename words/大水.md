## Notes
- [大 (char)](../characters/大%20(char).md) "great, large" + [水 (char)](../characters/水%20(char).md) "water" — great waters; an inundation.

大水 and [[洪水]] are near-synonyms for flood, with overlapping usage. 大水 is more colloquial and more common in southern Chinese varieties; 洪水 is the dominant modern written Chinese and formal term. In biblical Chinese translation, both appear: the Great Flood of Noah is rendered 大洪水 or simply 洪水, while 大水 appears in Psalms and prophetic texts for the imagery of overwhelming waters.

The Yellow River (黃河) is the historical backdrop against which Chinese civilization understood 大水. Flooding of the Yellow River was a recurring catastrophe across three millennia — not a once-in-a-generation event but a constant feature of North China governance. The legendary figure of 大禹 (the Great Yu) is defined entirely by his mastery of flood control: unlike his father Gun, who tried to dam the waters, Yu channeled them, dredging river courses for thirteen years without returning home, becoming the paradigm of selfless public service. 大禹治水 — "Yu the Great tames the flood" — remains a living idiom for large-scale infrastructure effort.

The Huang He's notorious unpredictability gave rise to the political concept that legitimacy was connected to hydraulic order: a dynasty that could not control the rivers was one whose 天命 (Mandate of Heaven) was slipping.
