## Notes
- [弓 (char)](../characters/弓%20(char).md) "bow" + [道 (char)](../characters/道%20(char).md) "the Way; discipline" — same 道-suffix pattern as [[書道]], [[柔道]], [[剣道]], [[空手道]]

**弓道** (*gōngdào*, Japanese *kyūdō*) is the Japanese art of archery — the discipline of the bow framed as a path of self-cultivation rather than a purely martial or competitive skill. The 道 suffix (Japanese *dō*) marks it as one of several modern reframings of older craft or warrior traditions into lifelong practices of inner development: alongside [[柔道]] (*jūdō*), [[剣道]] (*kendō*), [[空手道]] (*karate-dō*), and [[書道]] (*shodō*), it belongs to the Japanese cultural category of *dō* disciplines in which physical mastery and moral-spiritual formation are understood as inseparable.

The older term is **弓術** (*kyūjutsu*, "bow technique"), the martial archery transmitted through Japan's medieval warrior class. 弓術 was war-practical: speed, power, mounted accuracy. The transition to 弓道 as the standard term came in the early twentieth century and was shaped by two pressures: the demilitarisation of the warrior arts after the Meiji Restoration, and a conscious effort to reformulate them as vehicles for national-character development. The word 弓道 was advocated and popularised by Uno Yōzaburō (宇野要三郎) around 1919; the Dai Nippon Butoku Kai (大日本武徳会) standardised technique and terminology across regional schools. The Zen Nihon Kyūdō Renmei (全日本弓道連盟, All Japan Kyudo Federation), founded in 1953, now governs the art globally and maintains the canonical form sequences (*sharei* for ceremonial shooting, the instructional *kyōhon* for technique).

The bow of 弓道 is the **和弓** (*wakyū*), a long asymmetric bow of approximately 221 cm — by far the longest bow in active use among world archery traditions. Its grip sits well below the geometric centre, with roughly two-thirds of the bow above the hand and one-third below. This asymmetry distinguishes it from every other major bow tradition and is generally attributed to the practical constraints of nocking and drawing while mounted. Traditional construction laminates bamboo, wood, and leather through a complex multi-year process; fibreglass and carbon composites are now standard for practice. The prescribed draw method places the arrow on the right side of the bow for right-handed archers — again inverting the convention of most other traditions — and the release uses a gloved thumb-draw (the *yugake*) rather than a finger release.

Eugen Herrigel's *Zen in der Kunst des Bogenschießens* (1948; English: *Zen in the Art of Archery*) introduced 弓道 to a wide Western readership, framing it as a vehicle for Zen enlightenment. Herrigel studied under Awa Kenzō (阿波研造), whose approach was idiosyncratic even within Japan and would not have been recognised as representative by most *kyūdō* practitioners of the time. Scholars — notably Yamada Shōji — have argued that Herrigel substantially misunderstood his teacher's instruction, and that the Zen framing is largely a retrospective Western projection. The book nonetheless fixed the association of 弓道 with Zen in Western popular culture, and that association has in turn influenced how the art is packaged and explained to foreign students.

Korean **국궁** (*gukgung*, 國弓, "national bow") is the cognate traditional Korean archery, using a shorter composite reflex bow of horn, bamboo, and sinew — far more powerful for its size than the *wakyū* and optimised for longer distances. The Sino-Vietnamese compound **궁도** (*gungdo*) — equivalent to 弓道 — is used in Korean for the art form, but 국궁 designates the Korean tradition specifically, which is distinct from Japanese 弓道 in equipment, technique, and competitive format. Vietnamese **cung đạo** is the Sino-Vietnamese rendering; Vietnamese traditional archery is not a living competitive tradition in the same sense, and *cung đạo* is used primarily when referring to the Japanese discipline.
