## Notes
- [象](../characters/象.md) "elephant; image, likeness" + [形 (char)](../characters/形%20(char).md) "shape, form" — one of the [[六書]] (liùshū), the traditional six-category classification of Chinese characters, and traditionally placed first among them.

**象形** (*xiàngxíng*, lit. "imaging the shape") characters are direct, stylized depictions of a concrete object: the graph's outline traces the thing itself, rather than combining sound and sense the way later [[形声]] characters do. The textbook examples are 山 ("mountain," three peaks), 水 ("water," a flowing stream), 木 ("tree," trunk and branches), 日 ("sun," a circle with a stroke), 月 ("moon," a crescent), and 人 ("person," a standing figure in profile) — and the vocabulary's own character pages cite this category often, including for [[立]] itself, whose oracle-bone form depicts a person standing on the ground.

象形 characters are comparatively few — most modern estimates put true pictograms at well under 5% of the script, since most concepts cannot be drawn directly — but they sit at the historical root of the whole system, and the other structural categories are often described as successive departures from this original pictorial principle: [[指事]] adds an abstract mark to a pictographic base, [[会意]] combines two or more meaningful graphs (sometimes themselves 象形 in origin), and 形声 layers a phonetic hint on top of a semantic, often once-pictographic, component.

In Japanese pedagogy 象形文字 (*shōkei moji*) is taught as the first of the four "structural" categories of 六書, alongside 指事文字, 会意文字, and 形声文字 (the remaining two, 転注 and 仮借, describe usage rather than structure and are not yet in the vocabulary). Korean (상형문자, *sanghyeong munja*) and Vietnamese (chữ tượng hình) scholarly traditions preserve the same framework and terminology, again tracing to the Han-dynasty *Shuowen Jiezi*. Attested across Mandarin, Cantonese, Japanese, Korean, and Vietnamese.
