## Notes
- [我 (char)](../characters/我%20(char).md) "I, me" + [等 (char)](../characters/等%20(char).md) "etc., rank"

我等 is "we, us" in the formal/classical register — 等 functioning as the same classical collective-plural suffix seen in [[人等]] ("persons") and, historically, in 爾等/尔等 ("you all") and 彼等 ("they"). Modern colloquial "we" is instead 我們/我哋 (built with 們/哋, not 等) — genuinely different words that were previously, incorrectly, listed as aliases of this page; removed, since they don't actually spell 我等.

Mandarin 我等 (wǒděng) survives mainly in classical/literary Chinese and set legal phrasing rather than everyday speech, where 我們 is universal. Cantonese 我等 (ngo5dang2) patterns the same way, formal/literary against everyday 我哋. Japanese is the one member of the family where the X等-pattern "we" is still genuinely current: 我等/我ら (われら, warera) is a real, actively used formal/literary word for "we" — found in oaths, anthems, and formal declarations — read with the native kun'yomi われ (ware, "I," archaic/literary) rather than an on'yomi. Korean 아등 (a-deung) is likewise a real, attested classical/literary "we," one of a small family of near-synonyms built the same way (오등, 여등, 아배, 오배) — famously, the closely related 吾等 (오등) opens the 1919 Korean Declaration of Independence ("吾等은 玆에 我 朝鮮의 獨立國임과…", "We hereby proclaim the independence of Korea…"), though 아등 itself is a separate, if parallel, formation. Vietnamese ngã đẳng is attested the same way, alongside công đẳng ("you gentlemen," 公等) and nhĩ đẳng ("you all," 爾等) — the same X-等/X-đẳng pattern productively forming classical-register plural pronouns, exactly mirroring 人等's own note on this construction.

kwin is false: 我 alone matches its own Sino-Korean reading exactly (아=아), but 等 does not (Dan'a'yo 둥 vs Korean 등 — a vowel mismatch), so the compound's `kwin` comes out false.
