## Notes
- [台 (char)](../characters/台%20(char).md) "platform; machines"

台 in Dan'a'yo carries a dual role: as a noun it preserves the core meaning of 臺, the traditional form it simplifies — a raised surface serving as a foundation, stage, or display base; as a classifier (量詞) it counts machines, vehicles, computers, and large appliances, following the productive use established in Mandarin Chinese. The simplified character 台 has absorbed two other traditionally distinct graphs — 颱 (typhoon, now 台風 in modern Chinese) and the archaic first-person pronoun 台 (yí, used in literary texts predating 我) — making it one of the more semantically overloaded simplifications in the sinographic system. In Dan'a'yo, 一台電視 (one television set), 三台電腦 (three computers), and 一台自動車 (one automobile) illustrate the classifier function.

The classifier sense diverges and converges across the CJKV sphere. In Mandarin, 台 (tái) is the standard measure word for machines and large appliances: 一台电视 (one television set), 一台手术 (one surgical procedure — extended sense). In Japanese, 台 (DAI) counts machines, vehicles, and large furniture: 一台のパソコン (one personal computer), 三台の車 (three cars); 台所 (daidokoro, "kitchen") preserves an older sense of "work platform." In Cantonese, 台 (toi4) refers to a raised platform or table surface: 一台戲 (one theatrical performance — measuring events performed on a stage). Korean 대 (台) appears in Sino-Korean technical compounds and is used colloquially as a classifier for machines and vehicles: 자동차 한 대 (one car). In Vietnamese, đài generalises to towers and broadcasting stations: đài truyền hình (television station), đài phát thanh (radio station).

kwin is true: Dan'a'yo 대 exactly matches Korean 대. 台 reads 대 (dai/ㄉㄚㄧ) in Dan'a'yo — MC 定母 (d) + 咍韻 (ʌi) gives a voiced stop onset with the -ai diphthong. Both Dan'a'yo and Sino-Korean map MC 定母 to a plain stop initial and MC 咍韻 to the -ai/-ae vowel, and both write the result as 대 in Hangul. The MC 咍韻 is one of the more regularly preserved finals in both systems, consistently giving -ai in Dan'a'yo and -ae in Korean — written identically as ㅐ, producing the same 대 in both.
