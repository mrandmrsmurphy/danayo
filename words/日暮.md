## Notes
- [日 (char)](../characters/日%20(char).md) "day, sun" + [暮](../characters/暮.md) "dusk, twilight, late" — the close of the day; stand-in for [[暮]], which cannot appear independently

**日暮** (*rìmù*) is dusk — the moment when the day ends and darkness begins. The compound is more literary than colloquial: everyday Mandarin reaches for **傍晚** (*bàngwǎn*, evening) or **黃昏** (*huánghūn*, twilight) where 日暮 belongs to verse, prose poetry, and formal writing. The classical image of 日暮 carries weight beyond the hour: it is a moment of transition, vulnerability, and the passage of time made visible.

The 暮 character depicts the sun (日) setting among grass or reeds (艹/莫), encoding dusk directly in its graphic form. It extends from literal evening into temporal metaphors for old age, decline, and the end of an era: **暮年** (*mùnián*, one's twilight years), **暮氣** (*mùqì*, the air of decline and exhaustion). The compound **日暮途窮** (*rìmù túqióng*, the day ends and the road is exhausted) is a classical idiom for desperate, irrecoverable crisis — when time has run out and there is nowhere left to go. Its opposite is **朝氣蓬勃** (*zhāoqì péngbó*, morning energy overflowing) — youth, vitality, the dawn side of the same temporal axis.

日暮 appears throughout the Tang lyric tradition as a stock image of melancholy and separation: travellers who have not yet reached their destination, soldiers far from home, officials in exile watching the light fail. Li Bai's **靜夜思** opens not with 日暮 but with moonlight, yet the mood is identical — the quiet, lonely end of a day that puts distance into relief.

Japanese **ひぐれ** (*higure*) is the native reading of 日暮れ (often written with the trailing れ) and is the standard word for dusk in modern Japanese: the Sino-Japanese compound にちぼ (*nichibo*) is read in classical or technical contexts but ひぐれ dominates in living usage. Korean **일모** (*ilmo*) is the Sino-Korean reading, used in literary and formal contexts; everyday Korean uses **황혼** (黃昏, hwanghon) for twilight and **해질녘** (native, the time of sunset) for dusk. Vietnamese **nhật mộ** (*nhật* from 日, *mộ* from 暮) is the Sino-Vietnamese literary compound; common Vietnamese uses **hoàng hôn** (黃昏) or **chạng vạng** (native) for the same hour.
