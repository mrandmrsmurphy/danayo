## Notes
- [鳳](../characters/鳳.md) "(male) phoenix" + [凰](../characters/凰.md) "female phoenix"

鳳凰 is the paired mythological bird of Chinese cosmology. Unlike the Western phoenix, it carries no death-and-rebirth cycle: it is a creature of perfect virtue that manifests only when a sage ruler governs or when the realm is at peace. Its plumage is said to incorporate all five elemental colours; its song encompasses the five notes of the pentatonic scale; it eats only bamboo seeds and drinks only from pure springs. The earliest textual appearances are in the Shangshu and the Zuozhuan, where sightings serve as omens of virtuous rule rather than dramatic transformation.

The compound is a hendiadys — 鳳 and 凰 are nominally male and female birds of the same species, but in almost all literary and artistic contexts they function as a set rather than as separately distinguished individuals. The pairing of phoenix with dragon (龍鳳) became the emblematic coupling of empress and emperor in the imperial period. The phoenix appears extensively in architectural ornamentation, court embroidery, and ceremonial regalia across Chinese, Japanese, Korean, and Vietnamese cultural traditions, where it retained its associations with imperial legitimacy and cosmic order.

Across the CJKV sphere the compound is stable: Mandarin 鳳凰 (fènghuáng), Japanese 鳳凰 (hōō, historically ほうわう in classical kana), Korean 봉황 (bonghwang), Vietnamese phượng hoàng. The kwin=false marking reflects a systematic phonological divergence: Dan'a'yo 풍황 (pung + hwang, concatenated from the sinographic readings) versus Korean 봉황, where 鳳 corresponds to 봉 in Korean Sino-Korean phonology but to pung in Dan'a'yo — a consistent vowel difference in this phonological class.
