## Notes
- [詐](../characters/詐.md) "to deceive; to defraud" + [取](../characters/取.md) "to take; to seize"

詐取 combines 詐 — to deceive, to act falsely — with 取 — to take or seize — forming the legal compound for obtaining something by deception. The graphemic logic is precise: 詐 (phonetic 乍, semantic 言) embeds falseness of speech, while 取 (會意 of hand seizing an ear, originally a war-trophy gesture) denotes the act of appropriation. Together they specify the crime of fraudulent acquisition rather than simple theft: the key element is that the victim was deceived into yielding what was taken.

The term is used across all five source languages but occupies different registers. In Mandarin, 詐取 (zhàqǔ) appears in legal statutes and newspaper reports of financial fraud, but everyday speech more commonly uses 欺騙 (deceive) or 詐騙 (swindle). In Japanese, 詐取 (sashū) is a legal term found in criminal law — specifically in the law of fraud (詐欺罪, *sagie sai*, covers obtaining by deception) — but the broader word 詐欺 (sagi) is the standard term in public discourse. Korean 사취 (sa-chwi) appears in similar legal contexts. The compound is less common in Vietnamese, where gian lận (fraud) or lừa đảo (swindle) serve the colloquial function.

In Dan'a'yo, 詐取 functions as the transitive verb of fraudulent acquisition. The kwin=false marking reflects the phonological divergence between Dan'a'yo 자촛 (ja + cou) and Korean 사취 (sa-chwi), where both 詐 and 取 map to different vowels across the two systems.
