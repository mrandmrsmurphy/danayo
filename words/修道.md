## Notes
- [修](../characters/修.md) "to cultivate; to refine; to repair" + [道 (char)](../characters/道%20(char).md) "Way; path; doctrine"

修道 denotes the deliberate practice of spiritual cultivation — the disciplined effort to refine oneself in accordance with a transcendent Way. 修 carries the sense of methodical improvement or repair of something that has strayed from its proper form; 道 here is not merely a physical path but the cosmological and ethical principle that underlies Daoist, Buddhist, and Confucian thought alike. Together they describe the practitioner's sustained effort to align with that principle through meditation, ritual observance, ethical conduct, and self-examination.

The compound appears across all three major religious traditions of East Asia. In Daoism, 修道 describes the cultivation practices aimed at achieving harmony with the Tao, including inner alchemy (內丹) and ritual practice. In Buddhism, the term is used for the path of practice leading to enlightenment, often distinguished from 悟道 (awakening to the Way) as the sustained effort that precedes or follows illumination. In Neo-Confucianism, 修道 echoes the Zhongyong's formula 修道之謂教 — "refining the Way is called teaching" — placing self-cultivation at the center of moral pedagogy. The compound extends naturally into institutional contexts: 修道院 (monastery or convent) and 修道士 (monk) derive from it across Chinese, Japanese, Korean, and Vietnamese.

In Japanese, 修道 (shūdō) survives primarily in compound forms — 修道院, 修道会 — rather than as a standalone; everyday speech uses 修行 (shugyō, ascetic practice) for the same concept. Korean 수도 (sudo) is parallel: 수도원 (monastery) is common, while standalone 수도 for spiritual practice competes with 수행 (suhaeng). Dan'a'yo 修道 follows the classical Chinese form, using it as the general noun for spiritual discipline without denominational specification.
