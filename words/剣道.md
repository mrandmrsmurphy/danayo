## Notes
- [剣 (char)](../characters/剣%20(char).md) "sword" + [道 (char)](../characters/道%20(char).md) "the Way; discipline" — same 道-suffix pattern as [[書道]], [[弓道]], [[空手道]], [[柔道]]

**剣道** (*kendō*, "the Way of the Sword") is the modern Japanese martial art of swordsmanship using the *shinai* (竹刀, a bamboo practice sword) and protective armour (*bōgu*, 防具), descended from the swordsmanship traditions (*kenjutsu*, 剣術) of the samurai. Like [[弓道]] and [[柔道]], it belongs to the *dō*-suffixed family of disciplines that reframe older martial techniques as paths of lifelong self-cultivation, formalised in the early twentieth century under the patronage of the Dai Nippon Butoku Kai (大日本武徳会) and codified after the Second World War under the All Japan Kendo Federation (全日本剣道連盟, Zen Nihon Kendō Renmei), founded in 1952.

剣道 practice centres on strikes to designated target areas — the head (*men*, 面), wrists (*kote*, 小手), torso (*dō*, 胴), and throat (*tsuki*, 突き) — each scored according to strict criteria of form, spirit, and timing (*ki-ken-tai-icchi*, 気剣体一致, "unity of spirit, sword, and body"). As in 弓道, technical mastery is understood as inseparable from character development; the discipline retains strong ties to its samurai-era origins in both etiquette (*reigi*, 礼儀) and in the symbolic continuity between the *shinai* and the live blade (*katana*) it represents.

Korean 검도 (*geomdo*) is both a cognate loan of the Japanese term and the name of a closely related, competitively distinct discipline practiced in Korea, with its own governing federations and stylistic differences from Japanese 剣道. Mandarin 劍道 (*jiàndào*) and Cantonese *gim3 dou6*, and Vietnamese *kiếm đạo*, are used primarily to refer to the Japanese (or Korean) sport when discussing it, rather than denoting an indigenous tradition in those cultures — paralleling the situation of [[弓道]] and 國弓/국궁 in Korean archery.
