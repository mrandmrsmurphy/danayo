## Notes
- [厨](../characters/厨.md) "kitchen; cook" + [房](../characters/房%20(char).md) "room" — stand-in for [[厨]], which cannot appear independently
- 厨房 names the room where 厨 — "kitchen, cook" — happens, the most literal kind of compound: the function plus the space it occupies. The word keeps this plain transparency across the Sinosphere as the standard term for a kitchen, whether domestic or professional: Mandarin chúfáng and Cantonese cyu4 fong4 (which can also extend to mean "cook, chef" by metonymy — the room standing in for the person who works in it), Sino-Korean 주방 (jubang, the modern, slightly more technical word favored for restaurant and appliance-equipped kitchens, set against the older native 부엌), Sino-Japanese ちゅうぼう (chūbō, likewise the more formal or commercial register, beside native daidokoro 台所), and Sino-Vietnamese trù phòng, which sits alongside the everyday native nhà bếp. Across all four living readings, the same pattern recurs: the Sino-Xenic compound supplies the formal or institutional word for "kitchen," while each language keeps its own native term for the cozier, more domestic version of the same room.
