## Notes
- [氷](../characters/氷.md) "ice" + [水](../characters/水.md) "water" — stand-in for [[氷]], which cannot appear independently

**A pleasing parallel semantic extension across two languages**: both Japanese and Korean have taken this literal "ice water" compound and extended it to a specific summer dessert — shaved ice with syrup/toppings. Japanese 氷水 has several attested readings (こおりみず, こおりすい, ひみず; the compound appears with this dessert sense as far back as Sei Shōnagon's Pillow Book, c. 1000 CE), functioning as a synonym of かき氷 (kakigōri). Korean 빙수 (bingsu) has undergone the identical drift and is now the standard, internationally-recognized name for Korean shaved-ice dessert (patbingsu, etc.) — independently arriving at the same figurative extension as Japanese, not a borrowing of one from the other. Mandarin bīngshuǐ and Cantonese bing1 seoi2 keep the plain literal sense, "ice water" (the drink). Vietnamese băng thủy is a real but classical/Buddhist-register Sino-Vietnamese compound (verified via search); the everyday word for ice water is the native nước đá.
