## Notes
**Form.** 吸金 is 吸 “to absorb; to take in” + 金 “metal,” following the rule for lanthanides.

**Etymological situation.** _Samarium_ derives from the mineral **samarskite**, itself named after the Russian official Samarsky. This is a **purely eponymic chain** with no usable semantic content in Greek/Latin roots. Under your constraints (no phonetic borrowing), the etymology is effectively unusable.

**Motivation.** Shift to a **salient physical property**:

- samarium compounds and alloys are notable for **neutron absorption** (e.g., Sm-149 in nuclear control rods)
- “absorbing metal” is a concise, meaningful descriptor

吸 captures the act of taking in or absorbing, which maps well onto this nuclear property.

**Type of formation.**  
A **property-based designation** used when etymology fails:

- replaces an opaque proper-name origin
- emphasizes a scientifically relevant behavior

**Fit within the system.**

1. Meaning-based, no phonetic compromise.
2. Uses 金 appropriately for a metal.
3. Provides a clear, high-frequency symbol (吸).
4. Aligns with your fallback strategy (cf. 硬金, 磁金).

**Comparative CJKV forms.**

- Modern Chinese: 钐 (phonetic)
- Japanese: サマリウム (phonetic)
- Korean: 사마륨 (phonetic)
- Vietnamese: samari (phonetic)

Modern forms are phonetic; 吸金 introduces a functional semantic label.

**Clarity and issues.**

- 吸 is general (“to absorb”), not unique to samarium; many substances absorb.
- However, within a fixed mapping, uniqueness is enforced by assignment rather than intrinsic meaning.
- The neutron-absorption property is not universally known at an elementary level, but it is scientifically grounded.

**Conclusion.**  
吸金 is a reasonable and consistent solution. With no viable etymology to preserve, a property-based name centered on absorption is appropriate and fits your system’s logic.
