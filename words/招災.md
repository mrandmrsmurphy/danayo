## Notes
- [招](../characters/招.md) "to summon, to beckon" + [災](../characters/災.md) "disaster" — literally "to summon disaster," i.e. to bring ruin upon oneself through one's own actions.

招災 (zhāozāi) is a verb-object compound that pairs the beckoning sense of 招 — normally used for calling someone over with a wave of the hand, as in [[招手]] — with 災, "calamity" (cf. [[災害]], [[災殃]]). The metaphor treats disaster as something one can unwittingly "wave over" through folly, transgression, or recklessness, framing misfortune as the consequence of one's own conduct rather than blind chance.

The compound is best attested as the first half of the four-character idiom [[招災引禍]], "to invite disaster and draw calamity," where it is paired with its near-synonym [[引禍]]. The doubling of 招 and 引 — both verbs of summoning or drawing something toward oneself — intensifies the sense of self-induced causation. The chengyu page glosses the classical parallel 自招禍患 ("to bring calamity upon oneself"), found in Chinese Bible translations rendering the principle that sin provokes its own judgment.

The on-yomi しょうさい (SHOU + SAI), Sino-Korean 초재 (chojae), and Sino-Vietnamese chiêu tai follow the same compositional pattern across all four traditions, and all four readings can be read directly off the corresponding segment of [[招災引禍]]'s own pronunciation fields. Note the Korean 諺文 spelling 촛재 (with a final ㅈ-cluster on 招) versus the plain Sino-Korean reading 초재 — these differ, reflecting the usual divergence between Dan'a'yo's phonological transcription and standard Sino-Korean orthography.

For usage in context, see [[招災引禍]].
