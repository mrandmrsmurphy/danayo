## Notes
- [石 (char)](../characters/石%20(char).md) "stone; rock" + [像](../characters/像.md) "statue; image; likeness"

石像 is the standard compound for a statue made of stone, as opposed to bronze (銅像), wood (木像), or clay (土像). The distinction matters in East Asian material culture, where the medium is semantically significant: stone statues carry associations of permanence and monumental scale, and 石像 appears most frequently in the context of Buddhist temple guardians, ancestral memorial figures, and political monuments. The phonetic logic of 像 (phonetic 象, the elephant — itself an image of a large and impressive thing) reinforces the connotation of weighty, imposing representation.

Across the CJKV sphere the compound is stable and transparent. Mandarin shíxiàng, Japanese せきぞう (sekizō), Korean 석상 (seoksang), and Vietnamese tượng đá all denote the same category. In Japanese, 石像 competes with the broader term 彫像 (chōzō, "carved figure," medium-neutral) and the more specific 仏像 (butsuzō, Buddhist image), with 石像 reserved for the material specification. In Korean, 석상 appears frequently in descriptions of archaeological finds — the famous Easter Island moai are referred to as 석상 in Korean media, as are the stone figures of Jeju Island's dol hareubang.

The kwin=false marking reflects the phonological divergence between Dan'a'yo 석샹 (seg + syang, concatenated from the sinographic readings) and Korean 석상 (seoksang), where 像 maps to 상 (sang) in Korean but to 샹 (syang) in Dan'a'yo — the palatalisation difference is systematic across this character class.
