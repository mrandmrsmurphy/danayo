## Notes
**Form.** 磁金 is [磁](characters/磁.md) “magnet; magnetic” + [金 (char)](characters/金%20(char).md) “metal,” following the rule for lanthanides as 金-elements.

**Etymology.** _Neodymium_ comes from Greek νέος (_neos_) “new” + δίδυμος (_didymos_) “twin,” i.e., “new twin,” referring to its separation from praseodymium. The etymon is relational and not easily mapped to a single, distinctive graph.

**Motivation.** 磁 shifts from etymology to a **salient modern property**:

- neodymium is the key component of high-strength permanent magnets (Nd–Fe–B)
- “magnetic metal” is a strong, widely recognized association

This makes the name informative in a way the Greek is not.

**Type of formation.**  
A **property-based designation** used when the original etymology is unsuitable:

- retains distinctiveness versus 韮金 (praseodymium)
- avoids forcing “new/twin” into an awkward or colliding form

**Fit within the system.**

1. Meaning-based, no phonetic borrowing.
2. Uses 金 appropriately for a metal.
3. Provides a clear, high-frequency symbol (磁).

**Comparative CJKV forms.**

- Modern Chinese: 钕 (phonetic)
- Japanese: ネオジム (phonetic)
- Korean: 네오디뮴 (phonetic)
- Vietnamese: neodymi (phonetic)

Modern usage is phonetic; 磁金 introduces a functional semantic label.

**Clarity and issues.**

- 磁 is not unique to neodymium; many elements exhibit magnetism (Fe, Co, Ni).
- However, neodymium’s role in **strong permanent magnets** is distinctive enough to justify the choice within a fixed mapping.

**Conclusion.**  
磁金 is a pragmatic and effective solution. It abandons the weak Greek etymology in favor of a defining modern property, maintaining clarity and distinctiveness.
