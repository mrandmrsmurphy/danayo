## Notes
- [父](../characters/父.md) "father" + [親](../characters/親.md) "parent" — stand-in for [[父]], which cannot appear independently

父親 is "father" — reinforcing 父 with the general noun 親 "parent," the direct counterpart of [[母親]]. 父 cannot appear as an independent vault entry on its own, so 父親 is what legitimizes it.

Mandarin 父親 (fùqīn) is a genuine everyday word, more formal/written than the affectionate 爸爸 (bàba). Cantonese 父親 (fu6can1) patterns the same way. Japanese 父親 (ちちおや, chichioya) is a real, common, neutral word for talking *about* someone's father in the third person — 彼の父親 ("his father") — distinct from the direct-address terms お父さん (otōsan) or the casual パパ (papa), exactly mirroring 母親/ははおや's own role. Korean 부친 (buchin) is a real formal/written-register word — appearing in biographies, official documents, and news writing, the direct counterpart of 모친 — while everyday spoken Korean uses 아버지 (abeoji) or 아빠 (appa). Vietnamese phụ thân is a real, formal/classical word from literature and ritual contexts, the counterpart of mẫu thân — everyday Vietnamese instead says cha, bố, or ba.

kwin is true: both 父 and 親 individually match their own Sino-Korean readings exactly (부=부, 친=친), so the compound comes out true — the word's frontmatter previously stored `kwin: false` incorrectly; corrected here, since 父親's own Dan'a'yo 부친 is in fact identical to the real Korean word 부친.
