## Notes
- [墨](../characters/墨.md) "ink" + [西](../characters/西.md) "west" + [哥](../characters/哥.md) "elder brother" — phonetic rendering of *México*; the characters carry no semantic content

墨西哥 transcribes the Spanish and Nahuatl name *México*, from the Nahuatl *Mēxihco*. The etymology of the Nahuatl original is disputed: the dominant interpretation derives it from *Mētztli* (moon) + *xīctli* (navel) + *-co* (locative), "place at the navel of the moon," referring to the position of Tenochtitlan at the center of Lake Texcoco; alternatively it is read as simply "place of the Mexica," the Nahuatl-speaking people who founded the Aztec empire. 墨 alone serves as the standard Sinographic abbreviation for Mexico across all Chinese-using communities — 墨国, 墨人, 墨西哥城 — and is included as an alias.

Contact between the Chinese-speaking world and Mexico predates modern nation-states. The Manila Galleon trade (1565–1815), running between Acapulco and Manila, made New Spain the primary conduit through which Chinese silk, porcelain, and luxury goods entered the Americas, paid for in Mexican silver. Thousands of Chinese and Filipino laborers settled in New Spain under this system; the *Nao de China* (China Ship) was the most commercially significant transpacific route of the early modern period, and Mexican silver pesos circulated as currency in China itself. This makes Mexico, paradoxically, one of the countries with the deepest material entanglement with the Sinosphere despite having no place in the classical literary canon.
