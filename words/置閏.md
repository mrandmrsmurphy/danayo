## Notes
- [置 (char)](../characters/置%20(char).md) "put; place; install" + [閏 (char)](../characters/閏%20(char).md) "intercalary"

The act and system of intercalation — inserting an extra unit of time (a day, month, or second) into a calendar to re-synchronize it with astronomical cycles. 置 (to place, to install) + 閏 (intercalary) = "to install an intercalary [period]." Where [[閏日]], [[閏月]], and [[閏秒]] name the inserted units, 置閏 names the practice and principle.

The need for intercalation is structural: the natural cycles that humans wish to track do not divide evenly into one another. The solar year (~365.2422 days) does not contain a whole number of lunar months (~29.5306 days); neither fits neatly into a week of 7 days. Any calendar that tries to track both the Sun and the Moon must periodically insert extra time to prevent drift — without intercalation, the seasons would cycle through all calendar months within a few decades.

**The classical East Asian lunisolar system** is the most elaborate intercalation tradition in the Sinosphere. The traditional Chinese calendar tracks both the Moon (each month beginning at new moon) and the Sun (the year divided into 24 solar terms, 節気). A standard lunar year of 12 months contains only about 354 days — 11 days shorter than the solar year. Left uncorrected, the months would drift through the seasons: New Year would fall in summer within a generation. To prevent this, an extra **閏月** (intercalary month) is inserted **7 times in every 19-year cycle** — the Metonic cycle, independently discovered by Greek astronomer Meton of Athens (432 BC) and by Chinese astronomers of the Warring States period.

The choice of *which* month becomes the leap month is not arbitrary. Chinese calendrical astronomers determined it by identifying which lunar month contained no **中気** (principal solar term) — essentially, which month was "empty" of a Sun milestone. That month became the 閏月, inserted as a repetition of the preceding month. This rule produced a mathematically elegant and astronomically accurate system that remained in use across China, Korea (as 음력), Japan, and Vietnam (as âm lịch) for over two millennia.

The imperial astronomical bureau — China's **欽天監** (Qīntiānjiàn), Japan's **陰陽寮** (Onmyōryō), Korea's **관상감** (Gwansanggam) — was responsible for computing the placement of leap months and publishing the official calendar each year. Errors in the calendar, or unauthorized publication of an alternative calendar, were serious state offenses: the calendar was a symbol of imperial authority, and correct 置閏 was a measure of dynastic legitimacy. The emperor's mandate to govern was understood to include governing time itself.
