## Notes
- [田](../characters/田.md) "field; paddy field" + [鼠](../characters/鼠.md) "rat; mouse"

Field voles and field rats — rodents inhabiting agricultural land, meadows, and grasslands rather than human dwellings (compare [[家鼠]]). The two main groups called 田鼠 in Chinese:

**Voles** (subfamily Arvicolinae) — small, round-bodied, short-tailed rodents with blunt snouts. The reed vole (*Microtus fortis*) and plateau vole (*Microtus oeconomus*) are common across northern China. Unlike rats and mice, voles primarily eat grasses and plant material rather than seeds and stored grain, making them major pests in pasture and hay agriculture.

**Asian rice field rats** (*Rattus argentiventer*, *Rattus losea*, and relatives) — medium-sized rats that feed on rice plants and grain. Devastating to paddy agriculture across Southeast and East Asia. Rice field rats caused an estimated 5–15% crop loss across rice-growing regions of Asia annually, with local outbreaks causing losses exceeding 50%.

Rodent control in agriculture has been a persistent challenge throughout East Asian history. Mass poisoning campaigns, biological control using owls and snakes, and coordinated community trapping have all been employed with varying success. During Mao Zedong's **Four Pests Campaign** (四害運動, 1958–1962), rats — specifically 田鼠 and 家鼠 — were designated alongside sparrows, flies, and mosquitoes as enemies to be eradicated by mass mobilization. The ecological consequences of the campaign, particularly the near-elimination of sparrows, were severe.

In classical Chinese literature, the field rat appears in the *Book of Songs* (詩経) in the poem **碩鼠** (Shuòshǔ, "Big Rat") — an allegory in which the oppressive lord is addressed as a fat rat consuming the peasants' grain: 碩鼠碩鼠，無食我黍 ("Big rat, big rat, do not eat my millet!"). This is one of the earliest surviving examples of political allegory in Chinese poetry.
