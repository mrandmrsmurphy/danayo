## Notes
- [個 (char)](../characters/個%20(char).md) "individual items"

個 is the Dan'a'yo general-purpose classifier for individual, discrete items — 量詞, counting any object not already covered by a more specific measure word: 一個人 (one person), 三個問題 (three questions), 兩個月 (two months), 數個理由 (several reasons). The character is a 形声 compound: semantic 人 ("person") + phonetic 固 (OC *kaːs, "solid; firm"), originally meaning "a single, discrete unit; an individual person." The classifier function of 個 is the most semantically bleached in Dan'a'yo — it can substitute for almost any other classifier in informal or unclear contexts, functioning as a catch-all where the specific measure word is unknown or irrelevant. 個 also serves as the Dan'a'yo stand-in for the character [[個]].

The CJKV classifier landscape for the general individual classifier converges strongly on 個/个. Mandarin 个 (gè) is the most frequent classifier in the language, usable with almost any noun: 一个人 (one person), 一个问题 (one problem), 一个苹果 (one apple), 一个国家 (one country). Its frequency makes 个 the default measure word when no other applies, and it can substitute freely for more specific classifiers in informal speech. Cantonese 個 (go3) functions identically as the generalized individual classifier: 一個人 (one person), 一個問題 (one question), 一個蘋果 (one apple). Japanese 個 (こ/KO) counts small, compact, round, or discrete objects — 三個のリンゴ (three apples), 五個の卵 (five eggs), 一個の問題 (one problem) — but is less universally substitutable than Mandarin 个; Japanese uses the native つ (tsu) as the true general counter. Korean 개 (個/개) is a productive classifier for discrete objects: 사과 세 개 (three apples), 문제 두 개 (two problems), 달걀 한 개 (one egg) — colloquially common and nearly as flexible as Mandarin 个. Vietnamese cái: một cái bàn (one table), một cái ghế (one chair); Sino-Vietnamese cá/個 is literary while cái dominates colloquial usage.

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 그 does not match Korean 개. 個 reads 그 (gǝ/ㄍㄜ) in Dan'a'yo — MC 見母 (k) + 歌韻 (ɑ) gives a velar stop onset with a central back vowel. Dan'a'yo maps the MC 歌韻 (ɑ) to the central ㄜ vowel (그/gǝ), while Sino-Korean 개 (gae) maps the same MC final to -ae, a front vowel. The MC 歌韻 produced ㄜ (-ǝ/그) in Dan'a'yo and -ae (개) in Korean — divergent vowel outcomes from the same open back MC source.
