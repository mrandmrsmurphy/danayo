## Notes
- [欠 (char)](../characters/欠%20(char).md) "yawn; lack" + [乏](../characters/乏.md) "scarcity, lack" — near-synonym compound doubling down on the sense of insufficiency; stand-in for [[乏]], which cannot appear independently.

欠 pictographically depicts a kneeling person with an open mouth — yawning, gaping — and from that image of an "opening" or "gap" came the extended sense of lacking or owing something. 乏, per Shuowen, is graphically 正 ("correct, proper") written in reverse: a visual pun on falling short of what is proper or complete. Stacking two independently-arrived-at images of insufficiency into one compound is characteristic of classical Chinese word formation, where near-synonym pairs reinforce and generalize a shared sense rather than narrowing it.

The word shows a clean four-way split by which "lack" character each tradition actually favors day to day. In Japanese, 欠乏 (けつぼう) is the unmarked, everyday term — it appears constantly in ordinary and technical registers alike (ビタミン欠乏症 "vitamin deficiency," 睡眠欠乏 "sleep deprivation"), because 欠 is the standard shinjitai form used across the modern Japanese lexicon for this sense. Mandarin and Korean instead overwhelmingly favor the near-synonym compound built on 缺 rather than 欠: 缺乏 (quēfá) is the ordinary, ubiquitous Chinese word for "to lack, to be short of," and Korean 결핍 (缺乏) is likewise the dominant modern form, with 흠핍 (欠乏) surviving only as a rarer, more literary alternate. 欠乏 is therefore fully real and attested everywhere, but its status ranges from the default choice (Japan) to a marked, secondary one (China, Korea) — a case of the same underlying compound surviving in every tradition while each language quietly picked a different lead character for its most common form.
