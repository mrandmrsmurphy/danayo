## Notes
- [龍 (char)](../characters/龍%20(char).md) "dragon; reptile"

龍 in Dan'a'yo is the noun for "dragon" and serves as the determiner (限定詞) for reptiles — appearing in naming compounds to classify scaly, cold-blooded creatures: 恐龍 (dinosaur — lit. "terror dragon"), 地龍 (earthworm — lit. "earth dragon"), 龍蝦 (lobster — lit. "dragon shrimp"), 龍巻 (tornado — lit. "dragon roll"), 草亀 (Chinese pond turtle, where 亀 = turtle). The character is a 象形 pictograph of the mythical composite dragon creature — combining features of snake, fish, deer, and crocodile — one of the most visually complex pictographs in the sinographic system and itself Kangxi radical 212. Dan'a'yo maintains the full traditional form 龍, rejecting the simplified 龙 and Japanese shinjitai 竜 (which reverts to the traditional form in Japanese compound contexts anyway). 龍 also serves as the Dan'a'yo stand-in for the character [[龍]].

Mandarin 龙 (lóng, simplified): the dragon is a benevolent, rain-bringing symbol of imperial power and good fortune in Chinese culture — entirely distinct from the malevolent Western dragon: 龙年 (Year of the Dragon), 龙舟 (dragon boat), 恐龙 (dinosaur), 烏龍 (Wulong; oolong tea — lit. "black dragon"). Cantonese 龍 (lung4): 龍舟 (lung4 zau1, dragon boat), 龍蝦 (lung4 haa1, lobster), 九龍 (Gau2lung4, Kowloon — lit. "Nine Dragons"). Japanese 龍/竜 (りゅう/RYUU): 竜巻 (tatsumaki, tornado — lit. "dragon roll"), 恐竜 (kyōryū, dinosaur), 龍安寺 (Ryōan-ji, the famous rock garden temple). Korean 용 (龍, yong): the Korean dragon is similarly benevolent to the Chinese; 이무기 is the creature that aspires to become a dragon. Vietnamese long (龍): rồng is the colloquial native word for dragon; Sino-Vietnamese long (龍) appears in place names — Long Thành (Dragon City) — and historical royal terminology.

kwin is true: Dan'a'yo 룡 exactly matches Korean 룡. 龍 reads 룡 (lyong/ㄌ⼄ㄫ) in Dan'a'yo — MC 來母 (l) + 鍾韻 (ɨoŋ) gives a lateral initial with the compound vowel -yong and -ng final. Both Dan'a'yo and Sino-Korean map MC 來母 to l/r- and MC 鍾韻 to -yong, retaining the -y- medial. This contrasts with [[共]] (ㄍ⼄ㄫ/굥 vs Korean 공), where Dan'a'yo preserves the 鍾韻 -y- medial but Korean drops it — with 來母 the -y- medial is preserved in both systems, yielding identical 룡 forms.
