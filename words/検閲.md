## Notes
- [検](../characters/検.md) "to examine; to inspect" + [閲](../characters/閲.md) "to inspect; to review; to read through"

検閲 combines two verbs of examination — 検 (to check, to investigate, from 木 + 僉, suggesting methodical scrutiny) and 閲 (to read through, to review, from 門 + 兌, the image of something passing through a gate of scrutiny). Together they denote the institutional act of inspecting content — texts, performances, correspondence, broadcasts — with authority to suppress or alter what fails to pass. The Dan'a'yo word 検閲 is also the stand-in for [閲](../characters/閲.md), which is too bounded in use to stand alone.

The history of 検閲 in East Asia tracks the history of state power over expression. In China, systematic censorship of books dates from the Qin dynasty's burning of books (焚書坑儒, 213 BCE); the Qing dynasty's literary inquisition (文字獄) destroyed thousands of texts and executed authors for subversive content. In Meiji and Imperial Japan, the 検閲制度 (censorship system) operated through the Home Ministry and military authorities, suppressing socialist literature, anti-war writing, and eventually any content deemed threatening to national unity — a system that extended to occupied Korea and Taiwan. Modern usage of 検閲 has expanded to include internet censorship (網絡検閲, internet filtering), algorithmic content moderation, and the auto-censorship practiced by platforms operating in restrictive environments.

In Korean, 검열 (geomnyeol) carries the same weight and is used in both historical and contemporary contexts — describing Japanese colonial censorship, Cold War-era South Korean military censorship, and ongoing debates about platform regulation. Vietnamese kiểm duyệt uses the Sino-Vietnamese forms with the same range of reference. The alias 検閲 uses the traditional form of 閲 (閱).
