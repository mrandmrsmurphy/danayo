## Notes
- [沈](../characters/沈.md) "sink; submerge" + [没](../characters/没%20(char).md) "drown; sink; vanish beneath" — stand-in for both [[沈]] and [[没]], neither of which can appear independently
- 沈没 takes two characters that already mean almost the same thing — 沈, "to sink, to settle downward," and 没, "to drown, to vanish beneath the surface" — and welds them into the single, unambiguous word for a ship going under. The doubled emphasis gives the compound its weight: this isn't a gentle settling but a complete, often catastrophic submersion, the kind that ends voyages and headlines news broadcasts. Every reading keeps that gravity intact — Mandarin chénmò and Cantonese sam2 mut6, Sino-Japanese ちんぼつ (chinbotsu, the standard word for a vessel's sinking), Sino-Korean 침몰 (chimmol, famously associated with maritime disasters and, figuratively, with power or strength draining away), and Sino-Vietnamese trầm một all describe the same downward, final motion. It is a compound built almost entirely of synonyms — and that redundancy is the point: when something truly sinks, one verb for "sink" apparently isn't strong enough.
