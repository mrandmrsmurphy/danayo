## Notes
- [五 (char)](../characters/五%20(char).md) "five" + [官](../characters/官.md) "official; organ"

The five sense organs — the body's official channels for perceiving the world. Classical Chinese medicine used 官 ("official, functionary") as the term for a bodily organ with a defined role, on the analogy of government officials each managing a specific function of the state.

The five organs and their correspondences in traditional Chinese medicine ([[五臓]]):

| Sense organ | Sense | Associated 臓 | Element |
|-------------|-------|--------------|---------|
| 耳 ears | 聴 hearing | 腎 kidneys | Water |
| 目 eyes | 視 sight | 肝 liver | Wood |
| 口 mouth | 味 taste | 脾 spleen | Earth |
| 鼻 nose | 嗅 smell | 肺 lungs | Metal |
| 舌 tongue | — | 心 heart | Fire |

In Chinese medicine, the condition of each sense organ was read as a diagnostic window into its associated internal organ: deteriorating vision suggests liver weakness; ringing ears or hearing loss suggests kidney deficiency; loss of smell, lung problems. This system of correspondences made the face a kind of map of internal health — a physician could assess a patient's organ systems partly through careful observation of the face and its five channels.

In modern colloquial Chinese, however, 五官 is most commonly used in the sense of **facial features** — the combined appearance of the face's openings and prominences. 五官端正 (wǔguān duānzhèng, "the five features are proper and upright") is a standard compliment meaning someone has regular, well-proportioned, attractive facial features. The phrase appears on official documents (identity cards, military fitness assessments), job advertisements, and in everyday conversation about appearance. 五官立体 (three-dimensional features) describes a face with pronounced, sculpted features rather than flat ones — a beauty standard in East Asia associated with Western and mixed facial structure.
