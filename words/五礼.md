## Notes
- [五 (char)](../characters/五%20(char).md) "five" + [礼 (char)](../characters/礼%20(char).md) "rite, ceremony" — the Five Rites, one of the Six Arts (六芸) of Zhou-dynasty noble education.

五礼 (wǔlǐ) is the first of the [[六書|六芸]] (Six Arts) listed in the **《周禮·地官司徒·保氏》** ("Offices of Earth, the Protector") as the curriculum taught by the 保氏 official to the sons of the nobility. The Five Rites classify all ceremonial activity into five domains:

1. **吉禮** (jí lǐ) — auspicious rites: sacrifices to heaven, earth, and ancestors
2. **凶禮** (xiōng lǐ) — inauspicious rites: funerals and mourning
3. **賓禮** (bīn lǐ) — guest rites: the reception of envoys and visiting dignitaries
4. **軍禮** (jūn lǐ) — military rites: oaths, reviews, and campaigns
5. **嘉禮** (jiā lǐ) — festive rites: weddings, feasts, and capping ceremonies

This fivefold scheme became foundational to the entire later Chinese ritual tradition, and the same five-part division reappears in dynastic ritual codes for over two millennia. As one of the Six Arts, 五礼 was paired with [[六楽]] (the Six Musics), [[五射]] (the Five Archery Techniques), [[五馭]] (the Five Charioteering Techniques), [[六書]] (the Six Writings), and [[九数]] (the Nine Numbers) — together forming the core of a Zhou nobleman's education.

The Sino-Japanese ごれい, Sino-Korean 오례, and Sino-Vietnamese ngũ lễ readings are all straightforward compositional renderings, reflecting how thoroughly this Zhou administrative vocabulary was absorbed across the CJKV ritual and educational traditions.
