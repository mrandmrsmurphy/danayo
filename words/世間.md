## Notes
- [世](../characters/世.md) "world, generation" + [間](../characters/間.md) "between, among"

世間 denotes the human world as a social space — the realm of ordinary people, public life, and shared experience, as opposed to the realm of rulers, deities, or the afterlife. The compound is literary in Chinese (shìjiān) and carries a slightly elevated or reflective tone; it appears in Buddhist and Daoist writing to contrast the mundane world (世間) with transcendence, and in classical poetry to evoke the transience of human affairs.

In Japanese, せけん (seken) has evolved into everyday vocabulary and is among the most culturally loaded words in the language. It refers not merely to "the world" in a geographic sense but to "the eyes of society" — the awareness that one is being observed and judged by one's community. Concepts like 世間体 (sekentei, "public face") and 世間知らず (sekenshi-razu, "naïve, unworldly") extend from it. Japanese scholars of culture, including Ruth Benedict and Chie Nakane, have identified 世間 as a key organizing concept of Japanese social life, distinct from Western notions of "the public" or "society."

In Korean (세간), the term is used in formal and literary contexts. The Vietnamese Hán-Việt reading thế gian is used in religious and philosophical writing, particularly in Buddhist texts.
