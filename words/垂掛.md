## Notes
- [垂](../characters/垂.md) "hang; dangle; droop" + [掛](../characters/掛%20(char).md) "hang; suspend" — stand-in for [[垂]], which cannot appear independently
- 垂掛 stacks two verbs of hanging on top of each other to describe something fixed at one end and left to droop downward under its own weight — a banner flying from a pole, a vine trailing from a wall, a lock of hair falling loose. It is essentially interchangeable with the more common 懸掛, and the Chinese sources that gloss it (Mandarin chuíguà, Cantonese seoi4 gwaa3) treat the two as straightforward synonyms. The compositional Sino-Japanese reading すいか is a small curiosity in its own right: it lands on exactly the same syllables as 垂下 (suika), the everyday Japanese word for "hanging down, drooping," built from the same first root 垂 (sui) plus a different but near-synonymous second element. Sino-Korean 수괘 likewise collides — purely by coincidence of sound, not sense — with 隨卦 (suegwae), the name of a hexagram in the Book of Changes, a reminder of how thickly populated the Sino-Korean phonetic space is with unrelated homophones built from common syllables.
