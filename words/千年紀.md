## Notes
- [千](../characters/千.md) "thousand" + [年 (char)](../characters/年%20(char).md) "year" + [紀](../characters/紀.md) "era unit" — a numbered historical millennium; 紀 elevates [[千年]] from duration to named periodisation unit, exactly as in [[世紀]] (century)

**千年紀** (*qiānniánjì*) is the counted historical millennium: the First Millennium, the Second Millennium, the Third Millennium. Where [[千年]] answers "how long?" — a span of a thousand years — 千年紀 answers "which one?" It names a unit of historical periodisation, ordinated and bounded. The 紀 suffix is the same that forms [[世紀]] (century, literally "world-era unit"): 紀 marks not raw duration but a named segment of historical time that can be counted, referenced, and placed in sequence.

The **第三千年紀** (Third Millennium) began on 1 January 2001 AD. The common popular assumption that it began on 1 January 2000 reflects the intuitive appeal of the round number, but the Gregorian calendar has no year zero — the first year of the first millennium was 1 AD, so each millennium ends on a year whose last three digits are 000, and the next begins on the year ending in 001. The Third Millennium of the Common Era thus runs from 2001 to 3000 AD inclusive.

The approach of the year 2000 AD generated the Y2K problem — the millennium bug — in which two-digit date fields in legacy software threatened to misread the year 2000 as 1900. The global effort to audit and patch affected systems in the late 1990s was one of the largest coordinated technology operations in history, and the anticlimactic rollover on 31 December 1999 / 1 January 2000 has since become a cultural reference point for both the power and the fragility of infrastructure assumptions.

The concept of a millennium as a historical unit carries religious weight in several traditions. In Christian eschatology, the **千年王国** (*chiliasm* or *millennialism*) — drawn from Revelation 20 — describes a thousand-year reign of Christ on earth before the Last Judgement; the approach of the year 1000 AD generated genuine apocalyptic expectation in parts of medieval Europe. Jewish and Islamic chronological traditions use different base years and different period units, so the Gregorian millennium boundaries carry no inherent significance within them.

Japanese **千年紀** (*sennenki*) is used in scholarly and formal historical contexts alongside the loanword ミレニアム (*mireniamu*, from Latin *millennium*). Korean **천년기** (*cheonnyeongi*) is the standard Sino-Korean term. Vietnamese **thiên niên kỷ** is the Sino-Vietnamese compound used in formal historical writing.
