## Notes
- [巴 (char)](../characters/巴%20(char).md) + [基](../characters/基.md) + [斯](../characters/斯.md) + [坦](../characters/坦.md) — pure phonetic rendering of *Pākistān*

巴基斯坦 has no semantic content: the four characters are borrowed for sound alone. The source name *Pākistān* is itself a coinage: Choudhry Rahmat Ali invented it in 1933 as both an acronym (Punjab, Afghania, Kashmir, Indus, Sindh, balocisTAN) and a meaningful Urdu-Persian compound — پاک (*pāk*, "pure") + ستان (*-stān*, "land") — "Land of the Pure." The country became independent in 1947 as the Muslim-majority partition of British India.

The territory of modern Pakistan was the heartland of the ancient Sinospheric encounter with South Asia. The northwest — Gandhara (犍陀羅), centered on what is now Peshawar and the Swat valley — was the first major Buddhist civilization encountered by Chinese pilgrims heading west. Both Faxian (法顯, 399–412 AD) and Xuanzang (玄奘, 629–645 AD) passed through and described it in detail; the stūpas and monasteries of Gandhara were among the most sacred sites in the Buddhist world. The Khyber Pass was the eastern gateway of the Silk Road, making the region a conduit for the transmission of Buddhism, silk, and ideas between the Sinosphere and the Mediterranean.

Compare [[獅子国]] (Sri Lanka) and [[孟加拉]] (Bangladesh), where the Dan'a'yo names carry traceable Sinospheric etymology. 巴基斯坦 is a straight phonetic loan, as there is no single classical Chinese name covering the full extent of the modern state.
