## Notes
- [夜 (char)](../characters/夜%20(char).md) "night" + [叉 (char)](../characters/叉%20(char).md) "fork; prong"

A phonetic transcription of Sanskrit **यक्ष** (yakṣa; feminine: yakṣī) — a broad class of nature spirits in Hindu, Buddhist, and Jain mythology. The Chinese characters are purely phonetic and carry no meaning beyond the borrowed sound: 夜 (night) + 叉 (fork) was selected to approximate "yaksha" in Middle Chinese pronunciation, not to describe the being. Variant transcriptions include 藥叉 and 閱叉.

In **Hindu and Jain cosmology**, yakshas are ambivalent nature spirits associated with forests, fertility, and hidden treasure. The yaksha king Kubera is the god of wealth in Hindu tradition, and his retinue of yakshas guard the treasures of the earth. They can be benevolent protectors or dangerous assailants depending on whether they are honored or offended.

In **Buddhist cosmology**, yakshas were absorbed as guardian deities (護法善神) and appear among the celestial retinue of Vaiśravaṇa (毘沙門天), the heavenly king of the north. As Buddhism traveled from India to China, the yakshas' character darkened in popular imagination. Chinese Buddhist literature and folk religion increasingly portrayed 夜叉 as ferocious hell-beings — servants of Yama (閻魔), the king of the dead, tasked with hunting down and punishing sinful souls. The image of the 夜叉 as a fearsome, hook-wielding demon became standard in Chinese temple iconography and opera.

In **Japanese**, やしゃ (yasha) follows the same trajectory from Buddhist cosmology into popular imagery. Figuratively, 夜叉 describes a person — especially a woman — with a terrifying temper or fearsome appearance. The expression 女夜叉 (onna yasha, "female yaksha") denotes a fierce, formidable woman; it functions as both an insult and a backhanded compliment depending on context. The same figure appears in *Noh* theatre masks and in family crests (家紋).
