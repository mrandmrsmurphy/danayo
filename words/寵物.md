## Notes
- [寵](../characters/寵.md) "favor; pamper; spoil" + [物](../characters/物%20(char).md) "thing; creature" — stand-in for [[寵]], which cannot appear independently
- 寵物 is built from the plainest possible logic: a "thing" (物) that is "favored, doted on, spoiled" (寵) — and from that simple union the modern Sinosphere has built its word for "pet." Mandarin chǒngwù and Cantonese cung2 mat6 carry this everyday sense directly, and Sino-Vietnamese sủng vật follows it exactly, glossed as "động vật được cưng nuôi trong nhà" — an animal pampered and kept in the home, whether dog, cat, bird, or rabbit. Japanese took the same two characters down a narrower historical path: ちょうもつ (chōmotsu) doesn't mean "pet" at all, but rather a court favorite — most often a favored mistress or concubine, someone doted upon and kept close, the same emotional core (寵, favor and indulgence) directed at a person rather than an animal. The compound is a small case study in how identical building blocks can drift to cover very different referents — the modern Chinese-sphere "beloved animal" and the older Japanese "beloved person" are really the same idea, 寵, simply pointed at different objects of affection.
