## Notes
- [無 (char)](../characters/無%20(char).md) "lack, without" + [我 (char)](../characters/我%20(char).md) "I, self"

無我 is the Sinitic rendering of the Buddhist doctrine of *anattā* (Pali) / *anātman* (Sanskrit): the teaching that no permanent, unchanging self exists within or behind the phenomena of experience. It is one of the three marks of existence in Theravāda Buddhism (alongside 無常, impermanence, and 苦, suffering) and a central concern of Mahāyāna philosophy, where it is extended from the selflessness of persons (人無我) to the selflessness of phenomena (法無我).

The term entered the CJKV literary tradition through the translation of Buddhist sūtras beginning in the Han dynasty, and it remains in active use across all five languages in religious, philosophical, and psychological discourse. In Japanese (むが, muga), 無我 carries an additional secular sense — complete absorption in an activity to the point of self-forgetfulness, as in 無我夢中 (muga-muchū, "absorbed, in a trance"). This usage, while derived from the Buddhist original, has taken on a life of its own in everyday Japanese. In Vietnamese (vô ngã), the term is standard in Buddhist writing and appears in the compound literature of the Trúc Lâm Zen tradition. In Korean (무아), it is used in both Seon (Zen) Buddhist contexts and in philosophical writing.
