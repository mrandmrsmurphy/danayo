## Notes
- [苦 (char)](../characters/苦%20(char).md) "bitter, suffering" + [役](../characters/役.md) "service, labor" — hard labor; forced labor; drudgery

In traditional Chinese legal thought, 苦役 designated penal servitude — labor imposed as punishment, as distinct from military service (兵役) or corvée (徭役). The bitterness of the compound is not incidental: 役 already carries the weight of compelled obligation, and 苦 signals that this obligation is painful rather than merely dutiful.

The compound has a notable constitutional life in Japan: Article 18 of the 1947 Constitution explicitly prohibits 苦役 (involuntary servitude) in any form, making it one of the few classical Sinitic compounds embedded in postwar constitutional language. In everyday Japanese the word retains a formal, somewhat literary register.

Korean 고역 has broadened considerably from the penal sense: it is now the ordinary colloquial word for any task that is exhausting, thankless, or burdensome — sitting through a long ceremony, explaining the same thing a fifth time, or caring for a difficult person. The original connotation of forced labor has faded into the general sense of something you endure rather than enjoy.
