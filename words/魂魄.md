## Notes
- [魂 (char)](../characters/魂%20(char).md) "yang soul; spiritual spirit" + [魄](../characters/魄.md) "yin soul; bodily spirit"

**魂魄** (*húnpò*) is the classical Sinographic term for the soul in its complete form — the paired spiritual and bodily aspects of the psyche conceived together. The compound is a dvandva uniting two distinct and complementary principles.

In classical Chinese cosmology, 魂 (*hún*) is the yang soul — luminous, active, and heaven-bound. It is associated with consciousness, intellect, and breath; at death, the *hun* ascends to heaven. 魄 (*pò*) is the yin soul — dense, embodied, and earth-bound. It is associated with the physical senses, vitality of the flesh, and the bones; at death, the *po* descends with the corpse into the earth. The distinction appears prominently in the *Zuozhuan* and *Liji*, where proper funerary ritual is framed as ensuring the 魂 is guided safely aloft while the 魄 is laid properly to rest. Failure in this — the soul becoming homeless — produces the dangerous *gui* (鬼), the restless ghost.

The canonical count varies by source. The *Suwen* (素問) and Daoist texts typically speak of three *hun* (三魂) and seven *po* (七魄): the three yang souls governing life-force and spiritual activity; the seven yin souls governing physical drives and appetites. The idiom **魂飛魄散** (*húnfēi pòsàn*, "hun flies, po scatters") describes extreme terror or death, the total dissolution of the self.

In Buddhist-influenced East Asian thought, the 魂魄 framework was layered alongside the ātman/anātman (無我) debate and the concept of the *vijñāna* (識), creating syncretic models of consciousness that remain influential in popular religion across the Sinosphere. In Japanese, 魂魄 (*konpaku*) carries the same classical weight and appears in literary and formal religious registers; the vernacular usually prefers 魂 (*tamashii*) alone. Korean 혼백 (*honbaek*) is the standard compound, widely used in funerary and shamanic (*mudang*) contexts. Vietnamese *hồn phách* is the Sino-Vietnamese calque; native Vietnamese religiosity more commonly uses *hồn* alone or in other compounds.
