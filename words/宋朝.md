## Notes
- [宋](../characters/宋.md) "Song (dynasty)" + [朝 (char)](../characters/朝%20(char).md) "dynasty, court; morning"

The Song dynasty (960–1279) followed the chaos of the Five Dynasties period and is conventionally split into the Northern Song (960–1127, capital at Kaifeng) and Southern Song (1127–1279, capital at Hangzhou, after the Jurchen Jin conquered the north). It is remembered less for military strength — the Song lost territory to the Liao, Jin, and finally the Mongols — than for an extraordinary flowering of civil culture: the rise of Neo-Confucianism under thinkers like Zhu Xi, the maturation of woodblock printing and the first use of movable type, the spread of paper money, and continued development of the compass and gunpowder weapons that would later transform warfare worldwide.

朝 ("dynasty, court," also literally "morning" — the time of day rulers held audience) is the general suffix for naming a ruling house, parallel to its use in 唐朝, 明朝, 清朝. 宋 itself derives from the State of Song, a vassal state of the Zhou dynasty traditionally associated with the descendants of the Shang royal line; the founder of the Song dynasty, Zhao Kuangyin, took the name from his power base at Songzhou. Note the character distinction with [[蘇]] (Soviet/revival), which shares the Dan'a'yo syllable ㄙㄛ but not ㄙㄛㄫ — 宋 is unrelated to 蘇 despite superficial similarity in romanization.
