## Notes
- [乾 (char)](../characters/乾%20(char).md) "dry; to dry out" + [浄](../characters/浄.md) "clean; pure; undefiled" — stand-in for [[浄]], which cannot appear independently
- A near-synonym compound built on a logical chain: dryness implies the absence of mess and residue, and so came to imply cleanliness itself — "dry-and-pure" as "spotless." Mandarin gānjìng and Cantonese gon1 zeng6 (commonly written with the traditional 乾淨/淨 or simplified 干净/净, both kept here as aliases) use the compound as the everyday word for "clean," with a secondary sense of "completely, with nothing left" (as in 吃乾淨, "eat it all up"). The pairing is rarer outside Chinese — Japanese keeps 乾浄 (けんじょう, kenjō, the form that gives this page its filename) mainly as a more formal or literary near-synonym of きれい/清潔, and Sino-Korean 건정 (geonjeong) underlies the somewhat dated verb 건정하다, "to be spotlessly clean; to finish a task cleanly," both standing in the shadow of their far more common rivals.
