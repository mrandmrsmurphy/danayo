## Notes
- [石 (char)](../characters/石%20(char).md) "stone; rock" + [灰 (char)](../characters/灰%20(char).md) "ash; lime" — literally "stone-ash"

**石灰** (*shíhuī*) is quicklime — calcium oxide (CaO), produced by calcining limestone (石灰石, *shíhuīshí*, CaCO₃) in a kiln at temperatures above 840°C. The name is descriptively exact: stone (limestone) reduced by fire to ash (powder). The reaction is 石灰石 → 石灰 + CO₂; the resulting white powder is highly reactive and exothermic when mixed with water, producing slaked lime (消石灰, Ca(OH)₂) with the release of considerable heat. The distinction between 石灰 (quicklime, CaO) and 消石灰 (slaked lime, Ca(OH)₂) is important in technical usage; colloquially 石灰 encompasses both.

Lime has been a foundational material in East Asian civilisation since antiquity — in mortar for masonry, plaster for walls, preservation of foodstuffs, and soil amendment in agriculture. Excavations at Erlitou (二里頭, c. 1900–1500 BC) reveal lime plastering; the Great Wall was bonded in part with lime mortar mixed with sticky rice. Lime kilns (石灰窯) were a common feature of the pre-modern Chinese landscape, and 石灰 trade was a significant rural industry.

The compound gave rise to one of the most celebrated poems in the Chinese literati tradition: **石灰吟** (*Shíhuī Yín*, "Ode to Lime") by **于謙** (*Yú Qiān*, 1398–1457 AD), the Ming statesman and hero of the defence of Beijing against the Oirat Mongols. The poem uses the entire lifecycle of lime — quarried from deep mountains by hammer blows, fired in kilns, ground to powder — as an extended metaphor for incorruptible moral character. Its closing couplet, 粉骨碎身渾不怕，要留清白在人間 ("Bones ground to powder, body shattered — still unafraid; I only wish to leave purity and whiteness in the world"), is among the most-quoted lines in the classical tradition and remains in the standard Chinese school curriculum today.

In Japanese, 石灰 reads せっかい (*sekkkai*) — the 石 undergoes the standard moraic nasal + stop assimilation before the voiceless initial of 灰. Korean 석회 (*seokhoe*) is the regular Sino-Korean compound. Vietnamese *thạch hôi* is the Sino-Vietnamese form; vernacular usage prefers *vôi* (from 灰, *vôi*) alone for lime in construction and agricultural contexts.
