## Notes
- [嫁](../characters/嫁.md) "go out in marriage (of a woman)" + [娶](../characters/娶.md) "take a wife (of a man)" — a dvandva compound naming marriage from both sides; stand-in for [[娶]], which cannot appear independently

**嫁娶** (*jiàqǔ*) is a dvandva compound that names the institution of marriage by joining its two complementary actions: 嫁, the bride's departure from her natal household to join her husband's, and 娶, the groom's act of receiving and taking a wife. Where [[結婚]] (*jiéhūn*) names marriage as a bond and contract, 嫁娶 names it as a social transaction with two parties — the giving and the taking — each described from their own perspective.

The asymmetry encoded in the compound reflects the classical East Asian understanding of marriage as an exchange between families. A woman **嫁** — she goes out (*嫁* contains the element *家*, "house," signifying she moves houses). A man **娶** — he takes (*娶* contains *取*, "take"), receiving the woman into his household. These are not merely grammatical distinctions but structural ones: the rites, duties, and social meanings attached to each role were separately elaborated in the ritual literature (禮記, *Lǐjì*) and legal codes (唐律, *Táng Lǜ*).

The compound 嫁娶 appears frequently in legal and administrative contexts — regulations governing the age of marriage, the proper forms of the betrothal ceremony (婚禮), the conditions under which divorce (離婚) or remarriage was permitted, and the ritual obligations of both parties' families. The **六禮** (Six Rites of Marriage), codified in the Zhou ritual tradition and elaborated through the Han, describe the sequence of exchanges — inquiry, announcement, gifts, betrothal, invitation, and reception — that together constituted a valid 嫁娶.

In modern usage, 嫁娶 is largely literary and formal. Everyday Chinese uses 結婚 for "to get married" without distinguishing between the parties' perspectives; the gendered 嫁 (used of women) and 娶 (used of men) remain in active use individually in Chinese and Korean, but the compound 嫁娶 as a frozen pair is primarily found in classical quotation, legal history, and literary registers. Japanese **かしゅ** (*kashu*) is similarly restricted to formal and classical contexts. Korean **가취** (*gachui*) is classical; modern Korean uses 결혼 (*gyeolhon*). Vietnamese **giá thú** is the Sino-Vietnamese compound for marriage used in formal and legal writing.
