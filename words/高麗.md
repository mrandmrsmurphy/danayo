## Notes
- [高 (char)](../characters/高%20(char).md) "high" + [麗](../characters/麗.md) "beautiful" — literally "high and beautiful," echoing the older name [[高句麗]] (Goguryeo), whose founders Goryeo's own founder claimed descent from.

Goryeo was the Korean dynasty that succeeded [[統一]][[新羅]] (Unified Silla) in 918, when the warlord Wang Geon unified the peninsula's fractured "Later Three Kingdoms" under his rule, and it lasted until 1392, when General Yi Seong-gye deposed the last Goryeo king and founded [[朝鮮]] (Joseon). Goryeo gave Korea its mature institutions of statecraft — a centralized bureaucracy staffed through civil service examinations modeled on the Chinese system, and a deep state patronage of Buddhism that produced, among other achievements, the carving of the Tripitaka Koreana (高麗大藏經) onto over 80,000 woodblocks. The dynasty endured repeated Khitan invasions in the 11th century and, in the 13th century, was reduced to a tributary of the Mongol Empire after decades of Mongol invasions, a subjugation that shaped Goryeo's final century and a half.

高麗 is also the direct source of the English exonym "Korea." Persian and Arab traders who reached the peninsula via Silk Road and maritime routes during the Goryeo period carried the name back westward as forms like "Korea" or "Cory"; Marco Polo later rendered it "Cauli" in his travels. These transliterations passed into European usage and eventually settled, in English, as "Korea" — making 高麗 one of the rare Dan'a'yo proper nouns whose Sinitic name and English name trace back to the very same Korean word, rather than to independent borrowings.
