## Notes
- [四 (char)](../characters/四%20(char).md) "four" + [川 (char)](../characters/川%20(char).md) "river"

四川 (Sichuan, "four rivers") is a landlocked province of southwestern China, named for four major rivers: the Min (岷江), Tuo (沱江), Jialing (嘉陵江), and Wu (烏江). The Sichuan Basin, ringed by mountains, has been one of China's most populous and agriculturally productive regions since antiquity — praised in classical texts as 天府之國 (tiānfǔ zhīguó, "the land of heaven's abundance"). The provincial capital is Chengdu (成都).

Sichuan cuisine (四川菜, Sichuan cai; 川菜, chuān cai) is among the most influential culinary traditions in the Chinese-speaking world, defined by the distinctive numbing-spicy combination of Sichuan pepper (花椒, huājiāo) and chili — a flavor profile described as 麻辣 (málà). Sichuan pepper produces a unique tingling paralysis of the tongue (the "numbing" quality, 麻) that distinguishes it from ordinary heat. Dishes such as 麻婆豆腐, 夫妻肺片, and 水煮魚 are now known internationally.

In Korean, 사천 (四川) is also the name of a city in South Gyeongsang Province (四川市, Sacheon), though the characters and origin are entirely unrelated to the Chinese province.
