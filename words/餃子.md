## Notes
- [餃](../characters/餃.md) "dumpling; crescent-shaped filled dough" + [子](../characters/子.md) "diminutive suffix; child; small thing"

餃子 is "jiaozi; dumpling; gyoza" — the East Asian filled dumpling made from a thin dough wrapper folded around seasoned filling, then boiled, steamed, pan-fried, or deep-fried. 餃 (a crescent-shaped filled dough food) + 子 (the diminutive/nominalising suffix ubiquitous in Chinese food names: 包子, 栗子, 餃子). Jiaozi are among the most culturally central foods in Chinese cuisine — traditionally eaten at Lunar New Year for their resemblance to ancient gold ingots (元寶, yuánbǎo), symbolising wealth for the new year.

Mandarin 餃子 (jiǎozi) is the standard name for boiled or pan-fried Chinese dumplings; 鍋貼 (guōtiē) specifies the pan-fried potsticker variant. Cantonese 餃子 (gaau2 zi2) or specifically 水餃 (seoi2 gaau2, boiled dumplings); the dimsum shrimp dumpling is 蝦餃 (haa1 gaau2). Japanese ぎょうざ (gyōza) is a phonetic borrowing of 餃子's Mandarin reading — the Japanese version is typically pan-fried (焼き餃子, yaki-gyōza); the word entered Japanese via Manchurian cuisine in the early 20th century and is now a staple of Japanese cuisine. Korean 교자 (餃子, gyoja) or 만두 (mandu, the broader word covering various filled dumplings including Korean-style steamed varieties).

Vietnamese há cảo (蝦餃 in Sino-Vietnamese) refers specifically to Cantonese-style shrimp dumplings — a narrower usage than Mandarin 餃子's general dumpling category. The HSK Level 1 placement reflects jiaozi's status as fundamental vocabulary for any student of Chinese — as culinarily and culturally central to Chinese identity as pasta to Italian, making 餃子 one of the first food words in Chinese language education. Dan'a'yo's reading 굣즈/gyaujǝ/ㄍ⼄ㄨㄐㄜ preserves the phonological shape of both characters through the standard concatenation system.
