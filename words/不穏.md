## Notes
- [不 (char)](../characters/不%20(char).md) "not" + [穏](../characters/穏.md) "calm; stable; peaceful" — the negation of [[平穏]]; a state of unease, instability, or threatening undercurrent

**不穏** (*bù wěn*) is the negation of 穏 — the absence of calm, the presence of something unsettling, unstable, or portending trouble. The word operates across a spectrum from the physical to the political to the atmospheric, and its exact shade of meaning varies meaningfully across the CJKV sphere.

In **Chinese**, 不穩 (*bù wěn*) is primarily concrete: physically unsteady, structurally insecure, or situationally volatile. A ladder that wobbles is 不穩; a political situation on the verge of disorder is 不穩. The extended form **不穩定** (*bù wěndìng*, "unstable, lacking stability") is far more common in formal and political prose than the bare compound, and 不穩 itself tends to appear in colloquial or literary rather than bureaucratic registers. The antonym is **穩定** (*wěndìng*, stable) rather than [[平穏]] (*píngwěn*, "smooth and stable," calm waters), though both serve as positive counterparts.

In **Japanese**, 不穏 (*fuon*) has a distinctly more ominous and politically charged quality. It suggests not merely instability but something threatening, suspicious, or subversive — a disquieting atmosphere before trouble breaks out. 不穏な空気 ("an ominous atmosphere"), 不穏な動き ("suspicious/ominous movements"), 不穏分子 ("subversive elements, disruptive elements within a group") — the word gravitates toward contexts of social tension, conspiracy, or impending crisis. This sense of foreboding — the calm before the storm, the feeling that something dangerous is gathering — is the primary register of 不穏 in Japanese literary and journalistic usage.

In **Korean**, 불온 (*buron*) carries the same politically charged edge as Japanese 不穏, and perhaps more so: 불온문서 (不穩文書, "subversive document"), 불온사상 (不穩思想, "subversive ideology"), 불온세력 (subversive forces). In the context of the Korean 20th century — colonial censorship, the ideological conflict of division, decades of authoritarian governance — 불온 acquired a specifically political valence as the label for anything deemed dangerous to social or state order. The word carries the trace of that history even in contemporary usage.

In **Vietnamese**, **bất ổn** is the standard compound for instability — political, economic, or social — and is the natural term in news and analysis: tình trạng bất ổn ("a state of instability"), bất ổn chính trị ("political instability"). It is more neutral in register than the Japanese or Korean forms, closer to the Chinese sense of factual unsteadiness than the Japanese sense of atmospheric menace.
