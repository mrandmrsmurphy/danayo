## Notes
- [談](../characters/談.md) "to discuss; to talk about" + [話 (char)](../characters/話%20(char).md) "speech; spoken word; topic"

談話 pairs 談 (to discuss, to engage in extended conversational speech — from 言 and 炎, suggesting speech that warms or intensifies) with 話 (speech, the spoken word, a topic or subject of conversation — from 言 and 舌, tongue over speech). Together they form the compound for conversation or verbal exchange, covering both the informal register (chatting, talking things over) and the formal register (an official statement or press release). This dual coverage — intimate conversation and institutional utterance — makes 談話 one of the more range-spanning words in the CJKV lexicon.

The formal register is particularly well-developed in Japanese. 談話 (danwa) in political journalism most often refers to an official statement issued by a government official, especially the 官房長官談話 (Chief Cabinet Secretary statement) — a formal public pronouncement that carries policy weight without rising to the level of a cabinet resolution. The most famous example is the Kono Statement (1993, 河野談話), which acknowledged the coercive recruitment of comfort women during WWII; its status as a 談話 rather than a formal government resolution has been a sustained point of political controversy about its legal weight. In Chinese, 談話 (tánhuà) covers both conversation and official talk; the compound 談話錄 is a record of conversations or dialogues. In Korean, 담화 (damhwa) appears in formal political contexts — 대통령 담화 is a presidential address — while everyday conversation uses 대화 (對話) or colloquial terms.

The kwin=false marking reflects the divergence between Dan'a'yo 담홰 (dam + hwai) and Korean 담화 (damhwa) — 話 maps to 화 in Korean Sino-Korean phonology but to 홰 (hwai) in Dan'a'yo.
