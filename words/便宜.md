## Notes
- [便 (char)](../characters/便%20(char).md) "convenient; cheap; plain" + [宜](../characters/宜.md) "suitable, fitting" — an independent compound; neither character's own `stand_in` points here (便's is bare `便`, 宜's is `適宜`)

**A genuine heteronym pair, not an error — real content bug corrected**: 便宜 has two established Mandarin readings with different meanings. The stored fields described biànyí, "convenient, appropriate" (surviving today mainly in the set phrase 便宜行事, "act as one sees fit") — but the far more common, HSK-relevant everyday meaning is piányi, "cheap, inexpensive," the ordinary adjective used constantly in shopping/pricing contexts (这个太便宜了, "this is so cheap"). Corrected `mandarin` and the primary `english` gloss to the piányi/"cheap" reading, verified via search. Cantonese was similarly corrected: `bin6 ji4` followed the "convenient" pattern, but the real standard Cantonese reading for this specific compound is `pin4 yi4` (matching the "cheap" reading) — though in everyday Cantonese speech, the native word 平 (ping4) is actually far more common than 便宜 itself for "cheap."

**Genuine cross-linguistic asymmetry, disclosed rather than forced**: Japanese べんぎ (bengi) and Korean 편의 (pyeonui) are both real, standard, everyday words — but for the *other* sense, "convenience, expediency" (便宜を図る, "to arrange accommodations"; 편의점, "convenience store"), not "cheap" at all. Neither language uses this compound for "cheap" (Japanese 安い, yasui; Korean 싸다, ssada, are the real words for that). So the same two characters name two different concepts depending on the language: "cheap" in Mandarin/Cantonese (matching this word's own HSK relevance), "convenient" in Japanese/Korean.

Vietnamese left blank — no clear single Sino-Vietnamese compound was confirmed distinguishing the two senses cleanly; the everyday Vietnamese word for "cheap" is native rẻ.
