## Notes
- [賢](../characters/賢.md) "wise; worthy; of good virtue" + [淑](../characters/淑.md) "pure; gentle; refined" — a synonym compound for a woman of cultivated virtue; wise, graceful, and morally sound

**賢淑** (*xián shū*) describes a woman of refined character in the classical Confucian mould: wise (賢) in judgment and conduct, and pure (淑) in moral disposition and manner. The compound draws on two of the central terms in the traditional vocabulary of feminine virtue. 賢 covers intellectual and practical virtue — a 賢妻 (worthy wife) manages the household well and supports her husband's endeavours; a 賢母 (worthy mother) raises children well. 淑 covers the inner quality of moral purity and gentleness — the word appears in the Shijing (詩經) in the opening poem 關雎 (*guānjū*): 窈窕淑女，君子好逑 ("The graceful and virtuous maiden is a good match for the gentleman"), one of the most quoted lines in the Chinese literary tradition.

Together, 賢淑 represents the summation of the classical ideal: not beauty (美), not obedience alone, but the integration of moral and intellectual cultivation into a composed, capable, and gentle character. The word appears in formal praise — eulogy, biography, epitaph — and in the literary description of female characters of high standing. In modern usage, particularly in Chinese, 賢淑 has become somewhat archaic; it appears in traditional contexts, in names, and occasionally in ironic or nostalgic usage when evoking classical gender ideals.

The character 淑 is itself largely restricted to this role: it appears almost exclusively in compounds of feminine virtue — 淑女 (graceful woman), 淑德 (virtuous character), 賢淑, 溫淑 (gentle and pure). Its rarity and specificity make 賢淑 the natural stand-in compound for the syllable ㄙㄨㄎ as contributed by 淑, since no more common word carries 淑 in a position where its phonetic value is clear.

In **Korean**, 현숙 (*hyeonseuk*) functions both as an adjective — 현숙한 여성 ("a woman of refined virtue") — and, very commonly, as a given name for women. The name 賢淑/현숙 was among the most popular Korean women's given names of the mid-twentieth century, reflecting the continued cultural prestige of the virtues the compound names. In **Japanese**, 賢淑 (*kenshuku*) is infrequent in modern usage; the classical feminine virtue vocabulary has largely retreated to literary and historical contexts. Vietnamese **hiền thục** (*hiền* from 賢, *thục* from 淑) remains in use in formal and literary registers to describe a woman of gentle and cultivated character, alongside the native compound *nết na* for similar meanings.
