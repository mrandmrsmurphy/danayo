## Notes
- [燕](../characters/燕.md) "swallow (bird)" + [子](../characters/子.md) (noun suffix) — stand-in for [[燕]], which is too bounded to stand alone in Dan'a'yo.

燕 is the character for the barn swallow (family Hirundinidae), and 子 functions here as a colloquial nominalizing suffix common in Mandarin Chinese — the same suffix that appears in [[獅子]] "lion," [[椅子]] "chair," and dozens of other everyday nouns. The compound thus means simply "swallow," with 子 adding no semantic content beyond making the word sound more natural in spoken Chinese.

The barn swallow (Hirundo rustica) is one of the most culturally loaded birds in the East Asian world. In Chinese poetry and folk tradition it is a symbol of spring, homecoming, and conjugal fidelity — pairs of swallows nesting under the eaves appear throughout the Shijing (詩經) as emblems of faithful love. The Shang royal house traced its mythological origin to a swallow's egg, making 燕 one of the few birds to anchor a dynastic founding myth. The state of Yan (燕), one of the Warring States, took its name from the bird.

Cross-linguistically, 燕子 (yànzi) is the standard Mandarin term. In Japanese the Sino-Japanese reading (えん, EN) is essentially never used for the bird; instead the native word つばめ (tsubame) is universal, and is written with the same character 燕. Korean likewise uses the native 제비 (jebi) rather than any Sino-Korean form. Vietnamese uses con én / chim én, where én is native. This makes 燕子 a striking case of pan-Sinosphere divergence: one character, four completely different spoken words across the non-Mandarin languages.

As a secondary meaning, 燕子 can refer to a shuttlecock in certain Chinese regional usages, the feathered projectile's silhouette in flight evoking the swallow's forked tail.
