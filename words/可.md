## Notes
- [可 (char)](../characters/可%20(char).md) "can; may; able; permissible"

可 is the possibility/permission modal in Dan'a'yo — "can; may; able; permissible" — prefixing or postfixing to a verb to mark that the action is possible or allowed: 可食 (can eat; edible), 可去 (may go; it is permissible to go), 可読 (readable; legible). Among the Dan'a'yo mood particles, 可 marks positive possibility or permission, contrasting with [[不]] (negation), and appearing in compounds: [[可以]] (can; may), [[可不]] (can not; able to decline), and the double negation [[不可不]] (cannot not = must necessarily). In classical Chinese, 可 (OC *kʰaːlʔ) was the primary permissive/possibilitative modal — a function directly carried into Dan'a'yo. 可 also serves as the Dan'a'yo stand-in for the character [[可]].

Mandarin 可以 (kěyǐ, can; may; it is permissible), 能 (néng, can; be able to — physical/inherent ability), 会 (huì, can; know how to — learned ability). Cantonese 可以 (ho2 ji5, can; may), 識 (sik1, know how to; can — Cantonese native modal), 得 (dak1, can; permissible — postverbal). Japanese できる (dekiru, can; be able to — native potential), 〜られる (rareru, potential suffix: 食べられる can eat), 可能 (kanō, possible; ability). Korean 할 수 있다 (hal su itda, can; be able to), -ㄹ 수 있다, 가능하다 (ganeunghada, possible; feasible). Vietnamese có thể (can; be able to), được (can; allowed to — permissive modal), khả năng (可能, possibility; capability).

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 크 does not match Korean 가. 可 reads 크 (kǝ/ㄎㄜ) in Dan'a'yo — MC 溪母 (kʰ, aspirated velar stop) + 歌韻 (ɑ). Dan'a'yo preserves the MC aspiration of 溪母 as ㄎ (aspirated k-), while Sino-Korean 가 gives an unaspirated lenis initial ㄱ (g-), indicating that MC 溪母 deaspirated in this position. The MC vowel 歌韻 (ɑ): Dan'a'yo renders it as -ə (ㄜ/크), while Korean gives -a (가). Both the initial (aspirated ㄎ vs unaspirated ㄱ) and the vowel (-ə vs -a) differ — the same double pattern as [[他]] (ㄊㄜ/트 vs 타), where Dan'a'yo preserves MC aspiration and shifts 歌韻 to -ə, while Korean loses the aspiration and retains -a.
