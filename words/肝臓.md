## Notes
- [肝](../characters/肝.md) "liver" + [臓](../characters/臓.md) "organ, entrails" — stand-in for [[肝]], which cannot appear independently

**A fourth instance this sweep of the recurring Sino-Xenic homophone pattern, and the most solidly documented one yet**: Korean 간장 (gan-jang) is a genuine dictionary-recognized homograph, listed on Wiktionary as two entirely separate etymological entries under the identical spelling — Etymology 1, the everyday household word for "soy sauce" (native 간 + Sino 醬, "sauce"), and Etymology 2, this compound's own reading, the Sino-Korean medical term for "liver" (from 肝臟/肝臓, "liver-organ"). Both are real, independently common words; the soy-sauce sense is by far the more frequent one in everyday speech, with `characters/肝.md`'s own bare native/Sino-Korean reading 간 alone being the more common way to just say "liver" outside clinical registers.

Mandarin gānzàng, Cantonese gon1 zong6, Japanese かんぞう (kanzō), and Vietnamese gan (matching one of `characters/肝.md`'s own attested Vietnamese readings, alongside can and gang) round out the standard clinical/anatomical term for the organ across the sphere.
