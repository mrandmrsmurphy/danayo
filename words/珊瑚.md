## Notes
- [珊](../characters/珊.md) "coral" + [瑚](../characters/瑚.md) "coral"

珊瑚 is a semantic doublet: both 珊 and 瑚 mean coral, and their pairing creates a single disyllabic word from what are separately bound morphemes — neither 珊 nor 瑚 appears productively in modern Chinese without the other. The pair almost certainly entered the written language as a transliteration of a non-Chinese word for coral, given that the phonetic components (刪 for 珊 and 瓠/胡 for 瑚) don't have obvious phonological motivations in the Old Chinese of the Chinese heartland. The jade radical 王/玉 in both characters reflects the high value placed on red coral as a precious material, alongside gold, silver, and gems.

Coral occupies a significant place in East Asian material culture and religious symbolism. In Buddhism, coral (珊瑚) is traditionally listed among the saptaratna (七宝, seven treasures), alongside gold, silver, lapis lazuli, crystal, pearl, and agate — the precious substances that adorn the pure land of the Buddha. Red coral branches were prized decorative objects in imperial courts; Qing dynasty official caps bore coral decorations as rank insignia. The coral reefs of the South and East China Seas have been sites of sustained harvesting, trade, and, in modern times, territorial dispute and environmental concern.

Across the CJKV sphere the compound is stable: Mandarin 珊瑚 (shānhú), Japanese 珊瑚/さんご (sango), Korean 산호 (sanho), Vietnamese san hô. The kwin=false correction here: Dan'a'yo 산홋 (san + hou, with 瑚's full 羅馬字 hou giving the terminal consonant 훗) differs from Korean 산호 (sanho), where 瑚 maps to 호 without a final consonant in Korean Sino-Korean phonology.
