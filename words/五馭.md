## Notes
- [五 (char)](../characters/五%20(char).md) "five" + [馭](../characters/馭.md) "to drive (a chariot), to control" — the Five Charioteering Techniques, the fourth of the Six Arts (六芸) of Zhou-dynasty noble education.

五馭 (wǔyù), more commonly written 五御 in later sources, is the fourth item in the **《周禮·地官司徒·保氏》** curriculum, following [[五礼]], [[六楽]], and [[五射]]. The five techniques of ceremonial chariot-driving are:

1. **鳴和鸞** (míng hé luán) — "sounding the harmony-bells": controlling the chariot's speed so that the bells mounted on the harness ring in even rhythm
2. **逐水曲** (zhú shuǐ qū) — "following the bends of a stream": driving accurately along a winding watercourse without straying
3. **過君表** (guò jūn biǎo) — "passing the ruler's marker": threading the chariot precisely through a narrow gateway or marker without striking it, as etiquette demands when passing the ruler
4. **舞交衢** (wǔ jiāo qú) — "dancing at the crossroads": turning the chariot smoothly and elegantly at an intersection, as if performing a dance
5. **逐禽左** (zhú qín zuǒ) — "driving game to the left": maneuvering pursued game to the charioteer's left, the position from which the archer in the chariot could shoot

Like [[五射]], these were as much exercises in bearing and self-control as in practical skill — a Zhou nobleman's command of the chariot was inseparable from his ritual deportment.

The Dan'a'yo form uses [[馭]] (aliased to 驭), the character given in the 《周禮》 text quoted in [[translation/周礼 (book)]]; the more common modern form 五御 (with 御) is recorded as an alias.

As one of the Six Arts, 五馭 sits alongside [[五礼]], [[六楽]], [[五射]], [[六書]], and [[九数]].
