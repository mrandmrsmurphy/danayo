## Notes
- [漢](../characters/漢.md) "Han, Chinese" + [詩](../characters/詩.md) "poem"

漢詩 refers to poetry composed in classical Chinese — written in Literary Sinitic (文言文) using the regulated forms and tonal conventions of the Tang and earlier traditions. Across the CJKV sphere, the word specifically denotes Chinese-character poetry as a literary tradition practiced by non-Chinese authors: Japanese poets who wrote in classical Chinese, Korean scholars composing *hansi* (한시), and Vietnamese literati writing *Hán thi* all participated in the same pan-East Asian literary culture, using a shared written language that none of them spoke natively.

In Japan (かんし, kanshi), the tradition flourished from the Nara period through the Edo era; poets like Sugawara no Michizane (845–903) wrote kanshi of high literary distinction. The Heian court considered kanshi a mark of refinement alongside waka. In Korea, hansi (한시) was the prestige literary form of the Joseon scholar-official class and is considered a branch of Korean literature. In Vietnam, the tradition of Hán thi produced major works including the poetry of Nguyễn Trãi and Hồ Xuân Hương (who also wrote in Nôm). This shared tradition is one of the defining features of the East Asian cultural sphere that Dan'a'yo draws upon.
