## Notes
- [火 (char)](../characters/火%20(char).md) "fire" + [紅 (char)](../characters/紅%20(char).md) "red; crimson" — 火 specifies the quality of the red; compare 深紅 (deep red), 淡紅 (pale red)

**火紅** (*huǒhóng*) describes a red of maximum brilliance and heat — the vivid, glowing red of open flame, live embers, or molten metal. The compound is a colour-modifier construction in which 火 functions adjectivally, specifying not merely that something is red but that it is red in the particular way that fire is red: luminous, intense, radiating. It is the colour of festival lanterns at their brightest, of sunrise and sunset at their most dramatic, of silk that catches the light fully.

In modern Chinese, 火红 carries a productive figurative extension meaning *thriving, booming, in full glory* — as in 火红的年代 ("the blazing years," a nostalgic or celebratory phrase for a time of vigorous national energy) or 生意火红 ("business is blazing," i.e. doing extremely well). This extension follows naturally from the original: fire at its peak is associated with vitality, intensity, and forward movement. The same metaphorical range is found in English *blazing* and *burning*.

Korean 화홍 (*hwahong*) is the regular Sino-Korean reading; the colour compound is literary rather than everyday, where 빨간 or 붉은 cover the same ground natively. Vietnamese *hoả hồng* is the Sino-Vietnamese form. Japanese かこう (*kakō*) is the formal Sino-Japanese reading but rarely encountered in modern usage, where 真っ赤 (*makkà*) or 緋色 (*hiiro*) are preferred for similarly intense reds.
