## Notes
- [已 (char)](../characters/已%20(char).md) "-ed; perfective"

已 is the perfective aspect marker in Dan'a'yo — "-ed; (has) done; completed" — postfixing to a verb to indicate the action is realized and complete: 食已 (has eaten; ate), 去已 (has gone; gone away). It marks completion regardless of when — the event is done, not necessarily past. Among the Dan'a'yo aspect markers, 已 (perfective/realized) contrasts with [[未]] (not yet; unrealized) and [[将]] (will; prospective). In classical Chinese, 已 (OC *ləʔ) was a verbal particle indicating completion — a function directly preserved in Dan'a'yo. Note: [[矣]] is a distinct classical particle sometimes conflated with 已 in secondary sources. 已 also serves as the Dan'a'yo stand-in for the character [[已]].

The comparison chart: Mandarin 了 (le, perfective suffix after verb: 吃了 ate; has eaten), 已经 (yǐjīng, already; has done). Cantonese 咗 (zo2, perfective suffix: 食咗 ate; has eaten). Japanese た/てしまった (ta / te shimatta, past / done and finished: 食べた ate; 食べてしまった finished eating). Korean -았/었다 (-at/-eot-da, past/perfective verb ending: 먹었다 ate; has eaten), 이미 (imi, already). Vietnamese đã (pre-verbal perfective: đã ăn has eaten / already ate; đã đi has gone).

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 으 does not match Korean 이. 已 reads 으 ('ǝ/ㄜ) in Dan'a'yo — MC 以母 (j, palatal glide) + 之韻 (ɨ). The MC palatal glide 以母 drops to a null initial in both Dan'a'yo and Korean, leaving a vowel-initial syllable. The divergence lies in the vowel: MC 之韻 (ɨ) gives Dan'a'yo -ə (ㄜ/으), a mid-central vowel, while Sino-Korean 이 gives the high front -i vowel. Both systems drop the initial; only the vowel grade differs — Dan'a'yo -ə vs Korean -i.
