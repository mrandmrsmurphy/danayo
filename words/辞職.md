## Notes
- [辞](../characters/辞.md) "to take leave; to decline" + [職](../characters/職.md) "post; duty; official position"

辞職 combines 辞 — to take formal leave, to decline, to relinquish — with 職 — an official post or delegated duty. The graphemic history of 辞 (traditional 辭) layers 辛 (hardship, punishment) and 舌 (tongue), evoking the difficulty of formal speech acts of refusal and farewell; in classical usage 辭 covered both "to say farewell" and "to decline/refuse," and both senses are active in 辞職 — the resigned official both refuses to continue and formally takes leave. The compound has thus always carried a double valence of volition and rupture.

In the Confucian political tradition, withdrawal from service had specific moral weight: resigning from office under a corrupt or unjust ruler was itself a virtuous act, distinct from both dismissal and demotion. The resigned official implicitly declared the ruler unworthy of loyal service, making 辞職 a morally coded term in classical political writing. In modern administrative contexts the moral drama has flattened: 辞職 (Chinese cízhí, Japanese jishoku, Korean 사직 sajik, Vietnamese từ chức) is now the standard formal term for submitting a letter of resignation, appearing in employment law, parliamentary procedure, and corporate governance across all four writing-using CJKV languages.

The kwin=false marking reflects the divergence between Dan'a'yo 치직 (ci + jig) and Korean 사직 (sajik), where 辞 maps to 사 in Korean Sino-Korean phonology but to 치 in Dan'a'yo — a consistent initial consonant difference for characters in this Middle Chinese initial class.
