## Notes
- [二 (char)](../characters/二%20(char).md) "two" + [心 (char)](../characters/心%20(char).md) "heart"

Literally "two hearts" — the state of having a divided or wavering heart. 二心 describes the moral condition of a person whose loyalty, devotion, or intention is split: serving two masters, feigning allegiance while plotting otherwise, or being unable to commit fully to one path.

In classical Chinese political thought, 二心 is one of the gravest character defects a minister or subject could possess. The loyal official was expected to have 一心 (one heart, undivided loyalty) toward his lord; 二心 implied the possibility of betrayal. The *Analects* and other classical texts repeatedly praise the virtue of single-minded loyalty (忠, zhōng) in contrast to the duplicity implied by 二心.

In Japanese, the kun'yomi **ふたごころ** (futagokoro, literally "two-heart") is a poetic and literary word appearing in classical literature and waka poetry to describe wavering feelings, inconstancy in love, or the painful pull between two loyalties. It carries a more elegiac and romantic tone than the Chinese 二心, which is primarily a moral and political indictment.

The term also appears in Buddhist contexts to describe the mind that cannot fully commit to practice — distracted by worldly concerns while nominally pursuing enlightenment.
