## Notes
- [五 (char)](../characters/五%20(char).md) "five" + [戒](../characters/戒.md) "warn; admonish; precept"

The Five Precepts are the foundational ethical code of lay Buddhism — the most basic level of moral discipline expected of all Buddhist practitioners, not only monks and nuns. They are:

1. **不殺生** — refrain from taking life (non-violence toward all sentient beings)
2. **不偸盜** — refrain from taking what is not given (no stealing or fraud)
3. **不邪淫** — refrain from sexual misconduct (broadly: conduct that harms others through sexuality)
4. **不妄語** — refrain from false speech (no lying, slander, or idle chatter)
5. **不飮酒** — refrain from intoxicants (alcohol and substances that cloud the mind)

The Five Precepts are observed by lay followers across Theravāda, Mahāyāna, and Vajrayāna traditions, though interpretation and emphasis vary. Ordained monks and nuns follow far more extensive codes: the Theravāda Pātimokkha contains 227 rules for monks and 311 for nuns. The Five Precepts represent the ethical minimum — the threshold between a life that supports and one that obstructs the Buddhist path.

In East Asian Confucian-Buddhist synthesis, the Five Precepts are frequently mapped onto the Five Virtues (五常, [[五常]]): 仁 (benevolence → no killing), 義 (righteousness → no stealing), 禮 (propriety → no sexual misconduct), 智 (wisdom → no intoxication), 信 (faithfulness → no lying). This parallel made Buddhism more legible to a Confucian audience and helped facilitate the religion's acceptance in China and East Asia.
