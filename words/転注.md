## Notes
- [転](../characters/転.md) "to turn, to change" + [注](../characters/注.md) "to pour; to annotate" — one of the [[六書]] (liùshū), the traditional six-category classification of Chinese characters; the most obscure and disputed of the six.

**転注** (*zhuǎnzhù*, lit. "turning and pouring/annotating") is traditionally described as characters whose meaning has been extended or "redirected" by usage, such that an existing graph comes to stand for a related but distinct concept — a kind of sanctioned semantic drift rather than a method of graphic construction. The textbook example, 考 and 老 (both "old; to examine"), illustrates the idea: two characters originally close in form and meaning came to be used for related senses, each "annotating" the other.

Unlike the first four categories (象形, 指事, 会意, 形声), which describe *how a character is built*, 転注 and 仮借 — the last two of the 六書 — describe *how an existing character is used or reinterpreted*. Because of this difference in kind, and because the *Shuowen Jiezi*'s original examples are genuinely ambiguous, 転注 has been the subject of disagreement among traditional and modern scholars for centuries; some treat it as a minor footnote, others as evidence that the "six categories" were never meant to be six mutually exclusive types of character construction at all, but a mixed list of formation methods and usage conventions.

In Japanese pedagogy 転注 is typically mentioned but explicitly set aside as "not used" in practical character analysis — the four structural categories (象形, 指事, 会意, 形声) cover essentially all characters that need classifying, while 転注 and 仮借 (phonetic loan) remain primarily of historical and philological interest. The term and its place in the traditional scheme are preserved likewise in Korean (전주, *jeonju*) and Vietnamese (chuyển chú) grammatical tradition.
