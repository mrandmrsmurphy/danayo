## Notes
- [細 (char)](../characters/細%20(char).md) "fine; thin; delicate; minute" + [胞](../characters/胞.md) "womb; sac; placenta"

細胞 is a Meiji-era Japanese neologism coined to translate the German word Zelle (cell), itself borrowed from Latin cella (small room). The compound was created in Japan in the 1870s–1880s as part of the enormous project of translating Western scientific vocabulary into Chinese characters, and spread from Japanese scientific writing into Chinese, Korean, and Vietnamese — one of the clearest examples of the reverse flow in which Japanese-coined sinographic compounds became standard across the entire CJKV sphere rather than the other way around.

The graphemic choice is transparent: 細 (fine, thin, minute — the quality of being extremely small or delicate) describes the scale of the cell, while 胞 (womb-sac, the enveloping membrane of a fetus) provides the metaphor of a bounded, membrane-enclosed unit of life. The compound thus encodes both the smallness and the membrane-bounded integrity of the biological cell. From this base, the scientific vocabulary extends: 細胞核 (nucleus), 幹細胞 (stem cell), 細胞分裂 (cell division), 細胞膜 (cell membrane), 赤血細胞 (red blood cell), 白血細胞 (white blood cell).

Across the CJKV sphere: Mandarin 细胞 (xìbāo, simplified), Japanese さいぼう (saibō), Korean 세포 (sepo), Vietnamese tế bào. The kwin=false marking reflects the divergence between Dan'a'yo 세뱟 (sei + byau) and Korean 세포 (sepo) — 胞 maps to 포 in Korean Sino-Korean phonology but to 뱟 (byau) in Dan'a'yo, a consistent vowel divergence in the labial-initial final class.
