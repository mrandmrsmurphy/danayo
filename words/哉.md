## Notes
- [哉 (char)](../characters/哉%20(char).md) "O (vocative)"

哉 is the vocative case particle — 呼格, marking direct address: "O!" It attaches to the name or title of the addressee before the predicate: 孔子哉，何為也 (O Confucius, what are you doing?), 善哉！ (Excellent! How good! — here used as an exclamatory SFP). At the end of a noun phrase before the verb, 哉 is a pure vocative marker; at the end of a complete utterance it functions as an exclamatory sentence-final particle (感詞), expressing admiration, alarm, or lament. In the comparison chart, 哉 parallels Mandarin 啊/呀 (ā/ya, exclamatory particle), Cantonese 呀 (aa3), Japanese よ (yo, exclamatory SFP), Korean 아/야 (a/ya, the vocative suffix), Vietnamese ơi (vocative marker). 哉 also serves as the Dan'a'yo stand-in for the character [[哉]].

Mandarin 啊/呀 (ā/ya): 朋友啊！(O friend!; Hey friend!), 太好了啊！(Oh, that's great!). These are modern colloquial equivalents; classical Chinese used 哉 and 乎 extensively as final particles. Cantonese 呀 (aa3): 你好呀！(Hello!; Hi there!), 係囉呀！(That's right!). Japanese よ (yo): exclamatory and assertive SFP — 行くよ！(I'm going!), 綺麗だよ！(It's beautiful!). The Japanese vocative is formed not by a particle but by addressing someone directly, often with ね or よ appended. Korean vocative: 아/야 (a/ya) suffixes to the addressee's name depending on whether it ends in a vowel or consonant — 영수야！(Hey Youngsu!), 선생님！(Teacher!). Vietnamese ơi: Ơi anh！(Hey you!; Listen up!), Trời ơi！(Oh heavens!; Good grief! — lit. "O heaven!"), em ơi (dear younger one — term of address).

kwin is true: Dan'a'yo 재 exactly matches Korean 재. 哉 reads 재 (jai/ㄐㄚㄧ) in Dan'a'yo — MC 精母 (t͡s) + 咍韻 (ʌi) gives the affricate ㄐ (j-) with the -ai diphthong in both Dan'a'yo and Sino-Korean, producing identical 재 forms. Note that 哉 (ㄐㄚㄧ/jai/재) is a Dan'a'yo homophone of [[在]] (ㄐㄚㄧ/jai/재, "to exist").
