## Notes
- [中 (char)](../characters/中%20(char).md) "middle" + [旬](../characters/旬.md) "ten-day period of the month"

中旬 is "the middle ten days of the month" — 中 (middle) + 旬 (a ten-day interval). Classical East Asian calendars divided each month into three 旬: 上旬 (first ten days), 中旬 (middle ten days), and 下旬 (final ten days). The system reflects the base-10 structure of the ancient Chinese calendrical system — months were divided into three decades of ten days each, parallel to the ten Heavenly Stems (天干) cycle that ran alongside. The 旬 system pre-dates the seven-day week's adoption in East Asia and remains in formal use in weather forecasts, historical writing, and official correspondence.

Mandarin 中旬 (zhōngxún) is the standard formal term: 七月中旬 (the middle ten days of July; mid-July). Cantonese 中旬 (zung1 ceon4) similarly. Japanese 中旬 (ちゅうじゅん, chūjun) is still in regular use in formal writing, news, and weather reports: 一月中旬 (mid-January). Korean 중순 (中旬, jungson) is the standard: 삼월 중순 (mid-March). Vietnamese trung tuần (Sino-Vietnamese: 中=trung, 旬=tuần) is the equivalent.

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 중슌 differs from Korean 중순 in the second syllable — 旬 reads 슌 (syun/ㄙ⼜ㄋ) in Dan'a'yo but 순 (sun) in Sino-Korean. The difference is the medial ⼜ (labial glide w): Middle Chinese 旬 (邪 initial + 諄 final, a 合口 rhyme) yields Dan'a'yo ㄙ⼜ㄋ, preserving the labialised quality from the 合口 (rounded) rhyme class, while Sino-Korean 순 drops the medial rounding. This labial-medial preservation is a regular feature of Dan'a'yo phonology for MC 合口 finals.
