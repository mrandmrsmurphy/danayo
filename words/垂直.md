## Notes
- [垂](../characters/垂.md) "hang; dangle" + [直 (char)](../characters/直%20(char).md) "straight; erect; aligned"

垂直 is "vertical; perpendicular" — 垂 (to hang; to dangle) + 直 (straight; erect; aligned). The compound literally means "hanging straight" — the direction defined by a plumb line, something suspended freely under gravity, which by definition traces the vertical axis. This etymology is parallel to the English "perpendicular" (from Latin perpendiculum, plumb line) and to German senkrecht (sinking straight): all three derive the concept of verticality from the image of a hanging weight. As a Dan'a'yo 性詞, 垂直 describes any line or surface exactly vertical, and more broadly any two lines or planes that intersect at a right angle (perpendicular to each other).

Mandarin 垂直 (chuízhí) is the standard mathematical and scientific term: 垂直線 (vertical line), 垂直平面 (vertical plane), 互相垂直 (mutually perpendicular). Cantonese 垂直 (seoi4 zik6). Japanese 垂直 (すいちょく, suichoku) is the standard term in mathematics, science, and architecture: 垂直線 (vertical line), 垂直に立てる (to set vertically), 垂直尾翼 (vertical stabiliser, on an aircraft). Korean 수직 (垂直, sujik): 수직선 (vertical line), 수직으로 (vertically), 수직 낙하 (vertical drop). Vietnamese thùy trực (Sino-Vietnamese: 垂=thùy, 直=trực); everyday Vietnamese uses thẳng đứng (standing straight) for "vertical" and vuông góc (square angle/right angle) for "perpendicular."

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 쥐직 differs from Korean 수직 in the first syllable. 垂 reads 쥐 (jui/ㄐㄨㄧ) in Dan'a'yo but 수 (su) in Sino-Korean — MC 禪母 (d͡ʑ, voiced palato-alveolar affricate) gives Dan'a'yo ㄐ (j-), while Sino-Korean gives ㅅ (s-). This j-/s- correspondence from MC 禪母 is a regular split: Dan'a'yo preserves the affricate character of 禪 as ㄐ (j), while Sino-Korean reduces it to the plain fricative ㅅ (s). The second syllable is identical in both systems: Dan'a'yo 직 = Sino-Korean 직, with MC 澄母 (ɖ) giving ㄐ/ㅈ and the 職開 final giving -ig/-ik in both.
