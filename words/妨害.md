## Notes
- [妨](../characters/妨.md) "to hinder; to obstruct; to block" + [害](../characters/害.md) "harm; damage; to injure"

妨害 means "to obstruct; to hinder; to interfere with" — a verb compound combining 妨 (to block, to impede) and 害 (to harm, to damage). The two characters cover complementary aspects of interference: 妨 is the obstruction itself (blocking access, preventing action), and 害 adds the dimension of consequent harm. Together, 妨害 implies obstruction with harmful effect — disrupting an activity, impeding a process, or interfering with someone's exercise of a right. The nuance of 害 gives 妨害 a slightly stronger connotation than 妨碍 (碍 = obstacle without implied harm), though in practice the two have converged.

Mandarin 妨害 (fánghài) is the formal and legal standard for interference or obstruction: 妨害公務 (obstruction of public duties), 妨害通信 (interference with communications), 妨害社會秩序 (disturbing public order). Japanese 妨害 (ぼうがい, bōgai) is both the legal term and the everyday word for interference — 妨害電波 (jamming signals), 妨害工作 (sabotage), 業務妨害 (obstruction of business, a criminal offence). Korean 방해 (banghae) is the standard everyday word for interruption, disturbance, or obstruction: 방해하다 (to obstruct/disturb), 방해물 (obstacle). Vietnamese cản trở (obstruct, impede) is the natural equivalent.

Japanese usage has effectively merged 妨害 and 妨碍/妨礙 — both are pronounced ぼうがい and used interchangeably in modern Japanese, despite the different second character (害 = harm vs 碍 = obstacle). This merger is reflected in the aliases 妨碍 (simplified second character) and 妨礙 (traditional form). In classical Chinese, 妨碍 (fáng'ài) was slightly the more common form; modern Mandarin legal texts now prefer 妨害 in formal statutes. Dan'a'yo uses 妨害 as the primary entry, retaining the 妨碍/妨礙 variants as aliases for cross-referencing across CJKV sources.
