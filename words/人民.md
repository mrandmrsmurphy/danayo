## Notes
- [人 (char)](../characters/人%20(char).md) "person" + [民](../characters/民.md) "the people; citizenry" — stand-in for [[民]], which cannot appear independently

Mandarin rénmín, Cantonese jan4 man4, and Japanese じんみん (jinmin) are all neutral, standard words for "the people" (人民日報, "People's Daily"; 人民元, "renminbi/yuan"). Vietnamese nhân dân is likewise the standard, neutral term (verified as real and common; also used in official state naming, Cộng hòa Xã hội Chủ nghĩa Việt Nam's own institutions).

**Korean 인민 carries a real, heavily documented political charge that the other four languages lack**: verified via search that 인민 has been strongly associated with communism and North Korea in South Korean usage since the 1940s — South Korea's own constitutional drafters replaced 인민 with 국민 specifically to avoid this association, and North Korea's official name for itself, 조선민주주의인민공화국 (DPRK), uses 인민 as its own ideologically loaded term for "the people" (the revolutionary masses, explicitly excluding capitalists/landlords in North Korean usage). South Koreans today default to 국민 or 시민 instead. Kept 인민 as the honest reading (it directly matches this word's own hanja, 人民, unlike 국민 which is 國民), but flagged the connotation explicitly rather than presenting it as neutral.
