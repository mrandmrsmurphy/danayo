## Notes
- [注](../characters/注.md) "pour; annotate" + [音](../characters/音.md) "sound; pronunciation" — literally "phonetic annotation"

**注音** (*zhùyīn*) means phonetic notation — the practice of annotating characters with symbols representing their pronunciation. It is most commonly encountered as the abbreviation for **注音符號** (*zhùyīn fúhào*, "phonetic notation symbols"), the Bopomofo system, and in Dan'a'yo it names both the system and the `注音:` frontmatter field that every character and word entry carries.

The 注音符號 system was developed by a committee of Chinese linguists convened at the Convention for the Unification of Pronunciation (讀音統一會) in 1913, with official promulgation by the Republic of China government in 1918. Its 37 symbols — 21 consonants, 13 vowels, and 4 tone diacritics — are derived from simplified or partial forms of existing characters: ㄅ from 勹, ㄆ from 攵, ㄇ from 冂, and so on. This character-derived origin gives the system an organic connection to the script it annotates, unlike purely alphabetic systems. The informal name **Bopomofo** comes from the first four symbols of the system (ㄅㄆㄇㄈ).

注音符號 remains the official phonetic system for Mandarin in Taiwan today and is central to literacy education, dictionary indexing, and phonetic annotation on children's texts. It contrasts with [[拼音]] (*pīnyīn*, Pinyin), the Latin-alphabet system adopted in mainland China from 1958 and now the global standard for Romanizing Mandarin. The two systems are in competition as pedagogical standards, and their difference marks the Taiwan/mainland orthographic divide as clearly as 旧字体 vs. 简体字 marks the character divide.

In Dan'a'yo, the Bopomofo inventory is extended and redeployed to represent Middle Chinese phonology. Standard Bopomofo symbols cover modern Mandarin initials and finals; Dan'a'yo adds or repurposes symbols for Middle Chinese distinctions not present in modern Mandarin (such as the voiced obstruents and the entering-tone finals). The `注音:` field in every vault entry records the Dan'a'yo phonological value of that character or word in this system — making 注音 at once an object of description and the instrument of description throughout the vault.

The broader meaning of 注音, beyond the specific symbol system, is the general practice of adding phonetic guides to characters — what Japanese calls 振り仮名 (*furigana*) and Korean calls 독음 (*dogeum*). In this sense, the ruby-text annotations throughout the vault (`<ruby>...</ruby><rt>...</rt>`) are all acts of 注音.
