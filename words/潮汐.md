## Notes
- [潮](../characters/潮.md) "tide; current" + [汐](../characters/汐.md) "evening tide" — stand-in for both [[潮]] and [[汐]], neither of which can appear independently
- 潮汐 is built on a distinction that older Sinitic vocabulary drew more finely than modern speech does: 潮 named the morning flood-tide, the water that "comes in with the dawn," while 汐 was reserved for the evening ebb and flow — the counterpart return of the water as day gave way to night. Paired together, the two characters span a full daily cycle, and the compound has settled into the ordinary scientific and everyday word for "tide" as a phenomenon, the regular rise and fall of the sea governed by the pull of moon and sun. The old morning/evening split survives mostly as etymological trivia now — no contemporary speaker reaches for 潮 or 汐 individually to mark time of day — but it explains why the compound reads as a totality rather than a redundancy: not "tide-tide" but "the tide, in its complete daily round." Mandarin cháoxī, Cantonese ciu4 zik6, Sino-Japanese ちょうせき (chōseki), Sino-Korean 조석 (joseok), and Sino-Vietnamese triều tịch all carry the word as the default, register-neutral term for tides — found equally in oceanography textbooks and beach-side conversation.
