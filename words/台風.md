## Notes
- [台 (char)](../characters/台%20(char).md) "platform; pedestal" (absorbs 颱) + [風 (char)](../characters/風%20(char).md) "wind"

台風 names the tropical cyclones of the western Pacific — the storms that form over warm ocean water and strike the coastlines of China, Taiwan, Japan, Korea, the Philippines, and Vietnam with destructive regularity. The traditional orthography is 颱風 (颱 being a character coined specifically for the typhoon phenomenon), but in simplified Chinese and Japanese the simplified form 台風 has become standard, and Dan'a'yo follows the simplified convention. The alias 颱風 preserves the traditional form.

The word entered world languages through Cantonese and Hokkien contact with European sailors. Portuguese and English received it as *tufão* / *typhoon* via Arabic *ṭūfān* (طوفان, "tempest, deluge") — itself possibly reinforced by or confused with Greek *Τυφών* (Typhon, the storm giant of Greek mythology). The phonological path is debated, but the word appears in English from at least the late sixteenth century, making it one of the earliest sinographic loanwords into European languages.

Across the CJKV sphere the compound is consistent: Mandarin 台风/颱風 (táifēng), Japanese 台風 (taifū), Korean 태풍 (taepung), and Vietnamese bão (which uses the native term rather than a Sino-Vietnamese calque, though typhoon-specific vocabulary can also use 台風 in formal registers borrowed from Chinese). The kwin=false marking reflects the Dan'a'yo 대뿡 (dai + fung) versus Korean 태풍, where both characters map to different vowels in the two systems.
