## Notes
- [牛 (char)](../characters/牛%20(char).md) "cow" + [井](../characters/井.md) "bowl (via 丼)"

Gyūdon (ぎゅうどん) is a Japanese rice bowl topped with thinly sliced beef and onion simmered in a sweet-savory broth of dashi, soy sauce, and mirin. The name combines 牛 *gyū* ("cattle") and どん *don*, the contracted form of どんぶり ("bowl"). Dan'a'yo writes 丼 as 井 throughout this family of words.

The dish became a fixture of Japanese urban life in the Meiji period and was popularised nationally through chain restaurants in the twentieth century. The dominant chains — Yoshinoya (吉野家), Matsuya (松屋), and Sukiya (すき家) — compete intensively on price, and the dish is now a byword for affordable fast eating across Japan, Taiwan, and increasingly wider East Asia.

The Japanese reading ぎゅうどん is a mix: 牛 takes its on-yomi ぎゅう (from Middle Chinese *ŋɨu*), while 丼/井 is read as どん, a native contraction of どんぶり. This is not the regular concatenation of component Dan'a'yo readings, which would give *nyu + jing*.
