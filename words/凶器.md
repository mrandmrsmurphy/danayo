## Notes
- [凶 (char)](../characters/凶%20(char).md) "unlucky; sinister" + [器](../characters/器.md) "container; vessel; implement"

A lethal weapon; an instrument used to kill or seriously injure — specifically the physical implement of a crime. The word literally means "sinister implement," 器 being the classical term for any crafted tool or vessel. Here the tool has been turned to its worst possible purpose.

In Chinese criminal law and journalism, 凶器 specifically refers to the **murder weapon recovered at or near a crime scene** — the physical evidence linking perpetrator to act. The standard forensic phrase **殺人凶器** (shārén xiōngqì, "the murder weapon") appears routinely in police announcements, court proceedings, and crime reportage. To 持凶器 ("carry a deadly weapon") is a separate criminal aggravator in Chinese law, distinct from the underlying offense.

In Japanese, **凶器** (kyōki) is equally central to crime reporting. Given Japan's extremely low rate of firearm violence, 凶器 in Japanese news most commonly refers to bladed weapons (刃物, hamono), blunt instruments (鈍器, donki), or improvised implements. Police announcements routinely note whether 凶器は発見された ("a weapon was recovered") and specify type and condition. The word appears in the set phrase **凶器準備集合罪** (the crime of assembling with weapons in readiness for group violence) — a specific Japanese criminal offense targeting organized gang violence.

An archaic secondary meaning preserved in classical Chinese texts: 凶器 also referred to **burial implements and grave goods** — the ritual objects associated with mourning and interment. This funerary sense survives in classical literature and preserves the older cosmological meaning of 凶 as "pertaining to death and ill-omened events" rather than specifically "criminal."
