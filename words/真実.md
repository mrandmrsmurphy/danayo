## Notes
- [真 (char)](../characters/真%20(char).md) "true; genuine" + [実](../characters/実.md) "substance; fruit; reality"

真実 joins 真 — that which is genuine, unfeigned, without adulteration — with 実 — substance, fruit, what actually exists as opposed to what is merely said or named. The compound translates as "truth" in the sense of a reality that corresponds exactly to what it appears to be: not simply an accurate proposition (which might be covered by 正確 or 正しい) but the quality of being genuinely, substantively real. The distinction runs through classical usage: 真 opposes counterfeit and performance; 実 opposes emptiness and name without content.

The compound appears across all five CJKV source languages as the standard elevated word for truth or authentic reality. Mandarin uses 真實/真实 (zhēnshí) in both philosophical and everyday registers; Japanese しんじつ (shinjitsu) is the standard literary term and appears in titles, journalism, and academic prose; Korean 진실 (jinsil) is similarly standard and carries strong moral weight (진실을 말하다 = "to speak the truth"). Vietnamese chân thật uses Sino-Vietnamese chân (真) + thật (actual), functioning as an adjective meaning "genuine; sincere." The Dan'a'yo form follows the Japanese shinjitai orthography, using simplified 実 rather than traditional 實.

The kwin=false marking reflects a phonological divergence in the second character: Dan'a'yo 싣 (sid, from 実's MC reading) ends in a stop /d/, while Korean 진실 ends in 실 (sil) with a lateral /l/ — the two final consonant values have evolved differently even from the same Middle Chinese source.
