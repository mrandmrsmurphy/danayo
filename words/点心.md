## Notes
- [点 (char)](../characters/点%20(char).md) "point" + [心 (char)](../characters/心%20(char).md) "heart" — literally "touch the heart [with a little something]." Neither character's own `stand_in` points here (both are bare self-standing characters); 点心 is an independent compound. `kwin: false` per the AND-rule: `characters/点 (char).md` is `kwin: false`, `characters/心 (char).md` is `kwin: true` → compound `false`.
- **A striking and important cross-linguistic sense divergence, documented rather than forced into agreement**: Mandarin diǎnxīn, Cantonese dim2 sam1 (the origin of the English loanword "dim sum"), and Japanese てんしん all mean "snack; light dishes; dim sum." Korean 점심 (jeomsim), however, is one of the most common everyday Korean words — and it means "lunch," the midday meal (아침/점심/저녁 "breakfast/lunch/dinner"), not a snack at all. A learner should not assume 점심 means the same thing as its Mandarin/Cantonese/Japanese cognates — this is a genuine, high-stakes false-friend risk. Vietnamese điểm tâm keeps the Sino sense of "light meal, snack" (also often used for "breakfast" specifically in Vietnamese usage), aligning with the Mandarin/Cantonese/Japanese cluster rather than Korean.
