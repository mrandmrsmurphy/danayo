## Notes
- [玄 (char)](../characters/玄%20(char).md) "black, mysterious" + [武 (char)](../characters/武%20(char).md) "martial, military"

玄武 (Xuánwǔ, "Black Warrior" or "Black Tortoise") is one of the Four Symbols (四象, Sìxiàng) — the four mythological creatures corresponding to the cardinal directions in Chinese cosmology. 玄武 guards the North, paired with the colour black and the element Water. It is traditionally depicted as a tortoise entwined with a serpent, an image of extraordinary longevity, cosmic stability, and yin-force power.

The compound 玄武 also gives its name to Xuanwu Lake (玄武湖) in Nanjing and, more fatefully, to the Xuanwu Gate (玄武門) of the Tang imperial palace — scene of the Xuanwu Gate Incident (玄武門之變, 626 AD), in which the prince Li Shimin ambushed and killed his two brothers before forcing his father Emperor Gaozu to abdicate in his favour. Li Shimin became Emperor Taizong, one of the most celebrated rulers in Chinese history, and 玄武門 has since become shorthand in Chinese historiography for dynastic fratricide at the moment of succession.

In Daoist religion, Xuanwu was elevated to major deity status — Xuantian Shangdi (玄天上帝, "Supreme Emperor of the Dark Heaven") — patron of the martial arts, the north wind, and the regulation of water. The Wudang Mountains (武當山), the great centre of Daoist practice in Hubei, take their name from his cult: 武當 meaning "fitting for [the martial virtue of] 武."

The four symbols together — [[青龍]] (Azure Dragon, East), [[白虎]] (White Tiger, West), [[朱雀]] (Vermilion Bird, South), and 玄武 (Black Tortoise, North) — appear throughout East Asian art, architecture, cosmological mapping, and geomancy (風水).
