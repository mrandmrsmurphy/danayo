## Notes
- [愛 (char)](../characters/愛%20(char).md) "love" + [人 (char)](../characters/人%20(char).md) "person" — neither character's own `stand_in` points here (both are bare self-standing characters). `kwin: false` per the AND-rule: `characters/愛 (char).md` is `kwin: true`, `characters/人 (char).md` is `kwin: false` → compound `false`.
- **A striking cross-linguistic sense divergence, documented rather than forced into agreement — genuinely worth flagging for learners**: in mainland Mandarin (the HSK-standard usage), 爱人 àirén is the neutral, standard word for "spouse" (husband or wife), NOT a dating partner or illicit lover — a common trap for learners (verified via search); Taiwan/Hong Kong Mandarin instead uses it closer to "lover," sometimes with an illicit connotation. Korean 애인 and Vietnamese người yêu both mean "boyfriend/girlfriend" (a neutral, unmarried dating partner). Japanese あいじん, however, specifically and commonly means "mistress; the other party in an affair" (a post-WWII semantic narrowing — before the war it simply meant "lover," per historical sources found via search). Calling someone's spouse 愛人 in Japanese would wrongly imply an affair — the four languages have genuinely diverged onto different, sometimes contradictory, senses of the same two characters.
