## Notes
- [四 (char)](../characters/四%20(char).md) "four" + [海](../characters/海.md) "sea" — the four directional seas enclosing [[天下]]

In classical Chinese cosmology, [[天下]] (All-Under-Heaven) is enclosed on four sides by the 四海 — [[東海]], [[西海]], [[南海]], and [[北海]]. These are not primarily geographic designations: the 四海 are the conceptual boundary of the civilized world, the outer limit beyond which nothing politically or morally significant exists. 四海之内 (within the four seas) means simply *the world*, or *everywhere*; it is used interchangeably with 天下 in classical prose. 四海之外 (beyond the four seas) means outside all civilization entirely.

The moral extension of the term is made explicit in the *Analects*: 四海之内，皆兄弟也 — "Within the four seas, all are brothers." The phrase appears in a passage where Zixia consoles a disciple who fears that, being far from home, he has no friends. The response asserts that the entirety of the civilized world — bounded by the four seas — constitutes a moral community. This usage makes 四海 one of the most frequently cited classical phrases in East Asian ethical literature.

Each sea carried different associations: [[東海]] was the most geographically stable, consistently identified with the East China Sea; [[南海]] was the most politically consequential, overlapping directly with the modern South China Sea; [[北海]] was the most mythologically rich, linked to the cosmic fish-bird of the Zhuangzi and to the exile of Su Wu; [[西海]] was the most geographically unstable, its referent shifting westward as the known world expanded. Together they constitute the spatial grammar of classical Chinese thought about the world.
