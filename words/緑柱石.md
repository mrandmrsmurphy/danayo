## Notes
- [緑 (char)](../characters/緑%20(char).md) "green" + [柱 (char)](../characters/柱%20(char).md) "pillar; column" + [石 (char)](../characters/石%20(char).md) "stone" — literally "green column stone"

**緑柱石** (*lǜzhùshí*) is beryl — the mineral beryllium aluminium cyclosilicate (Be₃Al₂Si₆O₁₈). The name is a transparent descriptive calque: it names the mineral by its two most diagnostic features — its characteristic hexagonal prismatic crystal habit (柱, "column") and its typical colouration (緑, "green"). Beryl belongs to the cyclosilicate group and forms elongated six-sided prisms, often of considerable size; single crystals measuring metres in length have been recorded.

Pure beryl is colourless; trace impurities produce the full range of gem varieties that have made it one of the most economically and culturally significant minerals across Eurasian history. Emerald (祖母緑, *zǔmǔlǜ*) is the chromium- and vanadium-coloured deep green variety, prized since antiquity in Egypt, Persia, and Mesoamerica. Aquamarine is the iron-coloured pale blue-green variety, its name a Latin calque of "sea water." Morganite (pink, manganese), heliodor (yellow, iron), and goshenite (colourless) complete the principal gem varieties. All share the same crystal structure; variety is entirely a matter of impurity.

The element beryllium (Be, atomic number 4) was first identified in 1798 by Louis-Nicolas Vauquelin, who isolated it from a sample of this mineral — whence the element's name. In Dan'a'yo it is called [[緑柱素]] (*logjuso*, ㄌㄛㄎㄐㄨㄙㄛ), a 素-suffix back-formation from this word following the established East Asian element-naming pattern. Within Dan'a'yo writing, beryllium may be abbreviated to 緑 alone.
