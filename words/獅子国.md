## Notes
- [獅](../characters/獅.md) "lion" + [子](../characters/子.md) "nominaliser/suffix" + [国](../characters/国.md) "country" — Lion Country; the classical Chinese calque of Sanskrit *Siṃhala*

獅子国 renders the Sanskrit name *Siṃhala* — from *siṃha* "lion" and the possessive suffix *-la* — as a transparent Sino-morpheme compound. An older Chinese orthography, 師子國, appears in the 佛國記 (Record of Buddhistic Kingdoms) of the pilgrim Faxian (法顯), who visited the island in 411 AD and spent two years there copying manuscripts unavailable in China. The choice of 師 for the lion in that earlier rendering reflects a distinct borrowing stratum; 獅 gradually displaced it as the standard character for lion.

Sri Lanka's centrality to East Asian Buddhism gave the name considerable cultural weight. The island was the primary custodian of the Pali Canon and Theravāda orthodoxy. For Chinese pilgrims and translators, 獅子国 was not merely a geographic location but a source of doctrinal authority. Faxian's account generated sustained interest, and subsequent travellers and embassy records through the Tang dynasty continued to use the name.

The Portuguese transliteration *Ceilão*, rendered into Chinese as 錫蘭 (Xīlán), displaced 獅子国 in diplomatic and commercial contexts from the sixteenth century onward. When Sri Lanka renounced the name Ceylon in 1972 and restored a form of the original *Siṃhala* root (through Pali *Sīhaḷa*), China adopted the phonetic transcription 斯里蘭卡 as the official modern rendering. 獅子国 survives in historical and literary registers.

Compare [[獅城]], the Lion City, which shares the same Sanskrit *siṃha* root but reaches Chinese via Malay *Singapura* (獅 + 城 rather than 獅子 + 国).
