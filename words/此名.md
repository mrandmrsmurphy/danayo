## Notes
- [此 (char)](../characters/此%20(char).md) "this; proximal demonstrative" + [名 (char)](../characters/名%20(char).md) "name; honorific person classifier"

此名 is the proximal honorific correlative — "this person (polite)" — using 名 as the respectful column marker rather than the neutral 人. In Dan'a'yo's correlative system, the 名 row is the polite counterpart to the 人 row across all seven columns: 此名/其名/彼名 are the deferential forms of 此人/其人/彼人; 何名 is the very polite "who?" contrasted with neutral 誰 and rude 何人. The semantic motivation for 名 as the honorific person classifier is that referring to someone by their "name" — their identity, title, or social standing — signals deference in a way that bare "person" (人) does not.

The cross-linguistic equivalents all use dedicated honorific person morphemes rather than marking politeness on the demonstrative alone. Chinese 這位 (zhè wèi, "this [位]") uses 位 (wèi), the standard honorific classifier for people in Mandarin: 這位先生 ("this gentleman"), 這位女士 ("this distinguished lady"). Japanese この方 (kono kata) uses 方 (kata), the polite nominaliser for people: この方はどなたですか ("Who is this distinguished person?"). Korean 이분 (i-bun) uses 분 (分/분), the polite person suffix: 이분이 누구세요 ("Who is this person?"). Dan'a'yo unifies these into 名 as the systematic honorific person column marker.

The 名 row is the only dimension of the Dan'a'yo correlative table that encodes pragmatic register rather than deixis or quantification. By building honorific person reference into the table itself, Dan'a'yo makes politeness a first-class grammatical feature of the correlative system — reflecting the deep importance of person-reference register in the CJKV linguistic sphere, where Japanese, Korean, Vietnamese, and formal Chinese all maintain elaborate pronoun-register distinctions. A learner who knows the 人 row can immediately derive the 名 row by substituting neutral with polite.
