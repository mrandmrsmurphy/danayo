## Notes
- [射](../characters/射.md) "shoot" + [策](../characters/策.md) "bamboo strip; policy question" — here 射 keeps its ordinary reading (shè in Mandarin, 사 in Korean), unlike its exceptional reading in [[僕射]]; the literal sense "to shoot/draw" survives intact, since the examination it names was named for the act of drawing a strip at random.

射策 was a written-examination method used to test candidates at the Han-dynasty [[太学]] and in recommendation systems such as [[孝廉]]: policy questions were written on individual bamboo strips (策), and a candidate would draw one strip at random — the "shooting" of the name — and write a response, with their score determined by the quality of the answer to whichever question chance had given them. It stood alongside other Han examination formats such as oral questioning (口試) and direct policy essays (對策, where the question was assigned rather than drawn at random), together forming an early, if irregular, ancestor of the much more systematized imperial examination (科舉) that would later structure officialdom across the Sinosphere.
