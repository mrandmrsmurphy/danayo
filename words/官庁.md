## Notes
- [官](../characters/官.md) "official; government" + [庁](../characters/庁.md) "hall; office" — stand-in for [[庁]], which cannot appear independently
- 官庁 fuses two ideas that, separately, already point at the same institution from different angles: 官 names the official, the person who serves the state, while 庁 names the hall or chamber where official business is conducted — and together they name the office or agency itself, the building and the bureaucracy in one word. The compound keeps this transparent, everyday sense everywhere it travels: Sino-Japanese かんちょう (kanchō) is the standard modern word for "government office," sitting alongside near-synonyms like 役所 (yakusho) and 当局 (tōkyoku); Sino-Korean 관청 (gwancheong) carries an identical meaning and identical transparency, doubling as the native gloss Korean dictionaries use to explain 庁 itself; and Sino-Vietnamese quan sảnh names the same kind of administrative authority. Mandarin guāntīng and Cantonese gun1 ting1 round out the set with the same plain, compositional logic — a word that, in every language it touches, simply names the place where the business of government gets done.
