## Notes
- [南](../characters/南.md) "south" + [海](../characters/海.md) "sea" — the Southern Sea; both the southernmost of the classical [[四海]] and the modern South China Sea

Of the four seas bounding [[天下]], 南海 was always the most geographically grounded. While 西海 shifted with the expanding western horizon, the Southern Sea was a real body of water the Han Chinese sphere could reach, sail, and administer. The Qin dynasty established 南海郡 (Nanhai Commandery) in 214 BC — one of the southernmost administrative units of the empire, centered on what is now Guangzhou — anchoring 南海 as both a cosmological limit and a practical fact. The Han extended this presence, and by the Tang dynasty, 南海 was a major node in the maritime Silk Road connecting China to Southeast Asia, India, and the Persian Gulf.

The classical and modern senses are therefore not in tension: the Southern Sea of the 四海 and the South China Sea of modern geography are the same body of water, named consistently across two millennia. This makes 南海 unlike [[西海]], whose referent wandered. 南海 stayed put.

The sea's cultural and religious significance is considerable. The Bodhisattva Guanyin (觀音) is traditionally associated with 南海, her island paradise Potalaka identified with 普陀山 off the Zhejiang coast — a framing that placed Buddhist compassion at the southern boundary of the world. The sea route across 南海 was the primary channel through which Buddhism, trade goods, and cultural exchange moved between the Sinosphere and mainland Southeast Asia.

In the modern era, 南海 has become the most contested body of water in East Asia. The overlapping territorial claims of China, Vietnam, the Philippines, Malaysia, Brunei, and Taiwan over the Spratly (南沙群島) and Paracel (西沙群島) island chains, framed by China's Nine-Dash Line (九段線), have made 南海 a central site of geopolitical tension in the twenty-first century. The name itself is now politically freighted: Vietnam uses *Biển Đông* (East Sea), the Philippines uses *West Philippine Sea*, while China and Dan'a'yo retain 南海.
