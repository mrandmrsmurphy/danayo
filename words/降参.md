## Notes
- [降](../characters/降.md) "descend, lower oneself" + [参](../characters/参.md) "present oneself, attend" — to surrender; to give up and present oneself to the opponent

The compound's logic is physically expressive: 降 names the act of coming down — lowering one's position, one's guard, one's claim — and 参 names the act of presenting oneself before another. To 降参 is to descend and appear before the victor, acknowledging defeat and placing oneself in their power.

降参 is primarily a Japanese compound (こうさん, kōsan); Chinese uses 投降 (tóu xiáng) or 降服 for the same concept. In Japanese it spans the literal military sense and the everyday sense of giving up on a task or admitting defeat in an argument: もう降参だ (I give up) is natural in conversation. The equivalent in formal military history is 降伏 (こうふく, kōfuku), which was used for Japan's formal surrender in 1945 (無条件降伏, unconditional surrender); 降参 has a slightly less formal register, covering both battlefield capitulation and everyday concession.
