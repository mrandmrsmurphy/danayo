## Notes
- [臘](../characters/臘.md) "Laba" + [八 (char)](../characters/八%20(char).md) "eight" + [粥 (char)](../characters/粥%20(char).md) "congee"

臘八粥 is the ritual porridge eaten on the morning of [[臘八節]]. Its defining feature is plurality: the dish must contain at least eight varieties of grain, legume, nut, or dried fruit — the number echoing both the date and the auspicious associations of 八 in Chinese culture. Common ingredients include glutinous rice, millet, red beans, peanuts, lotus seeds, red dates (jujubes), longan, walnuts, and chestnuts; wealthier households or temples might use twenty or more. Grains are soaked overnight and simmered for hours until the porridge thickens to a deep, sweet, dense mass.

The dish's history tracks the festival's own dual origin. Early Tang monastery versions were simple grain offerings tied to the Buddha's enlightenment commemoration. By the Song dynasty the court was preparing elaborate 臘八粥 distributed as imperial gifts — princes, high ministers, and abbots all received ladles from the palace kitchens. Under the Qing dynasty this tradition formalized further, with the emperor personally overseeing the distribution to the banner armies, Buddhist monasteries, and the diplomatic corps. The Imperial Kitchen at the Palace Museum still preserves records of Qing-era recipes running to over fifty ingredients.

Regional variation is wide. Northern Chinese versions lean sweet — red dates, wolfberries, raisins; Sichuan versions can be savory, finished with chili oil and pickled vegetables, the sweet porridge of the north replaced entirely by a warm, spiced breakfast. Buddhist temple versions omit the five pungent roots (五辛) and meat stock. The Shanxi variant adds a cooked persimmon patty on top.

The custom of giving 臘八粥 to neighbors — especially elderly neighbors — remains alive in many northern cities. Beginning the night before and finishing just before dawn, the preparation is itself part of the observance.

The closest East Asian parallel is Korean 팥죽 (red bean porridge), eaten on the winter solstice (冬至) rather than 臘八, with its own apotropaic function of warding off evil spirits. Japanese お粥 (o-kayu, [[粥]]) is structurally identical but culturally simpler — a sick-day food rather than a festival food. The shared root is the Zhou-dynasty 臘 sacrifice, in which grain offerings to the ancestors gradually domesticated into communal feasting.
