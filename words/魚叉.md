## Notes
- [魚 (char)](../characters/魚%20(char).md) "fish" + [叉 (char)](../characters/叉%20(char).md) "fork; prong"

A harpoon or fish spear: a long, barbed pole-weapon thrown or launched to pierce and hold large fish or marine mammals. The name is literal — a forked or pronged instrument for fish. (The standard written form in classical Chinese is **漁叉**, using 漁 "fishing" rather than 魚 "fish"; 魚叉 is a common variant.)

Harpoons are among the oldest specialized tools in the archaeological record, appearing in Paleolithic sites across Europe and Asia. Hand-thrown harpoon heads made of bone and antler, with backward-pointing barbs to prevent withdrawal, date to at least 90,000 years ago. The barb is the defining feature: a fishing spear (鑣) punctures; a harpoon holds.

The modern **harpoon cannon** was developed by Norwegian Svend Foyn in 1864, mounting an explosive-tipped iron harpoon on a deck gun. This invention transformed industrial whaling, making it possible to hunt the large rorquals (blue whales, fin whales) that were too fast to catch by traditional methods. The explosive grenade tip detonated inside the animal, killing it quickly and preventing it from sinking. Foyn's cannon is directly responsible for the dramatic reduction of many whale species in the 20th century.

Traditional hand-thrown 魚叉 remain in use for subsistence and artisanal fishing across East and Southeast Asia — for shallow-water spearfishing of reef fish, squid, and octopus. In Chinese opera and mythology, the 魚叉 is the standard weapon of aquatic warriors, river gods, and the hunters of sea monsters; Ne Zha famously wields a similar cosmic weapon in the sea.
