## Notes
- [漢](../characters/漢.md) "Han (Chinese)" + [語](../characters/語.md) "language" — neither character's own `stand_in` points here (漢's own points to `漢族`, 語's own to `言語`); 漢語 is an independent compound. `kwin: false` per the AND-rule: `characters/漢.md` is `kwin: true`, `characters/語.md` is `kwin: false` → compound `false`.
- **Content removed**: `korean` previously read `중국어파` ("the Sinitic branch [of a language family]," a linguistics classification term) — not a reading of 漢語 at all. Corrected to `한어`, the genuine Sino-Korean compound reading (matching `characters/漢.md`'s own `한` and `characters/語.md`'s own `어`), used as a more formal/literary synonym alongside the everyday 중국어.
- **A genuine and important cross-linguistic sense divergence, documented rather than forced into agreement**: in Mandarin and Cantonese, 汉语/hon3 jyu5 is a standard cover term for "the Chinese language(s)" (Sinitic languages collectively). Japanese 漢語 (kango), however, does *not* mean "the Chinese language" at all — it is a linguistics term for "Sino-Japanese vocabulary," words of Chinese origin absorbed into the Japanese lexicon, contrasted with native 和語 (wago) and other-language loanwords 外来語 (gairaigo). The Japanese word for "the Chinese language" is instead 中国語 (chūgokugo). A learner should not assume 漢語 means "Chinese language" in Japanese.
