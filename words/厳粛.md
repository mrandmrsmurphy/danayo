## Notes
- [厳 (char)](../characters/厳%20(char).md) "strict; stern; rigorous" + [粛](../characters/粛.md) "solemn; reverent; quietly attentive" — a synonym compound for the gravity of serious occasions; the silence and weight of ceremony or consequence

**厳粛** (*yán sù*) describes a quality of sober, undeniable gravity — the atmosphere of a courtroom delivering judgment, a state funeral, a moment of formal reckoning. The compound joins two words that individually shade toward sternness (厳: strict, rigorous, not to be trifled with) and reverent solemnity (粛: hushed, attentive, ritually correct), and together they name the state in which both are present: the occasion is serious, the decorum is demanded, and levity is excluded.

In **Chinese**, 嚴肅 (*yán sù*) is the everyday standard for both "solemn" and "serious." 嚴肅的態度 means a serious, earnest attitude — not necessarily funereal, but not casual. 嚴肅的場合 is a solemn occasion. The word spans the ceremonial (a memorial service) and the disciplinary (being called to account), and in official registers it appears frequently in phrases like 嚴肅處理 ("handle [this matter] with gravity/firmness") and 嚴肅紀律 ("strict discipline").

In **Japanese**, 厳粛 (*genshuku*) gravitates toward the ceremonial end: 厳粛な雰囲気 ("a solemn atmosphere"), 厳粛な式典 ("a solemn ceremony"). The phrase **厳粛な事実** ("a grave and unavoidable fact") is a fixed construction used when introducing something difficult that must be acknowledged — a sobering reality one cannot look away from. The register is formal; 厳粛 does not appear in casual speech.

In **Korean**, 엄숙 (*eomseuk*) closely parallels the Japanese usage: formal, ceremonial, associated with occasions that demand silence and composure. 엄숙한 분위기 ("a solemn atmosphere") is the most common collocation. In **Vietnamese**, **nghiêm túc** (*nghiêm* = 厳, *túc* = 粛 via Sino-Vietnamese) is one of the most common and productive words in the language for "serious, earnest, rigorous" — appearing in everyday contexts from workplace conduct to student behaviour, with a frequency and versatility considerably wider than the more narrowly ceremonial Japanese and Korean usages.
