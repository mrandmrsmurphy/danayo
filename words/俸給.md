## Notes
- [俸](../characters/俸.md) "official stipend, salary" + [給](../characters/給.md) "supply, provide" — remuneration provided by an institution to its officials or employees; stand-in for [[俸]], which cannot appear independently

**俸給** (*fèngjǐ*) is the classical and formal compound for salary — specifically the pay dispensed by a state or institution to its officials, soldiers, or employees. 俸 names the stipend or official emolument; 給 names the act of supplying or providing it. Together they describe not just the money but the institutional relationship: 俸給 is pay that flows from a hierarchy downward, not wages negotiated between equals.

The character 俸 belongs to the vocabulary of the imperial bureaucracy. **俸祿** (*fènglù*, stipend and emolument) was the standard compound for official compensation in the Han dynasty and throughout the imperial era — the 祿 component adding the notion of rank-linked benefit. Salaries in the imperial system were graded by rank and paid in grain (石, *shí*, *shi*), later in cash or a combination: a junior official might receive a stipend of a few hundred *shi* of grain equivalent per year, while senior ministers received thousands. The system of standardised rank-pay tables formed one of the administrative foundations of the bureaucratic state.

In modern Japanese, **ほうきゅう** (*hōkyū*) remains in everyday use for salary or pay, particularly in formal, institutional, or legal contexts: **俸給表** (*hōkyū hyō*, pay scale/salary table) appears in government employment regulations; **月額俸給** (*getsugaku hōkyū*, monthly salary) in employment contracts. Japanese distinguishes **俸給** (institutional pay, especially government) from **給料** (*kyūryō*, wages/salary in general) and **賃金** (*chingin*, wages, more specifically hourly or contracted pay) — with 俸給 carrying the most bureaucratic and formal register.

Korean **봉급** (*bonggeup*) is the standard everyday word for salary or monthly pay — equivalent in register to English "salary" rather than the more formal "emolument." It appears in common speech (봉급날, payday; 봉급쟁이, salaryman), workplace documents, and tax forms. The word is so thoroughly assimilated that its Sinographic components (俸 + 給) are rarely analysed in everyday usage. Vietnamese **bổng cấp** (*bổng* from 俸, *cấp* from 給) is the Sino-Vietnamese form used in formal and administrative contexts; modern Vietnamese more commonly uses **lương** (salary/wages, from native vocabulary) or **tiền lương** in everyday speech.
