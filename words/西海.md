## Notes
- [西](../characters/西.md) "west" + [海](../characters/海.md) "sea" — the Western Sea; one of the [[四海]] bounding [[天下]]

In classical Chinese cosmology, 天下 (All Under Heaven) is enclosed on four sides by the 四海 — the Eastern, Southern, Western, and Northern Seas. These are not primarily geographic: the 四海 are the conceptual boundary of the civilized world, the outer limit beyond which there is only wilderness and myth. 四海之内 (within the four seas) means simply *the world*; 四海之外 means beyond all known things. The Zhou king was the lord of all within the four seas.

Of the four, 西海 is the most geographically unstable. Early texts — the *Shan Hai Jing* (山海經), the *Book of Documents* (書經) — locate it somewhere to the west without precision, and as Chinese geographical knowledge expanded westward, the referent shifted accordingly: in Han dynasty accounts it touches the Caspian Sea or the Aral Sea; in Tang dynasty sources it can reach the Persian Gulf. What remained constant was not the location but the function — the western limit of the known world, the place from which the sun does not return. The mythological Queen Mother of the West (西王母) dwells near 西海 in the *Shan Hai Jing*, reinforcing its association with the far, numinous west.

Modern place names that reuse 西海 — the Korean name for the Yellow Sea, a county in Qinghai, a district in Beijing — are unrelated to this classical sense and are not the referent here. Compare [[東海]], [[南海]], [[北海]] for the other members of the 四海.
