## Notes
- [公 (char)](../characters/公%20(char).md) "honorific suffix; sir"

公 is the politeness suffix in Dan'a'yo — "sir; honorific" — suffixing to a personal name or title to add a respectful or deferential nuance: 明公 (Mr. Ming; Sir Ming), 先生公 (esteemed teacher; professor, sir). Among the Dan'a'yo topic-retaining particles (関詞), 公 functions as the honorific suffix that elevates the social register of an address — paralleling Japanese -さん (san) and Korean -님 (nim). The character 公 is a 会意 compound of 八 ("to deviate; opposite") and 厶 (private; individual), giving "the opposite of private; public," and by extension "the lord; the official; sir." In classical Chinese, 公 was a title of high nobility (duke; the highest grade of 五等爵位) before generalizing to a respectful address for any elder or superior. 公 also serves as the Dan'a'yo stand-in for the character [[公]].

The comparison chart: 公 parallels Mandarin 先生/老 (xiānsheng/lǎo, sir/elder prefix), Cantonese 先生 (sin1 saang1, Mr.; sir), Japanese さん (san, general honorific suffix), Korean 님 (nim, honorific suffix), Vietnamese (lexical honorifics rather than a grammatical suffix). Mandarin: honorifics are largely lexical — 先生 (Mr.; teacher), 女士 (Ms.; madam), 老师 (teacher — respectful address), 大人 (sir; your lordship — historical/literary). Cantonese 先生: 王先生 (Mr. Wang), 陳先生 (Mr. Chan). Japanese さん: 田中さん (Mr./Ms. Tanaka) — the most neutral broadly-applicable honorific; 様 (sama) for elevated formality, くん (kun) and ちゃん (chan) for informal registers. Korean 님 (nim): 선생님 (teacher — lit. "teacher + honorific"), 고객님 (valued customer); 씨 (ssi) for a neutral polite name suffix. Vietnamese: honorifics are expressed through the pronoun system — different pronouns (ông, bà, anh, chị) for different social positions — rather than a single suffix.

kwin is true: Dan'a'yo 공 exactly matches Korean 공. 公 reads 공 (gong/ㄍㄛㄫ) in Dan'a'yo — MC 見母 (k) + 東一韻 (uŋ) gives a velar stop onset with the -ong coda. Both Dan'a'yo and Sino-Korean map MC 見母 to a plain velar initial (ㄍ/ㄱ) and MC 東一韻 (uŋ) to plain -ong, producing identical 공 forms. The MC 東一韻 is among the most consistently preserved finals in both systems — simple -ong coda with no medial complications — making 公 a phonologically stable kwin: true case.
