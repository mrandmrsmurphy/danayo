## Notes
- [百 (char)](../characters/百%20(char).md) "hundred" + [家](../characters/家.md) "house, school of thought"

百家 uses 家 in its extended sense of "school of thought" or "tradition of learning" (as in 儒家, 道家, 法家), with 百 functioning hyperbolically as "a hundred, many, all manner of." The compound is most famous as the first half of 諸子百家 (the Hundred Schools of Thought), the collective name for the diverse philosophical traditions that flourished in China during the Spring and Autumn and Warring States periods (c. 770–221 BC). This era of intellectual pluralism — encompassing Confucianism, Daoism, Legalism, Mohism, and dozens of other traditions — became the foundational reference point for Chinese intellectual culture, and 百家爭鳴 ("a hundred schools contend") became the standard idiom for open intellectual debate.

In Japanese (ひゃっか, hyakka), 百家 appears chiefly in historical and philosophical writing. The Dan'a'yo chengyu 百家共承 ("the hundred schools hold in common") extends this classical reference into an affirmation of shared cross-cultural wisdom.
