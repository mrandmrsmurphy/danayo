## Notes
- [一 (char)](../characters/一%20(char).md) "one" + [処 (char)](../characters/処%20(char).md) "place"

一処 means "one place" and by extension "together; in the same location." The compound is classical in origin — 聚在一處 "gathered in one place" is a recurring phrase in Chinese prose and fiction — and functions as an adjectival or adverbial predicate: 放在一処 "put in the same place," 住在一処 "living together."

In Japanese, 一処 (ひとところ, hitotokoro) is read entirely with native vocabulary: 一 as hito- (one) and 処 as tokoro (place), the same kun reading shared with 所. The orthographic overlap between 処 and 所 for the tokoro reading is common in classical and premodern texts; modern Japanese tends toward 一所 or 一ヶ所 for formal counting purposes but retains ひとところ as an adverbial expression for "a single spot" or "one passage (in a text)."

In Korean, the concept has no single established Sino-Korean compound — the word file records the native equivalents 한데 and 한곳 ("together; one place"), which is why kwin is false. The Sino-Korean character sequence 일처 (ilcheo) would be phonologically transparent but is not the standard term.
