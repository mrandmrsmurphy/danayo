## Notes
- [可 (char)](../characters/可%20(char).md) "able to, may" + [不 (char)](../characters/不%20(char).md) "not" — able to refrain from an action; permitted not to do something.

This completes the modal square alongside [[不可]] ("unable to," i.e. forbidden) and [[不可不]] ("unable to not," i.e. must): 可 marks ability/permission, 不可 its negation, and 可不 negates the verb instead of the modal, giving "free not to." The two prefixes stack rather than fuse, so 可不 names the logical position in the paradigm rather than a fixed idiom borrowed from any source language.

Two unrelated homographs are worth flagging so they aren't confused with this entry: colloquial Mandarin 可不 (kěbù) is a fixed interjection meaning "indeed!, isn't that so!" (short for 可不是), with no compositional "able-to-not" sense at all; and Korean 가불 (假拂) is the everyday word for a salary advance, an entirely unrelated borrowing built on different characters that merely happens to share the same hangul shape as this word's bound-form reading. Neither collision reflects anything about this word's own derivation — both are coincidences of independently regular spelling.
