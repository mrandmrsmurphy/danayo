## Notes
- [火 (char)](../characters/火%20(char).md) "fire" + [鼠](../characters/鼠.md) "rat; mouse"

A mythological creature: a rat or mouse believed to inhabit regions of perpetual fire, possessed of fur that is incombustible and can be woven into fireproof cloth. The fire rat appears in both Chinese and Japanese classical tradition, where its extraordinary pelt is among the most desirable and impossible-to-obtain materials in the known world.

In **Chinese tradition**, the fire rat (火鼠) appears in texts as early as the *Classic of Mountains and Seas* (山海経, Shānhǎi Jīng) and in Tang dynasty encyclopedias, described as a creature living in southern seas near volcanoes, its white fur impervious to flame. Cloth woven from fire-rat fur — **火鼠裘** (huǒshǔ qiú, "fire-rat fur garment") — was said to be cleaned by placing it in fire rather than washing it in water; the flames would burn away dirt while leaving the cloth unharmed.

In **Japanese tradition**, the fire rat achieves its greatest literary prominence in *The Tale of the Bamboo Cutter* (竹取物語, *Taketori Monogatari*, c. 10th century CE) — the oldest surviving Japanese prose narrative. Kaguya-hime, the lunar princess found inside a bamboo stalk, is sought by five suitors, each of whom she sends on an impossible quest. One suitor, the Minister of the Right Abe no Miushi, is tasked with obtaining **火鼠の裘** (hinezumi no kawa ginu, "the fur robe of the fire rat of China"). He purchases an expensive foreign garment, claiming it to be the genuine article — but when tested in fire, it burns. The fraud is exposed; he fails.

The *Taketori Monogatari* is foundational to Japanese literary culture. It is referenced in *The Tale of Genji* as "the ancestor of all tales" (物語の出で来はじめの祖), and its five impossible objects — including the fire-rat robe — established a template for magical quests in Japanese fiction that persists in popular culture today.

The later association of 火鼠 with **asbestos** reflects the real-world discovery of a mineral fiber that does not burn. When medieval and early modern writers encountered asbestos cloth (traded along Silk Road routes from Central Asia), the material's properties seemed to confirm the mythological tradition. Japanese texts sometimes use 火鼠裘 as a synonym for asbestos cloth. The Sino-Japanese reading かそ (kaso) is used in more formal or historical contexts; ひねずみ (hinezumi) is the native Japanese reading.
