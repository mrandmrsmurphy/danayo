## Notes
- [健](../characters/健.md) "sturdy, robust" + [康](../characters/康.md) "at ease, peaceful" — a synonym compound generalizing over both components' more precise senses; no longer a `stand_in` for either, since [[健全]] (sturdy, sound) and [[康寧]] (at ease, well-being) now each carry one of the two distinct nuances 健康 used to blur together.

健康 is "healthy, sound, fit" in the broad, general-purpose sense — the everyday word for health (health insurance, a healthy diet, a doctor's checkup), as distinct from its own two more specialized offshoots [[健全]] (soundness in a structural/institutional sense — a healthy economy, a sound system) and [[康寧]] (peaceful well-being, closer to "tranquility" than physical fitness).

Mandarin 健康 (jiànkāng, "health; healthy" — 健康保険 not applicable here, but 健康检查 jiànkāng jiǎnchá "health checkup" is everyday vocabulary). Cantonese 健康 (gin6hong1). Japanese 健康 (けんこう, kenkō) is equally everyday and productive — 健康保険 (kenkō hoken, "health insurance"), 健康診断 (kenkō shindan, "medical checkup"). Korean 건강 (geongang) is just as central to daily life — 건강하다 ("to be healthy") is one of the most common adjectival verbs in the language, and 건강검진 (health checkup) parallels the Japanese term closely. Vietnamese kiện khang exists as the formal Sino-Vietnamese calque, but the far more common everyday word is sức khỏe ("health," native/mixed formation) — kiện khang survives mainly in more literary or set phrasing (an sinh kiện khang, "peace and good health," a New Year's greeting formula) rather than ordinary conversation.

kwin is false: 健 alone matches its own Sino-Korean reading exactly (건=건), but 康 does not (Dan'a'yo 캉 vs Korean 강 — a vowel mismatch), so the compound's `kwin` — true only when every constituent character matches — comes out false.
