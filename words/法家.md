## Notes
- [法 (char)](../characters/法%20(char).md) "law" + [家](../characters/家.md) "school of thought" — "the school of law," one of the major traditions among the [[諸子百家]] (Hundred Schools of Thought).

Legalism held that human nature is fundamentally self-interested and that social order therefore depends not on moral cultivation, as Confucians argued, but on clear, publicly known laws backed by strict and impartially applied rewards and punishments. Its most influential early proponent, Shang Yang (商鞅), used these principles to reorganize the state of Qin in the 4th century BC — abolishing hereditary privilege in favor of merit and military service, standardizing administration, and organizing the population into mutually accountable groups — reforms credited with making Qin powerful enough to eventually conquer and unify China in 221 BC. Han Feizi (韓非子), the school's most systematic theorist, synthesized earlier Legalist and Daoist ideas into a comprehensive theory of statecraft built on 法 (law), 術 (technique of rule), and 勢 (positional power), and his writings directly influenced Li Si (李斯), chief minister under the First Emperor.

Legalism's association with the short-lived and harshly authoritarian Qin dynasty — including the campaign against rival schools traditionally linked to the burning of books and burying of scholars — left it permanently associated with tyranny in later Chinese historiography, and the Han dynasty's adoption of Confucianism as state orthodoxy pushed Legalism out of explicit philosophical favor. Despite this, many of its administrative innovations — codified law, bureaucratic accountability, and centralized authority — were quietly absorbed into the Confucian-fronted imperial state that followed, giving Legalism a deeper and longer institutional afterlife than its reputation suggests.
