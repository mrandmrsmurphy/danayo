## Notes
- [陽 (char)](../characters/陽%20(char).md) "sunshine; yang; positive" + [子](../characters/子.md) "child; small particle; -er suffix"

陽子 is "proton" — 陽 (sunshine; yang; the positive/bright principle) + 子 (the standard suffix for subatomic particles in CJKV scientific vocabulary). In physics, the proton is the positively charged particle in the atomic nucleus; the naming is transparent: 陽 marks the positive charge, and 子 acts as the particle suffix (cf. [[電子]] electron, [[中性子]] neutron, [[分子]] molecule, [[原子]] atom). The alias 質子 is the Mandarin-preferred term for proton (zhìzǐ, "substance-particle"), reflecting a different translation tradition; Dan'a'yo follows the Japanese 陽子 (yōshi). Dan'a'yo 陽子 is a homophone of [[様子]] (manner; appearances) and [[養子]] (adopted child) — all three read 'yangjǝ (⼘ㄫㄐㄜ).

Mandarin uses 質子 (zhìzǐ) as the standard scientific term for proton rather than 陽子 (yángzǐ), which is not widely used in Mainland scientific writing. Cantonese 陽子 (joeng4 zi2). Japanese 陽子 (ようし, yōshi) is the standard Japanese term for proton — the source of the Dan'a'yo form; 陽子 is also a common female given name (ようこ, Yōko), though the reading differs (-し vs -こ). Korean 양자 (陽子, yangja) is used in Korean physics texts. Vietnamese dương tử (Sino-Vietnamese: 陽=dương, 子=tử); Vietnamese physics also uses the loanword prôton for the particle.

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 양즈 differs from Korean 양자 in the second syllable. 子 reads 즈 (jǝ/ㄐㄜ) in Dan'a'yo but 자 (ja) in Sino-Korean — MC 精母 (t͡s) + 之韻 (ɨ) gives Dan'a'yo the high central schwa (-ǝ), while Sino-Korean shifts to the low central vowel (-a). This 즈/자 split from MC 之韻 is consistent across all three homophones ([[陽子]], [[様子]], [[養子]]): the first syllable 양 is identical in both Dan'a'yo and Sino-Korean, as MC 以母 (j) + 陽韻 (ɨɐŋ) converges to yang in both systems.
