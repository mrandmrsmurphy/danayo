## Notes
- [夭](../characters/夭.md) "gentle, tender; young" + [折](../characters/折.md) "fold, break, cut short"

This word is the stand-in for [夭](../characters/夭.md), whose meanings of youth and tenderness would be ambiguous in isolation; 夭折 grounds it in its most defining usage.

夭折 describes a death or ending that comes too soon — the cutting short of a life, a project, or a potential that had not yet reached its fullness. The character 夭 originally depicted a gracefully bending young plant or person (its graphemic root 象形 sense is "gentle, tender"), but its usage gravitated toward the fragility implied by that youth: the young thing that bends is also the young thing that breaks. 折 (to break, to snap, to cut short) provides the ending. Together the compound evokes not just death but the particular tragedy of premature death — a talent extinguished, a life incomplete.

The compound is widely used across the CJKV languages in both literal and figurative senses. Literally, 夭折 refers to dying in childhood or young adulthood. Figuratively, it extends to any endeavor that ends before reaching its potential: a project 夭折了 (it fell through, it was cut short), a reform movement that 夭折 before implementation. In Japanese (ようせつ, yōsetsu), the word is documented from the Nara period (cited from a 756 AD text in Wiktionary) and appears in historical and literary writing. In Korean (요절), it is used in both biographical writing and casual speech to describe a young person's death, particularly that of artists and musicians.
