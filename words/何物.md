## Notes
- [何 (char)](../characters/何%20(char).md) "what; which; interrogative" + [物 (char)](../characters/物%20(char).md) "thing; object; material result"

何物 is the interrogative correlative in the object column, asking for the identification of a thing or object: "what is it? what kind of thing is this?" Like 何事, it is not a pure Dan'a'yo neologism — 何物 exists independently in classical Chinese and Japanese. The compound 何物 (hé wù) asks "what thing?" in classical Chinese texts and survives in Mandarin formal registers; in Japanese, 何物 (nanimono) carries a specific nuance of mystery or unidentified presence: 何物かが動いた ("something — unknown — moved"), 彼は何物か ("who/what is he really?").

Japanese 何物 (nanimono) occupies a slightly different semantic space from 何者 (nani-mono, written differently): 何者 typically refers to an unidentified person, while 何物 can refer to either a person or thing of unknown nature. The phrase 何物にも代えがたい ("irreplaceable, not exchangeable for anything") treats 何物 as a universal negative object ("no thing"). Mandarin 何物 (héwù) exists in literary registers meaning "what object?" but is rare colloquially; Cantonese 何物 (ho4 mat6) is similarly formal.

Dan'a'yo 何物 (*hamud*) is a homophone of [[荷物]] — the sinographic compound for luggage or cargo (荷 "burden, load" + 物 "thing"). Both words read ㄏㄚㄇㄨㄊ in Dan'a'yo phonology. This is a coincidence of character readings rather than a deliberate etymological connection, and context disambiguates the two — the interrogative correlative 何物 ("what thing?") vs the noun 荷物 ("load; baggage"). The homophone pair has no equivalent in Japanese, where 荷物 (nimotsu) and 何物 (nanimono) are phonologically distinct.
