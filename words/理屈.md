## Notes
- [理](../characters/理.md) "reason; principle; logic" + [屈 (char)](../characters/屈%20(char).md) "bend; yield; give way"

The compound encodes a particular image: reason (理) that bends or yields (屈) — not the straight, undefeated argument, but the one that has run out of ground to stand on.

The older and primary sense is the Chinese one. 理屈 appears in Liu Yiqing's *世說新語* (ca. 430 AD), making it a classical Chinese coinage. There it carries the meaning it still holds in modern Chinese: 理屈 is argument that has collapsed, reason that has yielded. The canonical set phrase 理屈詞窮 (*lǐqū cíqióng*, "reason exhausted, words depleted") describes someone left with nothing to say because their position has given way. To be 理屈 is to be in the wrong and to know it — or at least to have been shown so.

Japanese borrowed the compound through classical Chinese study and preserved this negative undertone in colloquial usage: 理屈をこねる ("to spin out specious reasoning") and 理屈っぽい ("overly argumentative; pedantically logical") both echo the original sense of reason strained past its limit. The more neutral Japanese meaning — りくつ as simply "reasoning; logic; the principle behind something" — is a later development, useful because りくつ filled a gap in everyday Japanese philosophical vocabulary. Dan'a'yo treats this neutral sense as secondary.
