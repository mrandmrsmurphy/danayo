## Notes
- [養](../characters/養.md) "to foster; to raise; to nourish" + [子](../characters/子.md) "child; son"

養子 is "adopted child; foster child" — 養 (to foster; to raise; to nourish) + 子 (child; son). The compound literally means "a raised child" — a child brought up by someone other than the birth parents. In East Asian legal and social traditions, adoption (特に養子縁組) historically served not only personal but institutional purposes: ensuring the continuation of a family line, the performance of ancestral rites, and the orderly transfer of inheritance when no biological heir existed. Adoption of adults was legal and common in Japan, a practice that persists in modern corporate Japan for passing business leadership within a family. Dan'a'yo 養子 is a homophone of [[陽子]] (proton) and [[様子]] (manner; appearances) — all three read 'yangjǝ (⼘ㄫㄐㄜ).

Mandarin 養子 (yǎngzǐ) is the standard written term for "adopted son" (養女, yǎngnǚ, for "adopted daughter"). Cantonese 養子 (joeng5 zi2). Japanese 養子 (ようし, yōshi) is the standard legal and everyday term: 養子縁組 (yōshi engumi, adoption proceedings), 養子に出す (yōshi ni dasu, to give a child up for adoption), 養子に入る (yōshi ni hairu, to be adopted into a family). The Japanese institution of 成人養子縁組 (adult adoption) allowed merchant and aristocratic families to take on a capable heir — a son-in-law or apprentice — when the biological heir was unsuitable; this tradition survives in modern Japanese family businesses. Korean 양자 (養子, yangja): 양자로 들이다 (to take someone in as an adopted child), 양자 결연 (adoption arrangement). Vietnamese dưỡng tử (Sino-Vietnamese: 養=dưỡng, 子=tử); everyday Vietnamese uses con nuôi (foster/adopted child).

kwin is false: Dan'a'yo 양즈 differs from Korean 양자 in the second syllable. 子 reads 즈 (jǝ/ㄐㄜ) in Dan'a'yo but 자 (ja) in Sino-Korean — the same MC 之韻 (ɨ) → Dan'a'yo schwa vs Sino-Korean -a split as in [[陽子]] and [[様子]]. The first syllable is identical: 養 reads 양 in both Dan'a'yo and Sino-Korean, MC 以母 (j) + 陽韻 (ɨɐŋ) converging to yang in both systems. All three homophones share exactly this pattern — 양 identical in both, 즈 vs 자 the consistent Dan'a'yo/Korean divergence across the set.
