[[Biblical Chengyu]]
### Pronunciation
- **Mandarin**: zūn jìng fù mǔ
- **Cantonese**: zeon1 ging3 fu6 mou5
- **Japanese (hiragana)**: そんけいふぼ
- **Korean (Hangul)**: 존경부모 (jon-gyeong-bu-mo)
- **Vietnamese (Sino-Vietnamese)**: tôn kính phụ mẫu

Note: The compound consists of two established two-character words: [[尊敬]] “to honor, respect” and [[父母]] “father and mother.”

### Literal Meaning
- **尊敬** to honor, revere, treat with esteem
- **父母** father and mother

Literal: “Honor and respect father and mother.”

### Figurative Meaning
A concise formulation of the filial commandment. It enjoins reverence, obedience, and grateful acknowledgment toward one’s parents. By extension, it may signify respect for generational authority and covenant continuity.

### Scriptural Source
This idiom condenses the Fifth Commandment:
- **Exodus 20:12**
- **Deuteronomy 5:16**

English:
> “Honor thy father and thy mother: that thy days may be long upon the land…”

Hebrew: _כַּבֵּד אֶת־אָבִיךָ וְאֶת־אִמֶּךָ_  
“Give weight to your father and your mother.”

Traditional Chinese translations typically render:
> “當孝敬父母。”

The Dan'a'yo form selects 尊敬 rather than 孝敬 to foreground reverent esteem rather than specifically Confucian filial terminology, while remaining fully consonant with the biblical imperative.

### Example Usage
> 社會之穩固，常繫於尊敬父母之風。  
> The stability of society is often bound to the practice of honoring father and mother.

### Cultural Notes
In the Decalogue, this commandment bridges duties toward God and duties toward neighbor. It is the first commandment accompanied by a promise.

The Hebrew verb “to honor” literally means “to give weight.” It implies seriousness, material support, and public respect, not merely inward feeling.

In East Asian contexts, filial piety is already culturally central. The biblical command aligns with but is not identical to Confucian structures. It is grounded in covenant relationship and divine authority rather than social harmony alone.

Within Dan'a'yo, 尊敬父母 functions as both moral law and cultural anchor. It underscores generational continuity and gratitude, while implying that parental authority is itself accountable to divine law.

### Tone and Sentiment
Reverent, stabilizing, covenantal. It calls for enduring gratitude and structured respect within family life.
