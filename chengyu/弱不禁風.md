## Literal Meaning
- [弱 (char)](../characters/弱%20(char).md) — weak; frail
- [不 (char)](../characters/不%20(char).md) — not; unable to
- [禁](../characters/禁.md) — withstand; endure; bear *(note: 禁 here is read jīn, "to endure," not jìn, "to prohibit")*
- [風 (char)](../characters/風%20(char).md) — wind

Literally: "So weak as to be unable to withstand even the wind."

## Extended Meaning

Used to describe a person — or occasionally a thing — of extreme delicacy and fragility. The image is of a body so thin and insubstantial that the ordinary force of a breeze would be enough to topple or injure it. In modern Chinese, the idiom is applied:

- to people who are visibly underweight, chronically ill, or constitutionally delicate
- to describe an aesthetic ideal of feminine fragility in classical literary and artistic contexts
- sarcastically, to someone who is overly precious or cannot cope with ordinary hardship
- occasionally to institutions, arguments, or plans that lack structural resilience

The phrase carries a dual valence: in classical and traditional literary usage, 弱不禁風 could be a compliment — evoking the ethereal, willowy beauty of a scholarly woman or a spiritual being. In modern everyday Chinese, it more often implies weakness or poor health that warrants concern, though it retains its literary beauty in poetic contexts.

## Source and Origin

The phrase is first securely attested in the poetry of **陸遊** (Lù Yóu, 1125–1210), the prolific Southern Song dynasty poet. In a poem from 1196, Lu You uses the image in a natural description — delicate red dragonflies hovering over lotus blossoms after rain, so fragile they cannot bear the wind:

> 荷花開後西湖好，載酒來時，不用旌旗，前後紅幢綠蓋隨。
> *...蜻蜓弱不禁風...*
> ("...dragonflies too frail to withstand the wind...")

Here the phrase is still literal, applied to a natural creature. The semantic leap to describing *people* — and specifically the delicacy of beautiful women and frail scholars — developed in subsequent Yuan and Ming dynasty literature.

The most culturally resonant use is in **《紅樓夢》** (*Dream of the Red Chamber*, Cao Xueqin, c. 1750), one of the four great classical novels of Chinese literature. The character **林黛玉** (Lín Dàiyù) is the archetype of 弱不禁風: perpetually ill with a chronic cough, heartbreakingly beautiful, emotionally hypersensitive, and gifted with poetic genius. Though the exact four-character phrase does not appear on every page, Daiyu's constitution is repeatedly described in equivalent terms — she weeps at falling petals, her body is "like a willow in the wind," and she is explicitly said to lack the physical constitution to withstand ordinary weather. Daiyu became so identified with this quality that 弱不禁風 in modern Chinese almost automatically evokes her image.

## Standard Form

**弱不禁風** is the standard form across Traditional Chinese, Japanese, and Korean contexts.

- 弱不禁风 — Simplified Chinese (風 → 风); dominant in Mainland China

The characters 弱, 不, and 禁 are stable across all scripts. Only 風/风 differs in simplified Chinese.

## Pronunciations
- **Mandarin (Pinyin):** ruò bù jīn fēng (4–4–1–1)
- **Cantonese (Jyutping):** joek6 bat1 kam1 fung1
- **Japanese (on-yomi, hiragana):** じゃくふきんふう (jakufukinfū)
- **Korean (Sino-Korean):** 약부금풍 (yak-bu-geum-pung)
- **Vietnamese (Hán–Việt):** nhược bất cấm phong

Note: Mandarin 禁 in this compound is read **jīn** (1st tone, "to endure/withstand"), not jìn (4th tone, "to forbid"). This tonal distinction — though the character is the same — is critical to the meaning. Cantonese similarly uses **kam1** (1st tone = withstand) rather than gam3 (3rd tone = prohibit).

## Cultural Notes

**In Chinese literature and aesthetics**, 弱不禁風 has been central to a particular ideal of feminine beauty for centuries — the fragile, pale, spiritually intense woman whose physical delicacy signals inner refinement. This ideal is inseparable from 林黛玉 and from the broader *《紅樓夢》* aesthetic, which prizes emotional sensitivity and literary talent above physical robustness. The phrase appears constantly in Chinese prose and poetry when describing beautiful but sickly women, and has been absorbed into film, television, and advertising in this sense.

**In modern Chinese**, the phrase has expanded beyond gender: 弱不禁風 describes anyone — male or female — who appears dangerously underweight, chronically unwell, or physically unable to cope with ordinary demands. It can be used with sympathy (for a sick child) or mild exasperation (for someone who collapses at slight hardship).

**In Japanese**, the compound じゃくふきんふう is recognizable to educated readers but relatively rare in everyday speech — the underlying Chinese literary context does not travel as naturally as more pan-East-Asian idioms. A Japanese speaker might instead reach for 線が細い (sen ga hosoi, "slender lines") or 体が弱い (karada ga yowai, "physically weak").

**In Korean**, 약부금풍 is a literary borrowing, found in formal writing and classical education but not common in everyday speech.

## Example Sentences

1. **Classical (Lu You, 1196):**
   蜻蜓弱不禁風，荷花雨後香。
   *"The dragonflies, too frail to withstand the wind; the lotus fragrant after rain."*

2. **Literary (《紅樓夢》context):**
   黛玉生得弱不禁風，一陣秋風便令她咳嗽不止。
   *"Daiyu was born with a constitution too frail to withstand the wind; a single autumn gust set her coughing without stop."*

3. **Modern Chinese:**
   她從小身體就弱，如今更是弱不禁風，稍微運動一下就喘。
   *"She was always sickly as a child; now she's completely frail — even light exercise leaves her gasping."*

4. **English:**
   She drifted through the room like someone 弱不禁風 — translucent, barely there, as if a window left open might carry her away.

## Sentiment

Classically **positive** when applied to a woman in literary contexts — evoking ethereal beauty, spiritual refinement, and poetic sensitivity. The fragility is aesthetic, even desirable.

In modern usage, **neutral to negative** — describing genuinely poor health or, with mild irony, excessive delicacy. The literary romance has largely drained from the phrase in everyday speech, leaving behind a clinical observation.

Always **literary in register** — this is not colloquial vocabulary. Its appearance in prose signals that the writer is reaching for a classical reference, whether sincerely or with a wink.
