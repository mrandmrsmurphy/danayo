## Meaning
**Literal meaning:** “Golden walls, moat of boiling water.”  
**Extended sense:** An impregnable defense; a position so strongly fortified that it cannot be attacked successfully.

## Source
From the **《漢書》**. The phrase appears in discussions of military strategy and the advantages of strong defensive positions within Han historiography.

## Classical background:
The two images reinforce each other. 城 (city wall) made of gold suggests perfect strength and incorruptibility. 湯池 describes a moat filled with boiling water, an impossible barrier to crossing. Together they form an exaggerated but vivid description of total defensive security.

Unlike many chengyu drawn from moral commentary, 金城湯池 belongs to the vocabulary of military and political analysis. It emphasizes strategic advantage created by geography, fortification, or preparation rather than courage or numbers.

The phrase also reflects a broader pattern in classical Chinese rhetoric: pairing two images of the same quality to intensify the concept rather than diversify it. Both halves express invulnerability.

## Modern usage
Across the region, 金城湯池 is used metaphorically for any strongly protected position, whether physical, political, or institutional. It appears in historical writing, political commentary, and sometimes business or organizational language to describe systems that are extremely difficult to penetrate or overturn.

## Usage note
金城湯池 does not merely praise strength. It describes a condition where attack itself becomes irrational because the defensive advantage is overwhelming.
