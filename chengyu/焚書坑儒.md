## Literal Meaning
- [焚](../characters/焚.md) — to burn; to set fire to
- [書](../characters/書.md) — books; writings; texts
- [坑 (char)](../characters/坑%20(char).md) — pit; trench; to bury alive
- [儒](../characters/儒.md) — Confucian scholar; the educated class

Literally: "Burn the books; bury the scholars."

The four characters compress two distinct historical events separated by a year into a single indictment. 焚書 (fén shū, "burn the books") refers to the systematic destruction of philosophical, historical, and literary texts ordered in 213 BC. 坑儒 (kēng rú, "pit the scholars") refers to the execution by live burial of several hundred scholars in 212 BC. The parallelism of the four characters — two characters of destruction, two characters of what was destroyed — makes the compound feel like a complete program: first destroy the knowledge, then destroy those who carried it.

## Extended Meaning

Used to describe any systematic suppression of intellectual life, persecution of scholars or thinkers, or destruction of cultural and written heritage by political authority. The idiom names a category of historical crime: the use of state power to eliminate ideas by eliminating both their material form (books) and the people who know them.

The idiom applies to:
- State-directed censorship and book destruction at any scale
- Persecution, imprisonment, or execution of intellectuals for their ideas
- Deliberate destruction of a culture's written heritage during conquest or revolution
- Any totalitarian campaign to reshape thought by eliminating its sources

焚書坑儒 is not a neutral historical descriptor — it is an accusation. To invoke it against a policy or regime is to place that policy in the company of the Qin dynasty's most condemned act. The phrase has become the shorthand, across all CJKV cultures, for the outer limit of ideological repression.

## Source and Origin

The events are documented in **《史記·秦始皇本紀》** (*Records of the Grand Historian*, Annals of Qin Shi Huang), compiled by **司馬遷 (Sima Qian)** c. 91 BC. Sima Qian, writing under the Han dynasty that replaced Qin, recorded these events as part of his verdict on Qin's political legacy.

**The book burning, 213 BC**: Chancellor **李斯 (Li Si)** memorialized the emperor with the argument that allowing scholars to compare antiquity with the present — finding the present wanting — was a source of political instability. He proposed the elimination of all books not pertaining to Qin's own history, agriculture, medicine, or divination. Emperor Qin Shi Huang (秦始皇) approved.

**《史記·秦始皇本紀》:**
> 丞相李斯曰：「…臣請史官非秦記皆燒之；非博士官所職，天下敢有藏詩、書、百家語者，悉詣守、尉雜燒之…」始皇可其議。
> *"Chancellor Li Si said: '…I request that all historical records not pertaining to Qin be burned by the archivists; all private possession of the Book of Odes, the Book of Documents, and the writings of the Hundred Schools should be handed over to administrators and burned together…' The First Emperor approved his proposal."*

**The burial of scholars, 212 BC**: When court alchemists failed to produce the promised elixir of immortality and then fled, the emperor's fury was directed at his advisors. More than 460 scholars (儒生) were accused of spreading rumor and confusion, and were executed by burial alive (坑殺) at Xianyang (咸陽).

> 乃自除犯禁者四百六十餘人，皆坑之咸陽，使天下知之，以懲後。
> *"He then personally selected those who had violated prohibitions — more than four hundred and sixty persons — and buried them all alive at Xianyang, making it known throughout the realm as a warning to those who came after."*

The four-character compound **焚書坑儒** linking the two events is a Han-dynasty crystallization. Sima Qian does not use this exact phrase — he records the two events separately — but Han Confucians, who had most to gain from condemning Qin's persecution of their tradition, consolidated them into a single indictment. By the Eastern Han the compound was established as the canonical verdict on Qin cultural policy, framing the two events as two expressions of the same totalitarian logic: destroy the books, then destroy the men who remember what the books contained.

**A note on historical accuracy**: Later scholarship (and some contemporary accounts) suggests the 坑儒 victims included alchemists and magicians as much as Confucian scholars proper. Sima Qian himself uses the term 諸生 (students/scholars generally) alongside 儒生. The Han framing as a specifically anti-Confucian persecution shaped the received tradition; the historical reality was probably messier. This does not diminish the phrase's moral force — the book burning was real, the mass execution was real — but it illustrates how 焚書坑儒 is as much a historical verdict as a historical description.

## Standard Form

The Dan'a'yo form **焚書坑儒** uses traditional characters. The simplified Chinese form:

- **焚書坑儒** — traditional Chinese, Japanese (= Dan'a'yo form)
- **焚书坑儒** — simplified Chinese (书 for 書)

焚, 坑, 儒 are stable across all scripts.

## Cultural Notes

**In Chinese**, 焚書坑儒 is one of the most loaded historical phrases in the language — a permanent term of condemnation for the Qin dynasty and, by extension, for any political authority that suppresses intellectual life. It appears in historical writing, political commentary, and cultural criticism at all levels of formality. The phrase was explicitly invoked during and after the Cultural Revolution (文化大革命, 1966–1976), when the Red Guards burned books, destroyed temples and libraries, and persecuted, humiliated, and killed scholars and intellectuals. The parallel was obvious: books burned, educated people buried — the structure of the idiom fit the events with devastating precision. Officially the comparison was taboo, but its weight in Chinese cultural memory was inescapable.

The phrase also appears in discussions of other historical book-burnings and intellectual persecutions across world history, serving as a cross-cultural reference point: Heinrich Heine's 1820 observation that those who burn books will eventually burn people (*wo man Bücher verbrennt, verbrennt man auch am Ende Menschen*) articulates the same logic as 焚書坑儒 from the opposite direction — and the two quotations are often cited together in Chinese intellectual writing.

**In Japanese**, ふんしょこうじゅ (funsyokōju) is a formal historical and political term, appearing in discussions of Chinese history, censorship, and totalitarianism. Japanese readers are familiar with the Qin events through classical Chinese historical education, and the phrase carries the same weight of historical condemnation as in Chinese. It appears in journalism and academic discourse when discussing state suppression of intellectual life.

**In Korean**, 분서갱유 (bun-seo-gaeng-yu) is well-established Sino-Korean historical vocabulary, familiar from classical education and appearing in discussions of historical tyranny, censorship, and intellectual persecution. Korea's own history of ideological suppression — both under colonial rule and during authoritarian periods of the 20th century — gives the phrase additional resonance.

## Example Sentences

1. **Classical (《史記·秦始皇本紀》, c. 91 BC):**
   乃自除犯禁者四百六十餘人，皆坑之咸陽，使天下知之，以懲後。
   *"He then personally selected those who had violated prohibitions — more than four hundred and sixty persons — and buried them all alive at Xianyang, making it known throughout the realm as a warning to those who came after."*

2. **Modern Chinese (historical commentary):**
   秦始皇焚書坑儒，試圖以恐怖手段統一思想，卻加速了秦朝的滅亡。
   *"Qin Shi Huang's burning of books and burial of scholars attempted to unify thought through terror, but only accelerated the collapse of the Qin dynasty."*

3. **Modern Chinese (contemporary application):**
   那場運動對知識分子的迫害，不亞於歷史上的焚書坑儒。
   *"The persecution of intellectuals during that movement was no less than the burning of books and burial of scholars of history."*

4. **English:**
   Every generation produces its own version of 焚書坑儒 — the names change, but the logic is always the same: first burn what people know, then burn those who knew it.

## Sentiment

**Strongly negative** — one of the most condemnatory phrases in the Chinese historical vocabulary. 焚書坑儒 is not merely descriptive; it is a verdict. To apply it to a policy or regime is to invoke the name of the act that ancient Chinese consensus regarded as among the worst crimes a ruler could commit against civilization: the deliberate destruction of accumulated knowledge and the deliberate murder of those who possessed it.

**Heavy in register** — this is not an everyday expression but a phrase of historical gravity, at home in formal condemnation, historical judgment, and political criticism. It carries the full weight of two thousand years of Confucian anti-Qin sentiment, reinforced by the 20th century's own parallel atrocities.
