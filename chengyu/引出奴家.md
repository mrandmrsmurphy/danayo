[[Biblical Chengyu]]
### Pronunciation
- **Mandarin**: yǐn chū nú jiā
- **Cantonese**: jan5 ceot1 nou4 gaa1
- **Japanese (hiragana)**: いんしゅつどか
- **Korean (Hangul)**: 인출노가 (in-chul-no-ga)
- **Vietnamese (Sino-Vietnamese)**: dẫn xuất nô gia

Note: [[奴家]] in later vernacular Chinese can mean “slave household” or, in other contexts, a self-reference in drama; here it strictly means “house of slavery.”

### Literal Meaning
- **引** to lead, draw forth
- **出** out of
- **奴** slave, bondage
- **家** house, household

Literal: “Led out from the house of slavery.”

### Figurative Meaning
A concise summary of the Exodus: God delivering Israel from bondage in Egypt. By extension, it signifies redemptive liberation from oppression.

### Scriptural Source
This idiom condenses the prologue to the Ten Commandments:
- **Exodus 20:2**
- **Deuteronomy 5:6**

English:
> “I am the LORD thy God, which have brought thee out of the land of Egypt, out of the house of bondage.”

Hebrew: _מִבֵּית עֲבָדִים_ — “from the house of slaves.”

Traditional Chinese translations often render:
> “將你從為奴之家領出來。”

The Dan'a'yo form preserves the juridical and covenantal weight of the declaration preceding the commandments.

### Example Usage
> 律法之始，先述引出奴家之恩，而後立誡命。  
> The law begins by recalling the grace of being led out from the house of slavery before establishing the commandments.

### Cultural Notes
Exodus 20:2 functions as covenant preamble. The commandments are grounded in prior redemption. Law follows liberation, not the reverse.

The phrase “house of slavery” signals systemic bondage, not merely personal hardship. It frames Israel’s identity as a redeemed people. In Christian theology, the Exodus becomes a paradigm of salvation, prefiguring deliverance from sin.

[[引出]] emphasizes guided liberation rather than escape by one’s own power. The initiative belongs to the divine redeemer.

Within Dan'a'yo, 引出奴家 often serves as shorthand for redemptive history and covenant identity. It can also function typologically, referring to spiritual emancipation.

### Tone and Sentiment
Grateful, covenantal, foundational. It recalls deliverance as the basis for obedience and identity.
