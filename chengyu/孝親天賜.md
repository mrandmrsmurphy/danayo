[[Biblical Chengyu]]
### Pronunciation
- **Mandarin**: xiào qīn tiān cì
- **Cantonese**: haau3 can1 tin1 ci3
- **Japanese (hiragana)**: こうしんてんし
- **Korean (Hangul)**: 효친천사 (hyo-chin-cheon-sa)
- **Vietnamese (Sino-Vietnamese)**: hiếu thân thiên tứ

### Literal Meaning
- **孝親** filial devotion toward parents
- **天賜** heaven-granted, divinely bestowed
Literal: “Honor parents, heaven bestows [blessing].”

### Figurative Meaning
A reformulation of the Fifth Commandment that emphasizes divine blessing rather than territorial inheritance. It connects filial obedience with promised life and favor from God.

### Scriptural Source
Primary source:
- **Exodus 20:12**
    > “Honor thy father and thy mother: that thy days may be long upon the land…”
- **Deuteronomy 5:16** — similar wording.

New Testament expansion:
- **Ephesians 6:2–3**
    > “Honor thy father and mother… that it may be well with thee, and thou mayest live long on the earth.”

The Apostle Paul universalizes the promise beyond the specific land of Canaan. Traditional Chinese translations render the Exodus promise in territorial terms (“在耶和華你神所賜你的地上得以長久”), while Ephesians broadens it to “在世長壽.”

孝親天賜 reflects this widening. The blessing is not merely geographic possession but divine gift of enduring life.

### Theological Framing
In Exodus, the promise is covenantal and tied to Israel’s land grant. In the New Testament, the promise is reaffirmed within a broader, trans-national church.

By shifting emphasis from “land” to “heaven-bestowed,” the maxim preserves continuity while allowing theological development. The reward is framed as divine beneficence rather than ethnic territory.

This aligns with the biblical trajectory from particular inheritance to universal hope.

### Example Usage
> 家風若立於孝親天賜之道，則代代蒙福。  
> If a household stands upon the way of honoring parents under heaven’s gift, generations receive blessing.

### Cultural Notes
孝 is deeply rooted in East Asian moral philosophy. In biblical theology, filial honor is grounded not merely in social harmony but in obedience to God.

天賜 deliberately avoids narrowing the promise to a political land grant. It evokes providence and gift. The phrase therefore bridges Old Testament covenant promise and New Testament expansion.

Within Dan'a'yo, 孝親天賜 functions as a softened yet faithful rendering of the Fifth Commandment’s promise. It preserves seriousness while allowing broader theological resonance.

### Tone and Sentiment
Encouraging, covenantal, stabilizing. It frames obedience as the pathway to divinely bestowed blessing rather than mere legal obligation.
