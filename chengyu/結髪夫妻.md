## Literal Meaning
- [結 (char)](../characters/結%20(char).md) — bind; tie; knot
- [髪](../characters/髪.md) — hair
- [夫 (char)](../characters/夫%20(char).md) — husband
- [妻 (char)](../characters/妻%20(char).md) — wife

Literally: "Husband and wife who bound their hair together."

The literal image is specific and historically grounded: in the Zhou and Han dynasty marriage ceremony, a bride and groom would cut a lock of each other's hair and tie the two strands together in a silk pouch kept for life — symbolizing permanent bodily and spiritual union. 結髪夫妻 therefore names not just *a* married couple, but specifically a couple whose marriage was sealed with this ritual.

## Extended Meaning

A husband and wife who have been together since youth — specifically, a couple whose marriage is the first for both, formed when they were young, and maintained through life's full course. The idiom carries three overlapping meanings:

1. **Historical/ritual**: a couple joined by the hair-binding ceremony (結髪)
2. **Biographical**: a couple who have been together since their youth; one's first and primary spouse
3. **Moral/emotional**: a couple whose bond represents the Confucian ideal of lifelong marital fidelity — a unity that does not waver under hardship, concubinage, poverty, or old age

A key nuance: 結髪夫妻 specifically designates the *first* marriage. In a society where wealthy men might take concubines or secondary wives, the 結髪之妻 (the wife of the hair-binding) was the primary, honored union — the one a man was morally obligated never to abandon. The idiom thus carries a weight of moral seriousness alongside its romantic beauty.

## Source and Origin

The hair-binding ceremony is documented from the Zhou dynasty onward. The earliest surviving literary uses of 結髪 as a figure for marital devotion appear in Han dynasty sources.

**《後漢書·竇融傳》** (Book of the Later Han, biography of Dou Rong, compiled by Fan Ye, 5th century CE):
> 結髮之妻，恩終不改。
> *"The wife with whom I bound hair — my affection for her never changed."*

This citation establishes the formula 結髪之妻 (the wife of the hair-binding) as the canonical expression for one's first wife, and sets the moral standard: the bond of the hair-binding ceremony creates an obligation of unchanging devotion (恩終不改, "grace that never changes at the end").

The equally important poetic source is **《孔雀東南飛》** (Southeast the Peacock Flies), one of the two great narrative poems of the Han dynasty *yuefu* tradition. The poem tells the tragedy of Jiao Zhongqing and his wife Liu Lanzhi, who are forced apart by his mother's cruelty. Before their separation, the husband laments:

> 結髮同枕席，黃泉共為友。
> *"We bound our hair together and shared the same pillow; in the Yellow Springs [afterlife] we will be companions."*

Here 結髪 has fully crystallized into the image of a sacred marital bond — one the speaker intends to honor beyond death. The couple in the poem do in fact die, are buried together, and the poem ends with the image of two entwined trees growing from their graves. This poem gave 結髪夫妻 its most enduring literary form and its association with love that persists past death and tragedy.

The four-character compound 結髪夫妻 (husband-wife who bound hair) is a later normalization of these earlier phrases, crystallizing by the Tang dynasty as the standard idiom.

## Standard Form

The Dan'a'yo form **結髪夫妻** uses the Japanese shinjitai character 髪 (simplified from traditional 髮 by dropping one stroke in the lower component). Otherwise the characters are identical across all scripts.

- **結髮夫妻** — Traditional Chinese (髮 for 髪)
- **结发夫妻** — Simplified Chinese (结 for 結, 发 for 髪/髮)

## Cultural Notes

**In Chinese literary and commemorative culture**, 結髪夫妻 belongs to a distinct genre of marital praise — the language of long marriages, epitaphs, and retrospective tributes. The phrase 結髪夫妻，白首同穴 ("a couple from the hair-binding, buried together in old age in the same grave") is a standard formula in commemorative prose, expressing the completed ideal of a marriage that held from youth to death. It is a compliment of the highest order: to call a couple 結髪夫妻 is to say their marriage achieved what Confucian ethics asked for.

The hair-binding ceremony itself disappeared from actual wedding practice before the Tang dynasty, but the phrase outlived the ritual. By the Song and Ming dynasties, 結髪夫妻 had fully shifted from a description of a specific ceremony to a general image of devoted lifelong marriage, regardless of whether any hair was actually tied. The phrase is now used in wedding toasts, anniversary commemorations, biographies, and elegies — anywhere the speaker wishes to honor the depth and duration of a couple's bond.

**In Japanese**, けっぱつふさい appears in classical literature, historical drama, and Noh and Kabuki scripts evoking old marital vows and loyal wives. It is recognizable to educated readers and carries the full weight of its Chinese classical resonance, but it is not a phrase of everyday modern Japanese speech — the same concept would more likely be expressed as 幼馴染の夫婦 (childhood-companion couple) or simply through the word 夫婦 (married couple) with qualifying language.

**In Korean**, 결발부처 is a literary and historical Sino-Korean term. It appears in classical narrative, moral literature, and historical biography emphasizing 정절 (jeongjeol, chastity and marital fidelity). Like the Japanese, it is not colloquial — in everyday Korean, the same concept would be expressed through 부부 (husband and wife) or native Korean expressions for long-married couples.

**In Vietnamese**, *kết phát phu thê* appears in Confucian moral poetry and traditional prose, functioning as part of the same Sinospheric vocabulary of conjugal virtue.

## Example Sentences

1. **Classical (《後漢書·竇融傳》, 5th c. CE):**
   結髮之妻，恩終不改。
   *"The wife with whom I bound hair — my affection for her never changed."*

2. **Classical (《孔雀東南飛》, Han dynasty):**
   結髮同枕席，黃泉共為友。
   *"We bound our hair together and shared the same pillow; in the Yellow Springs we will be companions."*

3. **Modern Chinese:**
   他們是結髮夫妻，從青梅竹馬到白頭偕老，走過了六十年風雨。
   *"They are a couple bound from the beginning — from childhood sweethearts to white-haired companions, having weathered sixty years together."*

4. **English:**
   The inscription on their shared stone read only: 結髪夫妻 — the hair-bound couple. Nothing else was needed.

## Sentiment

**Deeply positive**, with an undertone of solemnity. 結髪夫妻 is a phrase of honor and tribute — it names what a marriage is supposed to be and praises those who achieved it. The solemnity comes from the weight of the original ritual: hair was considered an intimate part of the body, inseparable from the person who grew it. To tie one's hair with another's was a permanent, irreversible act of union — not to be made lightly, not to be undone.

**Ceremonial and literary in register.** This is not a phrase used casually. It belongs to wedding toasts, anniversary speeches, biographical tributes, and elegies — occasions where the depth and endurance of a marriage is the subject. To use it in context is to invoke the full classical tradition of conjugal virtue.
