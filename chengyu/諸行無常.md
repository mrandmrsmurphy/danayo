**Literal meaning:** “All conditioned things are impermanent.”  
**Figurative meaning:** Everything in the world is transient; nothing that arises will last forever.

### Pronunciations
- **Mandarin:** zhū xíng wú cháng (1–2–2–2)    
- **Cantonese (Jyutping):** zyu1 hang4 mou4 soeng4
- **Korean:** 제행무상
- **Japanese:** しょぎょうむじょう（諸行無常）_shogyō mujō_
- **Vietnamese (Hán-Việt):** chư hành vô thường

### Variants
- **Traditional:** 諸行無常
- **Simplified:** 诸行无常
- Often appears as part of the full verse:  
    **諸行無常，是生滅法。生滅滅已，寂滅為樂。**  
    (“All conditioned things are impermanent; they arise and pass away. When arising and passing cease, serene extinction is bliss.”)

### Source / Origin
The phrase is a classical **Buddhist doctrinal formula** translating the Sanskrit teaching:

**सर्वसंस्काराः अनित्याः (sarva-saṃskārā anityāḥ)**  
“All conditioned phenomena are impermanent.”

It appears widely in **Buddhist scriptures**, including Chinese translations of early Buddhist texts preserved in the **Āgamas** and later Mahāyāna literature. The phrase became one of the **Three Marks of Existence** in Buddhism:

1. **諸行無常** — impermanence
2. **[[chengyu/諸法無我]]** — non-self
3. **[[chengyu/涅盤寂静]]** — nirvana is peace

These ideas summarize the Buddhist insight that everything produced by causes and conditions inevitably **changes, decays, and passes away**.

### Cultural Notes
- In **Japanese culture**, 諸行無常 is strongly associated with the opening line of the **_Heike Monogatari_ (平家物語)**:  
    _祇園精舎の鐘の声、諸行無常の響きあり_  
    (“The sound of the bells of the Gion Monastery echoes the impermanence of all things.”)
- Because of this literary association, the phrase often evokes themes of **historical rise and fall**, especially of political power.
- In **Chinese and Korean Buddhist contexts**, it retains a primarily **philosophical and religious meaning** referring to the impermanent nature of conditioned phenomena.

### Example Usage
**Mandarin:**  
佛教常說諸行無常，提醒人世間萬物皆會變化。  
_Buddhism often says that all conditioned things are impermanent, reminding us that everything in the world changes._

**Japanese:**  
平家物語は諸行無常の思想で知られている。  
_The Tale of the Heike is known for its theme of impermanence._

**Korean:**  
불교에서는 제행무상을 중요한 진리로 본다。  
_In Buddhism, impermanence is regarded as a fundamental truth._

### Sentiment
> **“All things that arise must pass away — the world is marked by constant change.”**
