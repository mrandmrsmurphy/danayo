## Literal Meaning
- [守](characters/守.md) — guard, protect, keep watch over
- [株 (char)](characters/株%20(char).md) — tree stump, root stock
- [待](characters/待.md) — wait, await
- [兎 (char)](characters/兎%20(char).md) — rabbit, hare

Literally: "Guard the stump; await the rabbit" — stand by the place where a lucky accident once happened and wait for it to happen again.

## Extended Meaning
守株待兎 describes the error of treating a past windfall as a repeatable formula: expecting that what worked once — or what seemed to work once — will work again under the same conditions, without understanding that the conditions have changed. It is the mistake of the man who experienced a fluke and then reorganized his life around waiting for the fluke to repeat.

The idiom applies across three overlapping situations:
1. **Passive reliance on luck** — waiting for fortune rather than working for it, because fortune once arrived uninvited
2. **Rigid adherence to precedent** — clinging to past methods without asking whether present circumstances are the same
3. **Mistaking correlation for causation** — treating the stump as the cause of the rabbit's death, and therefore guarding it

In everyday use the first sense is most common; in political and philosophical discourse the second is Han Fei's original target.

## Source and Origin
**《韓非子·五蠹》** (Han Feizi, chapter "Five Vermin," c. 233 BC):

> 「宋人有耕田者，田中有株，兔走觸株，折頸而死，因釋其耒而守株，冀復得兔，兔不可復得，而身為宋國笑。今欲以先王之政，治當世之民，皆守株之類也。」
>
> "A man of Song was plowing his field. In the field was a tree stump. A rabbit came running, struck the stump, broke its neck, and died. The man then dropped his plow and kept watch by the stump, hoping to catch another rabbit. He never caught another rabbit, and became a laughingstock throughout Song. Now, those who wish to govern the people of today using the policies of the former kings — they are all of the stump-watching type."

Han Fei (韓非, c. 280–233 BC) was a prince of the Han state and the foremost theorist of Legalism (法家). He wrote 《韓非子》 to argue that the art of statecraft must be grounded in present reality — clear law, consistent enforcement, and a clear-eyed view of human motivation — not in nostalgic reverence for the methods of ancient sage-kings.

The parable is polemical. Its target is Confucian conservatism: the position that the institutions of the Three Sovereigns and Five Emperors (三皇五帝) were perfect and that later rulers should govern by imitating them. Han Fei's reply is direct: the ancient sage-kings' methods were appropriate to their time — a sparse, simple world. To apply those methods mechanically to a populous, complex, commercially advanced state is to be the man by the stump. The rabbit died once by accident; the stump had nothing to do with it; waiting there is not wisdom, it is self-deception.

The parable thus operates at two levels simultaneously: the literal story of a foolish farmer, and a systematic argument about the epistemology of governance — what it means to learn from the past rather than merely imitate it.

## Cultural Notes
守株待兎 entered the Chinese educational canon as a children's parable long before most students encounter its Legalist context. The story of the hapless farmer by the stump is taught early and remembered clearly; its philosophical origin in a polemic against Confucian statecraft is typically encountered later, if at all. This layering — obvious fable on the surface, political argument underneath — is characteristic of the best classical parables.

Han Fei's own fate adds a tragic dimension to his body of work. He died in 233 BC in the prison of the Qin state, betrayed by his former classmate Li Si (李斯), who had recognized that Han Fei's ideas were superior to his own and arranged his elimination before he could displace him at court. The man who argued most clearly that power must be grounded in realistic assessment of human nature was destroyed by exactly the kind of court intrigue his philosophy sought to systematize and contain.

In Japanese, しゅちゅたいと is a recognized yojijukugo with the same meaning — passive reliance on past luck, failure to adapt. It appears in business and management writing as a critique of companies that succeed early and then coast, waiting for the same conditions to produce the same results, without recognizing that markets change.

The idiom pairs naturally with [[温故知新]] in an interesting tension: 温故知新 counsels returning to the old to discover the new; 守株待兎 warns against treating the old as a mechanical template for the present. The difference is the quality of attention: 温故知新 describes active, interpretive re-engagement; 守株待兎 describes passive, literal repetition.

## Example Sentences
1. 《韓非子·五蠹》:「兔不可復得，而身為宋國笑。」
   "He never caught another rabbit, and became a laughingstock throughout Song."
2. 一度成功した方法に固執して変化を拒むのは、まさに守株待兎だ。
   "Clinging to a method that succeeded once and refusing to change — that is precisely 守株待兎."
3. The startup's founders, having stumbled into a viral moment, spent the next two years in 守株待兎 — guarding the same product, waiting for the internet to find them again.

## Sentiment
Negative and mildly mocking. 守株待兎 is a critique of foolishness — specifically the self-deceptive foolishness of someone who mistakes a fluke for a law of nature. It is not sympathetic toward the subject; it implies both passivity and a failure of understanding. The register ranges from direct condemnation in formal writing to wry irony in casual commentary.
