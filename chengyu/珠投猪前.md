[[Biblical Chengyu]]
### Pronunciation
- **Mandarin**: zhū tóu zhū qián
- **Cantonese**: zyu1 tau4 zyu1 cin4
- **Japanese (hiragana)**: しゅとうちょぜん
- **Korean (Hangul)**: 주투저전 (ju-tu-jeo-jeon)
- **Vietnamese (Sino-Vietnamese)**: châu đầu trư tiền

### Literal Meaning
- **珠** pearl
- **投** to cast, throw
- **豬／猪** swine
- **前** before, in front of

Literal: “Cast pearls before swine.”

### Figurative Meaning
To offer something precious to those incapable of appreciating it. By extension, it cautions against exposing sacred or valuable things to contempt.

### Scriptural Source
This idiom condenses **Matthew 7:6**, part of the Sermon on the Mount:
> “Give not that which is holy unto the dogs, neither cast ye your pearls before swine, lest they trample them under their feet…”

Greek: _μὴ βάλητε τοὺς μαργαρίτας ὑμῶν ἔμπροσθεν τῶν χοίρων._

Traditional Chinese translations render the relevant clause:
> “也不要把你們的珍珠丟在豬前。”

The Dan'a'yo form compresses the syntax to a balanced four-character maxim.

### Example Usage
> 對頑梗之人講至理，無異珠投豬前。  
> To speak profound truth to the obstinate is like casting pearls before swine.

### Cultural Notes
Within its original context, the saying addresses discernment. It does not license contempt toward others but warns against imprudent disclosure of sacred teaching.

In Christian interpretation, the “pearls” often symbolize the gospel, wisdom, or holy mysteries. The warning concerns both protection of what is holy and avoidance of unnecessary provocation.

The structure parallels existing East Asian idioms about misplaced instruction, most notably [[対牛弾琴]]. However, 珠投猪前 carries stronger moral and covenantal weight because of its biblical origin and its explicit pairing of holy value with rejection.

In Dan'a'yo usage, the proverb typically signals prudential restraint rather than frustration alone. It affirms that truth is precious and that context matters.

### Tone and Sentiment
Cautionary, discerning, restrained. It counsels wisdom in speech and stewardship of what is sacred.
