## Literal Meaning
- [李](characters/李.md) — plum tree
- [下 (char)](characters/下%20(char).md) — beneath, under
- [瓜](characters/瓜.md) — melon, cucumber
- [田](characters/田.md) — field, cultivated land

Literally: "Under a plum tree; in a melon field" — two locations where an innocent action looks like theft.

The full proverb from which this four-character form condenses is:

> 「瓜田不納履，李下不整冠。」
> "In a melon field, do not stop to adjust your sandals; under a plum tree, do not raise your hands to straighten your hat."

If you bend down to fix your sandal in someone's melon field, it looks as though you are picking melons. If you lift your arms to adjust your hat beneath someone's plum tree, it looks as though you are reaching for plums. Both actions are innocent. Both look guilty. The proverb says: do not put yourself in situations where your innocent actions will be misread.

## Extended Meaning
李下瓜田 (or in Chinese ordering, 瓜田李下) describes the principle of **avoiding even the appearance of impropriety** — not just refraining from wrongdoing, but actively avoiding situations where wrongdoing could be suspected. The emphasis is not on actual guilt or innocence but on the social and perceptual reality that circumstances shape how actions are read.

The principle recognizes that reputation is a public good with real consequences. To subject yourself unnecessarily to suspicion is to damage trust — not only your own standing but also the community's ability to rely on visible signals of integrity. A gentleman, by this logic, owes it to others not merely to be virtuous but to be *visibly* virtuous — to arrange his conduct so that his innocence is legible.

This is related to the classical concept of 避嫌 (bì xián) — avoiding the appearance of conflict of interest or impropriety, the proactive management of how one's actions appear to others.

## Source and Origin
The proverb originates from the Han dynasty yuefu poem **《君子行》** (The Way of the Gentleman), preserved in **《楽府詩集》** (Collection of Yuefu Poetry, compiled by 郭茂倩 in the Song dynasty):

> 「君子防未然，不處嫌疑間。
>  瓜田不納履，李下不正冠。
>  嫂叔不親授，長幼不並行。
>  以礼自防閑，貽此後人看。」
>
> "The gentleman forestalls what has not yet happened; he does not place himself in positions of doubt.
> In a melon field, do not adjust your sandals; under the plum tree, do not straighten your hat.
> Brother-in-law and sister-in-law do not hand things directly to each other; elder and younger do not walk side by side.
> Guard yourself with propriety, and leave this for later generations to observe."

The poem is comprehensive in its ethic: it extends the melon-field/plum-tree principle to family interactions and generational conduct. All the examples share the same logic — it is not enough to *be* innocent; one must also make innocence *apparent* by not entering the circumstances where it might be doubted.

The four-character compound condensed the poem's central image: 瓜田李下 in Chinese (melon field + plum-tree-beneath) or 李下瓜田 in the Japanese arrangement used in this vault (plum-tree-beneath + melon field).

## Standard Form
Dan'a'yo uses **李下瓜田**, following the Japanese yojijukugo convention (李下 comes first). The standard Chinese form is **瓜田李下** (aliased in this vault).

## Cultural Notes
李下瓜田 belongs to a cluster of Confucian virtue concepts that are distinctly relational and perceptual rather than purely internal. In Western ethics, particularly Protestant traditions, the emphasis falls on inner conviction — what matters is what you actually did and why. In the Confucian framework operative here, virtue must be publicly enacted and socially readable; conduct that is internally innocent but externally ambiguous fails an important standard.

The Western formulation that comes closest is the Roman principle famously associated with Julius Caesar: *Caesaris uxor supra suspicionem esse debet* — "Caesar's wife must be above suspicion" (Plutarch/Suetonius). Caesar allegedly divorced his wife Pompeia after she was implicated — without personal fault — in the scandal surrounding Clodius's intrusion into the rites of the Bona Dea. Her actual innocence was not the point; the appearance of impropriety was itself sufficient. The structural logic is identical to 瓜田不納履，李下不整冠.

In modern usage, 瓜田李下 / 李下瓜田 applies wherever institutional integrity or personal reputation is at stake: judges who recuse themselves from cases involving people they know, officials who place assets in blind trusts, auditors who decline to review their own clients' work. The principle is now embedded in conflict-of-interest law and codes of professional conduct across East Asian legal and governance traditions.

## Example Sentences
1. 「瓜田不納履，李下不正冠。」— 《君子行》
   "In a melon field, do not adjust your sandals; under the plum tree, do not straighten your hat."
2. 李下瓜田の疑いを避けるため、彼は調達委員会の審議から自ら外れた。
   "To avoid even the appearance of 李下瓜田, he recused himself from the procurement committee's deliberations."
3. She returned the gift unopened — not because she suspected the donor of bad intent, but because 瓜田李下: the optics of accepting it could not be managed.

## Sentiment
Prudential and cautionary. 李下瓜田 is not a moral condemnation but practical advice — it acknowledges that the world reads appearances, not intentions, and counsels the wise person to manage appearances accordingly. The tone is the voice of experience rather than idealism: virtue is not enough; virtue must be seen to be virtue.
