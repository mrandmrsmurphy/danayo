[[Biblical Chengyu]]
### Pronunciation
- **Mandarin**: wù tān lín wù
- **Cantonese**: mat6 taam1 leon4 mat6
- **Japanese (hiragana)**: ぶどんりんぶつ
- **Korean (Hangul)**: 물탐린물 (mul-tam-rin-mul)
- **Vietnamese (Sino-Vietnamese)**: vật tham lân vật

### Literal Meaning
- **勿** do not
- **貪** covet, greedily desire
- **隣／鄰** neighbor
- **物** thing, possession

Literal: “Do not covet the neighbor’s goods.”

### Figurative Meaning
A concise expression of the prohibition against coveting what belongs to another. It addresses inward desire rather than outward theft, targeting the root of envy and discontent.

### Scriptural Source
This idiom condenses the Tenth Commandment:
- **Exodus 20:17**
- **Deuteronomy 5:21**

English (Exodus 20:17):
> “Thou shalt not covet thy neighbour’s house… nor any thing that is thy neighbour’s.”

Traditional Chinese translations often render this as:
> “不可貪戀你鄰舍的房屋…”

The Dan'a'yo form replaces 不可 with 勿 for classical brevity and compresses the extended list into the general term 物, encompassing all possessions.

### Example Usage
> 心若生妬，即犯勿貪隣物之戒。  
> If envy arises in the heart, one has already violated the command not to covet a neighbor’s goods.

### Cultural Notes
Unlike earlier commandments that regulate visible actions, this commandment governs internal disposition. It exposes the moral depth of the law. In Christian theology, it is often cited to show that sin begins in the heart, not merely in outward conduct.

貪 carries a strong connotation of grasping greed. The focus on 隣 underscores social proximity: sin disrupts covenant community. By using 物 rather than enumerating house, spouse, servants, livestock, the idiom universalizes the prohibition.

Historically, the Tenth Commandment has been linked to discussions of desire, contentment, and the roots of economic injustice. In Pauline theology, especially Romans 7:7, coveting becomes a paradigmatic example of inward transgression.

Within Dan'a'yo, the phrase serves both as legal shorthand and as moral introspection, highlighting the internal dimension of righteousness.

### Tone and Sentiment
Restrained, searching, ethically penetrating. It calls for inward discipline and contentment rather than merely outward conformity.
