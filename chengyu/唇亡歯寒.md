## Literal Meaning
- [唇 (char)](characters/唇%20(char).md) — lips
- [亡](characters/亡.md) — gone, lost, dead
- [歯 (char)](characters/歯%20(char).md) — teeth
- [寒](characters/寒.md) — cold

Literally: "The lips gone, the teeth feel cold."

## Extended Meaning
When two parties are in a relationship of mutual protection — one as the outer defense and one as the inner core — the fall of the outer immediately exposes the inner to the same danger. 唇亡歯寒 warns that the destruction of an ally or buffer leaves you vulnerable to precisely the fate you hoped they would absorb. The idiom carries a quality of inevitability: not "the teeth *might* feel cold" but "the teeth *will* feel cold." The logic is presented as natural law.

Used in geopolitical contexts (allied states, buffer nations, border territories) and by extension in interpersonal or organizational ones (partners, suppliers, protective institutions). It is typically invoked as a warning or, ruefully, after the fact.

## Source and Origin
**《左傳·僖公五年》** (Zuo Zhuan, Duke Xi of Lu, 5th year, 655 BC):

The state of Jin (晉) wished to attack the state of Guo (虢) but needed passage through the neighboring state of Yu (虞). Persuaded by Jin's lavish gifts, the Duke of Yu consented. His chief minister Gong Zhiqi (宮之奇) remonstrated against the decision:

> 虢，虞之表也；虢亡，虞必從之。諺所謂「輔車相依，唇亡齒寒」者，其虞虢之謂也。
> "Guo is the outer wall of Yu; if Guo falls, Yu must follow. The proverb says: 'the cheekbone and jaw depend on each other; when the lips are gone, the teeth feel cold' — this proverb speaks of Yu and Guo."

The Duke did not listen. Jin passed through Yu, destroyed Guo — then wheeled around on the return march and swallowed Yu as well. Gong Zhiqi's warning was fulfilled to the letter.

The text introduces the phrase as a 諺 (yàn, "proverb") — already a circulating saying in the 7th century BC, cited by Gong Zhiqi as folk wisdom his lord should have known. Its paired partner 輔車相依 ("cheekbone and jaw depend on each other") appears alongside it in the original but 唇亡歯寒 is the half that has lasted most durably in the language.

## Standard Form
**唇亡歯寒** — the Dan'a'yo form, using shinjitai 歯 for traditional 齒.
- 脣亡齒寒 — oldest traditional form: 脣 is the original character for "lips," now replaced by 唇 in most usage
- 唇亡齒寒 — standard traditional Chinese form
- 唇亡齿寒 — simplified Chinese
- 唇亡歯寒 — Dan'a'yo and Japanese form

## Cultural Notes
In Chinese political discourse, 唇亡歯寒 is the canonical formula for describing the mutual vulnerability of two allied parties. The phrase 唇齒相依 ("lips and teeth mutually depending") became the standard PRC diplomatic description of the Sino-North Korean relationship, a direct allusion to 唇亡歯寒 and its logic. The rationale for China's 1950 intervention in the Korean War was framed in exactly these terms.

The Zuo Zhuan context adds an ironic warning: the state of Yu was destroyed not despite having an ally but partly *because* it was persuaded to let its ally be destroyed. The gifts Jin offered Yu were the very riches taken from Guo. The Duke of Yu prioritized short-term gain over the protective relationship — and paid with his kingdom.

In Korean, 순망치한 is recognized in formal and literary registers; informal Korean tends to express mutual vulnerability more directly. In Japan, 唇亡歯寒 is taught in classical Chinese education and recognized as a yojijukugo (四字熟語), though the kundoku reading くちびるほろびてはさむし reflects the traditional Japanese method of reading classical Chinese phrase-by-phrase in native Japanese words.

## Example Sentences
1. **《左傳·僖公五年》:** 「輔車相依，唇亡齒寒，其虞虢之謂也。」
   "The cheekbone and jaw depend on each other; the lips gone, the teeth feel cold — this is what is meant by Yu and Guo."
2. 台湾と日本は唇亡歯寒の関係にある。台湾が倒れれば、日本も無事ではいられない。
   "Taiwan and Japan are in a 唇亡歯寒 relationship. If Taiwan falls, Japan cannot remain unharmed."

## Sentiment
Cautionary and solemn. 唇亡歯寒 is almost never an optimistic statement — it is either a warning issued before disaster, or a grim observation made in its wake. The register is formal to literary; it does not appear in casual speech. The idiom carries a fatalistic undertone: the outcome is treated as a matter of natural consequence, not opinion.
