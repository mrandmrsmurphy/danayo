## Literal Meaning
- [断](characters/断.md) — cut off, sever
- [章 (char)](characters/章%20(char).md) — chapter, passage, section of a text
- [取](characters/取.md) — take, select, extract
- [義](characters/義.md) — meaning, significance, the sense of a passage

Literally: "Cut the chapter; take the meaning" — sever a passage from its surrounding text and use it to express something the original did not intend.

## Extended Meaning
断章取義 describes selective quotation that distorts: pulling a line, phrase, or passage from its context to make it appear to say something the whole text does not say, or even contradicts. The charge applies to academic citation, journalism, legal argument, political speech, and literary interpretation — wherever partial quotation reverses or misrepresents the meaning of the whole.

The phrase carries a nuance that distinguishes it from simple error: 断章取義 implies intentionality, or at least a culpable carelessness about what the original actually meant. The cutter knows there is more text; they choose to stop where it suits them.

## Source and Origin
**《左傳·襄公二十八年》** (Zuo Zhuan, 28th Year of Duke Xiang, 545 BC):

> 「賦詩斷章，余取所求焉。」
> "In reciting poetry, I cut the chapter; I take from it what I seek."

The context is diplomatic: at ritual banquets during the Spring and Autumn period, officials recited poems from the 詩経 (Book of Songs) to communicate intentions that could not be stated directly. The 詩経 was a shared code — every educated official knew the poems — and the selection of which poem, and which stanza, to recite was itself a message. 断章 (cutting the chapter) was an acknowledged and accepted feature of this practice: you recited only the stanza relevant to your purpose, and both parties understood this.

This was not deception but convention. Diplomatic plausible deniability was built into the form: if the stanza you chose to quote happened to carry an implication you needed to communicate, neither party needed to make that implication explicit. The art was in the selection.

断章取義 became pejorative as the convention faded and selective quotation without acknowledgment came to be seen as manipulation. By the time the phrase settled into its modern usage, the original diplomatic context had been forgotten; what remained was the act of cutting alone, divorced from the ritual that had made it legitimate. Where the Spring and Autumn official both parties knew was cutting a passage, the modern practitioner of 断章取義 presents the cut as the whole.

## Cultural Notes
The Spring and Autumn banquet convention of 詩経 recitation is one of the most distinctive practices of early Chinese diplomatic culture. The 詩経 was not merely literature but a shared code with established interpretive conventions — every educated official could be expected to know the poems and to understand what it meant to choose one stanza over another. 断章取義 named a recognized technique before it named an abuse.

The transition from neutral term to accusation mirrors the broader development of Chinese rhetoric and hermeneutics. As direct speech became valued and indirect allusion became more dangerous in Qin and Han court culture, the elegant ambiguity of 断章取義 ceased to be an accomplishment and began to look like evasion. The practice was the same; the moral valuation reversed.

The Western conceptual equivalent is the principle of *in context* — so central to legal citation that courts distinguish between quotation that represents and quotation that distorts. The Latin tag "contextus" (context, literally "woven together") captures exactly what 断章取義 destroys: the weaving of meaning that gives any isolated passage its actual force.

In modern Chinese usage, 断章取義 appears frequently in media criticism, academic debate, and political commentary — wherever someone accuses another of misrepresentation by selective quotation.

## Example Sentences
1. 《左傳·襄公二十八年》:「賦詩斷章，余取所求焉。」
   "In reciting poetry, I cut the chapter; I take from it what I seek."
2. 彼の発言は断章取義されて、真意とはまったく逆の意味で報道された。
   "His words were taken out of context and reported to mean the precise opposite of what he intended."
3. To quote the first clause of the sentence and stop there is pure 断章取義 — it reverses the argument's conclusion while appearing to cite it.

## Sentiment
Negative and accusatory in modern usage. 断章取義 charges the subject with deliberate or at minimum careless distortion — misrepresenting a source by selective quotation. Its register is formal and combative; it appears most often in academic, journalistic, and political discourse as a direct charge against an opponent's use of evidence.
