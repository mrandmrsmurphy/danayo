- [千](characters/千.md) "thousand" + [山 (char)](characters/山%20(char).md) "mountains" + [万](characters/万.md) "ten thousand" + [水 (char)](characters/水%20(char).md) "waters"

**Literal meaning:** “A thousand mountains and ten thousand waters.”  
**Extended sense:** Vast distances; a long and difficult journey across many obstacles.

### Source and status
The expression appears in **Tang poetry**, notably in works by **張喬**, and became a common poetic formula. Because it originated as descriptive imagery rather than a specific anecdote, it sits somewhat between poetic phrase and idiom, though it is often treated as a chengyu in modern lexicons.

### Classical background
The phrase uses the conventional hyperbolic numerals 千 and 萬 to indicate magnitude rather than count. 山 and 水 together represent terrain in its most basic form. In Chinese landscape imagination, mountains and rivers define the structure of the world, and traversing them implies hardship, endurance, and distance.

In poetry, 千山萬水 frequently evokes separation. Travelers cross vast landscapes, lovers or friends are divided by geography, and journeys become metaphors for longing or persistence. The phrase therefore combines physical and emotional distance.

### Modern usage
Across the region, 千山萬水 is used for great geographical distance or for the difficulty of reaching a goal. It appears in literature, songs, speeches, and everyday language. The tone is poetic but not archaic.

### Usage note
千山萬水 emphasizes the accumulation of obstacles rather than a single barrier. The journey it describes is long not because of one difficulty, but because of many.
