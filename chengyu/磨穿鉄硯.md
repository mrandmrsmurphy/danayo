## Literal Meaning
- [磨 (char)](../characters/磨%20(char).md) — to grind; to polish; to wear down by friction
- [穿 (char)](../characters/穿%20(char).md) — to penetrate; to pierce; to wear through
- [鉄 (char)](../characters/鉄%20(char).md) — iron
- [硯](../characters/硯.md) — inkstone

Literally: "Grinding wears through the iron inkstone."

The inkstone (硯) is the slab on which the ink-stick is ground with water to produce ink for calligraphy and painting — one of the 文房四寶 (four treasures of the study). A stone inkstone wears from ordinary use over years of grinding; an iron inkstone should be impossible to wear through. 磨穿 (grind until pierced) describes not merely long use but an almost supernatural degree of sustained effort: even iron yields to sufficient persistence. The phrase names the moment when effort ceases to be merely admirable and becomes absolute.

## Extended Meaning

To pursue a goal in scholarship, writing, or craft with such relentless dedication that no hardship, failure, or apparent impossibility can halt it — to keep grinding long after ordinary perseverance would have given way. The image is of a single person against an object designed to outlast them, and winning.

Applied to:
- A scholar who failed examinations repeatedly but refused to stop studying
- A writer or calligrapher who produced work without pause until mastery arrived
- Any person whose dedication to a discipline was so extreme that it seemed to defy natural limits
- Retrospective tribute to the years of hidden effort behind a sudden visible success

The phrase belongs to the discourse of scholarly perseverance — specifically the East Asian tradition of learning as moral practice, where the effort of study is itself a cultivation of character regardless of outcome. To 磨穿鉄硯 is to have demonstrated, through physical evidence, that one's resolve was not merely claimed but enacted.

## Source and Origin

The idiom originates in the biography of **桑維翰 (Sāng Wéihàn, 892–946 CE)**, a minister of the Later Jin dynasty (936–947 CE), recorded in **《舊五代史·桑維翰傳》** (*Old Book of the Five Dynasties*, Biography of Sang Weihan).

When Sang Weihan prepared to sit the imperial jinshi examination, he was mocked on account of his surname: 桑 (sāng, "mulberry") was a homophone of 丧 (sāng, "mourning, funeral"), and detractors told him that a man named Sang had no business sitting state examinations — his name was inauspicious, and he should find another path. Sang Weihan's response was to commission an inkstone made of iron — a material that should be impossible to wear through — and to declare that he would continue writing until the iron itself was ground away. He would not stop.

**《舊五代史·桑維翰傳》:**
> 嘗鑄鐵硯以示人曰：硯弊，則易他業。
> *"He once made an iron inkstone and showed it to people, saying: 'When this inkstone is worn out, I will change to another pursuit.'"*

The iron inkstone was Sang's declaration of unconditional commitment: the condition for stopping — the iron wearing through — was designed to be unreachable. In 930 CE he passed the jinshi examination. He eventually rose to become one of the most powerful ministers of the Later Jin, serving under Emperor Gaozu.

The story crystallized into the four-character idiom 磨穿鉄硯 as shorthand for any dedication so extreme and sustained that even the hardest material yields to it — perseverance that is not merely long but categorically unlimited.

## Standard Form

- **磨穿鉄硯** — Dan'a'yo, modern Japanese (鉄 = shinjitai; 硯 = same in all traditions)
- **磨穿鐵硯** — traditional Chinese (鐵 for 鉄)
- **磨穿铁砚** — simplified Chinese (铁 for 鐵/鉄; 砚 for 硯)

磨 and 穿 are stable across all scripts.

## Cultural Notes

**In Chinese**, 磨穿鉄硯 appears in biographical writing, motivational literature, and educational discourse as the supreme image of scholarly perseverance. The phrase is applied to historical figures who achieved distinction after long years of hidden effort, to contemporary students who persisted through repeated examination failures, and to any craftsperson or artist whose mastery required an almost inhuman duration of dedicated practice. The inkstone setting gives the phrase a specifically literary and scholarly character: it praises not just any endurance but the endurance of the scholar — the person who commits their life to the written word. The iron inkstone as a chosen emblem (made deliberately, shown to others as a declaration) distinguishes Sang Weihan's perseverance from passive suffering: it was designed, announced, and witnessed.

**In Japanese**, ませんてっけん is a relatively specialized four-character idiom — less common in everyday speech than the most frequently heard 四字熟語, but well-known in educational contexts, biographical writing, and discussions of scholarly or artistic dedication. Japanese culture's deep valorization of mastery through sustained practice (道, dō — the way of a discipline) gives the concept natural resonance; the phrase fits comfortably alongside idioms like 一念岩をも通す (single-minded intent can penetrate even rock). It is more likely to appear in written formal contexts than in casual speech.

**In Korean**, 마천철연 is used in formal contexts to praise extreme scholarly or professional dedication, and appears in biographical narrative and motivational writing. The connection to the examination culture — which remains a defining feature of Korean society — gives the phrase particular resonance: the image of a scholar grinding through iron to pass the imperial examination speaks directly to the experience of students who must persist through repeated failures.

## Example Sentences

1. **Classical (《舊五代史·桑維翰傳》, compiled 974 CE):**
   嘗鑄鐵硯以示人曰：硯弊，則易他業。
   *"He once made an iron inkstone and showed it to people, saying: 'When this inkstone is worn out, I will change to another pursuit.'"*

2. **Modern Chinese:**
   他十年如一日，磨穿鉄硯，終於完成了這部三百萬字的歷史巨著。
   *"For ten years without letup, 磨穿鉄硯, he finally completed this three-million-character historical masterwork."*

3. **Modern Japanese:**
   彼女の書道への情熱はまさにませんてっけん——三十年間、毎朝一時間を欠かさず練習し続けた。
   *"Her passion for calligraphy was truly ませんてっけん — for thirty years, she never missed a single morning hour of practice."*

4. **English:**
   He failed the bar examination four times and sat it a fifth — 磨穿鉄硯, grinding until even iron gives way.

## Sentiment

**Unreservedly admiring** — 磨穿鉄硯 is always a tribute. It describes a quality of dedication that the listener is expected to admire without reservation: the person who wears through the iron inkstone is a figure of moral as well as intellectual distinction. The phrase carries no ambiguity or irony; the grinding away of iron is not suffering to be pitied but achievement to be honored.

**Specifically scholarly** — unlike general perseverance idioms, 磨穿鉄硯 is anchored in the scholar's world: the inkstone, the ink, the examination. It is not the perseverance of the warrior or the farmer but of the person who sits alone and writes. This specificity gives it a particular warmth in literate East Asian contexts: it names not merely endurance but the right kind of endurance, in the right place, for the right purpose.
