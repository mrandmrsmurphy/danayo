## Literal Meaning
- [羊](characters/羊.md) — sheep
- [頭 (char)](characters/頭%20(char).md) — head
- [狗](characters/狗.md) — dog, hound
- [肉 (char)](characters/肉%20(char).md) — meat, flesh

Literally: "A sheep's head; dog meat" — displaying the premium product while delivering the inferior one.

The idiom is a compressed form of the fuller phrase **掛羊頭賣狗肉** (hang a sheep's head, sell dog meat): the shop hangs a sheep's head above the door as its sign, but what it actually sells is dog meat. The four-character form drops the verbs and leaves only the two products — the advertised and the actual — in stark juxtaposition.

## Extended Meaning
羊頭狗肉 describes any form of deliberate misrepresentation in which the outward sign contradicts the actual content: a product sold under false pretenses, a credential that misrepresents the bearer, a promise that conceals the real intention. The idiom implies not mere error but bad faith — the seller knows what the sign says and knows what is actually being sold.

Its scope extends beyond commerce: political platforms that promise reform and deliver the status quo, institutions whose stated mission diverges from their actual practice, teachers who profess one doctrine and embody another. Wherever the exterior is dressed to deceive, 羊頭狗肉 applies.

## Source and Origin
The four-character form derives from the longer **掛羊頭賣狗肉** — "hang up a sheep's head, sell dog meat" — which appears in Song-dynasty Chan (Zen) Buddhist literature. The earliest clear attribution is to records compiled in the Chan transmission texts:

**《景德傳燈錄》** (Record of the Transmission of the Lamp, compiled by 道原 Daoyuan, 1004 CE):

> 「掛羊頭，賣狗肉。」
> "Hanging a sheep's head; selling dog meat."

The Chan context is telling: the phrase was coined by Chan masters to criticize teachers who advertise the dharma while delivering nothing genuine — who wear the robes of an enlightened teacher while trafficking in spiritual emptiness. It begins as a charge of religious fraud before becoming a general-purpose idiom for any false representation.

The commercial logic of the image is historically grounded. In the preindustrial Sinosphere, **sheep and lamb** (羊肉) were relatively expensive and prized meats; **dog** (狗肉) was a cheaper, lower-status food associated with commoners and soldiers. A shop displaying a sheep's head was advertising a premium product. To deliver dog meat under that sign was not only a commercial crime but a social insult — substituting the coarse for the refined.

The four-character condensation 羊頭狗肉 drops the verbs (掛/hang, 賣/sell) and leaves only the nouns — the advertised sheep's head and the actual dog meat — standing in implied opposition. The compression makes the accusation sharper: the whole fraud is visible in the two objects alone.

## Standard Form
**羊頭狗肉** (standard form across traditional Chinese and Dan'a'yo)
- 羊头狗肉 — simplified Chinese form; aliased in this vault

The longer form **掛羊頭賣狗肉** (挂羊头卖狗肉) remains current in Chinese, especially in formal or emphatic contexts. The four-character reduction is the standard set phrase.

## Cultural Notes
The idiom's moral force is sharper in the Confucian ethical context than it might first appear. A central Confucian virtue is the alignment of inner character with outward appearance — the person of 仁 (humaneness) does not perform virtue but embodies it; the person of 禮 (ritual propriety) is not merely observing form but genuinely enacting the relationships the form represents. To hang a sheep's head and sell dog meat is thus not only commercial fraud but a fundamental violation of the Confucian requirement that outside and inside agree.

The choice of animals is also not neutral. 狗 (the common dog, as opposed to the more dignified 犬) appears throughout Chinese idiom as a marker of something base, servile, or contemptible: 狗盗 (dog thief), 狗腿子 (lackey, running dog), 狗肉 as an insult. Selling dog meat under false pretenses adds the specific insult of 狗 to the general crime of misrepresentation.

In Japanese, ようとうくにく is a recognized yojijukugo used in business and legal contexts — false advertising, misrepresentation, bait-and-switch practices. The idiom is less common in everyday speech than in Chinese but appears regularly in written commentary on commercial and political fraud.

The nearest English equivalent idiom is **"crying up wine and selling vinegar"** (British English, largely archaic) — advertising premium goods while delivering inferior ones. The more current English equivalent is **bait and switch**.

## Example Sentences
1. 掛羊頭賣狗肉，欺世盜名，終必敗露。
   "Hang a sheep's head, sell dog meat — cheat the world to steal a name; in the end, the fraud will be exposed."
2. 鑑定書付きと言っていたが、実は偽物だった。まさに羊頭狗肉だ。
   "He claimed the item came with a certificate of authenticity, but it was a fake — a perfect case of 羊頭狗肉."
3. The campaign promised structural reform but delivered cosmetic adjustments — 羊頭狗肉 in political form.

## Sentiment
Negative and accusatory. 羊頭狗肉 is not a neutral description of error but a direct charge of deliberate deception. It is used to expose fraud after the fact — to name what has already been perpetrated — never as a neutral observation or a mere possibility. Its register ranges from colloquial (in everyday accusation) to formal (in legal and journalistic commentary), but the tone is consistently sharp and critical.
