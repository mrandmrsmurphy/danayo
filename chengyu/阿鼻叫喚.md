[Misc. Chengyu](chengyu/Misc.%20Chengyu.md)
- "Lowest of 八熱地獄"
- [[阿鼻]] + [[叫喚]]

**Literal meaning:** “The cries and wailing of Avīci.”  
**Extended sense:** Scenes of extreme suffering, chaos, or torment; uproarious shouting as if from hell.

**Source and formation:**  
The phrase arises from Buddhist vocabulary transmitted into Chinese through translations of Indian scriptures. 阿鼻 refers to **Avīci**, the lowest and most severe hell in Buddhist cosmology. The term appears widely in translated texts such as the **阿毘達磨俱舍論** and other doctrinal works.

阿鼻 itself is a phonetic rendering of Sanskrit **Avīci**, meaning “without interval,” referring to uninterrupted suffering. The compound 阿鼻叫喚 therefore evokes the cries of beings enduring punishment in the deepest hell.

**Classical background:**  
Buddhist descriptions of hell frequently emphasize sound. Screams, wailing, and lamentation convey the scale of suffering more vividly than physical description alone. 叫喚 (“shouting and crying out”) captures this auditory imagery.

In religious texts the reference is literal. In later literary usage it becomes figurative, applying the imagery of hell to worldly scenes of chaos or distress.

**Modern usage:**  
Across the region, 阿鼻叫喚 is used metaphorically for situations filled with shouting, panic, or extreme disorder. It may describe riots, battlefield confusion, disasters, or comedic exaggerations of noisy turmoil. The tone can range from dramatic to ironic depending on context.
