## Literal Meaning
- [跳](characters/跳.md) — jump, leap
- [梁](characters/梁%20(char).md) — roof beam, rafter
- [跋](characters/跋.md) — trample, stride recklessly
- [扈](characters/扈.md) — retinue; insolently overbearing

Literally: "Leaping on the beams, trampling everything underfoot."

## Extended Meaning
Running rampant; acting in a domineering, lawless, and utterly unrestrained manner. Used to describe villains, corrupt officials, warlords, or any powerful actor who defies authority and causes chaos. The compound carries a specifically contemptuous force: not just powerful, but undignified in their chaos — wild, trampling, out of control.

## Source and Origin

The chengyu fuses two independent classical phrases with distinct origins.

**跳梁** derives from the Zhuangzi (莊子), where small creatures leaping on roof beams serve as images of chaotic, low-energy disruption — animals acting beyond their station, heedless of natural order. The image is deliberately comic: not a great threat, but an inescapable nuisance.

**跋扈** is rooted in the *Hòu Hàn Shū* (後漢書, Book of Later Han), in the biography of the regent Liang Ji (梁冀):

> 「跋扈將軍」
> ("The domineering general")

Liang Ji served as regent for over two decades, installing child emperors, poisoning one who spoke against him (Emperor Zhi, r. 145–146 AD), and accumulating wealth and power that dwarfed the throne itself. His downfall in 159 AD — engineered by Emperor Huan with the help of palace eunuchs — became the template in Chinese political culture for how unchecked power eventually collapses under its own excess.

The combination of the two phrases into a single four-character compound crystallized in Tang and Song dynasty usage, merging the Zhuangzi image of reckless disorder with the Han image of tyrannical dominance into a single idiom of comprehensive lawlessness.

## Cultural Notes
In Chinese political writing, 跳梁跋扈 is a strong condemnation applied to those who abuse power with brazen disregard for law or propriety. It appears in historical commentary, political satire, and literary fiction where a villain or corrupt official acts with open contempt for authority. The Liang Ji association gives it a specifically courtly and institutional resonance — this is the language of power gone rotten from within.

The compound 跳梁小丑 (tiàoliáng xiǎochǒu, "a clownish rampager") is a common modern variant, often used dismissively to belittle a troublemaker as contemptible rather than genuinely dangerous.

In Japanese, 跋扈 (ばっこ) is the more commonly used half, appearing independently in journalism and criticism. The full four-character compound ちょうりょうばっこ is known primarily through classical Chinese study.

## Example Sentences
1. 奸賊跳梁跋扈，天下大亂。
   "Villains ran rampant, and the realm fell into chaos."
2. The regent had grown so domineering — *tiàoliáng báhù* — that even the emperor dared not speak against him.

## Sentiment
Strongly negative; a term of condemnation. Conveys not only moral disapproval but a kind of contempt — the target is both dangerous and undignified, powerful but lacking any legitimacy.
