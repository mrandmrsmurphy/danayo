### Literal Meaning
- **[禍](characters/禍.md)** calamity, disaster
- **[延](characters/延.md)** extend, prolong
- **[子孫](words/子孫.md)** descendants ([子](characters/子.md) + [孫](characters/孫.md))

Literal: “Calamity extends to descendants.”

### Pre-Biblical Literary Background

禍延子孫 is a classical-style expression attested in historical and moral discourse in Chinese tradition. It was used to describe political downfall, clan punishment, or the long shadow of wrongdoing within family lines. The phrase functions generically, without inherent theological specification.

• The idea that the consequences of one person’s wrongdoing affect later generations appears in classical Chinese moral literature. For example, a family maxim recorded in _《朱柏廬治家格言》_ includes the phrase **「匿怨而用暗箭，禍延子孫」** as a warning that hidden grievances and malicious actions can bring disaster to descendants.

• A related conceptual pair in classical Chinese thought comes from _《周易·坤·文言》_ and associated commentary:  
**「積善之家，必有餘慶；積不善之家，必有餘殃。」**  
This means roughly “a home that accumulates good deeds will have extra blessing; a home that accumulates bad deeds will have extra calamity.” Although not using the exact four-character formula, this pattern illustrates the older moral concern that generational fortune or misfortune extends beyond an individual’s life.

### Biblical Application
Though not a direct biblical quotation, the phrase aligns closely with the theological principle articulated in:
- **Exodus 20:5**
- **Deuteronomy 5:9**
- **Exodus 34:7**

English (Exodus 20:5):
> “Visiting the iniquity of the fathers upon the children unto the third and fourth generation of them that hate me.”

Hebrew:  
_פֹּקֵד עֲוֹן אָבוֹת עַל־בָּנִים_  
“Visiting the iniquity of fathers upon children.”

Traditional Chinese translations typically render:
> “追討他的罪，自父及子，直到三四代。”

In biblical theology, this generational extension is covenantal and conditional. It applies to those who persist in hatred of God, and it is immediately contrasted with mercy shown “unto thousands” of those who love Him.

### Theological Clarification
禍延子孫 in biblical usage must be carefully framed:
1. It does not teach mechanical fate.
2. It does not deny individual responsibility.
3. It describes the social and covenantal consequences of entrenched sin.

Later prophetic texts nuance the principle:
- **Ezekiel 18** emphasizes personal accountability.
- **Jeremiah 31:29–30** rejects simplistic generational blame.

Thus, in Scripture, generational consequence coexists with moral agency.

### Example Usage
> 若一族長久悖逆，或見禍延子孫，然悔改亦可止其延。  
> If a household persists in rebellion, calamity may extend to descendants; yet repentance may arrest its course.

### Cultural Notes
Because 禍延子孫 predates biblical translation, its adoption into Dan'a'yo represents a deliberate theological reorientation. In classical discourse, it describes inherited misfortune. In biblical application, it describes covenantal judgment within a moral framework governed by a just and merciful God.

The asymmetry in Exodus is crucial: mercy extends far beyond judgment. The phrase, standing alone, can sound fatalistic; within biblical theology, it is restrained by divine patience and covenant promise.

Within Dan'a'yo, the expression serves as a warning about the historical weight of sin while presupposing the possibility of repentance and restoration.

### Tone and Sentiment
Sobering, cautionary, historically conscious. It warns that actions reverberate across generations, yet remains bounded by the larger biblical arc of mercy.
