## Literal Meaning
- [[時代]] — era, age, epoch
- [[錯誤]] — error, mistake

Literally: "An error of the age" — a mistake of time-period assignment; something placed or understood in the wrong era.

## Extended Meaning
時代錯誤 covers anachronism in the full sense: something chronologically out of place, belonging to a different era than the one in which it appears. This includes:
- An object, institution, custom, or belief that has survived from a past era into one where it no longer fits
- A creative error in which something is depicted in the wrong historical period (a mechanical clock in a Roman-era film; gunpowder in a Bronze Age novel)
- A mode of thinking or behaving that was appropriate in an earlier era but is now outdated

In Chinese and Japanese usage 時代錯誤 carries a mildly critical edge: calling something 時代錯誤 implies it is stuck in the past and out of step with present reality.

## Source and Origin
時代錯誤 is a wasei kango (和製漢語) — a Sino-Japanese compound coined in Meiji-era Japan to translate the concept of *anachronism* from European literary criticism and philosophy. The English word derives from Greek *ἀναχρονισμός* (ana-chronos, "back against time"), and entered European languages via Latin and French. Japanese translators paired [[時代]] (era, period) and [[錯誤]] (error, mistake) to produce a precise semantic calque: "an error concerning the time period."

As with most Meiji-era wasei kango — 電話, 経済, 哲学, 文化 — 時代錯誤 was later re-borrowed into Mandarin Chinese (*shídài cuòwù*) and Sino-Korean (시대착오, *sidaechak'o*), following the standard route by which Japan's Meiji translation vocabulary spread across the Sinosphere. The sub-compound 錯誤 alone ("error, mistake") was an existing Classical Chinese term; 時代錯誤 is the novel combination.

## Standard Form
**時代錯誤** — Dan'a'yo and Japanese standard form.
- 时代错误 — simplified Chinese
- 시대착오 — Korean Sino-Korean rendering; note that 착오 (*chak'o*) is the Korean phonological adaptation of 錯誤, distinct from both Mandarin *cuòwù* and Japanese *sakugo*

## Cultural Notes
時代錯誤 entered East Asian intellectual vocabulary through Meiji Japan's systematic project of translating Western scientific, philosophical, and literary concepts into Sino-Japanese compounds. The term is now fully naturalized across the Sinosphere and appears in academic writing, cultural criticism, and everyday discourse.

In Japanese, 時代錯誤な (じだいさくごな) functions as an adjective meaning "anachronistic" or "behind the times." Applied to a person, institution, or attitude, it implies they are clinging to the past in a way that is mismatched to present circumstances — not a strong insult but a clear criticism. It appears frequently in historical drama commentary, political analysis, and product reviews alike.

In Korean, 시대착오적 (*sidaechak'ojŏk*) is the adjectival form, similarly used in intellectual and political discourse to describe policies, institutions, or ideologies that have outlived their era.

## Example Sentences
1. 現代の政治にそのような考えを持ち込むのは、まさに時代錯誤と言わざるを得ない。
   "To bring such thinking into modern politics is precisely what one must call 時代錯誤."
2. Shakespeare's *Julius Caesar* contains a famous 時代錯誤: a clock strikes the hour in Act II — mechanical striking clocks did not exist in ancient Rome.

## Sentiment
Mildly critical. 時代錯誤 implies that something or someone is out of step with the times — an observation that usually carries a suggestion that change is called for. Not strongly pejorative; it can be used with scholarly detachment. When applied to a person rather than an object or idea, it is more pointed.
