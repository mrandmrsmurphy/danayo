## Literal Meaning
- [五](../characters/五%20(char).md) — five
- [臓](../characters/臓.md) — viscera, internal organ
- [六](../characters/六%20(char).md) — six
- [府](../characters/府.md) — used here as Dan'a'yo's stand-in grapheme for 腑 (bowel, hollow organ), not its own primary sense "government"

Literally: "The five viscera and six bowels."

## Extended Meaning
The entire inner body; by extension, one's whole being, innermost self, or deepest feelings. Not used in modern medicine, but immediately intelligible and stylistically neutral to slightly literary — typical collocations involve pain, shock, sincerity, or total exposure, as in speaking from the depths of oneself.

## Source and Origin
Unlike most chengyu, 五臓六腑 is not traceable to a single quotable line. It originates as a technical term within early Chinese medical theory, already well established by the time of the *Huangdi Neijing* (《黃帝內經》) and other Han-period medical writings, with roots traceable at least as far back as the *Lüshi Chunqiu* (呂氏春秋). Its later idiomatic uses grow out of this technical vocabulary rather than from poetry or historiography.

In traditional Chinese medicine, the five 臓/臟 (zàng) and six 腑 (fǔ) refer to functional systems rather than discrete anatomical organs — a cosmological, correlative category aligned with yin–yang, the Five Phases, and patterns of circulation and balance. As modern anatomical science displaced this framework, 五臓六腑 ceased to function as a literal medical description. What remained was the phrase itself, fossilized as a holistic image of "everything inside" — no longer productive as terminology, yet never quite stylized into a proverb either. It survives instead as a culturally legible bundle of characters whose meaning is grasped intuitively rather than analytically.

## Standard Form
**五臟六腑** (traditional) / **五脏六腑** (simplified) / **五藏六府** (older orthographic variant, using 藏/府 in place of 臟/腑). Dan'a'yo's own spelling, **五臓六府**, uses the Japanese shinjitai 臓 and the stand-in grapheme 府 for 腑.

## Cultural Notes
Across CJKV, 五臓六腑 is used metaphorically to mean "the whole body inside," often with real emotional or rhetorical force — a lexical relic that outlived the medical theory that gave it precision, but not the totalizing force of what it names. In Japanese in particular it's often felt as a single set phrase rather than four analyzable characters, much as an English speaker might use "heart and soul" without parsing the two words separately.

## Example Sentences
1. 他的話，句句刺入五臓六腑。
   "His words struck straight into my innermost being, every single one."
2. 悔しさが五臓六腑に染み渡った。
   "The frustration seeped through his entire being."
3. The news hit her *wǔzàng liùfǔ* — a grief that reached every part of her, body and soul.

## Sentiment
Intense and total — used when an emotion or sensation is felt not partially but throughout one's entire being. Neither positive nor negative in itself; the valence comes entirely from what's doing the affecting (grief, joy, shock, sincerity).
