[[Biblical Chengyu]]
(Occasionally expanded as **上下無偶像** for clarity.)
### Pronunciation
- **Mandarin**: shàng xià wú ǒu
- **Cantonese**: soeng6 haa6 mou4 ngau5
- **Japanese (hiragana)**: じょうげむぐう
- **Korean (Hangul)**: 상하무우 (sang-ha-mu-u)
- **Vietnamese (Sino-Vietnamese)**: thượng hạ vô ngẫu

Note: 偶 here carries the sense of “image, likeness, paired form.” 

### Literal Meaning
- **上** above
- **下** below
- **無** none, without
- **偶** likeness, image, carved figure

Literal: “Above and below, no likeness.”

### Figurative Meaning
A concise formulation of the prohibition against making images of God. It asserts that no form in heaven or on earth may serve as a representation of the divine.

### Scriptural Source
This idiom condenses the Second Commandment:
- **Exodus 20:4**
- **Deuteronomy 5:8**

English:
> “Thou shalt not make unto thee any graven image, or any likeness of any thing that is in heaven above, or that is in the earth beneath…”

Traditional Chinese translations commonly render “likeness” as 偶像 or 形像.

### Example Usage
> 上下無偶，故不可塑形以象神。  
> Since there is no likeness above or below, no form may be fashioned to represent God.

### Cultural Notes
The biblical prohibition arises within a Near Eastern world saturated with cult statues. Israel’s distinctiveness lay in worship of an unseen God. The spatial pair 上下 mirrors the Hebrew merism “heaven above… earth beneath,” a totalizing expression.

This saying emphasizes incomparability rather than merely banning sculpture. The command is theological before it is aesthetic: God transcends created form.

In Christian history, this commandment has shaped iconoclasm debates, Reformation controversies, and differing traditions regarding sacred art. Interpretations vary between strict aniconism and regulated representational practice. The idiom itself expresses the high-transcendence reading.

Within Dan'a'yo, the phrase can function either as legal shorthand for the Second Commandment or as a broader doctrinal affirmation of divine incomparability.

### Tone and Sentiment
Reverent, prohibitive, theological. It stresses transcendence and the incomparability of God.
