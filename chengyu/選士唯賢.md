[Dan'a'yo Chengyu](chengyu/Dan'a'yo%20Chengyu.md)

## Literal Meaning
- [選](../characters/選.md) — to select; to choose; to elect
- [士 (char)](../characters/士%20(char).md) — gentleman-scholar; candidate for office; person of standing
- [唯 (char)](../characters/唯%20(char).md) — only; solely; exclusively
- [賢](../characters/賢.md) — worthy; capable; virtuous; one who excels in both ability and character

Literally: "In selecting candidates, [judge] only by worthiness."

The syntax is classical: 選士 establishes the domain (the act of choosing people for roles) and 唯賢 imposes the exclusive criterion (only by worthiness). The particle 唯 carries real force — it is not "prefer the worthy" but "solely the worthy." Any other criterion is excluded. The phrase does not describe a preference but a rule.

The character 士 historically names the educated gentleman class — the stratum between the ruling aristocracy and the common people, the class from which officials were drawn. In the context of this chengyu, 士 reaches beyond its historical class designation to name any candidate for a position of responsibility.

## Extended Meaning

The governing principle for the conferral of authority and selection for any role in Dan'a'yo: worthiness (賢) — demonstrated ability and character — is the sole legitimate basis for selection. Birth, social status, wealth, personal loyalty, demographic representation, and seniority are all explicitly excluded. The phrase names both an ideal and a standard against which selection processes can be measured and criticized.

The principle operates at several levels:

1. **Selection principle**: membership, authority, and decision-making roles are conferred on those of demonstrated ability, not on those of inherited position or political connection.
2. **Legitimacy claim**: authority derived from selection by merit is legitimate; authority derived from any other basis is not.
3. **Critical standard**: any process that systematically elevates the less worthy over the more worthy — whether by nepotism, democratic majorityism, or procedural formality — falls short of 選士唯賢.
4. **Civilizational claim**: the community structured by 選士唯賢 is ordered by capability and contribution rather than ethnicity, birth, or political allegiance.

Two corollary principles flow from 選士唯賢:
- **擢用在賢** (promotion depends on proven worthiness): those who demonstrate superior ability and virtue should be advanced.
- **權勢不容** (power and birth must not decide): inherited status and political leverage are excluded as criteria.

## Source and Origin

選士唯賢 is a Dan'a'yo-coined idiom articulating a political and institutional philosophy with deep roots in the East Asian philosophical tradition. The chengyu was formulated in the classical four-character register specifically to give the principle the weight of settled doctrine — the grammar of a maxim, not a preference.

The philosophical lineage is ancient. The oldest and most systematic argument for merit-based selection appears in **《墨子·尚賢》** ("Honoring the Worthy," *Mozi*), in which Mozi (墨子, c. 470–391 BCE) argues at length that the first duty of a ruler is to identify and elevate the worthy, regardless of their birth:

> 故古者聖王之為政，列德而尚賢，雖在農與工肆之人，有能則舉之。
> *"The ancient sage-kings in their governance ranked virtue and honored the worthy — even those of peasant or artisan origin, if capable, were raised up."*

Mozi's 尚賢 (shàng xián, "honoring the worthy") explicitly rejects aristocratic heredity: the son of a worthy man is not necessarily worthy; the son of a common person may be. Position must follow demonstrated virtue and ability, not birth. This is the earliest and most radical formulation of what 選士唯賢 names.

The Confucian tradition, while more deferential to social hierarchy, also affirmed 舉賢才 ("promote men of virtue and talent") as a governing duty. The tension between Confucian hierarchy and Mohist egalitarian meritocracy runs through East Asian political philosophy; 選士唯賢 aligns closer to the Mohist position in its exclusivity — 唯, not 尚 (honor) or 舉 (promote), implies that worthiness is not merely preferred but is the *only* acceptable criterion.

The historical institutional expression of this principle in East Asia was the **科挙制** (*keju*, imperial examination system), established in its classical form under the Sui dynasty (589–618 CE) and refined across the Tang, Song, Ming, and Qing dynasties. The 科挙 selected officials through competitive written examination, theoretically open to all men regardless of birth — embodying 選士唯賢 in institutional practice. Its abolition in 1905 ended fourteen centuries of examination-based selection. Dan'a'yo's invocation of 選士唯賢 consciously inherits this tradition while extending it beyond written examination to a broader principle of demonstrated capability and character.

The formulation 選士唯賢 was chosen over alternatives (尚賢, 舉賢, 賢者治國) because:
- 選 (to select, choose) foregrounds the active process of selection rather than a general orientation toward the worthy
- 唯 makes the exclusivity explicit — this is not a tendency but a rule
- Together they name both the act and its exclusive criterion in four characters

## Standard Form

選士唯賢 is a Dan'a'yo original with no pre-existing classical Chinese form. The simplified Chinese orthography:

- **選士唯賢** — Dan'a'yo form (traditional characters)
- **选士唯贤** — simplified Chinese (选 for 選, 贤 for 賢)

士 and 唯 are stable across all scripts.

**Note on 唯**: In this compound 唯 is read in its classical function as an exclusive restrictive particle ("only; solely"), not in its Buddhist usage (as in 唯識, vijñaptimātratā, "consciousness-only"). In Japanese, the compound reads 唯 as い (i) rather than ゆい (yui); in Korean, as 유 (yu).

## Cultural Notes

**In the classical Sinosphere**, the idea that 選士唯賢 names had its grandest institutional expression in the imperial examination system. For over a millennium, the 科挙 was the primary means by which the Chinese state attempted to select its governing class by demonstrated knowledge and ability rather than hereditary station. The system was not perfect — examination content was narrow, corruption was common, and the examination class itself became a form of inherited cultural capital — but the principle it embodied was unmistakably 選士唯賢: that position should follow demonstrated merit.

The abolition of the 科挙 in 1905 and the subsequent century of democratic, nationalist, and communist alternatives to meritocratic selection give the principle both its historical depth and its contemporary polemical edge. To invoke 選士唯賢 in modern discourse is implicitly to criticize systems — democratic, oligarchic, or bureaucratic — that substitute other criteria for demonstrated worthiness.

**In Dan'a'yo specifically**, 選士唯賢 names the constitutional principle of the language community's self-governance: authority accrues to those who have demonstrated the linguistic, cultural, and intellectual competence relevant to the community's purposes, not to those who happen to have been born, elected, or appointed by external convention. This is an explicit departure from both democratic majoritarianism (which counts votes, not worth) and traditional aristocratic selection (which counts birth). The phrase functions in Dan'a'yo discourse as 声形和決 does for orthographic practice: a settled maxim that can be cited rather than debated.

**Across CJKV cultures**, the concept of meritocratic selection has different institutional histories. In Japan, the 科挙 was never adopted — Japanese aristocracy remained hereditary — though the Meiji period introduced competitive civil service examination on European models. In Korea, the 科挙 was practiced from the Goryeo period and had profound effects on Korean social structure. In Vietnam, a Vietnamese examination system modeled on the Chinese ran from the 11th to early 20th centuries. Each culture thus has its own relationship to the ideal of merit-based selection that 選士唯賢 names.

## Example Sentences

1. **Classical parallel (《墨子·尚賢上》, 5th–4th century BCE):**
   故古者聖王之為政，列德而尚賢，雖在農與工肆之人，有能則舉之。
   *"The ancient sage-kings in their governance ranked virtue and honored the worthy — even those of peasant or artisan origin, if capable, were raised up."*

2. **Corollary maxims:**
   選士唯賢，故擢用在賢，而權勢不容。
   *"Select candidates solely by worthiness — therefore promotion depends on proven worth, and power and birth must not decide."*

3. **In descriptive use:**
   単亜語の権威は選士唯賢を原則とし、出自や地位ではなく、能力と徳によってのみ決まる。
   *"Authority in Dan'a'yo is governed by the principle of 選士唯賢 — determined by ability and virtue alone, not by origin or position."*

4. **English:**
   The question of who speaks for Dan'a'yo is answered the same way it has always been answered in the tradition: 選士唯賢 — by those who are worthy, and only by those who are worthy.

## Sentiment

**Prescriptive and principled** — 選士唯賢 names a rule, not a tendency. The 唯 in the compound is its ethical core: it does not counsel preferring the worthy but *excluding* the unworthy, which is a harder and more demanding claim. The sentiment is not sentimental but structural: this is how authority should be constituted, not as an aspiration but as a binding constraint.

**Polemical in context** — because 選士唯賢 implicitly rejects democratic, hereditary, and procedural-equality alternatives, it carries a polemical edge when invoked in any governance discussion. To cite it is to take a position.

**Dan'a'yo-internal in primary register** — like 声形和決, this phrase belongs to the metalanguage of the Dan'a'yo community: its first home is the grammar and the foundational documents of the language, not the literature the language produces. It is a phrase about how the community governs itself, not about the world the community describes.
