The guiding maxim for social order in <ruby>単亜語<rt>ㄉㄚㄋ·ㄚ·⼄</rt></ruby> is **【選士唯賢】** (“Select men solely by merit”). This motto articulates a vision of community grounded in **virtue and ability rather than birth or privilege**.

Two principles flow from this maxim:

1. **擢用在賢** — promotion depends on worthiness: those of proven talent and virtue should be raised.
2. **權勢不容** — power and birth must not decide: inherited status is excluded as a basis for selection.

Thus **【選士唯賢】** affirms that the ideal community of <ruby>単亜語<rt>ㄉㄚㄋ·ㄚ·⼄</rt></ruby> is one of **meritocratic fairness**, echoing classical Confucian ideals while speaking to modern commitments to equity and capability.

## 選士唯賢

### Readings
- Mandarin: xuǎn shì wéi xián
- Cantonese: syun² si⁶ wai⁴ jin⁴
- Japanese (hiragana): せんし いけん
- Korean (Hangul): 선사유현 (seon-sa yu-hyeon)    
- Vietnamese: tuyển sĩ duy hiền

### Literal structure
- 選士: select persons, choose candidates    
- 唯: only, solely
- 賢: the worthy, the capable, the virtuous

The syntax is classical: “In selecting people, take only the worthy.”

### Interpretation
A governing maxim establishing merit as the sole criterion for selection.

### Operational meaning in Dan’a’yo
Applied to institutional design:

1. **Selection principle**  
    Membership, authority, and decision-making are based on demonstrated ability, not status, representation, or vote.
2. **Institutional stance**  
    The committee is explicitly:
    - not democratic (no equal vote regardless of competence)
    - not oligarchic (no fixed elite by position or origin)
    - but meritocratic (authority tracks competence)
3. **Evaluation model**  
    Implicitly analogous to the **科挙**:
    - assessment of knowledge and skill
    - advancement by demonstrated performance
    - legitimacy grounded in recognized excellence
4. **Civilizational claim**  
    Extends beyond the committee to a broader “Renewed Sinosphere”:
    - not ethnically defined
    - not culturally monopolized
    - ordered by capability and contribution

### Underlying principles
- **Ability over identity**
- **Function over formality**
- **Examination over inheritance or representation**

### Effect
- Concentrates authority in technically competent individuals
- Aims at higher decision quality and coherence
- Risks depend on how “賢” is defined and assessed (implicit but central)

### Concise definition
“A maxim of selection by merit alone: in choosing people, admit only the capable and worthy.”

### Nuance
唯賢 is strong and exclusionary. It does not say “prefer the worthy” but “only the worthy.” The phrase aligns closely with classical political ideals, especially exam-based bureaucratic selection, but is here abstracted into a general institutional principle.
