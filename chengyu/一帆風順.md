## Standard Form
**一帆風順**  
No widely used variant forms. The graph forms are stable across Traditional and Simplified Chinese, and are written identically in Japanese, Korean (Hanja), and Vietnamese contexts.


## Pronunciation
- **Mandarin (Pinyin):** yī fān fēng shùn
- **Cantonese (Jyutping):** jat1 faan4 fung1 seon6
- **Japanese (on-yomi, hiragana):** いっぱんふうじゅん
- **Korean (Hangul):** 일범풍순 (il-beom-pung-sun)
- **Vietnamese (Hán–Việt):** nhất phàm phong thuận

## Literal Meaning
- 一 — one
- 帆 — sail
- 風順 — favorable wind

Literally: “One sail with a favorable wind.”  
The image is of a boat whose sail is perfectly aligned with a following wind, allowing smooth and effortless travel.

## Figurative Meaning
A situation proceeding smoothly and without obstacles.  
Used for careers, studies, business ventures, journeys, or life in general.

It implies ease, momentum, and absence of resistance.

## Source and Origin
The metaphor derives from maritime experience, where a favorable wind ensured swift and safe travel. The fixed four-character form became common in late imperial vernacular literature (Ming–Qing period) and spread as a conventional auspicious expression.

## Cultural Notes
- Frequently used as a **blessing or congratulatory phrase**, especially at New Year, business openings, departures, or before major undertakings.
- Common in red banners, festive couplets, and formal greetings.
- Though nautical in origin, the expression is now fully abstract and does not evoke maritime imagery for most speakers.
- Slightly formulaic in tone, but still widely used in speech and writing.

## Example Sentences
1. May your new venture be _yī fān fēng shùn_.
2. His career has been one of steady success, almost _yī fān fēng shùn_.
3. Life is not always _yī fān fēng shùn_; obstacles are inevitable.

## Sentiment

Strongly positive.  
Auspicious, encouraging, and often ceremonial in tone.
