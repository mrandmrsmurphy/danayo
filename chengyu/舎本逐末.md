## Literal Meaning
- [舎](../characters/舎.md) — abandon, discard (= 捨)
- [本 (char)](../characters/本%20(char).md) — root, foundation, origin
- [逐](../characters/逐.md) — pursue, chase after
- [末](../characters/末.md) — tip, branch, end; trivial detail

Literally: "Abandon the root, pursue the branches."

## Extended Meaning
To neglect what is fundamentally important while fixating on minor, peripheral, or merely decorative concerns — losing the forest for the trees, or putting the cart before the horse. It is overwhelmingly negative and corrective in force: invoked to criticize a decision, a policy, or a habit of mind that has its priorities backward. It appears across registers — in political and economic commentary (a state that taxes farmers to fund spectacle), in pedagogy (a student who polishes calligraphy while neglecting comprehension), and in everyday admonition (fussing over packaging while the contents spoil).

## Source and Origin
The image of "root" (本) and "branch/tip" (末) as a metaphor for what is essential versus what is derivative runs throughout early Chinese statecraft and agricultural writing, where 本 specifically denoted agriculture — the foundation of the state — and 末 denoted commerce and craft, its secondary branches.

**《呂氏春秋・士容論・上農》** (compiled 3rd c. BC under 呂不韋) states the principle directly:
> "民舍本而事末，則不令；不令則不可以守，不可以戰。"
> ("If the people abandon the root [farming] and occupy themselves with the branches [trade], they become ungovernable — and the ungovernable can neither hold their ground nor go to war.")

**《戰國策・趙策四》** preserves a related and often-cited dialogue, in which the Dowager Queen Wei of Zhao receives an envoy from Qi who is offended that she asks first after the harvest and the people, only afterward after the king:
> "苟無歲，何以有民？苟無民，何以有君？故有問舍本而問末者耶？"
> ("Without a harvest, how could there be a people? Without a people, how could there be a ruler? How, then, could one ask after the branch before the root?")

The fixed four-character form 舍本逐末 / 捨本逐末 crystallized later from this root-and-branch vocabulary as a general-purpose idiom for misplaced priorities, well beyond its original agrarian and political context.

## Standard Form
**舎本逐末** is the Dan'a'yo standard spelling, using the Shōwa-era simplified form 舎 in place of 舍/捨 (see [[覧昭和決]]).
- 舍本逐末 — Mainland Chinese simplified form (捨 → 舍); also the form most often cited in dictionaries
- 捨本逐末 — traditional Chinese form, standard in Taiwan and Hong Kong; also the form underlying the Korean 捨本逐末 (사본축말)
- All three forms are read identically across the CJKV sphere; only the orthography of the first character varies

- **Vietnamese (Hán–Việt):** xá bổn trục mạt

## Cultural Notes
- In Chinese, the idiom retains a strong flavor of classical statecraft — it is reached for in commentary on economic policy (over-investing in image projects while underfunding agriculture or industry) as readily as in personal criticism.
- In Japanese, 舎本逐末（しゃほんちくまつ）is a relatively literary yojijukugo, more at home in formal writing or commentary than in casual speech; 本末転倒 (honmatsu tentō) is the more colloquial near-synonym for the same reversal of priorities.
- In Korean, 사본축말 carries the same classical, slightly bookish register as in Chinese, and is most often encountered in essays and editorials drawing on 故事成語 (gosa seongeo, "idioms from historical anecdotes").
- Across all three traditions the idiom is corrective rather than merely descriptive — it names an error in judgment, not a neutral state of affairs.

## Example Sentences
1. 為了眼前的小利而犧牲長遠的發展，豈不是舍本逐末？
2. Cutting the research budget to fund a flashier office is _shě běn zhú mò_ — gutting the very thing that makes the company worth investing in.
3. 会社の将来を支える人材育成を削って広告費だけ増やすのは、舎本逐末の典型だ。
4. He spent the whole semester perfecting his font choices and never once revised the argument — a textbook case of abandoning the root to chase the branches.

## Sentiment
Strongly negative and corrective; a judgment of misplaced priorities rather than a simple description.
Carries a faint classical-statecraft register in all three East Asian traditions — it sounds slightly more weighty than its everyday near-synonym 本末転倒/本末倒置, and is reached for when the speaker wants to frame the error as a failure of judgment about what truly matters, not just a mix-up of order.
