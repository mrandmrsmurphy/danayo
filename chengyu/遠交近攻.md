## Literal Meaning
- [遠 (char)](../characters/遠%20(char).md) — far; distant
- [交 (char)](../characters/交%20(char).md) — to associate with; befriend; conduct diplomacy
- [近 (char)](../characters/近%20(char).md) — near; close; neighboring
- [攻 (char)](../characters/攻%20(char).md) — to attack; to assault; to conquer

Literally: "Befriend those far away; attack those nearby."

The grammar is a parallel imperative couplet — 遠交 (ally with the distant) balanced against 近攻 (attack the near) — in the compact four-character form that makes Warring States strategic maxims so memorable and so easy to cite. The structure implies a single unified principle: the two actions are not separate policies but one coordinated strategy, the first enabling the second.

## Extended Meaning

A geopolitical and strategic principle: when seeking to expand power or territory, cultivate alliances with states or powers too distant to be immediate rivals, while directing military or competitive pressure against immediate neighbors. The logic is that distant powers pose little immediate threat and make useful buffers or counterweights, while nearby rivals are the primary obstacles to expansion and must be weakened or absorbed first.

The idiom describes a mode of strategic rationality defined by proximity rather than sentiment: friendship and enmity are not fixed by culture, kinship, or ideology, but by geography and interest. Those far away are useful allies *precisely because* they are far — they cannot threaten your flanks, and they may help distract your neighbors. Those nearby are the target *precisely because* they are near — every inch of their territory, once taken, stays taken.

In modern usage the phrase appears in:
- Analysis of international relations and balance-of-power politics
- Business strategy (cultivating partnerships in distant markets while competing aggressively in local ones)
- Historical commentary on Qin's unification of China and analogous expansions
- Political science discussions of alliance formation and encirclement logic

## Source and Origin

The strategy is explicitly attributed to **Fan Ju (范雎, also written 范睢, d. 255 BC)**, a statesman from Wei who rose to become Chancellor of Qin under King Zhao (秦昭王, r. 306–251 BC). The primary source is **《史記·范雎蔡澤列傳》** (*Records of the Grand Historian*, Biography of Fan Ju and Cai Ze), compiled by **司馬遷 (Sima Qian)**, completed c. 91 BC.

The historical context: Qin under its previous advisors had been attacking distant Qi in alliance with nearby Han and Wei — a policy Fan Ju argued was strategically disastrous. Territory won from distant Qi could not be held; meanwhile Han and Wei, the actual neighbors, were being allowed to consolidate and grow strong. Fan Ju proposed the reversal: make Qi a friend, attack Han and Wei.

**《史記·范雎蔡澤列傳》:**
> 王不如遠交而近攻，得寸則王之寸，得尺亦王之尺也。
> *"Your Majesty would do better to befriend those far away and attack those near — every inch gained is Your Majesty's inch, every foot gained is Your Majesty's foot."*

The phrase 得寸則王之寸，得尺亦王之尺 — "every inch gained stays gained" — captures the core insight: proximity determines permanence. Distant conquests are fragile; neighboring territory, once taken, is consolidated into the core. The logic proved decisive: Qin adopted Fan Ju's framework, systematically absorbed its near neighbors over the following decades, and completed the unification of China in 221 BC under the First Emperor.

The strategy was later codified as **Strategy 23** of the **《三十六計》** (*Thirty-Six Stratagems*), a military compendium of uncertain but likely late Ming or early Qing composition. This codification gave 遠交近攻 a second life as a formally named and numbered strategic principle — the kind of thing that appears in Chinese strategic manuals alongside the other 35 classic stratagems.

## Standard Form

The Dan'a'yo form **遠交近攻** follows the traditional Chinese orthography. The simplified Chinese form differs only in the first character:

- **遠交近攻** — traditional Chinese, Japanese
- **远交近攻** — simplified Chinese (远 for 遠)

交, 近, and 攻 are stable across all scripts.

## Cultural Notes

**In Chinese**, 遠交近攻 is one of the most cited Warring States maxims in discussions of geopolitics, international relations, and strategic theory. It is recognizable to any educated Chinese reader and requires no explanation — to invoke it is to place the current situation in a lineage of strategic calculation going back to the unification of China. It appears in newspaper commentary, diplomatic analysis, and academic international relations writing, applied freely to contemporary situations: Cold War bloc dynamics, China's Belt and Road partnerships, US encirclement strategies, regional alliance formations. The phrase carries a tone of realpolitik — analytically neutral but with the implication that pure interest, not sentiment, drives the strategy.

**In Japanese**, えんこうきんこう (enkōkinkō) is familiar to readers of history and strategy but is not high-frequency everyday vocabulary. It appears in historical contexts — discussions of Warring States China, Japanese Sengoku period strategy, or analyses of modern international relations — rather than in casual speech. The Sengoku period's own complex alliance dynamics make the concept resonant: Japanese historical readers often apply it to the maneuvering of domain lords (大名) seeking to expand by subduing neighbors while maintaining relations with distant powers.

**In Korean**, 원교근공 (won-gyo-geun-gong) is literary-historical Sino-Korean vocabulary, familiar from historical texts and formal political analysis. It appears in discussions of Korean Peninsula geopolitics — a geography that makes the concept particularly resonant, as Korea has historically been surrounded by larger powers whose interests collide across Korean territory. The idiom provides a classical frame for analysis of both ancient Korean diplomacy and modern peninsula dynamics.

## Example Sentences

1. **Classical (《史記·范雎蔡澤列傳》, c. 91 BC):**
   王不如遠交而近攻，得寸則王之寸，得尺亦王之尺也。
   *"Your Majesty would do better to befriend those far away and attack those near — every inch gained is Your Majesty's inch, every foot gained is Your Majesty's foot."*

2. **Modern Chinese:**
   這一外交布局，正是遠交近攻的現代版本——拉攏遙遠的盟友，孤立並施壓鄰近的對手。
   *"This diplomatic arrangement is precisely a modern version of 遠交近攻 — drawing in distant allies while isolating and pressuring nearby rivals."*

3. **Modern Japanese:**
   戦国の大名たちは遠交近攻の原則に従い、遠方の大名と同盟を結びつつ、隣国を攻略していった。
   *"The warlords of the Sengoku period followed the principle of 遠交近攻, forming alliances with distant lords while subduing their immediate neighbors."*

4. **English:**
   Every analyst describing the alliance as a hedge against regional rivals was, knowingly or not, restating Fan Ju's two-thousand-year-old formula: 遠交近攻.

## Sentiment

**Neutral to analytical** — a description of strategic rationality, not a moral judgment. The phrase carries no inherent positive or negative valence; it simply names a principle. In contemporary usage it sometimes carries a mildly Machiavellian undertone, since it names a strategy in which alliances are formed and broken on purely instrumental grounds — proximity and interest, not loyalty or kinship. To accuse a state of practicing 遠交近攻 is not necessarily a condemnation, but it does imply a coldly calculated approach to relations that leaves no room for sentiment.

**Elevated and literary in register.** This is strategic vocabulary from the classical tradition, at home in political analysis, historical writing, and formal commentary — not in casual conversation.
