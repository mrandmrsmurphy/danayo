>[!Tip] One of the two named pillars of [[文言現代]], the guiding motto for [[文法 - 04句法]] — the other being [[現代適応]]
[Dan'a'yo Chengyu](chengyu/Dan'a'yo%20Chengyu.md)

## Literal Meaning
- [文言](../words/文言.md) — Literary Chinese, the classical written register
- [継承](../words/継承.md) — to inherit, to succeed to, to carry on

Literally: "succession to the Literary Chinese [standard]."

## Extended or idiomatic sense
The principle that Dan'a'yo's grammar is not invented from a clean slate, but deliberately continues the inherited framework of Classical/Literary Chinese (文言) rather than being modeled wholesale on a foreign typology. Two features above all are preserved as direct inheritances rather than innovations: **topic–comment clause structure** (主題–述部構造), under which a sentence first establishes its discourse topic and only then states a comment about it — a structure Classical Chinese already permitted and that Korean and Japanese reading traditions reinforced rather than displaced over the following millennium — and **concision** (簡潔性), the terse, lightly-marked style in which copulas and particles may be omitted, especially in the 古風体 register.

文言継承 is not itself the overall guiding motto for Dan'a'yo syntax — that role belongs to [[文言現代]] ("Classical, Modern Day"), which holds the two halves of the syntax chapter's design in balance. 文言継承 names the *classical* half of that balance: what is kept from Literary Chinese unchanged. Its counterpart, [[現代適応]] ("Adaptation to modern speech"), names the *modern* half — the obligatory verbs and fixed SVO order required for the language to function as a usable contemporary medium rather than a purely archaic register. The two together are what 文言現代 reconciles.

## Source and Origin
Coined within 単亜語 itself (2026-06-19) to give a standalone name to a principle that [[文法 - 04句法]] (the syntax chapter) already described in passing, as one of the two named components of its guiding motto [[文言現代]], without yet having its own dedicated page. 文言継承 was built by directly joining two existing words, [[文言]] (Literary Chinese) and [[継承]] (to inherit, succeed to), rather than coining new morphemes — the literal sense and the idiomatic sense are nearly identical, in contrast to a citation-idiom like [[保頭断尾]], whose extended meaning departs further from its literal image. The character order 文言継承 (継承, the more culturally/traditionally flavored order for "succession," as in passing on a heritage) was preferred over 文言承継 (承継, the more legal/technical order, as in succession of contractual rights) as the better fit for naming inheritance of a literary-grammatical tradition rather than a transfer of legal entitlement.

## Standard Form
**文言継承** is the Dan'a'yo standard spelling, using the Shōwa-era simplified form 継 in place of 繼 (see [[覧昭和決]]).
- 文言繼承 — the traditional-character form, preserved here as an alias

## Invocation
Invoke 文言継承 when explaining *why* a Dan'a'yo grammatical choice preserves a Classical-Chinese-derived structure rather than adopting a more modern or more foreign alternative — most often topic-comment ordering, the tolerance for omitted copulas in 古風体, or the preference for compact, lightly-marked clause structure generally. Within the balance struck by [[文言現代]], it is the half that says *keep this from the classical tradition*; reach for its counterpart [[現代適応]] when the point being made is instead about what Dan'a'yo requires of modern usage (obligatory verbs, fixed SVO order) rather than what it preserves from Literary Chinese.

## Example Sentences
1. なぜDan'a'yoは主語を省略できるのか — それは文言継承の精神による。古典の簡潔性を引き継いでいるからだ。
2. 단어 순서를 다시 SOV로 강제하지 않는 이유는 문언계승이다 — 주제-설명 구조는 받아들이는 것이지, 다시 만드는 것이 아니다.
3. Dan'a'yo didn't invent topic-comment order — it inherited it. That's 文言継承: phonology and script were chosen, but grammar was carried forward.

## Sentiment
Doctrinal and foundational, not evaluative; a statement of what Dan'a'yo's grammar *inherits*, in the same register as [[文言現代]] itself and as siblings [[朝鮮正音]] (phonology) and [[覧昭和決]]/[[合漢再決]] (orthography) at the level of the language's overall design philosophy. Best invoked when justifying a grammatical conservatism — why an existing classical pattern was kept rather than replaced — rather than when criticizing a specific sentence or speaker.
