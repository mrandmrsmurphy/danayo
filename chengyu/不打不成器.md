[[Biblical Chengyu]]

### Pronunciation
- **Mandarin**: bù dǎ bù chéng qì
- **Cantonese**: bat1 daa2 bat1 sing4 hei3
- **Japanese (hiragana)**: ふだふせいき
- **Korean (Hangul)**: 부타부성기 (bu-ta-bu-seong-gi)
- **Vietnamese (Sino-Vietnamese)**: bất đả bất thành khí

### Literal Meaning
- **不** not
- **打** to strike, to discipline
- **成** to become, to form
- **器** vessel, implement, useful instrument

Literal: “Without striking, it does not become a vessel.”

The imagery evokes shaping metal or pottery. Raw material requires force and refinement to become useful.

### Figurative Meaning
Discipline is necessary for proper formation of character. Applied to children, it conveys the principle that correction, including corporal discipline, is part of moral training.

English parallel: “Spare the rod, spoil the child.”

### Scriptural Source
While the exact wording is not found in canonical Bible translations, the concept reflects several passages in **Proverbs**, especially:

- **Proverbs 13:24** — “He that spareth his rod hateth his son.”
- **Proverbs 22:15** — “Foolishness is bound in the heart of a child; but the rod of correction shall drive it far from him.”
- **Proverbs 23:13–14** — “Withhold not correction from the child.”

The English proverb “Spare the rod, spoil the child” derives historically from these texts, though not verbatim from Scripture. The Dan'a'yo idiom renders the same pedagogical principle in classical form.

### Example Usage
> 教養之道，在愛與戒並行；不打不成器，然亦不可過度。  
> The way of upbringing joins love and correction; without discipline one is not formed, yet excess is also wrong.

### Cultural Notes
In biblical wisdom literature, the “rod” symbolizes authority and corrective discipline, not uncontrolled anger. The goal is formation, not harm. The metaphor of 器 is significant in East Asian thought. A vessel suggests usefulness, purpose, and moral shape. Confucian educational ideals likewise stress disciplined cultivation.

Modern contexts complicate the reception of this maxim. Contemporary discussions distinguish between structured discipline and abuse. Within Christian theology, discipline is framed within covenantal love and parental responsibility, reflecting Hebrews 12:6 on divine chastening.

In Dan'a'yo usage, the saying emphasizes formation through correction rather than endorsing severity for its own sake.

### Tone and Sentiment
Didactic, firm, morally instructive. It conveys seriousness about character formation and parental responsibility.
