# 開天闢地 / 开天辟地

## Literal Meaning
- [開](../characters/開.md) — open
- [天](../characters/天%20(char).md) — sky, heaven
- [辟](../characters/辟.md) — used here as Dan'a'yo's stand-in grapheme for 闢, "to open, to clear (land)" — not its own primary sense "dispel, refute"
- [地](../characters/地%20(char).md) — land, earth

Literally: "To open up Heaven and cleave the Earth."

## Extended Meaning
1. The beginning of the universe; the creation of Heaven and Earth.
2. Any unprecedented or epoch-making event; the start of a new era.

Used mostly in the second, figurative sense in modern speech — praise for a genuinely first-of-its-kind achievement, invention, or turning point, framed at the scale of cosmic beginnings.

## Source and Origin
The phrase comes from ancient Chinese creation myths, especially the legend of Pangu (盤古), recorded in the Han-dynasty source *Sanwu Liji* (《三五歷紀》, quoted in turn in the *Book of the Later Han*):

> 混沌如雞子，盤古生其中。天地開闢，陰陽分判。
> In the beginning all was chaos, like an unhatched egg. Pangu was born within it; when Heaven and Earth were opened, Yin and Yang were divided.

開天闢地 literally describes this cosmic separation — 開天 "to open or unfold the heavens," 闢地 "to clear or establish the earth." By the Tang and Song periods it was already used figuratively to mean "since the beginning of time" or "utterly unprecedented."

## Standard Form
**開天闢地** (traditional) / **开天辟地** (simplified) — Dan'a'yo's filename uses 辟 as the standard stand-in grapheme for 闢 (see Literal Meaning).

## Pronunciations
- **Mandarin (Pinyin):** kāi tiān pì dì
- **Cantonese (Jyutping):** hoi1 tin1 pik1 dei6

- **Korean (Sino-Korean):** 개천벽지 (gaecheon byeokji) — rare, literary
- **Vietnamese (Hán–Việt):** khai thiên tích địa

## Cultural Notes
In Daoist cosmology, the phrase names the birth of duality (陰陽) from primal chaos (混沌). In Confucian historiography, it marks the starting point of human civilization or dynastic order. In modern usage generally, it's applied to any first or revolutionary act — "an event that opens Heaven and Earth."

The image invites an easy, often-drawn comparison with Genesis 1:1 ("In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth") — conceptually close enough that 開天闢地 has been used to render "God created the heavens and the earth" in classical-style Chinese theological writing (上帝開天闢地). Unlike Genesis, though, the Pangu myth is a single act with no later parallel in the same tradition; a re-creation or renewal (as in the Genesis flood narrative) would need to be marked explicitly as 再開天闢地, "a second opening of Heaven and Earth," since the base phrase on its own always points to the one, singular first beginning.

## Example Sentences
1. 自開天闢地以來，未有此事。
   "Since the opening of Heaven and Earth, such a thing has never happened."
2. 開天闢地の偉業。
   "An achievement as great as the creation of Heaven and Earth."
3. Từ khi khai thiên tích địa chưa từng có việc ấy.
   "Since the world was first opened, such a thing has never occurred."

## Sentiment
From the very opening of Heaven and Earth — the dawn of all things. A phrase of cosmic beginnings and world-making power, strongly positive, reserved for genuinely first-of-their-kind events.
