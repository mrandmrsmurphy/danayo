## Literal Meaning
- [自](../characters/自.md) — self; oneself; one's own
- [業 (char)](../characters/業%20(char).md) — karma; intentional action; deed; work
- [自](../characters/自.md) — self; oneself (repeated)
- [得](../characters/得.md) — to receive; to obtain; to get

Literally: "One's own karma; one's own receiving."

The structure is a doubled 自 parallel: 自業 (one's own karma/actions) + 自得 (one receives by oneself). Both halves begin with 自, emphasizing that the actor and the recipient of results are identical — the same person who performs the action is the same person who must receive its consequences. This structural doubling is the formula's moral claim in miniature: there is no escape from the results of one's own actions because the agent and the patient are the same.

業 here carries its Buddhist sense: not merely "work" or "occupation" (the common modern Chinese meaning) but **karma** — intentional action understood as generating inevitable consequences within the causal moral order of the universe. In Buddhist metaphysics, every intentional act (善業 good karma, 悪業 bad karma) leaves a trace that must eventually bear fruit for the actor.

## Extended Meaning

The principle that one's own actions produce consequences that return specifically to oneself — that moral causality is personal and inescapable. In modern colloquial use, the phrase applies to the situation of someone suffering the predictable consequences of their own choices: not random misfortune, but the fruits of what they themselves planted.

The idiom can describe:
- Someone who is suffering because of their own past poor decisions
- A situation where misfortune is clearly self-caused rather than externally imposed
- The general principle of karmic retribution — what goes around comes around
- An investor who loses money on a risk they themselves chose
- A liar caught in their own lies
- Any moment where the connection between someone's past action and their present suffering is direct and obvious

The phrase is generally applied to negative consequences — one's own bad karma returning as one's own suffering — though the Buddhist principle applies equally to good actions and good results. In practice, 自業自得 is nearly always the vocabulary of deserved misfortune rather than deserved reward: it is what one says when someone has brought trouble upon themselves.

## Source and Origin

The concept originates in **Buddhist karmic theory**, fundamental to both Theravāda and Mahāyāna traditions. The Sanskrit principle is expressed in the Pali term *atta-kamma* (one's own deed) — the doctrine that intentional actions (karma) generate consequences (vipāka) that bind specifically to the agent who performed them. Karma is neither transferable nor escapable; each being reaps exactly the results of its own accumulated intentional actions, in this life or in future existences.

In Chinese Buddhist translation literature, 業 became the standard rendering of Sanskrit *karma* (Chinese translators also used 羯磨, *jiémó*, for certain Buddhist procedural uses). The compound 自業 (one's own karma) appears throughout the Chinese Buddhist canon to emphasize personal moral accountability within the karmic system.

**From the 《大乗入楞伽経》 (Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra, Tang-dynasty translation):**
> 自業自得果，衆生各自受。
> *"From one's own karma one receives one's own fruit; each sentient being receives [the results] by themselves."*

This formulation captures the full Buddhist logic: the karma is yours (自業), the fruit is yours (自得果), and the receiving is yours alone (各自受). No one else can receive the consequences of your actions; no one else's karma belongs to you.

The four-character compound 自業自得, compressing the two essential terms (自業, 自得) into a single portable formula, crystallized in Chinese Buddhist vernacular usage and eventually passed into general Chinese and Japanese vocabulary — losing its specifically Buddhist technical meaning while retaining its causal logic. By the Song and Ming dynasties it had entered common speech as a formula for deserved consequences. In modern Chinese, Japanese, and Korean it operates primarily as everyday vocabulary, though educated speakers remain aware of the Buddhist layer.

## Standard Form

The Dan'a'yo form **自業自得** is identical to the traditional Chinese form. The simplified Chinese form differs only in the second character:

- **自業自得** — traditional Chinese, Japanese, Dan'a'yo (業)
- **自业自得** — simplified Chinese (业 for 業)

自 and 得 are stable across all scripts.

- **Japanese (on-yomi, hiragana):** じごうじとく (jigō jitoku)
- **Korean (Sino-Korean):** 자업자득 (ja-eop-ja-deuk)
- **Vietnamese (Hán–Việt):** tự nghiệp tự đắc

**Note on 業**: In this compound 業 is read *yè* (4th tone, "karma; occupation; deed") in Mandarin, and ごう (GŌ) in Japanese on-yomi — the Buddhist reading, not the more common modern Japanese ぎょう (GYŌ) used in words like 産業 (industry) or 授業 (lesson). The ごう reading preserves the Buddhist phonological tradition.

## Cultural Notes

**In Chinese**, 自業自得 is a fully naturalized idiom spanning from formal literary use to casual conversation. It is one of the most common ways to express the concept of deserved consequences — cleaner than the native 咎由自取 (the fault is self-sought) and carrying the added weight of karmic logic. To say of someone 這是他自業自得 ("this is his 自業自得") is both a factual observation (he caused this himself) and a moral verdict (therefore he deserves it). The Buddhist origin adds a note of inexorability: it is not just that he caused it, it is that causation of this kind cannot be avoided. The phrase appears in literary criticism, political commentary, personal advice, and ordinary conversation — always applied to someone receiving the fruits of their own poor choices.

**In Japanese**, じごうじとく (jigō jitoku) is high-frequency everyday vocabulary, among the most commonly known and used four-character idioms (四字熟語) in the language. It appears in journalism, fiction, casual conversation, and proverb collections. Japanese Buddhist culture gave the term an especially natural home — the 業 (karma) concept is deeply embedded in Japanese moral intuition even outside explicitly Buddhist contexts — but the phrase operates in modern Japanese without any particular religious flavor. It is simply the most precise available expression for "you brought this on yourself."

**In Korean**, 자업자득 (ja-eop-ja-deuk) is similarly common and naturalized, appearing in journalism, political commentary, and everyday speech. Korean Confucian and Buddhist culture both provided receptive soil for the karmic logic of the phrase; in modern Korean it carries no more religious weight than its Chinese or Japanese equivalents.

## Example Sentences

1. **Classical Buddhist (《大乗入楞伽経》, Tang dynasty translation):**
   自業自得果，衆生各自受。
   *"From one's own karma one receives one's own fruit; each sentient being receives [the results] by themselves."*

2. **Modern Chinese:**
   他明知風險還要一意孤行，現在破產了，真是自業自得。
   *"He knew the risks and insisted on his own course anyway — now that he's bankrupt, it's truly 自業自得."*

3. **Modern Japanese:**
   あれだけ周囲の忠告を無視してきたのだから、今さら文句を言っても自業自得というものだ。
   *"Having ignored everyone's advice for so long, complaining about it now is nothing but 自業自得."*

4. **English:**
   He had spent years cutting corners and dismissing warnings. When the collapse finally came, no one said they were surprised — 自業自得, in the end.

## Sentiment

**Negative to neutral** — almost always applied to deserved misfortune, with a clear undertone of "this was predictable and avoidable." The phrase can be delivered sympathetically (acknowledging that someone has suffered their own consequences without rubbing it in) or pointedly (as a verdict on someone who refused good advice). The Buddhist layer adds inexorability: the phrase does not merely say "you caused this" but implies that such causation is woven into the structure of things — moral causality is not a human judgment but a feature of reality.

**Common across all registers** — from Buddhist scripture to business journalism to casual conversation, 自業自得 is one of the most universally legible and frequently deployed idioms in the CJKV sphere. It requires no specialist knowledge to understand and carries no particularly elevated literary weight; it is simply the most exact available formula for its meaning.
