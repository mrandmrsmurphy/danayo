## Literal Meaning
- [破 (char)](../characters/破%20(char).md) — to break; to crush; to shatter decisively
- [頭 (char)](../characters/頭%20(char).md) — head
- [傷](../characters/傷.md) — to wound; to injure; to bruise
- [足 (char)](../characters/足%20(char).md) — foot; heel

Literally: "Crush the head; wound the foot/heel."

The four characters compress Genesis 3:15 into its essential asymmetry. 破 (crush, shatter) is the stronger verb: it denotes decisive, permanent destruction. 傷 (wound, bruise) is the lesser: it denotes pain and injury but not annihilation. The objects name the two combatants by their anatomical vulnerability: 頭 (head) for the serpent, 足 (foot/heel) for the woman's seed. Read together, the formula states both outcomes of the conflict — the wound that the seed will suffer and the destruction it will deliver — in four characters, with the verbs chosen to make the asymmetry explicit.

The Dan'a'yo formulation heightens the contrast already present in traditional Chinese Bible translations. Where the Chinese Bible often renders both verbs as 傷 (wound), 破頭傷足 uses 破 for the serpent's fate and 傷 for the seed's suffering — theological interpretation embedded in lexical choice.

## Extended Meaning

A poetic and theological summary of the enmity and its resolution announced in Genesis 3:15: the woman's seed will suffer (wounded heel) but will ultimately destroy the serpent (crushed head). By extension, any conflict in which suffering is real and temporary while victory is decisive and permanent — the pattern of the cross and resurrection — may be described in terms of 破頭傷足.

In Dan'a'yo, the phrase functions as a compressed creedal statement about redemptive history: not just "there is conflict" but "the conflict is asymmetric, and the asymmetry favors the seed."

## Source and Origin

The sole source is **Genesis 3:15**, the divine address to the serpent following the Fall, traditionally called the **protoevangelium** (first gospel) — the first announcement of redemption in Scripture.

**Hebrew (MT):**
> וְאֵיבָה אָשִׁית בֵּינְךָ וּבֵין הָאִשָּׁה, וּבֵין זַרְעֲךָ וּבֵין זַרְעָהּ — הוּא יְשׁוּפְךָ רֹאשׁ, וְאַתָּה תְּשׁוּפֶנּוּ עָקֵב
> *"I will put enmity between you and the woman, and between your offspring and her offspring — he shall bruise your head, and you shall bruise his heel."*

The Hebrew verb שׁוּף (*shūf*) is used for both actions, creating a formal parallelism: the same word describes what the seed does to the serpent's head and what the serpent does to the seed's heel. The asymmetry is in the objects, not the verbs — head versus heel, the mortal versus the painful.

**Traditional Chinese Bible translations** (Union Version, Chinese Standard Bible):
> 女人的後裔要傷你的頭；你要傷他的腳跟。
> *"The woman's offspring shall wound your head; you shall wound his heel."*

The Chinese Union Version renders both verbs as 傷 (wound), following the formal parallelism of the Hebrew. This smooths the asymmetry that theologians have always emphasized: the head-wound is mortal, the heel-wound is survivable.

**The Dan'a'yo formulation** 破頭傷足 makes that asymmetry lexically explicit. By choosing 破 (crush, shatter) for 頭 and 傷 (wound) for 足, it encodes the traditional theological reading — that the seed's victory over the serpent is decisive and permanent while the seed's suffering is real but not final — directly into the four characters. This is theological commentary in the form of translation choice: a creedal claim compressed into lexical selection.

**The Vulgate** (Latin, Jerome, 4th century CE) renders the verse: *ipse conteret caput tuum et tu insidiaberis calcaneo eius* — "he shall crush your head, and you shall lie in wait for his heel." The Vulgate's *conteret* (shall crush) for the head and *insidiaberis* (shall lie in wait for, shall strike) for the heel also differentiates the two verbs, and was historically influential in Western Christian interpretation of the verse as prophesying Christ's victory over Satan.

**The Septuagint** (Greek, c. 3rd–2nd century BCE) uses αὐτός σου τηρήσει κεφαλήν, καὶ σὺ τηρήσεις αὐτοῦ πτέρναν — "he shall watch for your head, and you shall watch for his heel" — using the same verb for both, as the Hebrew. The LXX reading opened patristic debates about whether the conflict is symmetrical (both watching) or asymmetrical. Mainstream Christian exegesis, following the Vulgate and the Hebrew's implication of different body parts' vulnerability, has held the asymmetrical reading.

## Standard Form

破頭傷足 is a Dan'a'yo coinage that does not appear as a fixed four-character compound in any Chinese Bible translation. It is a theological compression of Genesis 3:15, formulated in the classical four-character register to give the verse the portable, citable authority of a settled maxim. All four characters are stable across traditional and simplified scripts — there are no variant forms.

**Note on 破 vs 傷**: The deliberate lexical asymmetry in this formulation — 破 (crush, shatter) for the serpent's fate vs 傷 (wound, bruise) for the seed's suffering — is theologically load-bearing. 破 implies irreversible destruction; 傷 implies painful but survivable injury. The choice of different verbs is the Dan'a'yo formulation's interpretive contribution over Chinese Bible translations that use 傷 for both.

## Cultural Notes

**In Chinese Christianity**, Genesis 3:15 holds the same canonical status as in Western Christianity — the first hint of redemption in a fallen creation, the verse that makes the entire biblical narrative legible as a single arc from promise to fulfillment. Chinese theologians in the Reformed and evangelical traditions (the dominant streams of Chinese Protestantism) have generally followed the asymmetrical interpretation: the seed's crushing of the serpent's head is decisive, the serpent's wounding of the seed's heel is temporary. 破頭傷足 encodes this reading directly and gives it a memorable classical form.

**In Japanese Christianity**, the verse is rendered in classical Japanese Bible translations with comparable asymmetry. The Buddhist and Shinto backgrounds of Japanese culture give the serpent/combat imagery a somewhat different resonance — the serpent (蛇) in Japanese religious iconography is not purely malevolent — but the Christian interpretation of Genesis 3:15 stands independently. The Meiji-era Revised Bible and subsequent translations have shaped Japanese Protestant vocabulary.

**In Korean Christianity**, which has produced the largest Christian percentage population in East Asia (roughly 30% of the population), Genesis 3:15 is a widely known verse in a highly literate biblical culture. Korean evangelical Christianity, shaped by Presbyterianism and its descendants, treats the protoevangelium as fundamental to the structure of redemptive history. 파두상족 (pa-du-sang-jok) as a Sino-Korean formulation would be legible to Korean Christian readers and comprehensible without explanation.

**The patristic tradition**: the identification of Genesis 3:15 as the protoevangelium was articulated by Irenaeus of Lyon (c. 180 CE) in *Adversus Haereses*, where he reads the woman's seed as the incarnate Christ, the head-crushing as the resurrection, and the heel-wounding as the crucifixion. This reading entered both Eastern and Western Christian tradition and is the implicit background of 破頭傷足's theological claim.

## Example Sentences

1. **Scriptural source (Genesis 3:15, Hebrew/Chinese):**
   女人的後裔要傷你的頭；你要傷他的腳跟。
   *"The woman's offspring shall wound your head; you shall wound his heel."*

2. **Theological application:**
   十字之苦，似傷足；復活之勝，乃破頭。
   *"The suffering of the cross was like a wounded heel; the victory of the resurrection was the crushing of the head."*

3. **In descriptive use:**
   基督教的救贖敘事，可以破頭傷足四字概括：痛苦是真實的，勝利是決定性的。
   *"The Christian narrative of redemption can be summarized in four characters — 破頭傷足: the suffering is real, the victory is decisive."*

4. **English:**
   The whole drama of Scripture from Eden to the empty tomb is already present in miniature in Genesis 3:15 — 破頭傷足, the wounded heel and the crushed head, suffering and triumph held together in a single sentence.

## Sentiment

**Militant and hopeful** — the phrase holds tension without collapsing it. The suffering is named (傷足, the wounded heel) and not minimized; the victory is named (破頭, the crushed head) and declared decisive. The asymmetry of the verbs carries the theological claim: these are not two equal blows but a wound and a destruction, a temporary cost and a permanent resolution.

**Creedal in register** — this is a condensed theological statement, not a proverb or narrative idiom. It belongs to the discourse of confession and proclamation: what one says when one wishes to name the shape of redemptive history in four characters. Its classical register (the four-character maxim form) gives it the authority of settled doctrine, which is appropriate for the verse from which it is drawn — a verse that the Christian tradition has read as the first word of the gospel.
