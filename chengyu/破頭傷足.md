[[Biblical Chengyu]]

### Pronunciation

- **Mandarin**: pò tóu shāng zú
    
- **Cantonese**: po3 tau4 soeng1 zuk1
    
- **Japanese (hiragana)**: はとうしょうそく
    
- **Korean (Hangul)**: 파두상족 (pa-du-sang-jok)
    
- **Vietnamese (Sino-Vietnamese)**: phá đầu thương túc
    

Note: 破頭 and 傷足 are ordinary two-character compounds meaning “break the head” and “wound the foot.” Their pairing here is theological rather than conventional idiom.

---

### Literal Meaning

- **破** break, crush
    
- **頭** head
    
- **傷** wound, bruise
    
- **足** foot, heel
    

Literal: “Crush the head; wound the foot.”

---

### Figurative Meaning

A concise rendering of the mutual striking foretold in Genesis 3:15. It refers to the decisive defeat of the serpent and the costly suffering of the woman’s seed.

---

### Scriptural Source

This maxim condenses **Genesis 3:15**:

> “…he shall bruise thy head, and thou shalt bruise his heel.”

Hebrew:  
_הוּא יְשׁוּפְךָ רֹאשׁ, וְאַתָּה תְּשׁוּפֶנּוּ עָקֵב_  
“He shall strike your head, and you shall strike his heel.”

Traditional Chinese translations often render:

> “女人的後裔要傷你的頭；你要傷他的腳跟。”

The Dan'a'yo form heightens contrast: head broken versus foot wounded.

---

### Theological Context

Genesis 3:15 introduces enduring enmity and foretells asymmetrical conflict:

- The serpent inflicts injury.
    
- The seed delivers decisive destruction.
    

Christian theology traditionally reads this as the _protoevangelium_, anticipating Christ’s suffering and ultimate victory.

The lexical shift from “bruise” to **破** underscores finality. A wounded heel implies pain; a broken head implies defeat.

---

### Example Usage

> 十字之苦，似傷足；復活之勝，乃破頭。  
> The suffering of the cross was like a wounded heel; the victory of resurrection was the crushing of the head.

---

### Cultural Notes

Head and heel represent hierarchy and vulnerability. In ancient combat imagery, striking the head signifies mortal victory. The heel, though painful to wound, is not fatal.

By compressing the verse into balanced four-character form, 破頭傷足 frames redemptive history as conflict resolved through costly triumph.

The maxim must not be read as dualistic balance. The injury to the seed is temporary; the destruction of the serpent is decisive.

Within Dan'a'yo, 破頭傷足 serves as a poetic summary of redemptive struggle culminating in victory.

---

### Tone and Sentiment

Militant yet hopeful. It acknowledges suffering while affirming ultimate triumph.
