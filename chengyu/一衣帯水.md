## Literal Meaning
- [一 (char)](characters/一%20(char).md) — one, a single
- [衣](characters/衣.md) — garment, robe, clothing
- [帯 (char)](characters/帯%20(char).md) — belt, sash
- [水 (char)](characters/水%20(char).md) — water

Literally: "A single belt of water" — one narrow ribbon of water, as thin as the sash of a garment.

The metaphor is vividly scaled: the Yangtze River, China's greatest waterway and for centuries the boundary between north and south, is reduced to the width of a cloth belt. This is not geographical description but rhetorical compression — the speaker wills the river small.

## Extended Meaning
Used to describe two places that are geographically close despite being separated by a sea, strait, or river — "only a belt of water between them." In modern usage it connotes nearness, shared heritage, and mutual obligation across a narrow body of water.

The phrase's emotional register has shifted since its origin. Sui Wendi used 一衣帯水 to diminish the Yangtze — the water was *only* a belt, therefore crossable, therefore no excuse for inaction. Modern usage uses the same image to describe closeness — the water is real, but small; the shores are near. The emphasis has moved from will to geography, from "I can cross it" to "we are practically adjacent."

## Source and Origin
**《隋書·高祖本紀》** (History of the Sui, Annals of Emperor Gaozu):

Emperor Wen of Sui (隋文帝 Yang Jian, r. 581–604), having unified northern China, turned his attention south. The Chen dynasty still held the Yangtze basin. Before the invasion he is recorded to have declared:

> 「我為百姓父母，豈可限一衣帶水不拯之乎！」
> "As the father and mother of the people, can I allow a mere belt of water to stand between me and rescuing them?"

In 589 CE, Sui forces crossed the Yangtze and destroyed the Chen dynasty, unifying China for the first time since the fall of the Han — ending nearly three centuries of division between north and south. Yang Jian's phrase reframes the Yangtze not as a natural barrier but as a moral failure: a river between a ruler and his people is an affront, not an obstacle.

The phrase 一衣帯水 thus begins as a statement of will before it becomes a description of proximity. The earliest sense is "I refuse to let this water be large"; the modern sense is "the water between us is small."

## Cultural Notes
In the modern Sinosphere, 一衣帯水 is most often invoked to describe the Japan-China and Japan-Korea relationships: neighboring civilizations, ancient cultural bonds, and a sea between them that is narrow enough to cross in hours. The phrase appears regularly in diplomatic speeches, cultural exchange events, and statements of solidarity — a reminder that the water is a connection as much as a division.

The original military context has largely faded. What persists is the image: two shores, one strip of water, people who are nearly neighbors. For this reason 一衣帯水 is almost always used warmly — it implies that geographic proximity carries moral weight, that people separated by only a narrow strait have obligations to each other that more distant neighbors do not.

In Japanese, いちいたいすい is a standard yojijukugo, taught in school and recognized by most educated speakers. It appears most frequently in discussions of East Asian relations, particularly Sino-Japanese cultural history.

## Example Sentences
1. 「我為百姓父母，豈可限一衣帶水不拯之乎！」— 隋文帝
   "Can I allow a mere belt of water to prevent me from saving my people?" (Sui Wendi, before the conquest of Chen, 589 CE)
2. 日中両国は一衣帯水の隣国であり、その文化的絆は深く長い。
   "Japan and China are neighbors separated by only a belt of water; their cultural bonds are deep and long."
3. The two cities had been rivals for centuries, yet they were 一衣帯水 — close enough to see each other's lights across the channel on clear nights.

## Sentiment
Positive and evocative. In modern usage 一衣帯水 suggests nearness, intimacy, and shared destiny — the water is not a wall but a seam. It is typically deployed to emphasize connection rather than separation, and carries a tone of warmth and mutual recognition.
