[[Biblical Chengyu]]
### Pronunciation
- **Mandarin**: xiá yú niǎo shēng
- **Cantonese**: hat6 jyu4 niu5 saang1
- **Japanese (hiragana)**: かつぎょちょうせい
- **Korean (Hangul)**: 할어조생 (hal-eo-jo-saeng)
- **Vietnamese (Sino-Vietnamese)**: hạt ngư điểu sinh

### Literal Meaning
- **轄** to govern, control, exercise authority over
- **魚** fish
- **鳥** birds
- **牲** beasts, domesticated animals

Literal: “To rule fish, birds, and beasts.”

### Figurative Meaning
A compact expression of humanity’s dominion mandate: the charge to exercise authority over the animal creation. By extension, it may signify responsible stewardship of the natural world.

### Scriptural Source
The idiom condenses **Genesis 1:26–28**:
> “…let them have dominion over the fish of the sea, and over the fowl of the air, and over the cattle…”

Traditional Chinese translations commonly render “have dominion” as **管理** or **治理**, and list:
> “海裡的魚、空中的鳥、地上的牲畜…”

The Dan'a'yo form selects **轄** to emphasize jurisdictional authority rather than mere management.

### Example Usage
> 人受造之初，即蒙命轄魚鳥牲，而非任意毀壞。  
> From the beginning humanity was commanded to rule fish, birds, and beasts, not to destroy them at will.

### Cultural Notes
Genesis presents human dominion as derivative authority. Humanity rules as image-bearer under divine sovereignty. The command is royal yet bounded.

The choice of **轄** is significant. It connotes governance within assigned jurisdiction, not autonomous possession. This helps guard against readings that equate dominion with exploitation.

The triad 魚鳥牲 mirrors the Hebrew merism that spans sea, sky, and land. It signals comprehensive scope rather than exhaustive zoological taxonomy.

In theological discussion, this mandate has shaped debates about ecology, agriculture, technology, and animal ethics. Some traditions stress mastery; others emphasize stewardship. The Genesis text combines authority with accountability.

Within Dan'a'yo, 轄魚鳥牲 can function both as a doctrinal shorthand for the creation mandate and as a reminder of delegated rule under God.

### Tone and Sentiment
Dignified, vocational, covenantal. It affirms human authority while implying responsibility and restraint.
