## Literal Meaning
- [温](characters/温.md) — warm, reheat; to revisit
- [故](characters/故.md) — old, former, the past
- [知 (char)](characters/知%20(char).md) — know, understand
- [新 (char)](characters/新%20(char).md) — new

Literally: "Warm the old; know the new" — to revisit what one has already learned and discover fresh understanding within it.

The choice of 温 ("to warm, to reheat") is precise: the old knowledge is not studied again from scratch but *warmed* — like reheating food, the material is already known; the warming reveals new flavor, new nourishment that was not available at first encounter.

## Extended Meaning
温故知新 names a mode of learning in which depth precedes breadth. Rather than accumulating new information, one returns to what is already known and finds new meaning within it — meaning that was invisible at first reading because the reader lacked the maturity to perceive it.

In Confucian pedagogy, this is the mark of the qualified teacher: one who has internalized the classical texts so thoroughly that each return to them yields fresh illumination. The classics are not exhausted by study — they give more as the reader deepens. The "new" (新) is not new material but new understanding earned through reflection.

## Source and Origin
**《論語·爲政篇·第十一章》** (Analects, Book II: For Government, Chapter 11):

> 「溫故而知新，可以爲師矣。」
> "He who warms the old and thereby comes to know the new — such a one may be deemed a teacher."

The saying is attributed to Confucius (孔子, 551–479 BC). The full sentence contains a condition and a consequence: the ability to draw new understanding from classical sources is the *qualification* for teaching. The master does not define a teacher by credentials or seniority but by this specific cognitive capacity — the ability to re-enter already-known material and emerge with something genuinely new.

Book II of the Analects (爲政, "For Government") situates the remark in the context of governance: the ruler who can renew his understanding of antiquity is fit to guide the present. The saying thus carries both a pedagogical and a political dimension — it describes the kind of mind needed not only to teach but to govern well.

The four-character condensation 温故知新 drops the connecting particle 而 ("and thereby") of the original. The compression turns a conditional sentence into a compact formula: *warmth of the old → knowledge of the new*.

## Standard Form
**温故知新** (Dan'a'yo standard; uses 温, the form shared with Japanese and Mainland China)
- 溫故知新 — traditional Chinese form (溫 for 温); dominant in Taiwan and classical scholarship; aliased in this vault

No further orthographic variants. The four characters are stable across all scripts.

## Cultural Notes
温故知新 is among the most universally cited of Confucian educational maxims across the entire Sinosphere. Its scope extends well beyond classical study: wherever depth of understanding matters — medicine, law, craft, martial arts, statecraft — returning to foundational material with a matured eye is the method it recommends.

In Japan, おんこちしん is a recognized yojijukugo (四字熟語), taught in secondary school and used widely in educational mottos and discussions of scholarship. Japanese traditional arts (茶道, 武道, etc.) operate on exactly this principle: the learner revisits the same kata or forms for decades, and what they find there changes with each decade of practice.

In Korea, 온고지신 appears regularly in school mottos and educational discourse. The Joseon Confucian academies (書院, sowon) organized their curriculum around this principle: years of rereading the same small canon, with the expectation that the texts would yield more with each cycle.

The Western analogue most often cited is Newton's "standing on the shoulders of giants" — but the difference in emphasis is instructive. Newton's phrase foregrounds the new discovery enabled by prior work; 温故知新 foregrounds the *act of return* and the *depth of re-reading* as the mechanism of discovery. The new is not built on the old; it is found *within* the old by a mind that has grown enough to see it.

## Example Sentences
1. 《論語·爲政·十一》: 「溫故而知新，可以爲師矣。」
   "He who warms the old and thereby comes to know the new may be deemed a teacher."
2. 学習において温故知新の精神が大切だ。
   "In learning, the spirit of *on-ko-chi-shin* is essential."
3. Reading the Analects again after ten years — that is 温故知新 in practice: the same words, and a wholly different book.

## Sentiment
Positive and intellectually serious; associated with scholarship, depth, and the proper method of learning. Neither nostalgic (which would prize the old for its own sake) nor merely progressive (which would dismiss it) — 温故知新 holds past and new in productive tension. Its register is formal and classical; it is most at home in educational and scholarly discourse but is broadly applicable wherever learning is taken seriously.
