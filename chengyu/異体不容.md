[Dan'a'yo Chengyu](chengyu/Dan'a'yo%20Chengyu.md)

>[!info]
>For the orthographic practice this principle governs, see [[文法 - 03文字法]]

## Literal Meaning
- [異](../characters/異.md) — different; variant; other; divergent
- [体](../characters/体.md) — body; form; shape; physical instantiation
- [不 (char)](../characters/不%20(char).md) — not; prohibitive negation
- [容 (char)](../characters/容%20(char).md) — to permit; to accommodate; to give room to; to tolerate

Literally: "Variant forms are not tolerated."

The syntax is a compact prohibition: 異体 (variant forms) + 不容 (are not permitted). The verb 容 carries its sense of *accommodation* — giving room to, tolerating the presence of, allowing a place for. What 不容 names is not merely a preference for the canonical form but a structural denial of space to the variant: variants have no place in the system, cannot occupy a position in output, cannot be the answer to any query. The prohibition is active and absolute.

異体 (literally "different bodies") is a classical term for allographic variants — characters that encode the same morpheme in graphically distinct forms. The Chinese writing tradition accumulated thousands of such variants over millennia of regional divergence, manuscript transmission, and scribal practice. 異体不容 names the decision to collapse this multiplicity into a single standard form per character.

## Extended Meaning

The governing orthographic principle that each character in Dan'a'yo is assigned exactly one canonical form, and that all allographic variants, historical alternates, and graphically distinct but semantically equivalent forms are excluded from the output layer of the system.

The principle operates at several levels:

1. **Canonicalization**: every lexeme maps to a single 正体字 (standard form). All 異体字 are treated as inputs that may be recognized, but not as outputs that may be produced.
2. **Input normalization**: the input method (入力法) accepts variant forms and resolves them automatically to the canonical glyph before processing. A user typing the variant enters through the system; the system exits only through the standard.
3. **Variant collapse**: graphically distinct but phonologically or historically related forms may be absorbed into a single standard entry (転属). The merger is one-directional — the variant yields to the standard, not the reverse.
4. **Long-tail reduction**: rare or obscure forms (稀字) are absorbed into common characters (常用字), eliminating low-frequency variants and compressing the effective character inventory.

The practical effects are: a smaller and more stable character inventory; increased readability and learnability; and, by design, the intentional loss of paleographic and historical distinctions that have ceased to carry functional weight.

## Source and Origin

異体不容 is a Dan'a'yo-coined idiom, formulated to give the character normalization principle the authority of a settled maxim. Like 声形和決, it belongs to the metalanguage of the writing system — a phrase *about* the system, in the system's own register.

The principle it names is one of the oldest in East Asian script history. Every major program of character standardization has faced the same problem: a writing tradition accumulates variant forms over centuries of regional use, copyist variation, and scribal shorthand, and must periodically decide which form is the *standard* and which forms are merely *variants*. The decision is simultaneously philological (which form has the better historical claim?), functional (which form is most recognizable and stable?), and political (whose usage counts as authoritative?).

The foundational moment in the Chinese tradition was the **書同文** ("unify the script") decree of the Qin First Emperor (221 BCE), which abolished the divergent scripts of the six conquered states and mandated a single script — 小篆 (small seal script) — as the standard for the unified empire. This was the first systematic application of 異体不容 as a state policy: one form per character, imposed by administrative fiat.

Subsequent standardization projects followed the same logic:
- The **康熙字典** (Kangxi Dictionary, 1716 CE) established canonical forms and listed variants separately, defining a distinction between 正字 (standard forms) and 俗字 / 異体字 (popular/variant forms).
- **Simplified Chinese** (1956–1964) reduced thousands of complex characters to simplified forms, and in doing so made 異体不容 explicit in a new dimension: the simplified form IS the standard; the traditional form is, in simplified Chinese contexts, a variant.
- The **Jōyō kanji** list in Japan (periodically revised) similarly defines canonical forms for Japanese use: forms outside the list are either tolerated as 表外字 or discouraged.

Dan'a'yo's 異体不容 stands in this tradition while being more systematic: rather than leaving the variant/standard distinction implicit or historically negotiated, it states it as a constitutional principle. The choice of 不容 over milder alternatives (不用, 不取, 非標) is deliberate — it signals that variants are not merely suboptimal but structurally excluded.

## Standard Form

The Dan'a'yo form **異体不容** uses 体 (shinjitai, simplified from 體). The traditional Chinese form:

- **異体不容** — Dan'a'yo form (体 = shinjitai)
- **異體不容** — traditional Chinese (體 for 体)

異, 不, 容 are stable across all scripts.

**Note on the name**: The idiom contains 異体 (the thing it prohibits) in its own name — the term for variant forms is itself written with the shinjitai (variant) form 体 rather than the traditional 體. This is not accidental: Dan'a'yo's canonical form for this character IS 体 (it is the standard in the vault), so using 体 here is a demonstration of 異体不容, not a violation of it.

## Cultural Notes

**In the context of East Asian script history**, the tension between canonical forms and their variants is ancient and never fully resolved. The Chinese writing system, developed over three millennia and used across an enormous geographic and temporal range, accumulated an extraordinary variety of allographic multiplicity: the 康熙字典 lists over 47,000 character forms, the majority of which are variants, obsolete forms, or rare alternates of a much smaller inventory of functionally distinct characters. Every literate tradition within the Sinosphere has had to navigate this multiplicity.

**The Qin unification** (書同文) is the precedent: administrative power imposing a single canonical form as the condition of legibility across a territory. But the Qin standard itself evolved — 小篆 gave way to 隷書 and then 楷書 in practice — and each transition produced new variants at the boundaries. The principle 異体不容 captures what every standardization project aspires to but none fully achieves: a clean, unambiguous, one-to-one mapping between characters and their forms.

**In modern East Asian writing systems**, variant suppression takes different institutional forms. Simplified Chinese is the most radical: it mandated specific simplified forms and made the complex traditional forms non-canonical for mainland use. The Jōyō kanji list achieves something similar for Japanese: it defines which forms are standard for general use, with everything outside the list implicitly requiring justification. Taiwan's standard maintains the traditional forms as canonical, with simplified treated as variant. Korea's hanja are no longer in general use for most writing, reducing the practical problem substantially. Dan'a'yo's 異体不容 is explicit where other systems are implicit: it states the principle rather than enacting it through a list.

**The self-referential dimension**: 異体不容 contains the very phenomenon it prohibits — 体 is a shinjitai form, which is a variant of 體 by the logic of traditional Chinese orthography. Dan'a'yo's answer is that 体 is *the canonical form in this system*, not a variant within it — which is precisely what 異体不容 enables: the system defines what is standard, and everything else is variant relative to that definition.

## Example Sentences

1. **Definitional (from 文法 - 03文字法 context):**
   異体不容：各字唯一正体，異体字不出於輸出。
   *"異体不容: each character has exactly one standard form; variant character forms do not appear in output."*

2. **In descriptive use:**
   単亜語の正書法は異体不容を原則とし、字形の揺れを入力層で吸収し、常に正体字のみを出力する。
   *"Dan'a'yo orthography takes 異体不容 as its governing principle, absorbing graphical variation at the input layer and always outputting only the canonical form."*

3. **English:**
   Every writing system that has tried to manage variant forms has eventually arrived at the same rule: 異体不容 — one form per character, enforced absolutely, the rest are inputs not outputs.

## Sentiment

**Prescriptive and exclusionary** — 不容 is notably stronger than the milder options 不用 ("not used") or 不取 ("not chosen"). It implies active structural exclusion: the variant has no place, not just a lower priority. The tone is regulatory and absolute, appropriate to a constitutional principle rather than a stylistic preference.

**Self-referential by design** — the idiom contains a 異体 in its own name, and the system's resolution of this — by declaring 体 the canonical form — is a demonstration of the principle in action. The phrase cannot be stated without performing what it names.

**Dan'a'yo-internal in primary register** — like 声形和決 and 選士唯賢, this phrase belongs to the metalanguage of the system. Its natural home is the grammar and orthographic specifications; it is a phrase about how the writing system works, not a phrase produced by the writing system in ordinary use.
