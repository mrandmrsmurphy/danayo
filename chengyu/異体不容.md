## 異体不容

### Readings
- Mandarin: yì tǐ bù róng
- Cantonese: ji⁶ tai² bat¹ jung⁴
- Japanese (hiragana): いたい ふよう
- Korean (Hangul): 이체불용 (iche bul-yong)
- Vietnamese: dị thể bất dung

### Literal structure
- 異体: variant forms, allographic variants (異体字)
- 不容: not permitted, not admitted

The four-character form is compact and prescriptive, with a legal or regulatory tone.

### Interpretation
A strict orthographic principle:

Each character is assigned exactly one standard form; variant glyphs are not permitted as independent forms.

### Operational meaning in Dan’a’yo
This governs normalization at the level of character identity:
1. **Canonicalization**  
    Every lexeme maps to a single **正体字**. All **異体字** are treated as inputs only, not outputs.
2. **Input normalization**  
    An **入力法** accepts variant forms and resolves them automatically to the canonical glyph.
3. **Variant collapse**  
    Graphically distinct but phonologically or historically related forms may be **転属** into a single standard entry.
4. **Long-tail reduction**  
    Rare or obscure forms (**稀字**) are absorbed into common characters (**常用字**), eliminating low-frequency variants (the “冗尾”).

### Underlying principles
- **Orthographic unification**: eliminate allographic multiplicity
- **Statistical compression**: reduce character set entropy by collapsing low-frequency forms
- **Phonological alignment (partial)**: near-homophones may be merged when distinction is judged non-essential

### Effect
- Smaller, more stable character inventory
- Increased readability and learnability
- Loss of paleographic and historical distinctions by design

## Concise definition
“A rule enforcing a single canonical form per character, rejecting variant glyphs and normalizing all inputs to standardized forms.”

### Observation
The phrase is slightly stronger than typical usage of 不用 or 不取; 不容 implies active exclusion. It fits the system, where variants are not merely discouraged but structurally disallowed in output.
