[Dan'a'yo Chengyu](chengyu/Dan'a'yo%20Chengyu.md)

>[!info]
>For the orthographic practice this principle governs, see [[文法 - 03文字法]]

## Literal Meaning
- [声](../characters/声.md) — sound; voice; phonological realization
- [形 (char)](../characters/形%20(char).md) — form; written shape; graphic character
- [和](../characters/和.md) — harmony; coordination; in accord
- [決 (char)](../characters/決%20(char).md) — resolution; settled determination; fixed as principle

Literally: "The harmony of sound and form, fixed as a settled principle."

The four characters divide cleanly into two binomes. 声形 (sound-form) names the two components of a writing system that typically pull apart: the phonological and the graphic. 和決 names what is done to them: not merely coexistence (和 alone would suffice for that) but 決 — a deliberate resolution, a formal settlement, an established policy. The compound does not describe a natural state; it describes a design choice that has been made and held.

## Extended Meaning

The governing orthographic principle of Dan'a'yo: phonological annotation (声, realized as Bopomofo ruby text) and graphic character form (形, realized as Shinjitai kanji) are to be displayed together as co-equal and permanent features of the written system. Neither is subordinate; neither is transitional; neither is optional except under constraint.

The principle has three operational consequences:

1. **Dual-layer display**: standard Dan'a'yo text pairs a base layer of Shinjitai kanji (semantic and morphographic) with an annotation layer of Bopomofo (phonological), delivered via ruby markup.
2. **Permanence over pedagogy**: unlike writing systems that treat phonetic annotation as a learning scaffold to be removed once literacy is achieved, 声形和決 holds that the annotation is a permanent feature of the text — the phonological layer is always there for the reader, not merely for the novice.
3. **Priority under constraint**: when space or typographic conditions force a choice, characters (形) take precedence; the phonetic layer (声) may be omitted. The principle is co-presence as the ideal, not as the rule in all conditions.

## Source and Origin

声形和決 is a Dan'a'yo-coined idiom, created to name a design principle before that principle had a standard way to refer to itself. The four-character form was chosen deliberately: in the classical tradition, a four-character compound carries the weight of a settled maxim, something that can be cited as a rule rather than debated as a preference. By giving the principle a chengyu, Dan'a'yo places it in the register of established doctrine rather than ongoing discussion.

The principle itself is articulated in **[[文法 - 03文字法]]**, the chapter of the Dan'a'yo grammar devoted to orthographic and phonological display. The problem it addresses is real: languages in the CJKV sphere all use logographic scripts that suppress phonological transparency — a reader seeing 日 in Chinese, Japanese, or Korean cannot recover the pronunciation without language-specific knowledge. Dan'a'yo resolves this by making phonological annotation structural rather than optional: 声形和決 names this resolution.

The particular formulation 和決 — "harmonious-resolution" — was chosen over simpler alternatives (和合, 統一, 一致) because it captures both the relational quality (harmony: the two layers work together, not against each other) and the institutional quality (decision: this is a rule, not an emergent convention). The coupling is deliberate and permanent, which is what 決 adds to 和.

## Standard Form

声形和決 uses 声 (the shinjitai and simplified form) for the traditional 聲, following Dan'a'yo's general preference for the Japanese-conventional form where it exists. The traditional Chinese form:

- **声形和決** — Dan'a'yo form (声 = shinjitai/simplified)
- **聲形和決** — traditional Chinese orthography (聲 for 声)

形, 和, 決 are stable across all scripts.

## Cultural Notes

**In the context of East Asian writing systems**, the tension named by 声形 is ancient and structural. All major East Asian scripts are logographic at their core: characters encode meaning and morphology but suppress or complicate phonological access. Every major CJKV writing tradition has developed some mechanism for providing phonological annotation — Japan's furigana, Taiwan's Bopomofo pedagogy, Korea's historical idu and gugyeol systems, China's pinyin annotation in children's texts. What distinguishes Dan'a'yo's approach under 声形和決 is the permanence: annotation is not a scaffold for beginners but a constitutive feature of the writing system for all readers.

**Compared to Japanese furigana**: Japanese uses furigana extensively in children's literature, manga, and texts with unusual or technical readings — but removes it from adult literary prose, newspapers, and formal writing. The implication is that the phonological layer is for the benefit of those who do not yet know, and becomes unnecessary once knowledge is assumed. 声形和決 explicitly rejects this model: the phonological layer is for the benefit of the reading act itself, regardless of the reader's prior knowledge.

**Compared to Taiwanese Bopomofo pedagogy**: In Taiwan's educational system, Bopomofo (注音符號) is introduced first and used as the gateway to character literacy, then phased out as the learner gains character proficiency. Again the model is scaffolding: phonology serves form until form can stand alone. 声形和決 inverts this relationship: form and phonology co-exist permanently, neither serving as the gateway to the other.

**Phonological realism**: The Dan'a'yo Bopomofo system records Middle Chinese phonology, not any modern CJKV pronunciation. This means the 声 layer provides a stable, cross-CJKV phonological reference rather than a pronunciation guide for any single modern language. The harmony in 声形和決 is between the graphic-semantic layer (which encodes meaning across the CJKV sphere) and the phonological layer (which encodes the reconstructed common phonological ancestor of those languages). Both layers operate at the same level of abstraction.

## Example Sentences

1. **Definitional (from 文法 - 03文字法):**
   声形和決：声韻與字形，必以振假名相従，字形優先。
   *"声形和決: phonological syllable and graphic character must accompany each other through ruby annotation, with the graphic form taking precedence [under constraint]."*

2. **In descriptive use:**
   単亜語の正書法は声形和決を原則とし、振假名を一時的な補助ではなく永続的な要素と見なす。
   *"Dan'a'yo orthography takes 声形和決 as its governing principle, treating ruby annotation not as a temporary aid but as a permanent element."*

3. **English:**
   The question of whether to include pronunciation guides is settled in Dan'a'yo before it arises: 声形和決 — sound and form together, by rule, permanently.

## Sentiment

**Prescriptive and definitive** — 声形和決 is not a description of a tendency or a preference but the name of a rule. The 決 in the compound carries that force: this is settled. The tone is technical and institutional rather than poetic or evaluative; the idiom belongs to the discourse of system design rather than to narrative or literary use.

**Dan'a'yo-internal in register** — unlike the other chengyu in the vault, which are drawn from the common CJKV classical heritage, 声形和決 is a term of art specific to this language. It appears in the grammar and in metalinguistic discussion of Dan'a'yo's writing system; it would be opaque outside that context.
