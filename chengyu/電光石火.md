**Literal meaning:** “Lightning flash, spark from striking stone.”  
**Extended sense:** Extreme speed; instantaneous action; something happening in a flash.

**Source and background:**  
The pairing appears in medieval Buddhist and Chan texts, where lightning and the spark struck from flint were conventional metaphors for sudden awakening or immediate insight. The expression circulated in Song-era religious prose and was later absorbed into general literary usage. It does not derive from a single canonical historical anecdote, but from metaphorical language common in Buddhist discourse.

Lightning and the spark from stone both emphasize brevity and intensity. The image is not merely fast movement but sudden illumination. In Chan contexts, the metaphor often described enlightenment occurring without gradual mediation.

**Classical background:**  
電光 and 石火 are parallel images of momentary brilliance. The compound therefore doubles the idea of immediacy. Unlike idioms about haste or rashness, 電光石火 describes speed without judgment. It can signal efficiency, decisiveness, or sheer rapidity.

Over time the religious connotations faded, leaving a neutral idiom for extraordinary quickness.

**Modern usage:**  
Across the region, 電光石火 is used in sports commentary, journalism, narrative prose, and everyday speech to describe swift action or reaction. It is stylistically elevated but not archaic, and carries no necessary spiritual meaning in modern contexts.
