[[Biblical Chengyu]]
### Pronunciation
- **Mandarin**: wù wàng chēng míng
- **Cantonese**: mat6 mong6 cing1 ming4
- **Japanese (hiragana)**: ぶもうしょうめい
- **Korean (Hangul)**: 물망칭명 (mul-mang-ching-myeong)
- **Vietnamese (Sino-Vietnamese)**: vật vọng xưng danh

### Literal Meaning
- **勿** do not
- **妄** vainly, falsely, recklessly
- **称／稱** to utter, to invoke, to call
- **名** name

Literal: “Do not vainly invoke the Name.”

### Figurative Meaning
A concise formulation of the commandment prohibiting misuse of the divine name. It forbids false oaths, irreverent invocation, manipulative religious speech, and casual profanity directed toward God.

### Scriptural Source
This idiom condenses the Third Commandment:
- **Exodus 20:7**
- **Deuteronomy 5:11**

English:
> “Thou shalt not take the name of the LORD thy God in vain.”

Hebrew:  
_לֹא תִשָּׂא אֶת־שֵׁם יְהוָה אֱלֹהֶיךָ לַשָּׁוְא_  
“Do not lift up the name of YHWH your God to emptiness.”

Traditional Chinese translations often render this as:

> “不可妄称耶和华你神的名。”

The Dan'a'yo form removes 不可 in favor of 勿 for compact classical force, and omits explicit reference to the Lord, since 名 in this context is understood to refer to the divine Name.

### Example Usage
> 人若為私利而誓，乃犯勿妄称名之戒。  
> If a person swears for selfish gain, he violates the command not to invoke the Name in vain.

### Cultural Notes
In biblical thought, a “name” is not a mere label but represents presence, authority, and character. To misuse the Name is to misrepresent God himself.

Historically, Jewish tradition developed reverential practices around the Tetragrammaton, avoiding its pronunciation. Christian theology broadened the commandment to include:

- False or manipulative religious speech
- Hypocritical appeals to God’s authority
- Casual profanity
- Oaths made lightly or deceitfully

The verb 妄 highlights emptiness and falsehood, echoing the Hebrew _lashav_ “to vanity.” 称 emphasizes vocal invocation rather than abstract thought, underscoring speech as covenantal act.

Within Dan'a'yo, this chengyu functions both as legal command and ethical warning, reflecting a high theology of divine holiness and the power of words.

### Tone and Sentiment
Solemn, reverent, prohibitive. It carries the gravity of covenant law and the sanctity of the divine Name.
