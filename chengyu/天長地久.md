## Literal Meaning
- [天 (char)](characters/天%20(char).md) — heaven, sky
- [長 (char)](characters/長%20(char).md) — long, enduring, lasting
- [地 (char)](characters/地%20(char).md) — earth, ground
- [久 (char)](characters/久%20(char).md) — a long time, lasting duration

Literally: "Heaven is long; Earth is lasting" — a parallelism pairing 天長 (heaven endures) with 地久 (earth lasts), invoking the two halves of the cosmos as the measure of all permanence.

The structure is a classical 對 (duì, parallel couplet): four characters split into two two-character phrases that mirror each other — sky:long // earth:lasting. Each half carries the same meaning; together they reinforce and amplify it. This kind of parallelism is one of the defining features of classical Chinese literary aesthetics.

## Extended Meaning
天長地久 means: as permanent as the cosmos itself — as lasting as the sky above and the earth below, which have been here before human memory and will remain after it. Anything described as 天長地久 is being measured against the most enduring standard available.

In its most common modern usage, especially in Chinese, 天長地久 describes **eternal love** — a vow of permanence modeled on the permanence of the cosmos. It is the classical idiom for "forever" in romantic contexts, appearing in poetry, songs, wedding toasts, and declarations of devotion.

## Source and Origin
**《道德経·第七章》** (Tao Te Ching, Chapter 7, attributed to Laozi, c. 6th–4th century BC):

> 「天長地久。天地所以能長且久者，以其不自生，故能長生。是以聖人後其身而身先；外其身而身存。非以其無私邪？故能成其私。」
>
> "Heaven is enduring; Earth is long-lasting. The reason Heaven and Earth can endure and last long is that they do not live for themselves — therefore they can live long. For this reason the sage puts himself last and yet comes first; holds himself apart and yet remains. Is it not because he has no self-interest? That is how his self-interest is fulfilled."

The Taoist argument: heaven and earth endure not because they strive to endure but because they are empty of self-regard. The cosmos lasts because it does not try to last. The sage replicates this at the human level — by yielding, he leads; by stepping back, he remains. 天長地久 is thus the opening premise of a paradox about impermanence and self-transcendence: the things that last longest are the things that hold themselves least tightly.

The phrase escaped its philosophical context to become the greatest standard of permanence in the literary tradition — which is why the Tang poet Bai Juyi could use it as a foil:

**《長恨歌》** (Song of Everlasting Regret, Bai Juyi 白居易, 806 CE):

> 「天長地久有時盡，此恨綿綿無絶期。」
> "Heaven and Earth, however long they last, will someday end — but this sorrow stretches on, endlessly, without end."

《長恨歌》 tells the tragic love story of Emperor Xuanzong of Tang (唐玄宗) and his consort Yang Guifei (楊貴妃). Yang Guifei was executed in the An Lushan Rebellion (756 CE), and the poem portrays the Emperor's inconsolable grief. The emotional climax of the poem is the vow the lovers make:

> 「在天願作比翼鳥，在地願為連理枝。」
> "In heaven, may we be twin-birds flying together; on earth, may we be branches growing from the same root."

And then Bai Juyi delivers the final reversal: 天長地久有時盡 — even the eternal duration of heaven and earth has an end. But the sorrow born of their separation does not. By invoking and then exceeding 天長地久, Bai Juyi turned the phrase into the supreme measure of romantic permanence in Chinese letters, and so it has remained.

## Cultural Notes
天長地久 carries two registers that coexist in Chinese usage — philosophical and romantic — while in Japanese the philosophical register predominates.

**Philosophical**: In the Taoist tradition, 天長地久 frames a meditation on why the cosmos persists: not through force of will but through emptiness of self. This paradox — endurance through non-striving — runs through 道家 thought and reappears in Buddhist concepts of 無常 (impermanence) and 無我 (non-self). The phrase sits at the intersection of Taoism's cosmology and its ethics.

**Romantic**: Since 長恨歌, 天長地久 has been the standard phrase for vowed eternal love in Chinese culture. It appears in classical ci lyrics, in the titles of modern songs and films, and in everyday declarations of devotion. In contemporary Chinese pop music alone, 天長地久 has been the title of dozens of songs. When used romantically, it retains the Taoist paradox as an undercurrent: the lovers vow what even the cosmos cannot guarantee indefinitely — and that is precisely what makes the vow beautiful and heartbreaking.

**Japanese**: In Japan, 天長 (tencho) has additional resonance as a historical era name (天長, 824–834 CE) and as the root of 天長節 (Tennōsetsu), the traditional name for the Emperor's birthday — "the Day of Heaven's Endurance." The phrase てんちょうちきゅう is recognized but used more in literary and philosophical contexts than in everyday romantic expression, where Japanese tends toward other formulations.

**Korean**: 천장지구 is used in classical and literary contexts. The related phrase 해로동혈 (偕老同穴, "grow old together and share the same grave") is more common in Korean for the romantic vow of eternal fidelity.

## Example Sentences
1. 《道德経·第七章》:「天長地久。天地所以能長且久者，以其不自生，故能長生。」
   "Heaven endures; Earth lasts. The reason Heaven and Earth endure so long is that they do not live for themselves — therefore they live long."
2. 《長恨歌》:「天長地久有時盡，此恨綿綿無絶期。」
   "Heaven and Earth, however long they last, will someday end — but this sorrow stretches on, endlessly, without end."
3. 彼女は指輪を受け取り、静かに言った。「天長地久」と。
   "She accepted the ring and said quietly: 天長地久."

## Sentiment
Solemn and tender. 天長地久 speaks with the weight of the cosmos behind it — it does not promise something personal or contingent but invokes the most fundamental permanences of existence. In philosophical contexts the tone is contemplative and paradoxical. In romantic contexts it is the language of the deepest vow: I love you as long as there has been sky and earth.
