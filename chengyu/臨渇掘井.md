## Literal Meaning
- [臨](characters/臨.md) — draw near to, face, be confronted with
- [渇 (char)](characters/渇%20(char).md) — thirst; be thirsty
- [掘 (char)](characters/掘%20(char).md) — dig, excavate
- [井](characters/井.md) — well

Literally: "When thirsty, dig a well" — confronting thirst and only then beginning to dig the well that would have quenched it.

The image is exactly right. A well requires deep digging, time, and sustained effort. It cannot be sunk in the hour that thirst becomes unbearable. To begin digging only when already thirsty is not to solve the problem; it is to acknowledge defeat before starting.

## Extended Meaning
臨渇掘井 describes the failure to prepare in advance — making provisions only after the need has already arrived, when those provisions can no longer help. It is used as a warning or a charge against poor planning, against procrastination, against those who act only when forced to rather than anticipating what they will need.

The phrase applies to any domain where timely preparation is the difference between success and failure: medicine, governance, warfare, engineering, personal finance. In every case the logic is the same — the preparation required must precede the need it addresses, and scrambling at the last moment guarantees inadequacy.

## Source and Origin
The image appears most famously in **《黄帝内経·素問·四気調神大論》** (Yellow Emperor's Classic of Medicine, Plain Questions, Chapter on Regulating the Spirit According to the Four Seasons):

> 「聖人不治已病治未病，不治已亂治未亂。...夫病已成而後薬之，乱已成而後治之，譬猶渇而穿井，鬭而鑄錐，不亦晩乎！」
>
> "The sage does not treat disease already contracted — he treats disease before it arises; he does not govern disorder already present — he governs before disorder arises. ...To take medicine after disease has formed, to govern after disorder has broken out, is like drilling a well after you are already thirsty, or casting weapons only when battle has already started — is it not already too late?"

The parallel structure is deliberate: 渇而穿井 (thirsty, then drill a well) is paired with 鬭而鑄錐 (fighting, then cast weapons) to make the same point from two domains — medicine and war. Both express the principle that any adequate response must be prepared before, not during, the crisis it addresses.

The saying is also attributed to **《晏子春秋》** (Annals of Master Yan), the collection associated with Yan Ying (晏嬰, c. 578–500 BC), the renowned minister of Qi during the Spring and Autumn period. Yan Ying was celebrated precisely for his ability to anticipate problems and advise preemptive action — his attributed sayings are natural homes for proverbs about foresight.

## Cultural Notes
臨渇掘井 belongs to a family of Chinese proverbs about the importance of foresight — of acting before necessity forces your hand. Its closest sibling is [[亡羊補牢]] ("mend the sheep pen after the sheep are lost"), which describes a slightly different failure: 亡羊補牢 can be offered charitably as "better late than never," while 臨渇掘井 is purely critical. The well you start digging when already thirsty does not save you; it arrives too late to matter.

The 素問 context gives the proverb its most powerful frame: the book is a medical text, and its physician-authors are advocating for preventive medicine over reactive treatment. 不治已病治未病 — "treat disease before it arises" — is the principle; 臨渇掘井 is the reductio ad absurdum of the opposite approach. If you wait until the patient is already ill to investigate the cure, you are like a man who waits until he is parched to begin digging. The diagnosis may be complete well before the patient recovers.

The Japanese equivalent proverb is 渇して井を穿つ (かわいて ゐをうがつ) — literally the same image in kun-yomi, rendered as native Japanese rather than Chinese-origin compound words. The proverb is standard in both traditions and the image translates without loss.

Western parallels: "Don't dig your well after you're thirsty"; "Don't lock the stable after the horse has bolted"; the military maxim "train in peace, fight in war." The Roman *si vis pacem, para bellum* ("if you want peace, prepare for war") is the positive formulation of the same principle — preparation before need.

## Example Sentences
1. 《黄帝内経·素問》:「譬猶渇而穿井，鬭而鑄錐，不亦晩乎！」
   "Is it not already too late, like drilling a well after you are already thirsty, or casting weapons only when battle has started?"
2. 臨渇掘井では遅すぎる。災害に備えるなら、日頃から準備を怠らないことだ。
   "臨渇掘井 is too late. If you want to prepare for disaster, don't neglect preparation in ordinary times."
3. Starting cybersecurity training only after the breach is 臨渇掘井 — the well you begin then will not be ready before the next attack arrives.

## Sentiment
Critical and cautionary. 臨渇掘井 is deployed as a charge against inadequate foresight or as a warning to act before events force your hand. Its register is practical and urgent rather than philosophical — it does not counsel resignation to fate but demands action in advance of need. The tone is direct: this is how things go wrong, and there is no excuse for letting them.
