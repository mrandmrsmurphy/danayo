## Notes
- [未 (char)](characters/未%20(char).md) "before" + [雨 (char)](characters/雨%20(char).md) "rain" + [紬](characters/紬.md) "silk" + [謬](characters/謬.md) "bind"

**Literal meaning:** “Before the rain, bind and repair (the thatch).”  
**Figurative meaning:** To prepare in advance; to take precautions before trouble arises.

### Pronunciations
- **Mandarin:** wèi yǔ chóu móu (4–3–2–2)
- **Cantonese (Jyutping):** mei6 jyu5 cau4 mau4
- **Korean:** 미우주무
- **Japanese:** みうちゅうびゅう（未雨綢繆）_miu chūbyū_
- **Vietnamese (Hán-Việt):** vị vũ trù mưu

### Variants
- **Traditional:** 未雨綢繆
- **Simplified:** 未雨绸缪

- Sometimes shortened in modern usage to emphasize foresight.

### Source / Origin
The phrase comes from the **_Classic of Poetry_ (《詩經》)**, specifically **“Bin Feng: Chi Xiao” (《豳風·鴟鴞》, Poem 155)**.

The relevant lines describe a bird repairing its nest:

**迨天之未陰雨，徹彼桑土，綢繆牖戶。**  
“While the sky has not yet turned dark with rain,  
strip the mulberry bark and bind fast the windows and doors.”

The image is practical and concrete: before a storm arrives, one should **reinforce the nest or house**, securing openings against wind and rain.

From this scene, the expression **未雨綢繆** came to signify **foresight and preparation**, acting before danger or difficulty appears.

### Cultural Notes
- In **Chinese**, 未雨綢繆 is a standard expression for **prudence and advance planning**, often used in governance, education, and personal conduct.
- The idiom reflects a broader classical value: the wise person anticipates disorder and prepares beforehand.
- In **Japanese** and **Korean**, the phrase is understood in literary or formal contexts as emphasizing **preventive action and foresight**.

### Example Usage
**Mandarin:**  
企業應該未雨綢繆，以應對未來的風險。  
_Companies should prepare in advance to deal with future risks._

**Japanese:**  
問題が起こる前に未雨綢繆することが重要だ。  
_It is important to prepare before problems arise._

**Korean:**  
문제가 생기기 전에 미우주무의 자세가 필요하다。  
_A stance of preparing in advance is necessary before problems arise._

### Sentiment
> **“Before the rain falls, bind the house — foresight that prevents later trouble.”**
