[[Biblical Chengyu]]
### Pronunciation
- **Mandarin**: yáng yī è láng
- **Cantonese**: joeng4 ji1 ngo6 long4
- **Japanese (hiragana)**: よういがろう
- **Korean (Hangul)**: 양의아랑 (yang-ui-a-rang)
- **Vietnamese (Sino-Vietnamese)**: dương y ngạ lang

### Literal Meaning
- **羊** sheep
- **衣** clothing, garment
- **餓** hungry
- **狼** wolf

Literal: “A hungry wolf clothed in sheep’s garb.”

### Figurative Meaning
A person who outwardly appears gentle or harmless but inwardly harbors destructive intent. The addition of 餓 underscores predatory appetite rather than mere disguise.

### Scriptural Source
The idiom condenses **Matthew 7:15**:
> “Beware of false prophets, which come to you in sheep’s clothing, but inwardly they are ravening wolves.”

Greek: _λύκοι ἅρπαγες_ — “rapacious wolves.”

Traditional Chinese translations render the verse:
> “防備假先知…外面披著羊皮，裡面卻是殘暴的狼。”

The Dan'a'yo form intensifies the predatory dimension by using 餓 to echo the Greek sense of ravenous hunger.

### Example Usage
> 表面敬虔，內心詭詐，實乃羊衣餓狼。  
> Outwardly devout yet inwardly deceitful, he is a wolf in sheep’s clothing.


### Cultural Notes

The imagery belongs to pastoral life familiar in the ancient Near East. Sheep symbolize vulnerability and covenant community; wolves represent destructive intrusion.

In Christian theology, the warning concerns false teachers who imitate piety. The emphasis is not merely moral hypocrisy but doctrinal danger. The wolf’s disguise is strategic.

In East Asian literary culture, wolves already symbolize ferocity. The added pastoral layer introduces covenantal nuance. Within Dan'a'yo, the idiom is often applied to spiritual leadership, public morality, or ideological deception.

### Tone and Sentiment
Warning, vigilant, morally alert. It calls for discernment in judging character beyond appearances.
