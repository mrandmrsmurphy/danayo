## Literal Meaning
- [落](characters/落.md) — fall, drop; fallen
- [花](characters/花.md) — flower, blossom
- [流](characters/流.md) — flow, flowing
- [水 (char)](characters/水%20(char).md) — water

Literally: "Fallen blossoms, flowing water" — the image of petals that have left the branch and are now drifting on a current, carried wherever the water goes.

The image is compressed from a longer visual experience: spring blossoms falling, the stream below, the petals landing on the surface and being borne away. Two kinds of beauty — the transient flower and the ceaseless water — briefly joined in a moment that is already passing.

## Extended Meaning
落花流水 carries two emotionally distinct meanings that share the same visual source:

**Ruin and defeat** (the more common prose meaning): a scene of chaos, scattering, and total disarray — an army routed, a dynasty collapsed, a plan in pieces. The fallen petals and churning water suggest not gentle impermanence but violent disorder. Used of a defeated force: 打得敵人落花流水 — "beat the enemy until they scattered like fallen blossoms on water."

**Longing and parting** (the poetic meaning): the fallen blossom wishes to be carried by the water; the water carries the blossom away. Two beautiful, ephemeral things briefly in contact and then separated by the current of time. This reading appears in romantic contexts — two people drawn together and then parted by circumstance, or the feeling of spring passing and taking something irreplaceable with it.

The classical couplet that makes this emotional ambiguity explicit: 「落花有意，流水無情」 — "the fallen blossom has feeling; the flowing water has none." Here the flower *chooses* to fall toward the water (feeling, longing, intention), but the water flows on indifferently (time, fate, the beloved's heart). The blossom is carried away not because the water loves it but because the water cannot help flowing. This is the language of unrequited love.

落花流水 without the 有意/無情 qualifier can carry either sense — mutual longing if the context is romantic, ruin and scattering if the context is military or political.

## Source and Origin
The image of falling flowers and flowing water is among the oldest in Chinese poetry and saturates the 詩経, the Tang poets, and especially the Song dynasty ci (詞). The four-character compound crystallized in the **唐宋詩詞** tradition — Tang poetry and Song ci lyric — where 落花 and 流水 are among the most persistent paired images of impermanence, spring's passing, and irreversible loss.

The most celebrated poet associated with this imagery is **李煜 (Li Yu, 937–978)**, the last ruler of Southern Tang and one of the greatest ci lyricists. His poems return compulsively to falling flowers and flowing water as images of dynastic collapse, personal loss, and the irreversibility of time — feelings he lived out literally when his kingdom fell to Song forces in 975. His 《虞美人》 ends:

> 「問君能有幾多愁，恰似一江春水向東流。」
> "How much sorrow can you hold? Just like a whole river of spring water flowing east."

His 《相見歡》 opens:

> 「林花謝了春紅，太匆匆。無奈朝来寒雨，晚来風。」
> "The grove flowers have shed their spring red, too hastily — no help for it, morning cold rains, evening wind."

Li Yu did not invent 落花流水 as a four-character compound, but his emotional register — beauty, loss, the flowing away of what cannot be held — became the canonical context in which the phrase is felt.

In Japanese, らっかりゅうすい is used almost exclusively in the negative sense of chaotic rout or total defeat. The romantic nuance, which remains alive in Chinese and Korean poetry, has largely faded from Japanese usage.

## Cultural Notes
The 落花/流水 pairing recurs so persistently in classical East Asian poetry that it has become shorthand for a particular kind of feeling: the awareness that beauty is departing and cannot be called back. Cherry blossoms (桜, 桜花) in Japanese aesthetics, peach blossoms (桃花) in Chinese pastoral poetry, and spring blossoms generally are all variations on 落花 — the flower as the perfect but temporary. 流水 adds the dimension of time and movement: the water does not stop for the blossom; it carries it away and keeps flowing.

落花流水 sits within the broader aesthetic of 物の哀れ (mono no aware) in Japanese culture — the bittersweet awareness of impermanence — and of the Chinese aesthetic concept of 凄美 (qī měi, poignant beauty), where beauty and sadness are inseparable because what is most beautiful is also most fleeting.

The negative usage (rout, defeat, scattering) is related: the scene of fallen blossoms on churning water also looks like disorder and aftermath. The same image reads as lament or as ruin depending on whether you are watching spring or a battlefield.

In Korean, 낙화유수 is used in both senses and appears frequently in classical sijo (時調, lyric poetry) and in modern expressions of nostalgia or loss.

## Example Sentences
1. 「問君能有幾多愁，恰似一江春水向東流。」— 李煜《虞美人》
   "How much sorrow can you hold? Just like a whole river of spring water flowing east."
2. 味方の軍勢は落花流水のごとく敗走し、城は夕暮れ前に陥落した。
   "The allied forces fled in 落花流水 disorder, and the castle fell before dusk."
3. Spring had passed so quickly it felt like 落花流水 — she turned around and the petals were already on the water, already moving away.

## Sentiment
Dual. In poetic and romantic contexts, 落花流水 is melancholy and beautiful — the sadness of impermanence, of things beautiful and brief. In martial and political contexts it is unambiguously negative — total rout, chaotic collapse. The shared image holds both meanings without contradiction: fallen blossoms on water are simultaneously a scene of natural beauty and a scene of things hopelessly scattered and borne away.
