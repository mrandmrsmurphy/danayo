[Misc. Chengyu](chengyu/Misc.%20Chengyu.md)

**Literal meaning:** “Draw a snake and add feet.”  
**Extended sense:** To ruin something by unnecessary addition; to overdo a task and spoil an already complete result.

**Source:** From the **《戰國策》**, in an anecdote about a contest among retainers to draw a snake. The one who finished first began adding feet to his drawing while waiting for the others, only to lose when another participant completed a proper snake.

**Classical background:**  
The story is simple but precise. The contest rewarded speed and accuracy. A snake requires no feet. The first artist’s impulse to embellish turned success into failure. The moral does not condemn effort but misdirected effort. The work was already complete; improvement became distortion.

The narrative reflects a common theme in Warring States literature: the importance of appropriateness and restraint. The error lies not in ambition but in ignoring the natural limits of a thing.

**Modern usage:**  
Across the region, 画蛇添足 is widely used to criticize needless elaboration in writing, policy, craftsmanship, or everyday tasks. The tone is corrective rather than harsh, pointing out that simplicity would have been better than embellishment.
