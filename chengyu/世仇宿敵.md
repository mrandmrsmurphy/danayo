[[Biblical Chengyu]]
### Pronunciation
- **Mandarin**: shì chóu sù dí
- **Cantonese**: sai3 cau4 suk1 dik6
- **Japanese (hiragana)**: せいきゅうしゅくてき
- **Korean (Hangul)**: 세구수적 (se-gu-su-jeok)
- **Vietnamese (Sino-Vietnamese)**: thế cừu túc địch

Note: Both [[世仇]] and [[宿敵]] are established two-character compounds in CJK vocabulary, here joined into a four-character theological maxim.

### Literal Meaning
- **世仇** generational feud, age-long enmity
- **宿敵** sworn or longstanding enemy

Literal: “An age-long feud, a sworn ancient enemy.”

### Figurative Meaning
A compressed expression of the primordial enmity announced in Genesis 3:15. It denotes the enduring hostility between the serpent and the seed of the woman, often interpreted in Christian theology as the conflict between Satan and the redemptive line culminating in Christ.

### Scriptural Source
The phrase reflects **Genesis 3:15**:
> “And I will put enmity between thee and the woman, and between thy seed and her seed…”

Hebrew:  
_וְאֵיבָה אָשִׁית בֵּינְךָ וּבֵין הָאִשָּׁה_  
“I will put enmity between you and the woman.”

Traditional Chinese translations typically render:
> “我又要叫你和女人彼此為仇…”

The Dan'a'yo formulation intensifies the temporal dimension of that enmity. The hostility is not momentary but historical and transgenerational.

### Theological Scope
Genesis 3:15 has long been called the _protoevangelium_, the first announcement of the gospel. The enmity introduced is judicial and redemptive:
- It prevents total alliance between humanity and the serpent.
- It establishes a conflict that unfolds across history.
- It culminates in the bruising of the serpent’s head.

世仇 captures generational continuity. 宿敵 conveys deep-rooted, almost primordial opposition. Together they frame redemptive history as structured conflict.

### Example Usage
> 自墮落以來，世仇宿敵未嘗止息，直至應許之子得勝。  
> Since the Fall, the age-long enmity has not ceased, until the promised Son prevails.

### Cultural Notes
In CJK literature, 世仇 often refers to clan feuds transmitted across generations. 宿敵 suggests an adversary whose hostility is embedded in history. By appropriating these terms, Dan'a'yo recasts salvation history in epic moral terms.

The phrase must be interpreted carefully. The biblical text does not depict two equal cosmic principles in dualistic balance. The enmity is established by God and ordered toward eventual victory.

Within Dan'a'yo theology, 世仇宿敵 signals both conflict and promise. It frames history not as cyclical inevitability but as directed struggle leading toward decisive resolution.

### Tone and Sentiment
Epic, adversarial, anticipatory. It conveys long-standing hostility while implying eventual triumph.
