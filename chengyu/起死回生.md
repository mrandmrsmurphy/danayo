- [起 (char)](characters/起%20(char).md) "raise" + [死](characters/死.md) "dead" + [回 (char)](characters/回%20(char).md) "return" + [生](characters/生.md) "life"

**Literal meaning:** “Raise the dead and bring them back to life.”  
**Figurative meaning:** To rescue a situation from ruin; to revive something that seemed hopeless.

### Pronunciations
- **Mandarin:** qǐ sǐ huí shēng (3–3–2–1)
- **Cantonese (Jyutping):** hei2 sei2 wui4 saang1
- **Korean:** 기사회생
- **Japanese:** きしかいせい（起死回生）_kishi kaisei_
- **Vietnamese (Hán-Việt):** khởi tử hồi sinh    

### Variants
- **Traditional:** 起死回生
- **Simplified:** 起死回生
- Sometimes expanded in classical contexts as **起死人而肉白骨** (“raise the dead and give flesh to white bones”).

### Source / Origin
The imagery originates in **classical Chinese medical and philosophical writing**, where extraordinary physicians were praised for their ability to **save a patient who seemed certain to die**.

The broader metaphor appears in early historical literature, including passages in the **_Records of the Grand Historian_ (《史記》)** describing miraculous healing or dramatic recovery. The phrase later became a standard metaphor for **restoring life to something thought lost**.

In literary usage, the expression came to refer not only to medicine but to **any action that reverses a desperate situation**.

### Cultural Notes
- In **Chinese**, 起死回生 often refers to **saving a failing project, business, or plan**.
- In **Japanese**, the idiom is common in sports and drama, describing a **dramatic comeback or last-minute recovery**.
- In **Korean**, **기사회생** similarly refers to **escaping certain defeat and recovering success**.

### Example Usage
**Mandarin:**  
這個新策略讓公司起死回生。  
_This new strategy brought the company back from the brink of collapse._

**Japanese:**  
彼の一打がチームを起死回生させた。  
_His hit revived the team from near defeat._

**Korean:**  
그의 결정이 회사를 기사회생시켰다。  
_His decision brought the company back from the brink._

### Sentiment
> **“To revive what seemed already dead — a dramatic return from the edge of ruin.”**
