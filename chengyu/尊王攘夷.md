## Literal Meaning
- [尊](../characters/尊.md) — to revere; to honor; to elevate
- [王 (char)](../characters/王%20(char).md) — king; the king (specifically the Zhou king)
- [攘](../characters/攘.md) — to push aside; to repel; to expel
- [夷](../characters/夷.md) — barbarians; the Yi; non-Chinese peoples

Literally: "Revere the king; expel the barbarians."

The phrase is a political couplet in miniature: two imperative clauses, each two characters, defining a stance toward two opposing directions — the center (the king, the legitimate sovereign) and the periphery (the barbarians, the outsiders). 尊王 is a gesture of loyalty inward; 攘夷 is a gesture of rejection outward. Together they define a complete political identity: where one stands, and against whom.

## Extended Meaning

A political doctrine — not a metaphor — asserting two principles simultaneously: loyalty to the legitimate sovereign (uphold the king's authority; do not let it be undermined by regional warlords or rivals) and rejection of foreign or peripheral forces (expel the barbarians; do not let outside powers corrupt or occupy the realm).

In Chinese political philosophy, the phrase captures the classic Confucian two-front agenda: restoring the center against centrifugal fragmentation, and protecting the civilization against external encroachment. In Japanese history, it became the single most important political slogan of the 19th century.

The phrase can describe:
- A political movement combining nationalist loyalty with xenophobia
- A foreign policy stance that combines deference to established authority with aggressive rejection of outside influence
- Any political program defined by its double axis: who to honor and who to expel
- Historically, the specific doctrines of the Spring and Autumn hegemony system and the Japanese Bakumatsu movement

## Source and Origin

The phrase crystallizes from Spring and Autumn Annals discourse, specifically the **《春秋公羊傳》** (*Spring and Autumn Annals, Gongyang Commentary*), the commentary tradition attributed to **公羊高 (Gongyang Gao)** and his school, transmitted orally and committed to writing c. 3rd–2nd century BCE.

The historical occasion: **Duke Huan of Qi (齊桓公, r. 685–643 BCE)**, the first of the Five Hegemons (五霸) of the Spring and Autumn period, assembled the feudal lords in a series of covenants and led a coalition that: (1) maintained the formal authority of the Zhou king, whose power was declining, and (2) repelled incursions from the Rong and Di barbarian peoples pressing on the northern and western borders of the Chinese states.

**《春秋公羊傳》:**
> 葵丘之盟，尊周室，攘夷狄，禁暴扶弱，德之盛也。
> *"At the Covenant of Kuiqiu, [Duke Huan] honored the Zhou royal house, repelled the Yi and Di barbarians, suppressed the violent and supported the weak — this was virtue at its height."*

The Gongyang Commentary elevated Duke Huan's accomplishments into a theoretical statement: the legitimate hegemon acts as the protector of both the Zhou king's ritual authority (尊王) and the civilization's integrity against external pressure (攘夷). 孔子 (Confucius) himself endorsed this legacy:

**《論語·憲問》:**
> 微管仲，吾其被髮左衽矣。
> *"Without Guan Zhong [Duke Huan's minister], we would be wearing our hair unbound and our robes fastened to the left — that is, we would have become barbarians."*

The Confucian endorsement gave 尊王攘夷 its philosophical weight as a doctrine of civilizational self-preservation alongside political loyalty. The four-character compound as a fixed phrase crystallized through repeated citation of the Gongyang tradition in Han and later Confucian discourse.

**The Japanese transformation**: The phrase's greatest historical impact came in 19th-century Japan, where **尊王攘夷** (sonnō jōi) became the rallying slogan of the political movement that challenged Tokugawa shogunal authority and demanded the expulsion of Western powers following Commodore Perry's forced opening of Japan in 1853. The two principles mapped onto Japanese conditions: 尊王 became "revere the emperor" (calling for the restoration of imperial rule over the Tokugawa shogunate), and 攘夷 became "expel the barbarians" (the Western powers and their treaties). The movement, driven by lower-ranking samurai (志士, shishi) from domains like Satsuma and Chōshū, produced the Meiji Restoration of 1868 — which paradoxically ended by abandoning 攘夷 (Japan rapidly Westernized) while fulfilling 尊王 (imperial rule was restored). The phrase is inseparable from this history in Japanese political consciousness.

## Standard Form

The Dan'a'yo canonical character is **攘** (娘母陽韻, nyang), with 㐮 and 襄 as aliases. The vault filename retains 㐮 for legacy reasons. Variant forms in circulation:

- **尊王攘夷** — standard Chinese form; also the Dan'a'yo reading (攘 = 냥/nyang)
- **尊皇攘夷** — Japanese variant (皇, "emperor," for 王, "king") — reflects the Japanese context where the sovereign is the emperor, not a feudal king; this is the form most commonly seen in Japanese history texts
- **尊皇𭢯夷** — rare orthographic variant

The distinction between 王 (king) and 皇 (emperor) reflects different political contexts: 王 is the original Chinese form (the Zhou king was 王, not 皇); 皇 is the Japanese adaptation, appropriate since Japan's sovereign has always been 天皇 (emperor), not 王.

## Pronunciations
- **Mandarin (Pinyin):** zūn wáng rǎng yí (1–2–3–2)
- **Cantonese (Jyutping):** zyun1 wong4 joeng4 ji4
- **Japanese (on-yomi, hiragana):** そんのうじょうい (sonnō jōi)
- **Korean (Sino-Korean):** 존왕양이 (jon-wang-yang-i)
- **Vietnamese (Hán–Việt):** tôn vương nhưỡng di

**Note on Japanese pronunciation**: そんのう (sonnō) reflects the Japanese on-yomi of 尊王 — 尊 (son) + 王 (nō, with voicing and vowel lengthening). じょうい (jōi) reflects 攘夷 — 攘 (jō, from Middle Chinese via Sino-Japanese) + 夷 (i). The full reading そんのうじょうい (sonnō jōi) is one of the most recognizable political phrases in Japanese history.

## Cultural Notes

**In Chinese classical culture**, 尊王攘夷 names the political theory of the Spring and Autumn hegemonic system — the idea that a strong feudal lord could serve the failing Zhou dynasty by acting as its protector and enforcer. It is a term of historical analysis and Confucian political philosophy, appearing in commentaries on the Spring and Autumn Annals, in histories of the Warring States period, and in discussions of Chinese political theory. In modern China, the phrase appears in historical commentary and occasionally in discussions of sovereignty and territorial integrity, though it carries a distinctly archaic register.

**In Japan**, そんのうじょうい (sonnō jōi) is not archaic at all — it is one of the most historically charged phrases in the Japanese language, permanently associated with the Bakumatsu period (幕末, 1853–1868) and the political ferment that produced the Meiji Restoration. The phrase conjures the image of radical samurai loyalists (尊攘派, sonnō-jōi-ha), the assassinations and terrorism of the 1860s, the clash between the shogunate and the imperial court, and the eventual transformation of Japan into a modern nation-state. The paradox that the movement ended by eagerly embracing the very Western civilization it had pledged to expel is one of the great ironies of modern Japanese history. Every educated Japanese person knows this phrase and its history.

**In Korean**, 존왕양이 appears in historical scholarship on East Asian political thought and in discussions of the 19th-century East Asian response to Western imperialism. Korea's own 위정척사 (Wijeong Cheoksa, "Defend Orthodox, Reject Heterodoxy") movement of the same era shares much of the same intellectual structure — loyalty to Confucian orthodoxy combined with rejection of Western and Japanese intrusion — giving the Korean reading of 尊王攘夷 particular resonance.

## Example Sentences

1. **Classical (《春秋公羊傳》, c. 3rd–2nd century BCE):**
   葵丘之盟，尊周室，攘夷狄，禁暴扶弱，德之盛也。
   *"At the Covenant of Kuiqiu, [Duke Huan] honored the Zhou royal house, repelled the Yi and Di barbarians, suppressed the violent and supported the weak — this was virtue at its height."*

2. **Confucius on Guan Zhong (《論語·憲問》):**
   微管仲，吾其被髮左衽矣。
   *"Without Guan Zhong, we would be wearing our hair unbound and our robes fastened to the left — we would have become barbarians."*

3. **Modern Japanese (historical context):**
   尊王攘夷を掲げた志士たちは、やがて開国・文明開化の旗手へと変貌を遂げた。
   *"The shishi who had raised the banner of sonnō jōi eventually transformed into the standard-bearers of opening the country and civilizing reform."*

4. **English:**
   The pamphlet demanded both loyalty to the throne and the expulsion of every foreign concession — the classic double logic of 尊王攘夷, applied to a new century and a new empire.

## Sentiment

**Politically charged and historically weighty** — 尊王攘夷 is not a neutral description but a political declaration. In its original context it defined a moral stance; in its Japanese context it defined a historical movement. The phrase carries the full weight of two periods of political upheaval — the Spring and Autumn fragmentation of the Chinese world and the Meiji Restoration of Japan — and cannot be read as merely descriptive.

**Elevated and historical in register** across all CJKV languages. This is the vocabulary of political manifestos, historical analysis, and ideological debate — never casual speech. In Japanese especially, it evokes the entire atmosphere of the Bakumatsu period, its violence, its idealism, and its paradoxical resolution.
