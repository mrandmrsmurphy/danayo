## Literal Meaning
- [魑](../characters/魑.md) — mountain demon
- [魅](../characters/魅.md) — charm, bewitching spirit
- [罔](../characters/罔.md) — used here as Dan'a'yo's stand-in grapheme for 魍 (water/marsh demon), not its own primary sense "accuse"
- [両](../characters/両%20(char).md) — used here as Dan'a'yo's stand-in grapheme for 魎 (monster, sprite), not its own primary sense "both"

Literally: "Mountain and water spirits — demons and goblins." (魑魅 haunt mountains and forests; 魍魎, written here as 罔両, are spirits of water and mist.)

## Extended Meaning
Evil, deceitful, or malevolent forces; wicked people who sow confusion and corruption; all manner of dark, unseen powers. Always negative. Used both for actual supernatural beings (in folklore and horror contexts) and, more commonly today, figuratively for corrupt or scheming people.

## Source and Origin
The earliest reference is in the *Classic of Mountains and Seas* (《山海經》) and *Zuo Zhuan* (《左傳·宣公三年》), where these names describe specific nature spirits inhabiting mountains, rivers, and marshes:

> 山林川谷，丘陵坻障，魑魅魍魎，莫能逢之。
> In the mountains and rivers, hills and marshes dwell the demons and spirits — invisible and innumerable.

In ancient Chinese cosmology, every natural feature had its own spiritual counterpart: 魑魅 were said to haunt mountains and forests, 魍魎 the spirits of water and mist. Over time the four names became a fixed collocation meaning "all the unclean spirits," and later, metaphorically, "all corrupt or evil beings." By the Tang–Song period the idiom was already used to dramatize moral chaos or supernatural horror as much as literal spirits — Confucian political writing repurposed it for wicked courtiers and flatterers who "bewitch the ruler":

> 除去魑魅魍魎，使天下清明。
> Expel the demons and monsters, and the world will be pure again.

## Standard Form
**魑魅魍魎** (traditional) / **魑魅魍魉** (simplified). Dan'a'yo uses 罔/両 as simplified stand-in graphemes for the rarer 魍/魎. Sometimes shortened to just 魑魅 ("evil spirits") or 魍魎 ("monsters of the waters").

## Cultural Notes
In shamanic and Daoist belief, these spirits symbolized nature's hidden dangers — unseen energies causing disease or delusion; in Confucian political ethics the term shifted to describe wicked courtiers and flatterers. By the Tang–Song period it was used as much to dramatize moral chaos as literal supernatural horror. From a cosmological view the idiom symbolizes the dark underside of the Dao; from a moral view, the corruption of human hearts — "demons within" that must be expelled to restore harmony.

Usage across the sphere stays close to this dual literal/figurative range. In Chinese, it's figurative for corruption, deceit, or malicious intrigue: 要肅清政壇上的魑魅魍魎 ("We must purge the political scene of its demons and monsters"). In Japanese, 魑魅魍魎 (chimi mōryō) is extremely common in folklore, yōkai literature, and moral metaphor generally — "all manner of ghosts and monsters." In Korean, 치매망량 survives mainly in classical/literary use rather than everyday speech. In Vietnamese, *si mị vọng lượng* appears specifically in translations of the *Shan Hai Jing* or *Zuo Zhuan*, tied closely to its literal nature-spirit sense rather than the broader figurative use seen elsewhere.

Related, but distinct, idioms in the same "wicked beings" space: 妖魔鬼怪 ("demons, devils, and ghosts"), 牛鬼蛇神 ("monstrous beings and snakes," a metaphor for vile persons), and 正氣凜然 ("filled with righteous energy," the thing that dispels demons like these).

## Example Sentences
1. 世間魑魅魍魎，唯正道可鎮。
   "Among all the demons of the world, only the righteous Way can subdue them."
2. 政治の世界には魑魅魍魎がうごめく。
   "In the world of politics, monsters and demons writhe about."
3. 세상에는 치매망량이 가득하다.
   "The world is full of deceitful demons."
4. Trần thế đầy si mị vọng lượng, chỉ có chính đạo mới trấn áp được.
   "The world is filled with evil spirits; only the true Way can subdue them."

## Sentiment
All the lurking demons — the unseen powers of corruption, deceit, and chaos. Strongly negative; can be applied with real fear (folklore/horror register) or as pointed political condemnation.
