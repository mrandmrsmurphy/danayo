# 國士無雙 (国士无双)

## Literal Meaning
- [国](../characters/国.md) — nation
- [士](../characters/士%20(char).md) — scholar, gentleman
- [無](../characters/無%20(char).md) — lack, without
- [双](../characters/双%20(char).md) — pair, match

Literally: "A nation's scholar without a match."

## Extended Meaning
A peerless talent — someone of extraordinary ability, moral stature, or irreplaceable character, one of a kind in the nation. Used to praise an individual, not a group or an era; always admiring, never ironic in classical usage (though modern Vietnamese occasionally deploys it ironically — see Cultural Notes). Applies across domains: politics, scholarship, martial skill, art, or virtue generally.

## Source and Origin
The idiom originates from the *Records of the Grand Historian* (《史記·平原君虞卿列傳》) by Sima Qian (司馬遷, 1st century BC).

> 平原君曰：「士之有天下國士，曰毛遂也。」
> 毛遂曰：「臣聞之：國士無雙。」
> Lord Pingyuan said, "Among the scholars of the realm, there is none like Mao Sui." Mao Sui replied, "I have heard it said: a true national hero is without equal."

Mao Sui (毛遂), an unrecognized retainer of Lord Pingyuan (平原君) during the Warring States period, boldly volunteered to accompany a diplomatic mission. His courage and eloquence secured the alliance of Chu, saving the state of Zhao. His story exemplifies the self-made hero who emerges in crisis, and the idiom crystallized as praise for exactly that kind of unrecognized, then-proven talent.

## Standard Form
**國士無雙** (traditional) / **国士无双** (simplified) — both forms current.

Distinct from (not a variant of) several other idioms praising exceptional people: 一代英豪 ("a hero of an age"), 絶世無雙 ("unparalleled in the world"), 人中龍鳳 ("a dragon or phoenix among men"), and 英雄蓋世 ("a hero who overshadows the world"). A poetic inversion, 無雙國士, is occasionally seen in verse or eulogy.

## Cultural Notes
國士無雙 expresses the Confucian ideal of individual virtue and talent in service of the state. A 國士 ("man of the nation") was not merely a warrior or scholar, but someone whose extraordinary ability and integrity could change a country's fate — the *junzi* (君子) acting with courage and righteousness (義) even without recognition, embodying loyalty (忠) and moral independence. Over time the idiom broadened from a literal description of a state hero into a metaphor for peerless excellence in any field.

Usage across the sphere: in modern Chinese it praises extraordinary ability or moral stature. In Japanese, こくしむそう is widely used well beyond historical writing — samurai epics, manga, and other pop-culture genres apply it freely to any unmatched warrior or genius. Korean 국사무쌍 mostly keeps its classical, historical-or-ceremonial register. Vietnamese *quốc sĩ vô song* appears in historical writing and modern essays, sometimes used ironically to note how rarely anyone actually earns the title.

## Example Sentences
1. 韓信之智勇，古今國士無雙。
   "The wit and valor of Han Xin — unmatched among heroes of all ages."
2. 国士無双の剣士が、乱世に現れた。
   "A peerless swordsman appeared amid the age of chaos."
3. 그는 국사무쌍의 인물이었다.
   "He was a man without equal in the nation."
4. Trong lịch sử, hiếm ai được gọi là "quốc sĩ vô song".
   "In history, few have truly earned the title of 'a peerless hero.'"

## Sentiment
Strongly admiring; a superlative reserved for genuinely rare talent or character. Formal-to-literary register in every language; in Japanese pop culture it can shade slightly more casual, but the core sense of "unmatched" never weakens.
