## Literal Meaning
- [大 (char)](../characters/大%20(char).md) — large; great; major
- [同](../characters/同.md) — same; alike; together
- [小 (char)](../characters/小%20(char).md) — small; minor; lesser
- [異](../characters/異.md) — different; divergent; other

Literally: "Largely the same, differing in small ways."

The grammar is a tight parallel: 大同 (great sameness) / 小異 (small difference) — the two halves set against each other in four characters. What is shared is large; what differs is small. The structure implies a judgment: the differences, whatever they are, do not unseat the underlying identity.

## Extended Meaning

Describes two or more things that are fundamentally alike while differing in details — the shared core is substantial, the variations peripheral. Used to note that apparent differences are superficial, that two options or positions amount to roughly the same thing, or that family resemblance outweighs individual variation.

The idiom can describe:
- Two proposals, policies, or plans that are effectively equivalent despite surface variation
- Two texts, translations, or accounts that tell the same story with minor discrepancies
- Two products, methods, or approaches where the practical outcome is the same
- Regional dialects, local customs, or stylistic schools that share more than they differ

The tone is often gently dismissive of the differences: to say two things are 大同小異 is to suggest that whoever is dwelling on the distinctions is missing the larger picture. The differences are real but minor; the sameness is what matters. This can be pragmatic (don't be distracted by irrelevant variation) or slightly reductive (the distinctions you care about are not as significant as you think).

## Source and Origin

The phrase originates in the **《莊子·天下》** (*Zhuangzi*, "Tianxia" chapter, "All Under Heaven"), the final and most encyclopedic chapter of the *Zhuangzi*, which surveys the major schools of Warring States thought and their relationship to the Way. The chapter engages extensively with the **School of Names (名家, Mingjia)**, particularly with the logician **惠施 (Huishi, c. 370–310 BCE)** and his famous paradoxes about similarity, difference, and classification.

In the course of this discussion, the *Zhuangzi* distinguishes between different orders of resemblance:

**《莊子·天下》:**
> 大同而與小同異，此之謂小同異；萬物畢同畢異，此之謂大同異。
> *"Great sameness differs from small sameness — this is called 'minor difference within sameness.' All things being entirely alike or entirely different — this is called 'great difference within sameness.'"*

This passage is part of Zhuangzi's engagement with the logical paradoxes of the School of Names. The point being made is philosophical: the concept of "sameness" itself has gradations — a great sameness (大同) and a small sameness (小同) are both kinds of similarity, but they differ from each other (大同而與小同異). This is the "minor difference within sameness" (小同異): two things can be broadly alike while differing in particulars.

The condensed four-character idiom 大同小異 extracts from this philosophical apparatus its practical core: large similarity plus small difference. The Zhuangist paradox about the ontology of resemblance became, in ordinary usage, a convenient shorthand for "basically the same thing."

The phrase passed readily into the common literary and then everyday vocabulary of Chinese, Japanese, and Korean, losing the Daoist philosophical scaffolding but retaining the precise descriptive structure: large sameness outweighing small difference.

## Standard Form

The Dan'a'yo form **大同小異** is identical to the traditional Chinese form. The simplified Chinese form differs only in the last character:

- **大同小異** — traditional Chinese, Japanese (= Dan'a'yo form)
- **大同小异** — simplified Chinese (异 for 異)

大, 同, 小 are stable across all scripts.

## Pronunciations
- **Mandarin (Pinyin):** dà tóng xiǎo yì (4–2–3–4)
- **Cantonese (Jyutping):** daai6 tung4 siu2 ji6
- **Japanese (on-yomi, hiragana):** だいどうしょうい (daidōshōi)
- **Korean (Sino-Korean):** 대동소이 (dae-dong-so-i)
- **Vietnamese (Hán–Việt):** đại đồng tiểu dị

**Note**: 大同 (great harmony / universal commonwealth) is a separate and important concept in Confucian utopianism — the ideal social order described in 《禮記·禮運》where all people live in harmonious community. The 大同 in 大同小異 is unrelated, but a Chinese reader aware of the Confucian 大同 ideal will register the character combination with a slight double resonance.

## Cultural Notes

**In Chinese**, 大同小異 is a fully naturalized, high-frequency idiom available at every register from academic writing to casual conversation. It functions almost adverbially in modern Chinese — 兩者大同小異 ("the two are broadly the same") is a natural sentence in speech and writing alike. The idiom is especially common in comparative contexts: product reviews, policy analysis, translation criticism, and any discussion of regional or typological variation. It is one of the *yìwèi* (意味) chengyu that has completely shed its philosophical origin and operates as pure descriptive vocabulary.

**In Japanese**, だいどうしょうい (daidōshōi) is a recognized *yojijukugo* (四字熟語), listed in standard dictionaries and appearing in formal writing, journalism, and literary comparison. It is more formal than its Chinese equivalent — everyday Japanese is more likely to use similar expressions like ほぼ同じ (hotobondo onaji, "almost the same") in casual speech, but the four-character idiom appears naturally in written contexts without feeling archaic.

**In Korean**, 대동소이 (dae-dong-so-i) is one of the most commonly used four-character Sino-Korean compounds in everyday life — arguably more frequent in natural Korean speech than its Chinese or Japanese equivalents in their respective languages. Korean speakers reach for 대동소이 readily in conversation and journalism. Its position as a common-register expression (rather than a formal or archaic one) is notable: 대동소이 has fully entered the living vocabulary without the elevated associations that formal Sino-Korean often carries. This wide usage reflects the depth to which Sino-Korean compounds penetrated the Korean lexicon at all social levels.

## Example Sentences

1. **Classical (《莊子·天下》, c. 4th–3rd century BCE):**
   大同而與小同異，此之謂小同異。
   *"Great sameness differs from small sameness — this is called 'minor difference within sameness.'"*

2. **Modern Chinese:**
   這兩款手機功能大同小異，選哪一款主要取決於品牌偏好。
   *"The features of these two phones are broadly the same with minor differences — which one to choose mainly comes down to brand preference."*

3. **Modern Korean:**
   두 후보의 공약은 대동소이해서 유권자들이 차이를 느끼기 어렵다.
   *"The two candidates' pledges are essentially alike, making it hard for voters to sense any difference."*

4. **English:**
   Every regional dialect the team surveyed was, in the end, 大同小異 — the surface variation was real, but it concealed a common underlying grammar.

## Sentiment

**Neutral to mildly dismissive** — 大同小異 is not a compliment or an insult but an observation about relative magnitude. It says: the differences you might be focused on are smaller than the similarities you might be overlooking. In a debate, this can be conciliatory (let's not fight over small things) or deflating (your position is not as distinct as you think).

**Common and versatile in register** — one of the most practically useful four-character idioms, at home in academic comparison, everyday conversation, product reviews, and political commentary, with no particular ceremonial or emotional weight.
