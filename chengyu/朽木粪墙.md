## Literal Meaning
- [朽](../characters/朽.md) — rotten; decayed; crumbling
- [木 (char)](../characters/木%20(char).md) — wood; timber
- [糞 (char)](../characters/糞%20(char).md) — dung; filth; manure
- [牆](../characters/牆.md) — wall

Literally: "Rotten wood and a dung wall."

The two images are drawn directly from Confucius's rebuke in the Analects: 朽木 (rotten wood) is wood so far gone that a carver's chisel cannot bite into it — it splinters and crumbles rather than yielding to skill. 糞土之牆 (a wall of dung and earth) is a wall so poorly composed that a plasterer's trowel cannot smooth it — the material itself rejects the finishing work. The condensed idiom 朽木糞牆 strips away the surrounding sentence and preserves only the two defining images of incorrigible, hopeless material.

## Extended Meaning

A metaphor for a person who is beyond instruction, beyond cultivation, beyond improvement — someone in whom no effort of teaching can produce results. Where a skilled craftsman is defeated not by lack of skill but by the worthlessness of the material, a good teacher is defeated not by lack of patience or method but by the irredeemable hopelessness of the student. 朽木糞牆 names this condition: the fault lies not in the teacher, but in the material itself.

The idiom can describe:
- A student who refuses to learn and cannot be reached by any method
- A person of persistently corrupt or lazy character who ignores instruction
- An institution or practice so degraded that reform is futile
- A situation beyond repair — used rarely but with great force for inanimate objects or abstract systems

The phrase carries an undertone of resigned condemnation rather than active anger: the teacher (or reformer) has given up not in frustration but in recognition of a fundamental limit. This is not a passing criticism but a verdict — one of the sharpest in the classical repertoire.

## Source and Origin

The idiom originates in one of the most famous passages of the **《論語·公冶長》** (*Analects of Confucius*, Chapter 5, "Gong Ye Chang"), compiled by Confucius's disciples and later editors, c. 5th–4th century BCE.

The occasion: Confucius's student **宰予 (Zai Yu)**, also called Zai Wo, was found sleeping during the day — behavior considered deeply shameful in a period when students were expected to be in a constant state of learning and self-cultivation. Confucius responded with an outburst uncharacteristic for a teacher celebrated for patience:

**《論語·公冶長》5.10:**
> 宰予晝寢。子曰：「朽木不可雕也，糞土之牆，不可杇也；於予與何誅！」
> *"Zai Yu slept during the day. The Master said: 'Rotten wood cannot be carved; a wall of dung and earth cannot be plastered. As for Zai Yu — what is the use of reproving him?'"*

The phrase 何誅 — "what is the use of reproving him?" — is the pivot: Confucius is not threatening punishment but withdrawing the effort of teaching entirely. He has concluded that reproach itself is pointless, because the material (Zai Yu) cannot receive or benefit from it. This is the deepest possible condemnation a Confucian teacher can deliver.

The passage continues with a remark that became equally famous as an epistemological observation:
> 子曰：「始吾於人也，聽其言而信其行；今吾於人也，聽其言而觀其行。於予與改是。」
> *"The Master said: 'Formerly, when I heard a man's words, I trusted in his conduct. Now, when I hear a man's words, I observe his conduct. It is from Zai Yu that I have learned to make this change.'"*

The Zai Yu incident thus carries a double significance: it gave the language the metaphor 朽木糞牆 for an unteachable person, and it gave moral philosophy a canonical moment of updating one's epistemology in response to a concrete failure of trust.

The four-character condensation 朽木糞牆 (or 朽木粪墙 in simplified Chinese) strips the full sentence down to its two central images, making them portable as a standalone phrase of condemnation. The original Confucian context — its source in the most revered philosophical text in East Asian tradition — gives the condensed idiom a weight that far exceeds its four characters.

## Standard Form

The original Confucian text uses 糞土之牆 (a wall of dung-and-earth), with 土 (earth) alongside 糞. The four-character condensation drops 土 and combines 糞 directly with 牆, yielding 朽木糞牆 — the traditional Chinese and Dan'a'yo form.

- **朽木糞牆** — traditional Chinese (= Dan'a'yo form; 糞 and 牆)
- **朽木粪墙** — simplified Chinese (粪 for 糞, 墙 for 牆)

## Cultural Notes

**In Chinese**, 朽木粪墙 / 朽木糞牆 is one of the sharpest insults available in the classical register — its weight comes entirely from its Confucian source. To call someone 朽木粪墙 in a Chinese context invokes the moment when the most patient teacher in the tradition gave up on a student entirely. It is not casual speech; its classical register means it appears mainly in literary, historical, or deliberately elevated contexts — a sermon, an editorial, a biographical judgment. The related five-character phrase 朽木不可雕 (rotten wood cannot be carved) is somewhat more commonly quoted than the four-character form, being closer to Confucius's actual words.

**In Japanese**, the full idiom きゅうぼくふんしょう is encountered primarily in classical Chinese studies and literary contexts — commentary on the Analects, historical fiction set in the Chinese classical world, and the occasional elevated condemnation in formal prose. The phrase 朽木は彫るべからず ("rotten wood cannot be carved," a direct translation of Confucius's words) is a more naturally Japanese rendering and carries the same meaning with perhaps slightly broader currency. Neither form is everyday Japanese vocabulary.

**In Korean**, 후목분장 is Sino-Korean literary vocabulary, appearing in commentary on the 논어 (Analects) and in formal moral discourse. The Analects have been central to Korean Confucian culture for over a millennium; educated Korean readers recognize the reference even when the full four-character compound is not in active use.

The broader Confucian context in which this idiom lives — the ideal of tireless, patient instruction, and the devastation of encountering material that defeats even that patience — gives the phrase its moral force across all three cultures. It is not merely an insult; it is a philosophical verdict.

## Example Sentences

1. **Classical (《論語·公冶長》5.10, c. 5th–4th century BCE):**
   朽木不可雕也，糞土之牆，不可杇也；於予與何誅！
   *"Rotten wood cannot be carved; a wall of dung and earth cannot be plastered. As for Zai Yu — what is the use of reproving him?"*

2. **Modern Chinese:**
   這學生屢教不改，真是朽木糞牆，再苦口婆心也是枉然。
   *"This student refuses to change no matter how many times he is taught — he is truly rotten wood and a dung wall; all the patient counsel in the world would be wasted."*

3. **Modern Japanese:**
   何度指導しても改めようとしない彼を見て、師は朽木は彫るべからず、と嘆息した。
   *"Watching him refuse to change no matter how many times he was guided, the teacher sighed: 'Rotten wood cannot be carved.'"*

4. **English:**
   After three years of patient instruction that produced no change whatsoever, the old scholar set down his brush and said only: 朽木糞牆 — rotten wood and a dung wall. Nothing more needed to be said.

## Sentiment

**Strongly negative** — one of the most damning verdicts in the classical Chinese vocabulary. The phrase does not express active anger but something worse: the calm, final recognition that further effort is pointless. The Confucian frame makes it especially cutting: this is the judgment of a teacher who has exhausted patience, who no longer reproaches because reproach itself has been made irrelevant by the hopelessness of the subject.

**Elevated and classical in register.** Not an everyday insult — its classical source and literary weight mean it is reserved for deliberate, formal condemnation. To deploy it casually would misunderstand its gravity; to deploy it accurately is to make the hardest possible judgment in the tradition of Confucian moral discourse.
