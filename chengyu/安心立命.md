## Literal Meaning
- [安](../characters/安.md) — peaceful; settled; at ease; to bring to rest
- [心 (char)](../characters/心%20(char).md) — heart; mind; the locus of thought, feeling, and will
- [立 (char)](../characters/立%20(char).md) — to stand; to establish; to take a firm stance
- [命](../characters/命.md) — fate; life; mandate; Heaven's decree

Literally: "Bring the mind to peace; take a stance in fate."

安心 and 立命 are two classical binomes that describe two aspects of the same orientation. 安心 (settle the heart) means not merely to feel calm but to have brought the mind to a state of genuine, founded rest — a peace that is not disrupted by circumstance because it does not depend on circumstance. 立命 (stand firm in fate) means not merely to accept what happens but to establish oneself within the framework of fate or Heaven's mandate — to take a stable position in relation to what is unchangeable, and to act from that stability rather than against it.

The compound describes an achieved condition, not a momentary feeling: the peace of a person who has worked out what is truly fixed and what is truly theirs to do, and who has organized their life accordingly.

## Extended Meaning

To achieve genuine and founded inner peace by aligning one's heart and conduct with the moral order — to have resolved the fundamental questions of how one ought to live and what one's life is for, such that external circumstances, including hardship and death, can no longer fundamentally disturb the mind.

The idiom is more than a synonym for "peace of mind" in the casual sense. It carries the weight of the philosophical traditions from which its two halves are drawn:

- **安心** in the Chan Buddhist tradition: not the absence of disturbance but the discovery that the mind's true nature is undisturbable — resting in Buddha-nature
- **安心** in the Pure Land Buddhist tradition: the settled faith in Amida's compassion that resolves the fear of birth and death
- **立命** in the Mencian tradition: the posture of a person who has done everything within their power and can therefore accept what Heaven decrees, without resentment or panic

Applied to:
- A person who has achieved genuine equanimity through religious practice or philosophical cultivation
- Someone facing death, illness, or catastrophe from a position of inner composure
- The culmination of a lifetime of moral and spiritual practice
- Any state of deep existential settledness — not the peace of ignorance but the peace of understanding

## Source and Origin

The two components of 安心立命 are classical in origin and draw from both Confucian and Buddhist tradition.

**立命** derives from **《孟子·盡心上》:**
> 存心養性，所以事天也。夭壽不貳，修身以俟之，所以立命也。
> *"Preserving the mind and nurturing one's nature — this is how one serves Heaven. Neither short life nor long life makes any difference; cultivating oneself and awaiting what comes — this is how one 立命, stands firm in one's fate."*

Mencius's 立命 is the posture of a person who has done everything within their power and can therefore accept Heaven's decree without disturbance. It is not passivity but a particular kind of active acceptance — grounded in self-cultivation, not resignation.

**安心** enters Buddhist vocabulary through the Chan tradition. The most famous instance is in **《景德傳燈録》**, recording the exchange between Bodhidharma (達磨) and his future successor Huike (慧可):
> 慧可曰：我心未寧，乞師安心。師曰：將心來，與汝安。慧可良久，曰：覓心了不可得。師曰：我與汝安心竟。
> *"Huike said: 'My heart is not yet at peace; I ask the master to bring it peace.' The master said: 'Bring me the heart, and I will bring it peace.' After a long silence, Huike said: 'I have searched for the heart and cannot find it.' The master said: 'I have finished bringing your heart peace.'"*

The compound 安心立命 synthesizes the Mencian and Buddhist strands: the Confucian 立命 (stable orientation to fate) paired with the Buddhist 安心 (peace of mind as a cultivated state of the heart). In Pure Land Buddhism (浄土宗), 安心 acquired a further specific meaning — the settled faith in Amida's compassionate vow that dissolves the fear of birth and death, the foundation of the Pure Land practitioner's life.

The earliest datable instance of the compound in the Japanese Pure Land context appears in a text by 浄土宗 monks in Tokyo in 1891, composed in response to the Nōbi earthquake (濃尾地震) of October 28 of that year — one of the most destructive earthquakes in Japanese history. The monks used 安心立命 to name exactly what the survivors of such a catastrophe most needed: not merely comfort but a deep reorientation toward what is fixed and what is free, such that even the loss of everything material could not undo the inner ground.

## Cultural Notes

**In Chinese**, 安心立命 functions as a philosophically elevated expression for achieved inner peace — distinguished from ordinary contentment by the depth of its grounding. It appears in biographical writing about religious figures, philosophers, and anyone whose composure in adversity suggests a cultivated inner foundation. The phrase is used both in Confucian and Buddhist contexts: in Neo-Confucian writing it describes the person who has completed the work of self-cultivation; in Buddhist contexts it describes the practitioner who has resolved the fundamental fear of impermanence. In modern Chinese it can also appear in motivational and psychological writing as a literary way of naming deep equanimity.

**In Japanese**, あんしんりつめい is associated particularly with Pure Land Buddhism (浄土宗 and 浄土真宗), where 安心 (anjin) is a central technical term — the settled faith in Amida's primal vow that transforms the practitioner's relationship to birth and death. In this context, 安心立命 names the complete condition of the practitioner who has achieved anjin and organized their life accordingly. The phrase appears in Buddhist pastoral literature, temple discourses, and writings addressed to people facing death, illness, or catastrophe. Outside specifically religious contexts, it functions in Japanese as an elevated literary expression for profound composure.

**In Korean**, 안심입명 (the Korean reading uses 입 for 立 in this compound due to phonological rules) appears in Buddhist contexts, biographical narrative, and literary writing about equanimity. The Confucian dimension — 立命 as Mencian acceptance of Heaven's will — resonates with the Korean literary-philosophical tradition's engagement with the Mencius.

## Example Sentences

1. **Classical (《孟子·盡心上》, c. 4th century BCE):**
   夭壽不貳，修身以俟之，所以立命也。
   *"Neither short life nor long life makes any difference; cultivating oneself and awaiting what comes — this is how one 立命."*

2. **Classical (《景德傳燈録》, 1004 CE):**
   我心未寧，乞師安心。師曰：將心來，與汝安。
   *"My heart is not yet at peace; I ask the master to bring it peace. The master said: Bring me the heart, and I will bring it peace."*

3. **Modern Chinese:**
   歷盡滄桑之後，他終於達到了安心立命的境界——不再為得失所動，不再為榮辱所擾。
   *"After weathering every hardship, he had at last reached the state of 安心立命 — no longer moved by gain or loss, no longer disturbed by honor or shame."*

4. **English:**
   After thirty years of practice she had arrived at something the tradition called 安心立命 — not the peace of having nothing left to lose, but the peace of knowing exactly where she stood.

## Sentiment

**Deeply positive and aspirational** — 安心立命 names an achieved and genuine inner peace that is among the highest conditions described in the Confucian and Buddhist traditions. It is not the peace of ignorance, avoidance, or numbing, but the peace of a person who has done the work of understanding what is fixed and what is free, and who lives from that understanding. To say of someone that they have 安心立命 is the highest kind of tribute to their inner life.

**Specifically existential in register** — the phrase belongs to discussions of how one ought ultimately to live and face mortality, not to everyday praise of a calm temperament. It is used in elevated, philosophical, and religious contexts, not in casual speech.
