[[Biblical Chengyu]]
### Pronunciation
- **Mandarin**: yán dì guāng shì
- **Cantonese**: jim4 dei6 gwong1 sai3
- **Japanese (hiragana)**: えんちこうせい
- **Korean (Hangul)**: 염지광세 (yeom-ji-gwang-se)
- **Vietnamese (Sino-Vietnamese)**: diêm địa quang thế

### Literal Meaning
- **塩／鹽** salt
- **地** earth, land
- **光** light
- **世** world, age

Literal: “Salt of the earth, light of the world.”

### Figurative Meaning
A concise expression of Jesus’s description of his disciples as preserving, purifying, and illuminating influences within the world. It denotes moral distinctiveness and public witness.

### Scriptural Source
This idiom condenses **Matthew 5:13–14** from the Sermon on the Mount:
> “Ye are the salt of the earth… Ye are the light of the world.”

Greek:  
_Ὑμεῖς ἐστε τὸ ἅλας τῆς γῆς… τὸ φῶς τοῦ κόσμου._

Traditional Chinese translations render:
> “你們是世上的鹽… 你們是世上的光。”

The Dan'a'yo form unites the two metaphors into a balanced four-character pairing.

### Example Usage
> 信徒當自省，是否仍為塩地光世，而非隱匿無用。  
> Believers should examine whether they remain salt of the earth and light of the world, rather than hidden and ineffective.

### Cultural Notes
Salt in the ancient world preserved food, enhanced flavor, and symbolized covenant fidelity. Light signifies revelation, visibility, and moral clarity. The metaphors are vocational, not merely descriptive.

The pairing of 地 and 世 distinguishes spatial domain and historical sphere. Earth suggests created order; world implies human society in its moral condition.

In Christian theology, this saying grounds public engagement. Disciples are not removed from society but placed within it as transformative presence. Yet the imagery also carries warning: salt can lose savor; light can be hidden.

Within Dan'a'yo, 塩地光世 functions as an ethical summons. It compresses identity and mission into a single balanced maxim.

### Tone and Sentiment
Encouraging yet exhortative. It affirms calling while pressing responsibility and visibility.
