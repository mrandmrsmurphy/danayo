## Literal Meaning
- [暴 (char)](../characters/暴%20(char).md) — violent; sudden; excessive; without restraint
- [飲 (char)](../characters/飲%20(char).md) — to drink; drinking
- [暴 (char)](../characters/暴%20(char).md) — (same)
- [食 (char)](../characters/食%20(char).md) — to eat; food; eating

Literally: "Drinking to excess; eating to excess."

The structure is a doubled parallel: 暴飲 (excessive drinking) + 暴食 (excessive eating), each two characters with 暴 leading. 暴 here carries its sense of sudden, reckless, unrestrained action — the same character used in 暴風 (violent storm) and 暴君 (tyrant). Applied to eating and drinking, it names behavior that is explosive and without control: consuming in a reckless burst, far beyond what moderation or health permits.

The repetition of 暴 at the head of each half drives the point rhythmically — both things are done in the same spirit of excess, the drinking and the eating compounding each other.

## Extended Meaning

Describes a pattern of eating and drinking that is excessive, unrestrained, or wildly disproportionate to what is healthy. The idiom can refer to a single occasion of bingeing or to a habitual pattern of indulgence.

The phrase applies to:
- Overeating and overdrinking at a banquet, holiday, or celebration
- Habitual bingeing that damages health over time
- The combination specifically — excessive alcohol and excessive food together, which traditional medicine considered especially harmful
- Any situation of self-indulgence in food and drink taken beyond reason

The idiom carries a mild medical and moral warning tone: this is behavior that harms the body. It is not primarily a moral condemnation (unlike terms for licentiousness or debauchery) but a practical caution grounded in the tradition of dietary medicine. To describe someone's behavior as 暴飲暴食 is to note that they are damaging themselves, not that they are wicked.

## Source and Origin

The phrase crystallizes from the tradition of **養生文献** (yǎng shēng wénxiàn, "health cultivation literature") — the body of classical and medical texts concerned with regulating the body through diet, exercise, sleep, and emotional equilibrium. The foundational text of this tradition is the **《黄帝内経》** (*Yellow Emperor's Classic of Internal Medicine*, compiled from Warring States through Han periods), which establishes dietary moderation as a pillar of health and identifies excess in eating and drinking as a primary cause of illness.

**《素問·痹論》** (one chapter of the 《黄帝内经》):
> 飲食自倍，腸胃乃傷。
> *"When one doubles one's food and drink [beyond what is appropriate], the intestines and stomach are thereby harmed."*

This principle — that dietary excess, not just deficiency, injures the body — runs throughout classical Chinese medical and philosophical discourse. The Confucian tradition equally emphasizes moderation: the well-known passage 食不厭精，膾不厭細 (Analects, "Xiangdang") describes Confucius's careful standards for food preparation, implicitly condemning the opposite.

The four-character compound 暴飲暴食 pairs the two binomes 暴飲 and 暴食, each of which appears independently in classical texts before their combination. The compound is attested in its current form in late imperial and early modern Chinese writing and is in common use across Chinese, Japanese, and Korean.

**Note**: 暴飲暴食 does not have a single founding classical citation in the way that many chengyu do. It is a parallel compound formed from established vocabulary, whose currency as a fixed idiom reflects the continuing vitality of the dietary medicine tradition rather than derivation from a single famous passage. Both 暴飲 and 暴食 can be used independently in literary contexts.

## Standard Form

The Dan'a'yo form **暴飲暴食** uses 飲 (the standard character). Variant forms:

- **暴飮暴食** — variant using 飮 (an alternate form of 飲, occasionally seen in older texts and Japanese sources)
- **暴饮暴食** — simplified Chinese (饮 for 飲)

暴 and 食 are stable across all scripts.

## Pronunciations
- **Mandarin (Pinyin):** bào yǐn bào shí (4–3–4–2)
- **Cantonese (Jyutping):** bou6 jam2 bou6 sik6
- **Japanese (on-yomi, hiragana):** ぼういんぼうしょく (bōin bōshoku)
- **Korean (Sino-Korean):** 폭음폭식 (pok-eum-pok-sik)
- **Vietnamese (Hán–Việt):** bạo ẩm bạo thực

**Note on 暴**: In this compound 暴 is read *bào* (4th tone, "excessive; sudden; violent"), the reading used in 暴力 (violence), 暴風 (violent wind), etc. A second reading, *pù* (4th tone as well, "to expose; to dry in the sun"), is unrelated and not used here. In Japanese both 暴飲 (bōin) and 暴食 (bōshoku) can stand alone as compounds.

## Cultural Notes

**In Chinese**, 暴飲暴食 is a practical, medical-register expression used in health journalism, dietary advice, and everyday warnings about holiday or celebratory excess. It appears frequently in Chinese-language health content around the Lunar New Year and other festival periods, when overindulgence is common, and in articles about digestive illness. The phrase carries no stigma of moral failure — it simply names a behavior that damages the body. A doctor, a concerned friend, or a public health announcement all reach for 暴飲暴食 without ceremony. It is fully naturalized everyday vocabulary.

**In Japanese**, ぼういんぼうしょく (bōin bōshoku) occupies exactly the same register — practical, medical, appearing in health columns, year-end party (忘年会, bōnenkai) season warnings, and dietary advice. Both 暴飲 and 暴食 function as independent words in Japanese and frequently appear separately: 暴飲 describes a drinking binge, 暴食 describes eating until ill. The full four-character compound appears when the speaker wants to emphasize both dimensions together. New Year's and year-end festivities generate an annual wave of 暴飲暴食 cautions in Japanese media.

**In Korean**, 폭음폭식 (pok-eum-pok-sik) is the standard expression — note that Korean uses 폭 (暴 read as *pok*, "violent/sudden") rather than *bok*, reflecting the regular Korean Sino-Korean sound correspondence for 暴. Like the Chinese and Japanese, it appears primarily in health contexts and is neutral-practical in register.

## Example Sentences

1. **Classical (《素問·痹論》):**
   飲食自倍，腸胃乃傷。
   *"When one doubles one's food and drink, the intestines and stomach are thereby harmed."*

2. **Modern Chinese:**
   過年期間難免暴飲暴食，事後腸胃不適也是意料之中。
   *"During the New Year period it's hard to avoid bingeing on food and drink — the digestive discomfort afterward is entirely expected."*

3. **Modern Japanese:**
   年末の忘年会続きで暴飲暴食が続き、胃腸の調子がすっかり狂ってしまった。
   *"With one year-end party after another, the bingeing on food and drink continued, and my digestion fell completely out of order."*

4. **English:**
   The three-day festival ended the way it always did — with a week of recovery from 暴飲暴食, and promises that next year would be different.

## Sentiment

**Mildly negative, practical in tone** — 暴飲暴食 is a description of unhealthy behavior rather than a moral condemnation. The judgment it contains is medical rather than ethical: this harms your body. The phrase is used sympathetically as often as critically — it is as likely to appear in the voice of someone confessing their own excess as in the voice of someone reproving another's.

**Common and neutral in register** — everyday vocabulary across all CJKV languages, at home in health journalism, casual conversation, and annual festival warnings. No particular literary weight or ceremonial gravity; the phrase does its work matter-of-factly.
