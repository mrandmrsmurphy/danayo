## Literal Meaning
- [喜 (char)](characters/喜%20(char).md) — joy, gladness, delight
- [怒 (char)](characters/怒%20(char).md) — anger, ire
- [哀](characters/哀.md) — sorrow, grief, mournfulness
- [楽](characters/楽.md) — pleasure, enjoyment, contentment

Literally: "Joy, anger, sorrow, pleasure" — the four cardinal human emotions, naming the full spectrum of feeling.

Note the pairing: 喜 and 楽 are both on the positive side (gladness vs. settled enjoyment), while 怒 and 哀 occupy the difficult register (assertive anger vs. heavy grief). The four together cover the full range not as extremes but as distinct emotional modes.

## Extended Meaning
喜怒哀楽 is the standard Sinospheric formula for the totality of human emotional life — used where English speakers say "the full range of human feeling" or simply "emotions." It is not a list of four specific emotions so much as a shorthand for the complete spectrum, the way the four cardinal directions stand for all of space. To say that a work of art or a life contains 喜怒哀楽 is to say it encompasses everything feeling can be.

## Source and Origin
**《禮記·中庸·第一章》** (Book of Rites, Doctrine of the Mean, Chapter 1):

> 「喜怒哀樂之未發，謂之中；發而皆中節，謂之和。中也者，天下之大本也；和也者，天下之達道也。致中和，天地位焉，萬物育焉。」
> "The state in which joy, anger, sorrow, and pleasure have not yet been aroused is called *zhōng* (centrality, equilibrium). When they are aroused and each attains its due measure, that is called *hé* (harmony). Centrality is the great root of all-under-heaven; harmony is the universal path of all-under-heaven. When centrality and harmony are fully realized, heaven and earth settle into their proper positions, and all things flourish."

The Doctrine of the Mean (中庸) is traditionally attributed to 子思 (Zisi, c. 481–402 BC), grandson of Confucius, and was incorporated into the 禮記 (Book of Rites). Under Zhu Xi's Neo-Confucian curriculum it became one of the Four Books (四書), studied by scholars across the Sinosphere from the Song dynasty onward.

The passage's philosophical move is subtle and important: the problem is not the emotions themselves. The *unactivated* state of 喜怒哀楽 is called 中 — the emotion at rest is not disordered but grounded. When the emotions are activated and each response *hits its proper measure* (中節), the result is 和 (harmony). The classical prescription is not the suppression of emotion but its proper calibration — not less feeling, but well-ordered feeling. 喜怒哀楽 is thus not a list of dangers to be managed but of natural capacities to be cultivated.

## Standard Form
**喜怒哀楽** (Dan'a'yo standard; uses shinjitai 楽 in place of traditional 樂)
- 喜怒哀樂 — traditional Chinese form; aliased in this vault
- 喜怒哀乐 — simplified Chinese form; aliased in this vault

No variant orderings are in circulation. The four-character sequence is fixed across all languages and scripts.

## Cultural Notes
The ordering is not accidental. 喜 (joy) opens, 楽 (pleasure) closes, and the difficult emotions 怒 (anger) and 哀 (sorrow) sit in the interior — framed by the positive. The formula is balanced in itself, mirroring the 中和 it describes.

In traditional Chinese medicine (中醫), the emotions are understood to have direct physiological effects when excessive: 喜 in excess harms the heart; 怒 in excess harms the liver; 哀/悲 in excess harms the lungs; and so on through the organ-emotion correspondences of the Five Phase system. 喜怒哀楽 appears in medical contexts as a shorthand for emotional excess as a pathological vector — one reason to cultivate 中和 is literal health.

In Japan, きどあいらく has shed most of its classical philosophical weight and functions as ordinary vocabulary for "all kinds of feelings." A film is praised for capturing きどあいらく; a long life is said to have been full of きどあいらく. The phrase is understood by nearly everyone and used freely in casual speech — perhaps the most domesticated of all yojijukugo.

In Korea, 희로애락 is similarly a common literary and everyday phrase. Note the phonological shift: 怒 is 노 in Dan'a'yo (following the historical reading) but becomes 로 in standard Sino-Korean, producing the well-known 희*로*애락 rather than 희*노*애락.

The convergence with Aristotle's doctrine of the mean (μεσότης, *mesotēs*) — where virtuous emotional response hits the mean between excess and deficiency — has been noted by comparative philosophers. The 中庸's 中節 ("hitting the proper measure") and Aristotle's *mesotēs* are structurally parallel, though arrived at independently.

## Example Sentences
1. 《禮記·中庸·一》:「喜怒哀樂之未發，謂之中；發而皆中節，謂之和。」
   "When joy, anger, sorrow, and pleasure have not yet been aroused, that is called centrality; when aroused and each in due measure, that is called harmony."
2. 人生には喜怒哀楽がつきものだ。
   "Joy, anger, sorrow, and pleasure are inseparable parts of human life."
3. The novel traces its character's full 喜怒哀楽 — every shade of feeling from elation to grief — across forty years.

## Sentiment
Neutral and universal. 喜怒哀楽 does not praise or lament the emotions it names — it enumerates them without judgment. The register ranges from philosophical (in classical and medical usage) to casual (in everyday Japanese and Korean), but the scope is always complete: the phrase is cited to acknowledge the full breadth of human feeling, not to prescribe or restrict any part of it.
