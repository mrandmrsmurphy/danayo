**Literal meaning:** “The sound of the wind, the cry of cranes.”  
**Extended sense:** To panic at imagined threats; to mistake harmless signs for danger due to fear or tension.

### Source
From the **晉書**, in accounts of the aftermath of the Battle of Feishui (383 CE). The phrase describes soldiers so unnerved that even natural sounds seemed like approaching enemies.

### Classical background
The historical episode concerns the collapse of the Former Qin army after defeat by Eastern Jin forces. In the rout, morale deteriorated so completely that ordinary environmental noises, wind in the grass or cranes calling, were interpreted as hostile pursuit.

The imagery is precise rather than ornamental. 風聲 and 鶴唳 are not inherently ominous. Their menace is projected by the hearer. The idiom therefore captures psychological fragility under stress. It does not describe objective danger but subjective overreaction.

The phrase is often paired with 草木皆兵, another expression from the same narrative context, reinforcing the theme of fear-induced misperception.

### Modern usage
Across the region, 風聲鶴唳 is used to describe collective anxiety, rumor-driven panic, or hypersensitivity in tense situations. It appears in journalism, political commentary, and literary prose. The tone is descriptive and often mildly critical, pointing to nervous overinterpretation rather than justified alarm.
