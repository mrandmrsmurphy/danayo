## Literal Meaning
- [[神経]] — nerves; the nervous system
- [[衰弱]] — weakening, wasting, exhaustion

Literally: "Weakening of the nerves" — exhaustion or depletion of the nervous system.

## Extended Meaning
神経衰弱 originally denoted neurasthenia — the 19th-century medical diagnosis for chronic fatigue, anxiety, weakness, and emotional instability attributed to depletion of nervous energy. Since neurasthenia was removed from the American DSM in 1980, 神経衰弱 has drifted toward the colloquial sense of a nervous or mental breakdown: a collapse under the accumulated weight of overwork, chronic stress, or relentless pressure.

In East Asian usage the term retains particular cultural weight. It is associated with the pressures of exam culture, corporate overwork (過労), and the expectation of stoic endurance until the system simply breaks. A person with 神経衰弱 is not seen as weak-willed but as having been pushed past a physiological limit — the framing is sympathetic rather than critical.

## Source and Origin
神経衰弱 is a wasei kango (和製漢語), coined by Meiji-era Japanese physicians to translate "neurasthenia" — a diagnosis introduced by American neurologist George Miller Beard in 1869 and systematized in his *Practical Treatise on Nervous Exhaustion* (1880). Beard argued that neurasthenia was a disease of modernity: the nervous system possessed a finite reserve of "nerve force" (神経力), and the relentless demands of industrial civilization — the telegraph, the railway, the newspaper, the business schedule — depleted it faster than it could recover.

Japanese medical translators took [[神経]] (nerves; nervous system — itself a Meiji coinage for the Western anatomical concept) and combined it with [[衰弱]] (wasting, debilitation) to produce 神経衰弱 as a precise Sino-Japanese rendering. The compound spread to China (神经衰弱, shénjīng shuāiruò) and Korea (신경쇠약) in the early 20th century, during the same wave of Meiji-era medical and scientific vocabulary that reshaped both Mandarin and Korean. The WHO's ICD-10 still retains neurasthenia (F48.0) as a valid diagnosis, and it continues to be used clinically in China, Japan, and Korea.

## Standard Form
**神経衰弱** — Dan'a'yo and Japanese standard form, using shinjitai 経 (for traditional 經).
- 神經衰弱 — traditional Chinese form
- 神经衰弱 — simplified Chinese

Note: in Japan, 神経衰弱 is also the name of the card game known in English as Concentration or Memory — the game that "exhausts the nerves" by demanding sustained attentive recall.

## Cultural Notes
Neurasthenia reached its social peak in East Asia during the early-to-mid 20th century, serving partly as a politically neutral way to discuss what we would now call depression or anxiety. American anthropologist Arthur Kleinman documented in the 1980s that Chinese patients presenting with depressive symptoms were frequently diagnosed with 神经衰弱 (shénjīng shuāiruò) — the somatic framing was culturally safer and less stigmatizing than a psychiatric label, and the body's exhaustion was easier to explain to family members than a disorder of mood or thought.

In Japan, 神経衰弱 sits alongside 過労死 (karoshi, death from overwork) in the cultural vocabulary of work-related collapse. Both concepts frame breakdown as the predictable result of impossible external demands rather than personal failing — a distinction that matters enormously in a culture with a strong shame orientation around mental weakness. The card game of the same name has made the phrase a minor part of everyday vocabulary: playing a long round of 神経衰弱 is described as genuinely tiring.

In Korea, 신경쇠약 similarly carries the connotation of a person pushed past their limit — used in historical contexts (the psychological toll of the colonial period, the Korean War, rapid industrialization) and in contemporary discussions of workplace stress.

## Example Sentences
1. 彼は激務で神経衰弱になり、医師から三ヶ月の休養を命じられた。
   "He developed 神経衰弱 from overwork and was ordered by his doctor to rest for three months."
2. Beard's neurasthenia (神経衰弱) was modernity's signature illness — a civilization producing more stress than its citizens' nervous systems could sustain.

## Sentiment
Medically neutral, socially sympathetic. 神経衰弱 frames the sufferer as a victim of external pressure rather than personal weakness — the nervous system was depleted, not the character. Register is serious; it is not used ironically or lightly.
