## Literal Meaning
- [盛](characters/盛.md) — flourish, prosper, be at the height of power
- [者 (char)](characters/者%20(char).md) — one who; those who (nominalizer)
- [必 (char)](characters/必%20(char).md) — certainly, inevitably, without exception
- [衰](characters/衰.md) — decline, wane, diminish

Literally: "Those who flourish will certainly decline" — prosperity is not a stable state but a peak from which descent is inevitable.

## Extended Meaning
盛者必衰 is the Buddhist expression of impermanence (無常, *anicca*) applied to worldly power and success. It does not describe a possible outcome or a common pattern — it states a law: those at the height of their flourishing will decline. The inevitability is the point.

The phrase does not counsel despair. In its Buddhist context it delivers a teaching: attachment to one's flourishing is both futile and painful, because the flourishing will end. The appropriate response is not to prevent decline but to release the grip of attachment to the peak.

## Source and Origin
The principle of impermanence (諸行無常, *sabbe saṅkhārā anicca*) is foundational Buddhist teaching. The specific formulation 盛者必衰 appears in Buddhist sutra literature including the **仁王経** (仁王護国般若波羅蜜多経, *Renwang Jing*, Sutra for Humane Kings), where the transience of worldly power is linked to the duty of rulers to protect the dharma — precisely because their power is temporary, they must use it wisely.

The phrase achieved its most enduring literary expression in the opening of **《平家物語》** (The Tale of the Heike, early 13th century):

> 「祇園精舎の鐘の声、諸行無常の響きあり。沙羅双樹の花の色、盛者必衰の理をあらわす。おごれる人も久しからず、ただ春の夜の夢のごとし。」
>
> "The sound of the Gion Shōja bells echoes the impermanence of all things. The colour of the sāla flowers reveals the truth that the prosperous must decline. The proud do not endure; they are like a dream on a spring night."

《平家物語》 chronicles the Genpei War (1180–85) between the Taira (平家, Heike) and Minamoto (源氏, Genji) clans. The Taira had risen under Taira no Kiyomori to dominate all of Japan — court, military, religion. Their catastrophic fall, ending in total annihilation at the naval battle of Dan-no-ura (壇ノ浦, 1185), became the paradigm case of 盛者必衰 in Japanese historical consciousness. The opening lines of 平家物語 read as both prologue and verdict: the reader is told before the first event what the last event will mean.

## Cultural Notes
盛者必衰 belongs to the shared Buddhist-Confucian understanding of history as cyclical: dynasties rise, reach their height, and fall in accordance with a moral and cosmological order. This framework shaped how East Asian historians interpreted every major dynastic transition — the fall of the Tang, the Mongol conquests, the collapse of the Ming. Each was both historically explicable and cosmologically inevitable. 盛者必衰 provided the interpretive frame.

In Japan, the phrase is inseparable from 平家物語 and the figure of Taira no Kiyomori. For most Japanese speakers, 盛者必衰 (じょうしゃひっすい) immediately evokes the Taira at their height — the arrogance of Kiyomori, the overreach of his clan — and the music of their fall. The phrase and the epic are fused. This is why じょうしゃひっすい carries more emotional weight in Japanese than in Chinese: it does not merely state a principle but recalls a specific, achingly realized story.

盛者必衰 pairs with [[諸行無常]] (shōgyō mujō, "all things are impermanent") — the two together form the philosophical and aesthetic core of the Buddhist sensibility in Japanese classical culture, giving rise to mono no aware (物の哀れ): the bittersweet awareness that beautiful things pass, and that their passing is part of what makes them beautiful.

The Latin *sic transit gloria mundi* ("so passes the glory of the world") captures the same insight and has a similarly long pedigree in Western ecclesiastical and literary tradition. Both phrases frame worldly glory as inherently temporary — not merely empirical observation but spiritual teaching about what not to cling to.

## Example Sentences
1. 《平家物語》冒頭:「沙羅双樹の花の色、盛者必衰の理をあらわす。」
   "The colour of the sāla flowers reveals the truth that the prosperous must decline."
2. 天下を制した平家も、盛者必衰の理に従い壇ノ浦で滅亡した。
   "Even the Taira, who had mastered all under heaven, obeyed the principle of 盛者必衰 and perished at Dan-no-ura."
3. The investors who made fortunes in the boom understood too late that 盛者必衰 is not a poetic sentiment but a law — what rises must fall, and the only question is when.

## Sentiment
Solemn, philosophical, and elegiac. 盛者必衰 is not pessimistic but clear-eyed: it does not condemn flourishing, only its permanence. The tone is Buddhist in its equanimity — neither celebrating the rise nor lamenting the fall, but naming the pattern with unflinching calm. In Japanese especially, the phrase carries the particular sadness of something inevitably beautiful and inevitably lost.
