## Literal Meaning
- [令 (char)](../characters/令%20(char).md) — command; order; cause
- [行 (char)](../characters/行%20(char).md) — carry out; act; be put into effect
- [禁](../characters/禁.md) — prohibit; forbid
- [止](../characters/止.md) — stop; cease; desist

Literally: "What is ordered is carried out; what is prohibited is stopped."

The idiom compresses a parallel couplet — 令則行，禁則止 ("if ordered, do it; if prohibited, stop") — into four characters of maximum density. Both halves are identical in structure: [authority-act] → [immediate compliance].

## Extended Meaning

Perfect organizational discipline: commands are executed without question or delay; prohibitions are observed without evasion or exception. The phrase describes the ideal relationship between authority and those under it — one in which the gap between order and execution is zero.

令行禁止 is always a positive assessment. It praises a military unit that moves as one body, a bureaucracy that implements directives reliably, a school where rules are respected, or a leader whose word carries absolute weight. The converse — where orders are issued but ignored, or prohibitions observed only selectively — is one of the defining failures of governance in the Chinese political tradition.

## Source and Origin

The phrase crystallizes a formulation that runs through the major political and philosophical texts of the Warring States period (戦国時代, 475–221 BC).

The **《管子》** (*Guanzi*, a collection of political philosophy associated with Guan Zhong 管仲, the great minister of Qi, though compiled from the 5th century BC through the Han dynasty) contains the long form from which the four-character idiom was condensed:

> **令則行，禁則止，此所謂政令。**
> "If commanded, carry it out; if prohibited, stop — this is what is meant by governmental command."

The four-character form 令行禁止 appears **five times** in the Guanzi, **three times** in the **《荀子》** (Xunzi, c. 310–235 BC), **three times** in the **《韓非子》** (Han Feizi, c. 280–233 BC), and once in the **《逸周書》** (Lost Books of Zhou). Its repetition across the Legalist, Confucian, and military traditions of the period shows that it had already become a fixed phrase — a standard formulation for the criterion of effective rule — before the Qin unification of 221 BC.

The Xunzi provides the most rhetorically complete deployment:

> **《荀子·王制》:**
> 令行禁止，王者之事備矣。
> *"When commands are carried out and prohibitions are heeded, the affairs of a true king are complete."*

Here 令行禁止 is identified not merely as a desirable quality but as the **sufficient condition** for legitimate kingship: if a ruler achieves this, nothing else is lacking. The same logic appears in the military classics — the *Sunzi* (孫子), *Wuzi* (吳子), and *Six Secret Teachings* (六韜) — where command-discipline is treated as the foundation upon which all tactical and strategic capability rests. An army that achieves 令行禁止 is, in this framework, already half-victorious.

## Cultural Notes

**In Chinese governance and military discourse**, 令行禁止 has been a living phrase for 2,500 years without interruption. It appears in dynastic histories praising efficient administrators, in military training manuals, in the legal codes of successive dynasties (where the failure to 令行禁止 is identified as a cause of dynastic decline), and in contemporary CCP party documents praising disciplined governance. It is sufficiently embedded that it does not feel archaic — a Chinese reader encounters it today in official reports, corporate governance literature, and editorials without any sense that they are reading an antique expression.

During the COVID-19 pandemic response in China (2020–2022), 令行禁止 appeared constantly in official praise of communities and local governments that achieved comprehensive compliance with public health directives. In the People's Liberation Army, the phrase is institutional vocabulary: military training emphasizes 令行禁止 as a core value, and it appears in unit evaluations and officer assessments.

**In business and organizational management**, 令行禁止 describes what Western management literature calls "strong execution culture" — the quality of an organization where strategic decisions are reliably translated into operational action. Chinese management writing frequently cites 令行禁止 as the hallmark of Huawei's internal culture and of effective military-style leadership more broadly.

**In Japanese**, れいこうきんし (reikōkinshi) is recognizable in formal and classical contexts but not a phrase of everyday corporate speech. The concept maps onto 規律 (kiritsu, discipline) and 命令系統 (meirei keitō, chain of command), but lacks the dense classical resonance the four-character form carries in Chinese.

## Example Sentences

1. **Classical (荀子·王制, c. 3rd c. BC):**
   令行禁止，王者之事備矣。
   *"When commands are carried out and prohibitions are heeded, the affairs of a true king are complete."*

2. **Modern Chinese (military):**
   這支部隊紀律嚴明，令行禁止，在演習中表現出色。
   *"This unit has strict discipline — commands obeyed, prohibitions respected — and performed outstandingly in the exercise."*

3. **Modern Chinese (organizational):**
   一個真正高效的公司，必須做到令行禁止，否則再好的策略也無法落地。
   *"A truly efficient company must achieve total command discipline; otherwise, even the best strategy cannot be executed."*

4. **English:**
   The general prided himself on running a 令行禁止 operation — what he ordered happened, and what he forbade did not.

## Sentiment

Strongly **positive** across all contexts and registers. Unlike many chengyu that carry ambivalence or contextual reversal, 令行禁止 is unambiguously a compliment: it describes an ideal state that commanders, managers, and rulers have sought for millennia.

**Formal to ceremonial in register.** It does not appear in casual conversation; it belongs to official assessments, historical judgments, and formal praise. To apply it is to invoke the full weight of the Chinese political tradition's highest standard for effective governance.
