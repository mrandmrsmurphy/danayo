## Literal Meaning
- [一](../characters/一%20(char).md) — one
- [長](../characters/長%20(char).md) — long
- [一](../characters/一%20(char).md) — one
- [短](../characters/短%20(char).md) — short

Literally: "One long, one short."

## Extended Meaning
Mixed strengths and weaknesses; gains accompanied by losses; an uneven or balanced contrast rather than pure advantage. Unlike moralizing maxims, 一長一短 functions analytically — it names the condition of mixed assessment and leaves the judgment to context, rather than pushing toward a verdict.

## Source and Origin
Traditionally traced to *Records of the Grand Historian* (《史記》), Chapter 87, the Biography of Li Si (李斯列傳). In that context, the phrase is part of an evaluative register used to weigh policies, persons, or outcomes, emphasizing that real situations rarely admit unqualified judgments. The construction is archetypal Classical Chinese parallelism: pairing opposites compresses an argument into four characters, inviting comparison rather than conclusion. This made it especially useful in historiography and political reasoning, where balance and tradeoffs mattered more than a single clean verdict.

## Standard Form
**一長一短** (traditional) / **一长一短** (simplified) — both current.

## Cultural Notes
Across the region, the idiom is used to acknowledge pros and cons without dramatization. It appears in essays, editorials, classroom discussion, and everyday speech when someone wants to concede complexity or avoid absolutism. The tone is neutral and reflective rather than admonitory — compared with evaluative idioms that push toward a verdict, 一長一短 is deliberately incomplete, and its rhetorical force lies precisely in that restraint.

## Example Sentences
1. 此案有一長一短，不可一概而論。
   "This proposal has both merits and drawbacks — it can't be judged as simply good or bad."
2. どの方法にも一長一短があるから、状況に応じて選ぶべきだ。
   "Every method has its strengths and weaknesses, so you should choose according to the situation."
3. Every candidate has 一長一短 — no one is perfect, and the question is which tradeoffs you can live with.

## Sentiment
Neutral and reflective; concedes complexity rather than issuing a verdict. Signals that a situation deserves weighing, not slogans.
