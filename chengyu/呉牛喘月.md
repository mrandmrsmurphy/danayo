**Literal meaning:** “The Wu ox pants at the moon.”  
**Extended sense:** To be frightened by something harmless because of past experience; to react nervously to a perceived threat that is not actually present.

### Source
The phrase appears in the **《世說新語》**, a 5th-century collection of anecdotes about scholars and officials of the Wei–Jin period.

### Classical background
The image refers to oxen in the hot southern region of Wu. Having suffered under intense summer heat, they become so conditioned to the blazing sun that even when they see the moon, whose brightness vaguely resembles sunlight, they begin to pant in distress. The response is disproportionate because it is driven by memory rather than present reality.

The anecdote was used to describe a person who, after experiencing hardship or danger, becomes overly wary and reacts anxiously even to harmless circumstances. The phrase therefore captures the psychological residue of past suffering.

### Modern usage
Across the region, 呉牛喘月 is used to describe unnecessary fear or hypersensitivity born of previous experience. It appears in literary prose and commentary, often with a reflective or mildly sympathetic tone. The idiom focuses on the persistence of fear rather than foolishness.

### Usage note
The idiom highlights the lingering effect of past experience on perception. Fear learned under real conditions can persist even when the original cause has disappeared.
