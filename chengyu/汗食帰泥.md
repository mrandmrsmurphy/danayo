[[Biblical Chengyu]]
### Pronunciation
- **Mandarin**: hàn shí guī ní
- **Cantonese**: hon6 sik6 gwai1 nai4
- **Japanese (hiragana)**: かんしょくきでい
- **Korean (Hangul)**: 한식귀니 (han-sik-gwi-ni)
- **Vietnamese (Sino-Vietnamese)**: hãn thực quy nê

### Literal Meaning
- **汗** sweat
- **食** eat
- **帰／歸** return
- **泥** dust, soil, earth

Literal: “By sweat eat; return to dust.”

### Figurative Meaning
A compact summary of the curse upon Adam: labor marked by toil, and mortality ending in return to the ground. It captures both economic hardship and existential finitude.

### Scriptural Source
The idiom condenses **Genesis 3:19**:
> “In the sweat of thy face shalt thou eat bread, till thou return unto the ground; for out of it wast thou taken: for dust thou art, and unto dust shalt thou return.”

Traditional Chinese translations often render:
> “你必汗流滿面才得糊口…你本是塵土，仍要歸於塵土。”

The Dan'a'yo form selects 汗食 to compress “eat by sweat,” and 帰泥 to render the return to dust.

### Example Usage
> 自墮落以來，人間常態乃汗食帰泥。  
> Since the Fall, the human condition has been to eat by sweat and return to dust.

### Cultural Notes
Genesis 3 links labor and mortality. Work itself predates the Fall, but toil and frustration intensify afterward. The Hebrew emphasizes “sweat of your face,” underscoring bodily exertion.

帰泥 carries anthropological weight. Humanity is formed from the ground and returns to it. The phrase resonates with funeral liturgy and Ash Wednesday themes in Christian tradition.

The pairing of economic struggle and death suggests that the curse touches both daily life and ultimate destiny. Yet within Christian theology, this sentence stands in tension with later hope of resurrection.

In Dan'a'yo, 汗食帰泥 functions as a stark anthropological maxim. It may describe the fallen human estate, social realism about labor, or meditation on mortality.

### Tone and Sentiment
Grave, realistic, existential. It reflects toil, limitation, and the sober awareness of death.
