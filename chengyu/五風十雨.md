# 五風十雨 (五风十雨)

## Literal Meaning
- [五](../characters/五%20(char).md) — five
- [風](../characters/風%20(char).md) — wind
- [十](../characters/十%20(char).md) — ten
- [雨](../characters/雨%20(char).md) — rain

Literally: "Every five days a wind, every ten days a rain."

## Extended Meaning
A time of gentle weather and harmonious order — mild, timely winds and rains that neither drought nor flood, neither storm nor stagnation, sustaining a fertile land. Used figuratively for peace and prosperity under Heaven: a stable, well-governed realm where everything runs smoothly, without ever referring to actual weather in modern usage. Always positive; almost always paired with an implicit claim about good governance.

## Source and Origin
The idiom originates in the Han dynasty text *Huainanzi* (《淮南子·氾論訓》), a syncretic Daoist-Confucian classic compiled in the 2nd century BC:

> 五日一風，十日一雨，然後禾稼成熟。
> When there is a breeze every five days and a rain every ten, the grain ripens well.

The phrase described the ideal pattern of natural cycles — neither drought nor flood, neither windstorm nor stagnation — a climate of moderation and balance that ensured abundance. Over time, 五風十雨 came to symbolize political harmony and social peace, under the belief that the virtue of the ruler brings Heaven's favor, and Heaven's favor manifests in mild, timely weather.

## Standard Form
**五風十雨** (traditional) / **五风十雨** (simplified) — both forms current; no meaningful distinction beyond the general Mainland-simplified vs. Taiwan/Japan-traditional split.

Sometimes confused with (but distinct from) near-synonym idioms built on the same "favorable weather → good governance" image: 風調雨順 ("favorable winds and timely rain"), 國泰民安 ("the country prospers, the people live in peace"), and 天時地利 ("the harmony of Heaven's time and Earth's advantage"). These are related in theme, not variant spellings of this idiom.

## Cultural Notes
五風十雨 expresses the ancient conviction that cosmic harmony and moral virtue are intertwined. In Confucian cosmology, the state of the heavens mirrors the state of the realm — when a ruler governs with virtue (德治), the *qi* of Heaven and Earth flows smoothly, and gentle winds and timely rains bless the people; the phrase became a meteorological signature of a sage-king's reign. In Daoist reading it instead illustrates 自然之道, nature's self-regulation — neither excess nor deficiency, cyclical renewal, abundance tempered by restraint. Buddhist commentary has occasionally read the pairing of "wind" (change) and "rain" (nourishment) as an image of the Middle Way (中道), compassion and wisdom sustaining all beings together.

Usage varies little in register across the sphere — in every tradition this stays a learned, literary idiom rather than everyday speech. Modern Chinese uses it figuratively for peace, stability, and prosperity, often paired with claims of national flourishing. Japanese ごふうじゅうう is a learned literary term, occasionally appearing in essays or haiku commentary. Korean 오풍십우 appears in classical writing to describe periods of peace or a ruler's benevolent reign. Vietnamese *ngũ phong thập vũ* appears in poetic and historical prose as a metaphor for good governance.

## Example Sentences
1. 君行仁政，則五風十雨，年穀豐登。
   "When the ruler governs with benevolence, the winds and rains come in their time, and the harvest is rich."
2. 五風十雨の世、政治も安らかなり。
   "In an age of 'five winds and ten rains,' the polity too is tranquil."
3. 오풍십우라, 태평성세라 할 만하다.
   "It may well be called a time of five winds and ten rains — an age of peace and plenty."
4. Dưới triều vua hiền, ngũ phong thập vũ, dân an vật thịnh.
   "Under a virtuous king, there were five winds and ten rains — the people secure, the land prosperous."

## Sentiment
Strongly positive, auspicious, and literary. Invokes an idealized image of good governance rather than actual weather; almost always used to praise a ruler, an era, or a state of affairs as harmonious and well-ordered.
