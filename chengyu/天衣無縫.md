## Literal Meaning
- [天 (char)](../characters/天%20(char).md) — heaven; sky; celestial
- [衣](../characters/衣.md) — garment; robe; clothing
- [無 (char)](../characters/無%20(char).md) — without; lacking; there is no
- [縫](../characters/縫.md) — seam; stitching; join

Literally: "Heavenly garments have no seams."

The image is specific: the robes worn by celestial beings are not made by needle and thread — they are formed complete, without any join, any stitch, any evidence of construction. A seam is the visible trace of craft, the line where two edges were brought together by human work. The absence of a seam means the work is beyond craft entirely: perfect, seamless, showing no evidence of effort or process.

## Extended Meaning

Describes any work — literary, artistic, diplomatic, logical, or practical — so masterfully executed that no flaw, seam, or trace of labor can be detected. The surface is complete and smooth; wherever one looks, the joins are invisible. The idiom carries admiration for the highest level of craft, the kind that conceals itself.

The phrase can describe:
- A piece of writing whose argument flows without visible transitions or strain
- A performance with no breaks in character or lapses in technique
- A plan or stratagem with no detectable gaps or vulnerabilities
- An alibi, excuse, or narrative that holds together without any detectable contradiction
- Any finished work of such quality that its construction is invisible

A key nuance: 天衣無縫 is not just "excellent" — it specifically names *invisible craft*. The work of art that wears its labor plainly is not 天衣無縫. The idiom honors what conceals the hand behind it: the argument that reads like inevitability, the plan that seems to require no adjustment, the performance that feels effortless.

In modern Chinese usage, 天衣無縫 frequently appears in contexts of deception or strategy — an alibi or cover story so perfectly constructed that it contains no contradiction anyone can exploit. This application preserves the original image: the seam is what an opponent could pull apart; a seamless story gives them no purchase.

## Source and Origin

The idiom originates in a Tang dynasty *chuanqi* (傳奇) tale from **《靈怪録》** (*Records of the Miraculous and Strange*) by **張鷟** (Zhang Zhuo, c. 660–740 CE), one of the major collectors of supernatural fiction in the early Tang.

The story concerns **郭翰 (Guo Han)**, a man who on a summer night encounters a heavenly maiden descending from the sky. She visits him repeatedly, and in the course of their conversation he examines her celestial robes and notices they bear no seams. He asks her about it:

**《靈怪録·郭翰》:**
> 翰曰：「仙衣何無縫？」
> 女曰：「天衣本非針線為也。」
> *Guo Han said: "Why does your heavenly garment have no seams?"*
> *The maiden said: "Garments of heaven are not made with needle and thread."*

This exchange crystallized the phrase 天衣無縫. The heavenly maiden's answer is the logic of the idiom: what is divine in origin needs no needle, no thread, no join — it is simply complete. The image of divine craftsmanship without a seam became a natural metaphor for any human work so accomplished as to transcend the visible marks of labor.

The tale was widely preserved and retold; the phrase appears in the great Song dynasty miscellany **《太平廣記》** (*Extensive Records of the Taiping Era*, 978 CE), which collected hundreds of Tang *chuanqi* tales and gave many of them their lasting canonical form. By the Song dynasty, 天衣無縫 had fully separated from its supernatural origin and become a general-purpose idiom of literary and artistic praise.

## Standard Form

The Dan'a'yo form **天衣無縫** uses traditional characters throughout. The simplified Chinese form differs only in the last character:

- **天衣無縫** — traditional Chinese, Japanese (= Dan'a'yo form)
- **天衣无缝** — simplified Chinese (无 for 無, 缝 for 縫)

天, 衣 are identical across all scripts.

## Pronunciations
- **Mandarin (Pinyin):** tiān yī wú fèng (1–1–2–4)
- **Cantonese (Jyutping):** tin1 ji1 mou4 fung4
- **Japanese (on-yomi, hiragana):** てんいむほう (ten'i muhō)
- **Korean (Sino-Korean):** 천의무봉 (cheon-ui-mu-bong)
- **Vietnamese (Hán–Việt):** thiên y vô phùng

**Note on 縫**: In this compound 縫 is read *fèng* (4th tone, "seam; join"), the nominal reading. The verb reading *féng* (2nd tone, "to sew") is distinct in Mandarin, though both collapse to *fung4* in Cantonese and *ほう* in Japanese.

## Cultural Notes

**In Chinese**, 天衣無縫 is a standard idiom of high literary praise, appearing in criticism, journalism, and everyday speech when something is described as perfectly made or perfectly consistent. It has two dominant registers: the admiring (a flawless piece of writing, a seamless performance) and the strategic (an airtight alibi, a plan with no exploitable gap). Both are common. The idiom is fully naturalized and available at all levels of formality — a literature professor and a crime novelist both reach for 天衣無縫 without any sense of register mismatch, though it always carries a note of admiration for skill.

**In Japanese**, てんいむほう (ten'i muhō) is familiar to educated readers as a Sino-Japanese four-character idiom (*yojijukugo*) and appears in literary criticism, formal writing, and occasionally journalism. It is less common in everyday spoken Japanese than in Chinese, but the phrase is not archaic — it appears in reviews of literature, film, and theater, in discussions of diplomatic skill, and in legal contexts where an argument or testimony is described as airtight. The image of seamless heavenly cloth retains its elegance in Japanese.

**In Korean**, 천의무봉 (cheon-ui-mu-bong) is a Sino-Korean idiom of formal register, appearing in literary criticism, editorial writing, and discussions of strategic planning. Like the Japanese usage, it is more formal than colloquial — everyday Korean would more naturally express the same idea with native adjectives. The idiom is recognized by educated readers and carries the full weight of its classical Chinese resonance.

## Example Sentences

1. **Classical (《靈怪録·郭翰》, c. 7th–8th century CE):**
   翰曰：「仙衣何無縫？」女曰：「天衣本非針線為也。」
   *"Guo Han said: 'Why does your heavenly garment have no seams?' The maiden said: 'Garments of heaven are not made with needle and thread.'"*

2. **Modern Chinese (literary praise):**
   這篇文章結構天衣無縫，讀來毫無斧鑿之痕，令人歎服。
   *"The structure of this essay is flawlessly seamless — reading it, one finds no trace of the chisel, and can only marvel."*

3. **Modern Chinese (strategic):**
   他為自己安排的不在場證明天衣無縫，警方找不到任何漏洞。
   *"The alibi he had arranged for himself was seamlessly airtight — the police could find no gap anywhere."*

4. **English:**
   The story held together perfectly — every question had been anticipated, every timeline consistent, every detail accounted for. It was, in a word, 天衣無縫: not a seam in sight.

## Sentiment

**Positive** — a phrase of admiration for perfection and invisible craft. The original image of heavenly robes gives the idiom a faint celestial elevation: to be 天衣無縫 is to achieve something that transcends ordinary human labor.

**Neutral in the strategic sense.** When applied to alibis, plans, or deceptions, the idiom describes quality without moral judgment — it notes the absence of exploitable flaws, whether the thing being made seamless is a sonnet or a cover-up. The admiration is for the craft, regardless of what it is applied to.

**Versatile in register** — appearing comfortably in literary criticism, legal analysis, political commentary, and everyday admiring speech across the CJKV sphere.
