## Literal Meaning
- [焚](../characters/焚.md) — to burn; to set fire to
- [琴 (char)](../characters/琴%20(char).md) — qin; the guqin; a stringed instrument
- [煮](../characters/煮.md) — to boil; to cook in water
- [鶴 (char)](../characters/鶴%20(char).md) — crane

Literally: "Burn the qin; boil the crane."

The two acts are parallel outrages. The guqin (古琴) is not merely a musical instrument — it is the foremost symbol of scholarly cultivation in the Chinese tradition, associated with Confucian self-refinement, Daoist transcendence, and the spiritual life of the literati gentleman. To burn one for firewood is to treat the most refined object of culture as mere fuel. The crane is equally charged: in Chinese iconography it is the companion of immortals, the mount of Daoist sages, the emblem of longevity and transcendent purity. To boil one for soup is to reduce the very symbol of the sublime to a pot of broth.

Together the two acts form a compact image of someone entirely without aesthetic or spiritual sensibility — someone who, confronted with the beautiful, sees only the practical.

## Extended Meaning

Describes any act that destroys, degrades, or wastes something of refined beauty or cultural value through vulgar indifference — treating the elegant as merely functional, the sublime as merely edible, the irreplaceable as merely convenient. The person who commits 焚琴煮鶴 is not malicious; they simply lack the sensibility to recognize what they are destroying.

The idiom can describe:
- Tearing down a historic building for parking
- Serving a great wine in plastic cups at a catered lunch
- Using a rare manuscript as a doorstop
- Scheduling a committee meeting during the full moon viewing
- Any moment when the refined is sacrificed to the expedient without a second thought

A key quality: the offense is not cruelty but obtuseness. The philistine does not hate the beautiful — they simply do not register it. 焚琴煮鶴 names the specific pain of watching beauty be destroyed by someone who never noticed it was there.

## Source and Origin

The idiom originates in **《雜纂》** (*Zazuan*, "Miscellaneous Compendium") by **李商隱 (Li Shangyin, 813–858 CE)**, one of the most celebrated poets of the late Tang dynasty. The 《雜纂》 is not poetry but a collection of informal observations, lists, and witty taxonomies — a personal notebook of aesthetic and social judgments.

Within the 《雜纂》, Li Shangyin compiled a list of things he classified as **殺風景** (shā fēngjǐng, "killing the scenery" — ruining the elegance of a scene). The list is a catalog of aesthetic crimes:

**《雜纂·殺風景》:**
> 清泉濯足，花下曬褲，背山起樓，燒琴煮鶴，對花啜茗，松下喝道。
> *"Washing your feet in a clear spring; drying trousers under flowering trees; building a tower with its back to the mountain; burning the qin and boiling the crane; drinking tea hurriedly while facing flowers; shouting orders under a pine tree."*

Li Shangyin's original uses **燒琴** (shāo qín, "scorch the qin") rather than 焚琴 (fén qín, "burn the qin") — both characters mean to burn, but 焚 is more literary and became the dominant form of the idiom over subsequent centuries. The meaning is identical.

殺風景 itself became a standalone phrase in Chinese — any act that kills the mood or ruins an elegant atmosphere. But the specific image of burning the qin and boiling the crane proved the most memorable of Li Shangyin's examples, crystallizing into the four-character idiom 焚琴煮鶴 that outlasted the full list.

The idiom reflects Li Shangyin's sensibility as a poet of exquisite, melancholy elegance — a writer for whom the mismatch between beauty and its reception was a constant source of anguish. That 焚琴煮鶴 became the canonical form of his catalog suggests it captured something his readers recognized as the defining act of aesthetic obliviousness.

## Standard Form

The Dan'a'yo form **焚琴煮鶴** uses 焚 (the literary "burn"), following the more common post-Tang literary convention. The original 《雜纂》 text uses 燒 (the everyday "burn/scorch"), giving the older form:

- **焚琴煮鶴** — the standard post-Tang literary form (= Dan'a'yo form)
- **燒琴煮鶴** — Li Shangyin's original phrasing; retained as alias

All four characters are stable across traditional and simplified Chinese scripts — there are no simplified alternates.

## Cultural Notes

**In Chinese**, 焚琴煮鶴 belongs to a specific register of aesthetic and cultural criticism — the language of the refined person lamenting the philistine. It appears in literary essays, cultural commentary, and the rhetoric of preservation (of architecture, art, or musical traditions). The phrase carries a quality of pained wit rather than outrage: it is the idiom of someone who would rather mock the obtuse than fight them, who recognizes the futility of explaining why the qin matters to someone already stoking the fire. The associated concept of 殺風景 (scene-killing) remains active in modern Chinese — a mood-ruining remark, an ugly building beside a temple, an air conditioner mounted on a carved wooden gate can all be described as 殺風景.

**In Japanese**, ふんきんしゃかく is encountered primarily in literary and cultural contexts — criticism of insensitive development, destruction of traditional aesthetics, or the reduction of art to commercial utility. The underlying sensibility is deeply congruent with Japanese aesthetic traditions: the concept of 風情 (fūjō, "elegance of atmosphere") being destroyed by something utterly out of register resonates strongly. The idiom is not common in everyday speech but is recognizable to educated readers and appears in discussions of cultural loss.

**In Korean**, 분금자학 is a formal literary term appearing in classical criticism and essays on cultural heritage. The Confucian literati tradition in Korea gave the guqin and the crane the same symbolic weight they carry in Chinese culture, making the idiom's imagery immediately legible to any classically educated Korean reader. In modern Korean, the phrase appears in cultural commentary but is not colloquial.

## Example Sentences

1. **Classical (《雜纂·殺風景》, Li Shangyin, c. mid-9th century CE):**
   清泉濯足，花下曬褲，背山起樓，燒琴煮鶴，對花啜茗，松下喝道。
   *"Washing your feet in a clear spring; drying trousers under flowering trees; building a tower with its back to the mountain; burning the qin and boiling the crane; drinking tea hurriedly while facing flowers; shouting orders under a pine tree."*

2. **Modern Chinese:**
   在古剎旁修建停車場，簡直是焚琴煮鶴，令人扼腕。
   *"Building a car park beside an ancient temple is simply burning the qin and boiling the crane — it makes one clench one's wrists in frustration."*

3. **Modern Japanese:**
   せっかくの庭園に無粋なコンクリートの通路を作るとは、まさに焚琴煮鶴というほかない。
   *"To build a tasteless concrete pathway through this carefully designed garden is nothing other than burning the qin and boiling the crane."*

4. **English:**
   The developers had looked at the hillside pavilion, the ancient pines, the view across the river — and decided what the site really needed was a cell tower. 焚琴煮鶴. What else could you call it?

## Sentiment

**Negative** — a term of aesthetic condemnation, though the tone is more pained and wry than furious. The person being accused of 焚琴煮鶴 is not evil, which makes the situation worse: they are simply unable to see what they are destroying. The phrase belongs to a tradition of literati melancholy — the lament of the refined person living in an unrefined world — and carries that melancholy alongside its criticism.

**Elevated in register** — this is the vocabulary of cultural criticism and literary sensibility, not everyday complaint. To deploy 焚琴煮鶴 is to make an aesthetic judgment in the full weight of the classical tradition, invoking Li Shangyin's catalog of elegance-murder and placing the target in it.
