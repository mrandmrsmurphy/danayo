**Literal meaning:** “Words and actions are in agreement.”  
**Figurative meaning:** To practice what one says; consistency between speech and conduct.

### Pronunciations
- **Mandarin:** yán xíng yī zhì (2–2–1–4)
- **Cantonese (Jyutping):** jin4 hang4 jat1 zi3
- **Korean:** 언행일치
- **Japanese:** げんこういっち（言行一致）_genkō icchi_
- **Vietnamese (Hán-Việt):** ngôn hành nhất trí

### Variants
- **Traditional:** 言行一致
- **Simplified:** 言行一致
- Related expressions include **言行相符** (“words and actions correspond”) and **知行合一** (“knowledge and action are unified,” from Wang Yangming).
    

### Source / Origin

The idea is deeply rooted in **Confucian ethics**, where harmony between speech and action is a central virtue.

Early formulations appear in texts such as the **_Analects_ (《論語》)**, which emphasize that a **君子 (gentleman)** should be cautious in speech and earnest in action:

**“君子欲訥於言而敏於行。”**  
“The gentleman is slow to speak but quick to act.”

While the exact four-character idiom **言行一致** is a later standardization, it distills this long-standing principle: that **moral integrity requires alignment between what one says and what one does**.

### Cultural Notes

- In **Chinese**, 言行一致 is a common moral expectation, especially in evaluating **trustworthiness and character**.
    
- In **Japanese**, the phrase is often used in educational and ethical contexts, emphasizing **credibility and consistency**.
    
- In **Korean**, **언행일치** similarly refers to a person whose **actions match their words**, a key measure of integrity.
    

The idiom is strongly positive and often used as a **standard of personal virtue**.

### Example Usage

**Mandarin:**  
他一向言行一致，因此深受信任。  
_He consistently practices what he says, and is therefore deeply trusted._

**Japanese:**  
言行一致の人は信用される。  
_A person whose words and actions match is trusted._

**Korean:**  
그는 언행일치의 모범으로 여겨진다。  
_He is regarded as a model of consistency between words and actions._

### Sentiment

> **“Words and deeds as one — integrity proven in action.”**
