## Literal Meaning
- [一](../characters/一%20(char).md) — one
- [刻](../characters/刻.md) — a quarter-hour (a traditional time unit), or to carve
- [千](../characters/千.md) — thousand
- [金](../characters/金%20(char).md) — gold, money

Literally: "One quarter-hour is worth a thousand pieces of gold."

## Extended Meaning
A brief moment is exceedingly precious; time, once lost, cannot be repurchased. The exaggeration "a thousand gold" signals moral and aesthetic value rather than a literal price — this is not about wages or accounting, but about heightened awareness of a fleeting instant.

## Source and Origin
Attributed to a poem by Su Shi (蘇軾) of the Northern Song dynasty. The line reflects a literati sensibility shared across the classical Sinitic world, where cultivated leisure, fleeting beauty, and moral urgency all converge on the value of time. The expression arises from a poetic context rather than bureaucratic prose, which matters: 一刻 originally referred to a real traditional time unit, roughly fifteen minutes, not a vague "moment." Over time the phrase detached from its poetic setting and became a general admonition against wasting time.

## Cultural Notes
Used across CJKV languages to stress urgency, opportunity, or the irreplaceability of a moment. Common in educational, rhetorical, and even commercial contexts (advertising a limited-time offer, for instance), often without conscious awareness of its Song-dynasty literary origin — much like how an English speaker might say "time is money" without thinking of any particular source.

## Example Sentences
1. 春宵一刻値千金，花有清香月有陰。
   "A moment of a spring evening is worth a thousand pieces of gold — flowers give off their fragrance, the moon casts its shadow." (the Su Shi line the idiom is drawn from)
2. 試験前夜は一刻千金だ、無駄にできる時間はない。
   "The night before the exam, every moment counts — there's no time to waste."
3. In the final minutes of a close negotiation, every word is *yīkèqiānjīn* — one quarter-hour worth a thousand gold.

## Sentiment
Urgent but not anxious — an appreciative, almost wistful urgency rather than dread. Invoked to elevate the value of a fleeting moment, not to criticize wasted time after the fact.
