## Literal Meaning
- [一 (char)](../characters/一%20(char).md) — one
- [刀](../characters/刀.md) — blade
- [両 (char)](../characters/両%20(char).md) — both
- [断](../characters/断.md) — cut

Literally: "Cut in two with a single stroke of the blade."

## Extended Meaning
To make a clean break; to sever ties or resolve something decisively and completely. Always positive or neutral — praises resolve and clarity rather than criticizing rashness. Used both for concrete acts (ending a relationship, a contract, a partnership) and abstract ones (a decisive judgment, cutting through confusion).

## Source and Origin
The specific attestation for this idiom's modern currency is Zhu Ziqing's (朱自清) 1925 essay *The White Race, God's Favored Children* (《白種人——上帝的驕子》), written shortly after the May Thirtieth Incident:

> 決不婆婆媽媽的，決不粘粘搭搭的，一針見血，一刀兩斷，這正是白種人之所以為白種人。
> Never wishy-washy, never clingingly indecisive — straight to the point, one clean cut: this is precisely what makes a white person a white person.

Zhu uses the phrase as part of a character sketch of a white child he encounters on a Shanghai tram, reflecting more broadly on race, nationhood, and dignity in the aftermath of the incident. The underlying image — "one stroke of the sword" as a figure for total decisiveness — is much older than this specific essay, reflecting a long-standing martial trope in Han–Tang era literature where a single sword-stroke symbolized clarity and moral firmness; it was later absorbed into Buddhist, Confucian, and military rhetoric (in Chan/Zen usage, "to cut through delusion with one sword-stroke" describes sudden enlightenment, 頓悟). Zhu's essay is this idiom's specific, citable literary attestation in the vault's `origin` field, not the origin of the underlying image itself.

## Standard Form
**一刀兩斷** (traditional) / **一刀两断** (simplified) — both current. Occasionally seen in older texts extended to 一刀兩斷之決 ("a decision like cutting in two with one stroke").

## Cultural Notes
In Chinese, the idiom most often connotes ending a relationship — romantic, familial, or political — without lingering ties. In Japanese, 一刀両断 (ittō ryōdan) often conveys decisive judgment or rejection, such as cutting through falsehood or bureaucratic delay. In Korean, 일도양단 carries the same sense of deciding clearly and without hesitation. All three registers stay fairly formal/literary rather than casual conversation.

## Example Sentences
1. 他終於下定決心，與那家公司一刀兩斷。
   "He finally made up his mind to make a clean break with that company."
2. 彼は過去との関係を一刀両断にした。
   "He severed his ties with the past in one stroke."
3. 그는 망설임 없이 일도양단했다.
   "He made a clear, decisive cut without hesitation."

## Sentiment
To end or decide something decisively, cleanly, and irreversibly — like a single sword stroke dividing one into two.
