**Literal meaning:** “Hearing a hundred times is not equal to seeing once.”  
**Extended sense:** Direct experience surpasses repeated reports; firsthand observation outweighs hearsay.

**Source:**  
The saying appears in Han historical literature, including the **《漢書》**, associated with accounts of frontier diplomacy and the value of personal inspection over rumor. It circulated widely thereafter as a general maxim.

**Classical background:**  
The structure contrasts quantitative accumulation with qualitative immediacy. 百聞 represents repeated testimony, secondhand knowledge, or accumulated reports. 一見 represents direct encounter. The comparative 不如 shifts authority decisively toward perception.

Unlike purely moral aphorisms, this expression carries epistemological weight. It prioritizes empiricism over rumor and personal verification over inherited narrative. Its durability owes much to this practical orientation.

The phrase also reflects the administrative world of early imperial China, where officials were often dispatched to inspect distant regions. Reports might distort, exaggerate, or conceal. Seeing for oneself corrected the record.

**Modern usage:**  
Across the region, 百聞不如一見 is widely used in education, travel writing, journalism, and everyday speech. It can encourage experiential learning, personal visitation, or skepticism toward unverified claims. The tone is neutral and pragmatic.
