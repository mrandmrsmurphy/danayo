[[Biblical Chengyu]]
(Occasionally expanded as **道使人活，墨使人殺** in explanatory prose.)

### Pronunciation
- **Mandarin**: dào huó mò shā
- **Cantonese**: dou6 wut6 mak6 saat3
- **Japanese (hiragana)**: どうかつぼくさつ
- **Korean (Hangul)**: 도활묵살 (do-hwal-muk-sal)
- **Vietnamese (Sino-Vietnamese)**: đạo hoạt mặc sát

墨 “ink” stands in deliberate contrast to 道, functioning metaphorically for written letter.

### Literal Meaning
- **道** way, Word, Logos
- **活** to give life, to make alive
- **墨** ink, written letter
- **殺** to kill

Literal: “The Way gives life; the ink kills.”

### Figurative Meaning
A compressed rendering of the Pauline contrast between Spirit and letter. It asserts that the living divine Word, apprehended spiritually, brings life, whereas mere written code, taken in isolation from the Spirit, brings condemnation.

### Scriptural Source
The idiom condenses **2 Corinthians 3:6**:
> “For the letter killeth, but the spirit giveth life.”
Greek: _τὸ γὰρ γράμμα ἀποκτείνει, τὸ δὲ πνεῦμα ζωοποιεῖ._

Traditional Chinese translations typically render this as:
> “因為那字句是叫人死，精意是叫人活。”
Here, 字句 corresponds to “letter,” and 精意 or 聖靈 to “Spirit.” The Dan'a'yo form replaces 字句 with 墨 to sharpen the image: ink on the page versus living Word.

### Example Usage
> 徒守規条，不求道之活力，終至墨殺而非道活。  
> Clinging only to regulations without seeking the life of the Way results in the killing letter, not the living Word.

### Cultural Notes
In Pauline theology, the contrast is not between Scripture and Spirit, nor between Old and New Testaments as such. Rather, it is between law as external code and the Spirit’s inward work. Historically, this verse has been invoked:

- In debates over legalism.
- In Reformation discussions about Law and Gospel.
- In critiques of rigid literalism.

The choice of 道 is significant in East Asian Christian contexts. It resonates with Johannine theology, especially **John 1:1**, where the Logos is rendered 道 in translations. Thus 道活 subtly links Paul’s statement to the broader biblical theme of the living Word.

墨殺, by contrast, is intentionally stark. Ink is not evil in itself; Scripture is written in ink. The idiom critiques reduction of divine revelation to static text devoid of spiritual apprehension.

In a Dan'a'yo setting, the phrase serves as a warning against formalism while upholding the authority of revelation when spiritually received.

### Tone and Sentiment
Admonitory, theological, reformative. It cautions against lifeless legalism while affirming the vivifying power of the Spirit.
