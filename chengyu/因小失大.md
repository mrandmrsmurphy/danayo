## Literal Meaning
- [因](../characters/因.md) — because of; due to; following from
- [小 (char)](../characters/小%20(char).md) — small; minor; trivial
- [失](../characters/失.md) — to lose; to miss; to forfeit
- [大 (char)](../characters/大%20(char).md) — great; large; what matters most

Literally: "Because of the small, lose the large."

因 sets a causal relationship: it is the small thing — the petty gain, the minor detail, the trifling saving — that causes the large thing to be lost. 失大 (lose the large) is the consequence; 因小 (because of the small) is the mechanism. The four characters name a structural failure of proportion: the inability to subordinate minor considerations to the one thing that matters most, and the resulting destruction of that thing.

The idiom is not merely about losing something big by accident. It specifically indicts a choice — the choice to attend to the small at the expense of the large, to be consumed by trifles while the great thing slips away.

## Extended Meaning

To sacrifice a major interest, relationship, or objective because of excessive attention to a minor one — to let petty concerns destroy what is most important. The idiom covers a wide range of proportional failures:

- Saving money on a crucial component and suffering a catastrophic failure
- Quarreling over a trivial slight and destroying a major relationship or alliance
- Winning a minor argument and losing a major negotiation
- Skimping on a necessary investment and forfeiting the entire venture
- Prioritizing courtesy, custom, or face in a situation where decisive action is required

The phrase is always retrospective or preventative — it diagnoses a failure of strategic proportion. One does not say 因小失大 while making the decision; it is said afterward in judgment, or in advance as a warning.

## Source and Origin

The idiom's most famous classical illustration is the **Hongmen Banquet** (鴻門宴, 206 BCE), recorded in **《史記·項羽本紀》** (*Records of the Grand Historian*, House of Xiang Yu), the foundational text of Chinese historical narrative.

In the winter of 206 BCE, Xiang Yu's army vastly outnumbered Liu Bang's forces. Fan Zeng (范增), Xiang Yu's senior strategist, urged him to kill Liu Bang at the banquet — arguing that Liu Bang harbored imperial ambitions and must be eliminated while he was weak. Xiang Yu, moved by the occasion and reluctant to violate the rules of hospitality, hesitated; Liu Bang escaped with his adviser Zhang Liang's help. Fan Zeng's bitter reaction became one of the most quoted lines in Chinese history:

**《史記·項羽本紀》:**
> 唉！豎子不足與謀。奪項王天下者必沛公也，吾屬今為之虜矣。
> *"Alas! This boy is not worth counsel. The one who will take the realm from King Xiang is certainly the Lord of Pei. We shall be his prisoners now."*

Within fifteen years, Liu Bang had defeated Xiang Yu and founded the Han dynasty. The Hongmen Banquet became the canonical Chinese exemplar of 因小失大: Xiang Yu, prioritizing courtesy and the decorum of the moment (小) over the strategic necessity of eliminating his greatest rival (大), forfeited the empire.

The four-character compound 因小失大 crystallizes this lesson into portable form and appears throughout Han and post-Han political literature as the standard phrase for any failure of strategic proportion — the sacrifice of what matters most for the sake of what matters least.

## Standard Form

The Dan'a'yo form **因小失大** uses 因 (because of, causal) as the first character. Two classical variants differ only in the first character, each carrying a distinct moral weight:

- **因小失大** — Dan'a'yo; most neutral (因 = because of; a causal observation)
- **為小失大** — variant (為 = for the sake of; purposive — one actively pursues the small)
- **貪小失大** — variant (貪 = greedily coveting; the most moralizing form — small-mindedness is reframed as greed)

The 貪 form is the most common in modern Chinese and the basis of the Korean Sino-Korean reading 탐소실대. The 因 form has the longer classical pedigree and the more neutral register — it describes a structural failure rather than assigning the vice of greed. The 為 form is the least common. Simplified Chinese uses 为小失大 and 贪小失大 respectively.

## Cultural Notes

**In Chinese**, 因小失大 is a mainstay of political, business, and historical criticism — the standard phrase for diagnosing a failure of strategic proportion. It appears in editorial writing about policy failures (an official who cut safety costs and caused a disaster), business commentary (a company that saved on a critical component and collapsed), diplomatic narrative (a negotiator who won a minor point and lost the deal), and biographical assessment of historical figures. The Hongmen Banquet ensures that the phrase always carries an undertone of Xiang Yu's fate: even emperors and generals can destroy themselves by privileging the small. In modern Chinese, 因小失大 is used freely in formal and informal speech.

**In Japanese**, いんしょうしつだい is a well-established 四字熟語 in formal writing, business analysis, and historical commentary. It is somewhat less common in everyday conversation than in Chinese, but appears regularly in editorial and opinion writing where strategic failures of proportion need to be named. The Japanese register is more consistently formal than in Chinese.

**In Korean**, the 因 form 인소실대 is the classical reading, but the 貪 form 탐소실대 is more commonly encountered in modern usage. Both forms appear in formal writing, editorial commentary, and historical narrative. Korean Confucian emphasis on propriety and proportion gives the concept particular resonance: the person who fails because of small-mindedness is a figure of moral as well as strategic failure.

## Example Sentences

1. **Classical (《史記·項羽本紀》, c. 91 BCE):**
   唉！豎子不足與謀。奪項王天下者必沛公也，吾屬今為之虜矣。
   *"Alas! This boy is not worth counsel. The one who will take the realm from King Xiang is certainly the Lord of Pei. We shall be his prisoners now."*

2. **Modern Chinese:**
   他為了省下一筆採購費用，最終導致整個項目失敗——真是因小失大，得不償失。
   *"He cut the procurement budget to save a small amount, and ultimately caused the entire project to fail — a true case of 因小失大, gaining nothing and losing everything."*

3. **Modern Japanese:**
   細かいコスト削減にこだわった結果、肝心の品質が損なわれた。いんしょうしつだいとはまさにこのことだ。
   *"By fixating on minor cost reductions, they ended up compromising essential quality. This is precisely what いんしょうしつだい means."*

4. **English:**
   He saved three hundred pounds on the survey and spent thirty thousand on repairs six months later — 因小失大, the oldest failure in the book.

## Sentiment

**Critical and cautionary** — 因小失大 always names a mistake. It is used in retrospective judgment (this is what went wrong) or prospective warning (this is the mistake you are about to make), never as a neutral description. To say that someone has committed 因小失大 is to diagnose a failure of proportion and, implicitly, of wisdom: they did not know what mattered most, or knowing it, could not act accordingly.

**Proportional critique, not moral condemnation** — in the 因 form, the idiom describes a structural error rather than a moral failing. The 貪 form is more moralistic (small-mindedness reframed as greed); the 因 form is more analytical (cause and effect). In the 因 form, the lesson is strategic: see clearly what is large and what is small, and never sacrifice the former for the latter.
