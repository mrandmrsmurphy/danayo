## Origins

### 1. **Sen no Rikyū (千利休, 1522–1591)**
- The phrase is strongly associated with the Japanese **tea ceremony (茶道)**.    
- Sen no Rikyū taught that each tea gathering should be treated with utmost sincerity because it can never be repeated in exactly the same way.    
- He did not necessarily use the exact four-character phrase, but the idea is rooted in his teaching of _ichiza ichie_ (一座一会, “one sitting, one meeting”).    

### 2. **Yamanoue Sōji (山上宗二, d. 1590)**
- A disciple of Rikyū, he recorded in **《山上宗二記》** (Yamanoue Sōji ki) the ethos that each tea gathering is a once-in-a-lifetime event.    
- This is one of the earliest textual witnesses of the idea.    

### 3. **Ii Naosuke (井伊直弼, 1815–1860)**
- The daimyo and tea master who later codified the phrase in his treatise **《茶湯一会集》** (_Cha-no-yu Ichie-shū_, “Tea Gathering Anthology”), mid-19th century.    
- He popularized the fixed form **一期一会** as we know it.

## 一期一会
### Standard Form

**一期一会**

No character variants in standard usage. The phrase is written identically in Japanese and Chinese contexts. It is primarily a Japanese cultural expression; in Chinese it is understood but marked as Japanese in flavor.

### Pronunciation
- **Mandarin (Pinyin):** yī qī yī huì
- **Cantonese (Jyutping):** jat1 kei4 jat1 wui6
- **Japanese (native reading, hiragana):** いちごいちえ
- **Japanese (on-yomi):** いっきいちえ
- **Korean (Hangul):** 일기일회 (il-gi-il-hoe)
- **Vietnamese (Hán–Việt):** nhất kỳ nhất hội

### Literal Meaning
- 一 — one
- 期 — period, lifetime, appointed time
- 一 — one
- 会 — meeting

Literally: “One lifetime, one meeting.”

### Figurative Meaning
Each encounter is unique and will never occur in exactly the same way again.  
Therefore, one should treasure the present meeting as something singular and unrepeatable.

It expresses awareness of impermanence and attentiveness to the moment.

### Source and Origin
Closely associated with the Japanese tea master **Sen no Rikyū** and the philosophy of the **chanoyu** tea ceremony. The phrase was popularized in the Edo period, especially through tea culture writings.

Although composed of classical Chinese characters, it is a Japanese-coined idiom rather than a classical Chinese chengyu in origin.

### Cultural Notes
- Central to Japanese tea aesthetics: host and guest treat each gathering as once-in-a-lifetime.
- Reflects Buddhist-influenced sensitivity to impermanence.
- Commonly appears in calligraphy scrolls displayed in tea rooms.
- In modern Japanese, used broadly for meaningful encounters, graduations, farewells, or chance meetings.

In Chinese contexts, it is recognized but retains a distinctly Japanese cultural resonance.

### Example Sentences
1. 茶席之會，當以一期一会之心待之。
2. Their reunion felt like _ichigo ichie_—a moment that would never come again.
3. Graduation day reminded them that friendship, too, is _一期一会_.

### Sentiment
Reflective and poignant.  
Positive in tone, but tinged with transience and quiet solemnity.
