**Literal meaning:** “Paint the dragon and dot the eyes.”  
**Extended sense:** To add the final, decisive touch that brings something fully to life; the finishing detail that completes a work.

**Source:** The story appears in **歷代名畫記** by **張彥遠**, recounting an anecdote about the painter **張僧繇**.

**Classical background:**  
According to the account, Zhang Sengyou painted several dragons on a temple wall but deliberately left their eyes unfinished. When questioned, he replied that once the eyes were added the dragons would fly away. Pressed to demonstrate, he painted the pupils on two of them. Immediately thunder sounded and the painted dragons were said to have flown off the wall, leaving the remaining eyeless dragons behind.

Whether legendary or not, the story illustrates a principle of Chinese aesthetics. The eye is the point where vitality appears. The final stroke does not merely complete the image; it activates it.

The idiom therefore refers to a concluding detail that gives coherence or vitality to the whole work.

**Modern usage:**  
Across the region, 画龍点睛 is used to praise a final element that perfects an argument, artwork, performance, or plan. It often appears in literary criticism, rhetoric, and everyday commentary when a small addition transforms the whole.
