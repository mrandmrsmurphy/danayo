[Dan'a'yo Chengyu](chengyu/Dan'a'yo%20Chengyu.md)

## Literal Meaning
- [文](characters/文.md) — script, writing
- [音](characters/音.md) — sound, phonology
- [共 (char)](characters/共%20(char).md) — together, jointly
- [決 (char)](characters/決%20(char).md) — resolve, determine

Literally: "Script and sound resolved together."

## Extended Meaning

文音共決 is the guiding maxim of Literary Dan'a'yo orthography. It asserts that the written character (文) and its phonetic gloss (音) are inseparable in Literary mode: hanji alone leaves pronunciation ambiguous; Bopomofo alone loses semantic precision. Only the two together — hanji with ruby annotation — constitute a complete Dan'a'yo utterance in the literary register.

The idiom is prescriptive and absolute. It does not permit exceptions or stylistic latitude: Literary Dan'a'yo without ruby is an incomplete text; ruby without hanji is a phonetic transcription, not literature. 文音共決 names the policy of dual orthography and marks it as a resolved question, not an open one.

## Source and Origin

文音共決 is a coinage of the Dan'a'yo language project, formulated as a mnemonic policy statement for the orthographic rules of Literary Style (<ruby>文語<rt>ㄇㄨㄋ⼄</rt></ruby>).

The four characters were chosen with deliberate economy:

- 文 carries the weight of the classical Chinese tradition of *wenyan* (文言), script as the vessel of culture and learning.
- 音 invokes the phonological tradition — the rhyme tables, the fanqie system, and ultimately the Bopomofo annotation that makes Dan'a'yo's pronunciation explicit.
- 共 signals simultaneity and co-dependence: not "script *then* sound" or "sound *instead of* script," but both at once.
- 決 closes the phrase with finality: this is a settled matter, a resolution, not a suggestion.

The motto encapsulates the design intention of Dan'a'yo's Literary register: to be at once legible to readers of the Classical Chinese tradition and accessible to speakers of any of the five CJKV languages through the phonetic gloss. Neither Chinese Characters nor ruby annotation stands alone — both together secure the clarity and reach of the language.

## Cultural Notes

文音共決 belongs entirely to the Dan'a'yo metalinguistic tradition. It has no currency in Classical Chinese, Japanese, Korean, or Vietnamese discourse — its four characters, while individually common, do not form a recognized compound in any of the source languages. Its authority derives solely from its function as a constitutive rule of Literary Dan'a'yo.

In practice, the maxim governs the appearance of every literary text: a character without ruby is a typographical error; ruby without its corresponding hanji is a phonetic gloss, not a literary word. Editors and learners alike invoke 文音共決 as the canonical answer to the question *why must both appear?*

The idiom belongs to the same family of Dan'a'yo design maxims as [[一字一音]], which governs phonological determinacy, and stands in productive tension with it: 一字一音 guarantees that the sound can be read from the script; 文音共決 guarantees that both are always present together.

## Example Sentences

1. <ruby>文語<rt>ㄇㄨㄋ⼄</rt></ruby>において、すべての漢字には必ずルビが付く。文音共決、これが原則である。
   *(In Literary Dan'a'yo, every hanji must carry ruby. Script and sound resolved together — this is the principle.)*

2. The editor's note was brief: "文音共決." No further justification was given for the mandatory ruby annotation policy.

3. 文音共決之法，字無音則闕，音無字則虛。
   *(By the rule of script-and-sound-resolved-together: a character without sound is deficient; sound without a character is empty.)*

## Sentiment
Normative and authoritative in tone; neither positive nor negative in emotional valence.
Invoked as a citation of settled policy — the functional equivalent of *the rule is the rule*.
