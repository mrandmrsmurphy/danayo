[[Biblical Chengyu]]

### Pronunciation
- **Mandarin**: yǐn shí huān lè
- **Cantonese**: jam2 sik6 fun1 lok6
- **Japanese (hiragana)**: いんしょくかんらく
- **Korean (Hangul)**: 음식환락 (eumsik hwallak)
- **Vietnamese (Sino-Vietnamese)**: ẩm thực hoan lạc

### Literal Meaning
- **飲** drink
- **食** eat
- **歓／歡** joy, rejoicing
- **楽／樂** pleasure, delight

Literal: “Drink, eat, rejoice, delight.”

### Figurative Meaning
To indulge in food and pleasure without regard to higher or future concerns. Often conveys a spirit of short-sighted revelry, especially in the face of mortality or judgment.

Comparable to the English expression “Eat, drink, and be merry.”

### Scriptural Source
The phrase reflects biblical language found most clearly in:
- **Isaiah 22:13** — “Let us eat and drink, for tomorrow we die.”
- **1 Corinthians 15:32** — Paul cites the same line.
- **Luke 12:19** — The rich fool: “Eat, drink, and be merry.”

### Example Usage
> 世人但求飲食歓楽，不念来世之審判。  
> Many in the world seek only to eat, drink, and rejoice, without regard for the judgment to come.

### Cultural Notes
In Christian discourse, this saying is rarely neutral. In the Greco-Roman world, similar sentiments were associated with Epicurean resignation in the face of death. The biblical usage is typically ironic or critical, highlighting spiritual blindness.

Within Dan'a'yo, the idiom functions as a compact moral warning. It stands in tension with ascetic strands of Christian tradition, yet also contrasts with legitimate feasting traditions such as Passover or the Messianic banquet motif. The phrase therefore carries an implicit theological contrast: temporal indulgence versus eschatological hope.

Because the wording overlaps with common Chinese compounds for food and joy, it may superficially appear benign in broader CJK contexts. Its biblical resonance, however, shifts the tone toward admonition rather than celebration.

### Tone and Sentiment
Cautionary, morally critical, often tinged with irony.
