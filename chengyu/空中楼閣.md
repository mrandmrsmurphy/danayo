## Literal Meaning
- [空](../characters/空%20(char).md) — empty
- [中](../characters/中%20(char).md) — midst, within
- [楼](../characters/楼.md) — watchtower, storied building
- [閣](../characters/閣.md) — pavilion, chamber

Literally: "A pavilion in the air."

## Extended Meaning
Something magnificent in imagination but lacking real foundation — an unreal plan, theory, or dream. Can be used philosophically (of the illusory nature of appearances generally) or practically (of one specific impractical scheme); always at least mildly critical, never a compliment.

## Source and Origin
The phrase appears first in Buddhist literature, in the *Mahāprajñāpāramitāśāstra* (《大智度論》卷二十七, attributed to Nāgārjuna, translated into Chinese around the 4th century CE):

> 譬如空中樓閣，雖現似有，實不可得。
> Like a pavilion in the air — though it appears to exist, it cannot truly be attained.

In Mahāyāna Buddhist philosophy, this image illustrated the emptiness (śūnyatā) of phenomena — all forms are apparent but lack inherent substance. Confucian and other later literary writers adopted the same image more generally, for beautiful but baseless theories or projects, detached from the original doctrinal context.

## Standard Form
**空中樓閣** (traditional) / **空中楼阁** (simplified) — both current.

## Cultural Notes
In Chinese, the idiom can be philosophical (the illusory nature of the world) or practical (an impractical scheme): 這計畫如空中樓閣，不可行 ("This plan is like a pavilion in the sky — unworkable"). In Japanese, 空中楼閣 (kūchū rōkaku) is used metaphorically for abstract theories with no grounding in fact. In Korean, 공중누각 carries the same sense — "an airy castle," impractical but often elegant in conception. In Vietnamese, *không trung lâu các* keeps both the literal and metaphorical sense, "grand but unreal."

Read across three traditions of thought: in Buddhist terms, reality is transient and insubstantial, and what appears beautiful is fundamentally void. In a Confucian frame, theory must rest on practice, or governance and morality themselves become "air-pavilions." In modern usage generally, it's simply a warning against idealism unmoored from structure. The idiom is also frequently paired with 鏡花水月 ("flowers in a mirror, moon in water") as a matched set of illusory-beauty metaphors — 鏡花水月 for beauty seen but untouchable, 空中樓閣 for grandeur imagined but ungrounded — though the two are distinct idioms, not variants of each other.

## Example Sentences
1. 沒有實際基礎的理論，不過是空中樓閣。
   "A theory without real basis is nothing but a pavilion in the air."
2. 彼の理想は空中楼閣にすぎない。
   "His ideals are no more than a castle in the air."
3. 그의 계획은 공중누각이었다.
   "His plan was a tower floating in the air."
4. Giấc mơ ấy chỉ là không trung lâu các mà thôi.
   "That dream was merely a pavilion in the sky."

## Sentiment
Lofty yet rootless; dazzling yet empty — a castle built upon air. Mildly to strongly critical, aimed at ideas or plans that impress in the abstract but cannot be realized.
