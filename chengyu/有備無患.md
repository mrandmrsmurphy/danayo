## Literal Meaning
- [有 (char)](characters/有%20(char).md) — have, possess; there is
- [備](characters/備.md) — prepare; preparation, readiness
- [無 (char)](characters/無%20(char).md) — lack; there is no
- [患 (char)](characters/患%20(char).md) — disaster, calamity, affliction, trouble

Literally: "Having preparation, there is no calamity" — if you have prepared, trouble cannot reach you.

The structure is a conditional compressed into two parallel phrases: 有備 (having preparation) → 無患 (no disaster). The inverse is left implicit but unmistakable: 無備 (lacking preparation) → 有患 (there will be calamity). The phrase argues by symmetry.

## Extended Meaning
有備無患 is the classical Chinese formulation of preparedness as a universal principle: readiness in advance is the reliable protection against future trouble. The phrase applies to governance, warfare, medicine, infrastructure, emergency management, and personal life — wherever the difference between having prepared and not having prepared determines whether a crisis becomes a disaster.

The phrase is prescriptive rather than descriptive: it states a principle to act on, not an observation about the past. Its force is the force of practical wisdom — not a moral claim but a strategic one. Prepare, and you will be safe. The only question is whether you heed the counsel before or after the trouble arrives.

有備無患 pairs directly with its opposite: [[臨渇掘井]] ("preparing only when the need is already upon you"). Together they form a complete teaching — the positive prescription and the cautionary failure. 有備無患 says: prepare now. 臨渇掘井 says: this is what it looks like when you don't.

## Source and Origin
**《尚書·説命中》** (Shujing / Book of Documents, Yuming, Counsels of Shuo):

> 「惟事事乃其有備，有備無患。」
> "In all affairs, for each matter have preparation; having preparation, there is no disaster."

Context: Fu Yue (傅説) was a builder discovered by King Wu Ding of Shang (c. 1250 BC) working in forced labor on a construction project. The king recognized his wisdom in a dream, tracked him down, and appointed him as chief minister — one of the great stories in Chinese tradition of extraordinary talent found in humble circumstances. The speech from which this phrase comes is Fu Yue's counsel on governance: be prepared in all things, because preparation is the only reliable protection against disaster.

The 尚書 (Book of Documents) is one of the Five Classics (五経), the foundational texts of Confucian civilization, traditionally attributed to the earliest sage-kings and transmitted through Confucius. A phrase from the 尚書 carries the highest canonical weight available to classical Chinese rhetoric — it is cited as if from a founding document of civilization itself.

The companion sentence that frames the same principle from the other direction: 「居安思危，思則有備，有備無患。」 — "When at peace, think of danger; thinking of danger, you will be prepared; being prepared, there is no disaster." This extended form, also cited in the 《左傳》, is the full logical chain: peace → foresight → preparation → safety.

## Cultural Notes
有備無患 is among the most practically applied chengyu — it surfaces in military strategy, disaster risk reduction, public health, civil defense, and project management wherever preparation-before-crisis is the subject. It is the classical Chinese formulation of what the Boy Scout motto states in English ("Be Prepared") and Japanese scouting renders as 備えよ常に (sonaeyō tsune ni, "always be prepared").

In Japanese, the common proverb form is 備えあれば憂いなし (sonaeareba urei nashi) — "if you have preparation, there is no worry" — a direct kun-yomi rendering of 有備無患 that is widely recognized across all generations. The four-character on-yomi form ゆうびむかん is more formal and Classical but the kun-yomi proverb is more colloquially alive.

In Korea, 유비무환 is particularly prominent in military and civil defense contexts. South Korea's civil defense infrastructure — evacuation drills, emergency broadcast systems, shelter networks — operates under this stated principle. The phrase is standard in official communications about national security preparedness.

有備無患 belongs to a cluster of preparedness proverbs that together constitute a classical Chinese philosophy of foresight:
- 居安思危 — "when at peace, think of danger"
- 有備無患 — "having preparation, no disaster"
- 臨渇掘井 — the failure mode: "preparing only when the crisis has arrived"

The three form a sequence: first the mental posture (居安思危), then the action (有備無患), then the consequence of neglecting both (臨渇掘井).

## Example Sentences
1. 《尚書·説命中》:「有備無患。」
   "Having preparation, there is no disaster." (Fu Yue's counsel to King Wu Ding, c. 1250 BC)
2. 《左傳·襄公十一年》:「居安思危，思則有備，有備無患。」
   "When at peace, think of danger; thinking of danger, you will be prepared; being prepared, there is no disaster."
3. 備えあれば憂いなし — 有備無患の精神で、避難袋を今すぐ準備しておこう。
   "Better safe than sorry — with the spirit of 有備無患, let's prepare the emergency kit now."

## Sentiment
Practical and affirmative. 有備無患 is not fearful or pessimistic — it does not dwell on disaster but on the sufficiency of preparation. Its tone is confident: prepare, and there will be nothing to fear. The phrase is deployed most often as a call to action, a justification for investment in preparedness, or an endorsement of proactive planning before any visible threat has materialized.
