[Dan'a'yo Chengyu](chengyu/Dan'a'yo%20Chengyu.md)

## Literal Meaning
- [形 (char)](characters/形%20(char).md) — form, shape; bound form
- [助](characters/助.md) — help, assist
- [顅](characters/顅.md) — reveal, make manifest
- [理](characters/理.md) — principle, structure, underlying logic

Literally: "Form helps reveal structure."

## Extended Meaning

形助顅理 names the morphological principle of <ruby>単亜語<rt>ㄉㄚㄋ·ㄚ·⼄</rt></ruby>: bound forms — prefixes, suffixes, and postpositions — function not as decorative additions but as structural guides that make the logic of a sentence legible. What is implicit or ambiguous in Classical Chinese or Vietnamese becomes explicit; what Japanese and Korean speakers intuit through particles is carried intact into the SVO framework of Dan'a'yo.

The maxim elevates seemingly minor forms (形) — case particles, aspect markers, modality suffixes — to the rank of essential helpers (助). A sentence without them is grammatically incomplete; with them, the underlying principle (理) of the utterance is made visible (顅) to all learners across the CJKV sphere.

## Source and Origin

形助顅理 is a coinage of the Dan'a'yo language project, formulated as a companion maxim to [[一字一音]] and [[文音共決]]. Where those idioms address phonology and orthography respectively, 形助顅理 addresses morphology — the layer of small forms that bind the sentence together.

The four characters were chosen to capture a specific design decision:

- 形 (*form*) designates the morphological class at issue: not content words, but the small, often unstressed bound forms that carry grammatical information — the helpers and hinges of the sentence.
- 助 (*assist*) frames these forms as servants of meaning rather than obstacles to it. They do not impede the classical root; they clarify it.
- 顅 (*reveal, make manifest*) insists that this clarification is not redundant but essential: without the particle or suffix, the sentence's structural logic remains hidden to learners whose first language does not supply it by default.
- 理 (*principle, structure*) is the object made visible — not merely the meaning of an individual word, but the relational logic of the whole utterance: who acts, what is affected, when, how, under what condition.

The maxim responds directly to a tension in Dan'a'yo's classical heritage. Classical Chinese and Classical Vietnamese are largely analytic: grammatical relations emerge from word order and context, not from morphological marking. Japanese and Korean, by contrast, use rich particle systems to make these relations explicit. Dan'a'yo resolves this tension not by choosing one tradition, but by adopting the Japanese–Korean insight — that small bound forms are worth the cost — and encoding it as a design principle: 形助顅理.

## Cultural Notes

形助顅理 articulates a design choice that cuts across the major CJKV typological divide. Chinese and Vietnamese speakers learning Dan'a'yo may initially experience the bound forms as superfluous — their native grammars supply context and word order instead. Japanese and Korean speakers, accustomed to particles, will find the system familiar but reoriented to SVO word order. The maxim addresses both audiences: the forms are not noise, but signal; not burden, but guide.

The idiom belongs to the growing body of Dan'a'yo metalinguistic vocabulary alongside [[一字一音]] (phonological determinacy) and [[文音共決]] (dual orthography). Together these three maxims define the three layers of the language's design: sound, script, and structure. 形助顅理 completes the triad at the morphological level.

## Example Sentences

1. <ruby>単亜語<rt>ㄉㄚㄋ·ㄚ·⼄</rt></ruby>の助詞は小さくとも、文の理を顕す。形助顅理、これが文法の要である。
   *(Dan'a'yo's particles are small, but they reveal the structure of the sentence. "Form helps reveal structure" — this is the heart of the grammar.)*

2. A learner from a Classical Chinese background asked why the postpositions were required. The answer: 形助顅理 — the form exists to make the principle visible.

3. 形雖微而助理，是謂形助顅理之義。
   *(Though the form is small, it assists the principle — this is the meaning of 形助顅理.)*

## Sentiment
Explanatory and normative in tone; neither celebratory nor cautionary.
Invoked to justify the presence of bound morphology — small forms that might otherwise seem redundant.
Counterpart to [[文音共決]] at the morphological layer; both are design rationales, not cultural aphorisms.
