## Literal Meaning
- [色](../characters/色.md) — form; appearance; color; sensory phenomenon; *rūpa*
- [即 (char)](../characters/即%20(char).md) — precisely is; is none other than; immediately identical with
- [是 (char)](../characters/是%20(char).md) — is; to be
- [空 (char)](../characters/空%20(char).md) — empty; void; without inherent existence; *śūnyatā*

Literally: "Form precisely is emptiness."

即是 is a reinforced copula — stronger than 是 alone. It means not merely "is" but "is precisely, is none other than, is immediately identical with." The phrase asserts identity, not similarity or approximation: form does not resemble or tend toward emptiness; form IS emptiness. There is no form that is not empty, and no emptiness that is other than form.

色 (rūpa in Sanskrit): all phenomena as they appear — material form, color, sensory phenomena, the entire domain of what can be seen, touched, and experienced. In Buddhist philosophy 色 is the first of the five aggregates (五蘊) that constitute what is ordinarily called a "self" or "being."

空 (śūnyatā in Sanskrit): the absence of inherent, independent, self-sufficient existence. Phenomena do not have an intrinsic nature of their own; they arise in dependence on causes and conditions and have no fixed, standalone being. They are not nothing — they appear and function — but they are empty of the solidity and independence that unexamined experience attributes to them.

## Extended Meaning

The statement 色即是空 is always paired with its complement **空即是色** (emptiness is precisely form), forming a complete dialectical claim:

> 色即是空，空即是色。
> *Form is precisely emptiness; emptiness is precisely form.*

The two halves work together and correct each other. The first (色即是空) guards against reifying appearances — forms have no inherent existence, do not need to be clung to, do not need to be feared as permanent threats. The second (空即是色) guards against nihilism — emptiness is not a separate realm of void beyond phenomena; it is the very nature of phenomena as they are. The teaching is not that reality is nothing but that the apparent solidity of things conceals their dependence; and the apparent void of emptiness is always expressed through form.

In Chinese Buddhist discourse the phrase came to be used more broadly:
- As shorthand for the entire Prajnaparamita teaching on the insubstantiality of phenomena
- As a reminder that what seems most real and solid is not independently existent
- As encouragement to practitioners: attachment to things that appear solid causes suffering; insight into their emptiness liberates

In Japanese popular usage, 色 carries a second meaning — "sexuality, sensual desire, erotic attraction" — giving the phrase a well-known double reading: "lust is void; desire is emptiness." This reading is philosophically coherent (desire for things that lack inherent existence is itself a form of illusion) and has made the phrase a frequent touchstone in Japanese literature, film, and popular culture, where it can simultaneously invoke Mahayana philosophy and comment on the vanity of erotic attachment.

## Source and Origin

色即是空 is from the **般若波羅蜜多心経** (Prajnaparamitā Hridaya Sūtra, Heart Sutra), the most compressed statement of Prajnaparamita ("perfection of wisdom") philosophy in the Mahayana Buddhist canon. The sutra is presented as Avalokitesvara Bodhisattva (觀自在菩薩, Guanyin) explaining the nature of emptiness to the monk Shariputra (舍利子).

The most widely used Chinese translation is by **玄奘 (Xuánzàng, 602–664 CE)**, completed in 649 CE — the version recited daily in Buddhist temples throughout East Asia. An earlier translation by **鳩摩羅什 (Kumārajīva, 344–413 CE)** also survives.

The Sanskrit original:
> rūpaṃ śūnyatā, śūnyatā eva rūpaṃ
> *Form is emptiness; emptiness is form.*

**Xuanzang's Chinese:**
> 舍利子，色不異空，空不異色，色即是空，空即是色，受想行識，亦復如是。
> *"Shariputra: form is not different from emptiness; emptiness is not different from form. Form is precisely emptiness; emptiness is precisely form. Sensation, perception, mental formations, and consciousness — all are likewise."*

The passage proceeds through the five aggregates (五蘊: 色受想行識 — form, sensation, perception, mental formations, consciousness), stating that each is equally without inherent existence. 色即是空 is the first and most famous statement of a claim that applies to the entire structure of experience.

The philosophical background is the **Madhyamaka** tradition of Nāgārjuna (龍樹, c. 150–250 CE), which developed the doctrine of śūnyatā as the lack of svabhāva (own-nature) in all phenomena. The Heart Sutra distills this into the shortest possible formulation, and 色即是空 is the nucleus of that distillation.

## Standard Form

- **色即是空** — Dan'a'yo; modern Chinese; modern Japanese (即 = shinjitai of 卽)
- **色卽是空** — traditional Chinese; older Japanese texts (卽 for 即)

色, 是, and 空 are stable across all scripts. The simplified Chinese form is identical to modern traditional for this phrase.

## Cultural Notes

**In Chinese**, 色即是空 is one of the most widely recognized phrases in the entire Buddhist canon — recognizable to virtually any educated Chinese speaker regardless of religious affiliation. In the context of Buddhist practice, it is a doctrinal statement recited in sutras and used as a focus for meditation. In secular literary and philosophical usage it appears as a condensed invocation of the entire teaching on impermanence and insubstantiality: the beauty that fades, the wealth that vanishes, the accomplishment that crumbles — all are 色, and all are 空. The phrase appears in biographical writing, poetry, and essays whenever the writer wishes to invoke the Buddhist perspective on the transience of appearances.

**In Japanese**, 色即是空 (shikisoku-ze-kū) is among the most deeply embedded Buddhist phrases in the culture — known to virtually every Japanese person, recited in funerals and memorial services, quoted in literature and film across the full range from solemn to comic. The double meaning of 色 (form/phenomenon and erotic attraction/lust) has given the phrase a second life in Japanese popular culture: 色即ぜねれいしょん (a 2019 film) takes the title as its central conceit, and the phrase appears throughout Japanese manga and literature as a knowing philosophical comment on desire. The two readings — the profound and the playful — coexist without canceling each other; the humor depends on the profundity.

**In Korean**, 색즉시공 is a well-known Buddhist phrase that appears in sutra recitation, Buddhist education, and literary quotation. Korean Buddhist temples (primarily of the Zen/Seon tradition) recite the Heart Sutra regularly, giving the phrase a familiar liturgical resonance. In popular culture it appears as a philosophical shorthand for the insubstantiality of worldly things.

## Example Sentences

1. **Classical (《般若波羅蜜多心経》, Xuanzang tr. 649 CE):**
   舍利子，色不異空，空不異色，色即是空，空即是色，受想行識，亦復如是。
   *"Shariputra: form is not different from emptiness; emptiness is not different from form. Form is precisely emptiness; emptiness is precisely form. Sensation, perception, mental formations, and consciousness — all are likewise."*

2. **Modern Chinese (literary):**
   繁華散盡，色即是空，他終於明白了那些曾以為永恆的，不過是借來的光。
   *"The splendor exhausted, form is emptiness — he finally understood that what he had thought permanent was only borrowed light."*

3. **Modern Japanese (double meaning):**
   色即是空——恋に落ちた彼は、その言葉が哲学的な意味だけでないことを、身をもって知ることになった。
   *"色即是空 — having fallen in love, he was about to learn firsthand that the phrase had a meaning beyond the philosophical."*

4. **English:**
   She had spent forty years accumulating what seemed most solid, and lost it in a morning — 色即是空, form is emptiness, the stone that was never stone.

## Sentiment

**Philosophically profound; affectively liberating** — 色即是空 is not a statement of despair or nihilism. In the Mahayana Buddhist framework it delivers, the recognition that phenomena have no inherent existence is experienced as liberating: attachment to what was never solid dissolves, and with it the suffering that attachment generates. The phrase carries a quality of clear-eyed compassion — it tells the truth about appearances not to deny their beauty but to free the perceiver from a specific kind of suffering.

**Tonally complex in Japanese** — the double meaning of 色 (form/lust) allows the phrase to inhabit registers from solemn liturgy to philosophical humor to wry commentary on desire. This tonal range is specific to Japanese usage; in Chinese and Korean the phrase remains consistently within the register of Buddhist philosophical discourse.
