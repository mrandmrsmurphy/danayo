[[Biblical Chengyu]]

### Pronunciation
- **Mandarin**: shí rén bù miè
- **Cantonese**: sap6 jan4 bat1 mit6
- **Japanese (hiragana)**: じゅうにんふめつ
- **Korean (Hangul)**: 십인불멸 (sip-in-bul-myeol)
- **Vietnamese (Sino-Vietnamese)**: thập nhân bất diệt

### Literal Meaning
- **十** ten
- **人** persons
- **不** not
- **滅** destroy, wipe out

Literal: “For ten persons, not destroy.”

### Figurative Meaning
A concise summary of Abraham’s intercession for Sodom: if ten righteous people were found, the city would be spared. By extension, it signifies the preserving power of a righteous minority.

### Scriptural Source
The idiom condenses **Genesis 18:23–33**, especially verse 32:
> “Oh let not the Lord be angry, and I will speak yet but this once: Peradventure ten shall be found there. And he said, I will not destroy it for ten’s sake.”

Traditional Chinese translations render this along the lines of:
> “為這十個的緣故，我也不毀滅那城。”

The Dan'a'yo form removes narrative framing and preserves the juridical core: ten persons suffice to stay destruction.

### Example Usage
> 一地若有義者，或可成十人不滅之恩。  
> If a place has righteous ones, it may yet receive the mercy of “ten and not destroyed.”

### Cultural Notes
This episode stands at the intersection of divine justice and mercy. Abraham’s negotiation descends numerically from fifty to ten, revealing both bold intercession and divine patience.

In Jewish tradition, ten later became the minimum number for certain communal religious acts, though that development is historically distinct. In Christian interpretation, the passage illustrates the mediatorial role of the righteous and anticipates the broader theme that the many may be spared for the sake of the few.

Theologically, 十人不滅 emphasizes two principles:
1. God’s judgment is not arbitrary but responsive to righteousness.
2. A faithful minority has covenantal significance beyond its size.

Within Dan'a'yo discourse, the phrase often expresses hope that moral integrity in a remnant can restrain wider corruption or judgment.

### Tone and Sentiment
Hopeful yet solemn. It conveys restrained mercy, intercessory boldness, and the preserving weight of righteousness.
