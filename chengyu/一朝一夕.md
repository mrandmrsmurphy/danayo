### Standard Form

**一朝一夕**  
No major variant forms. Written identically in Traditional and Simplified Chinese. The same characters are used in Japanese, Korean (Hanja contexts), and Vietnamese classical usage.

### Pronunciation
- **Mandarin (Pinyin):** yī zhāo yī xī  
- **Cantonese (Jyutping):** jat1 ziu1 jat1 zik6
- **Japanese (on-yomi, hiragana):** いっちょういっせき
- **Korean (Hangul):** 일조일석 (il-jo-il-seok)
- **Vietnamese (Hán–Việt):** nhất triêu nhất tịch

### Literal Meaning
- 一 — one
- 朝 — morning (also “dynasty,” but here “morning”)
- 一 — one
- 夕 — evening

Literally: “One morning and one evening.”

### Figurative Meaning

A very short period of time.  
Most often used in negative constructions to indicate that something cannot be accomplished quickly.

Common implication: “not in a day or two.”


### Source and Origin
The pairing of 朝 and 夕 as a merism for a full day is classical. The fixed four-character form appears in early prose and historiographical literature to denote short duration. It became standardized as a chengyu expressing brevity or suddenness.

### Cultural Notes
- Frequently used in formal or literary contexts.
- Often appears in expressions such as:
    - 非一朝一夕之功 — “not the achievement of a single morning and evening”
- Carries a sober, measured tone.
- Common in moral, political, and educational discourse emphasizing gradual effort.

### Example Sentences
1. 學問非一朝一夕之功。
2. Trust cannot be built in _yī zhāo yī xī_.
3. Reform of such institutions is not achieved in a single morning and evening.

### Sentiment
Neutral in itself.  
Often used in serious or reflective contexts, emphasizing patience, duration, and long-term effort.
