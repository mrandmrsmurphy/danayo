[Dan'a'yo Chengyu](chengyu/Dan'a'yo%20Chengyu.md)

>[!info] For a demonstration of this principle in grammatical context, see [[文法 - 05形態]].

## Literal Meaning
- [修](../characters/修.md) — to cultivate; to refine; to practice; to repair
- [飾](../characters/飾.md) — to adorn; to modify; to attribute
- [先](../characters/先.md) — prior; first; in advance; preceding
- [行 (char)](../characters/行%20(char).md) — to go; to proceed; to act

Literally: "Modifiers go first."

修飾 names the grammatical relationship of modification or attribution — the adjectival, clausal, or genitive element that specifies, qualifies, or restricts a head. 先行 is "to go first, to precede, to lead." Together the phrase states a structural rule in its simplest form: whatever modifies something comes before it.

## Extended Meaning

修飾先行 is the foundational word-order principle of Dan'a'yo: every modifier precedes the element it modifies, without exception (with the narrow exceptions noted below). The rule applies uniformly across all grammatical categories:

**Nominal domain** — determiners, quantifiers, and attributive phrases precede the noun:
- 此人 (this person), 其書 (that book)
- 毎日 (every day), 何人 (which person), 皆民 (all people)

**Attributive constructions** — clausal and phrasal modifiers precede the noun and are marked by the linker 之:
- 捕已魚之男人 — "the man who caught the fish" (literally: caught-fish-LINK man)
- The entire modifying clause precedes 男人; 之 marks the attributive boundary

**Adverbial domain** — adverbials and manner expressions precede the verb they modify:
- The verb phrase is always the rightmost content element

**Genitive** — possessor or qualifying noun precedes head: A之B = "B of A"

**Exceptions** (limited right-branching): postpositions follow their complements, and tense/aspect markers follow the verb. These introduce minimal right-branching within an otherwise uniformly head-final structure.

The effect is a consistent left-to-right structure in which the reader accumulates all modifying material before arriving at the head. Phrase structure is predictable: knowing 修飾先行 is in force, a reader encountering a sequence of material before 之 or a final noun knows exactly what role that material plays.

## Source and Origin

修飾先行 is a Dan'a'yo original principle — part of the deliberate design of the language rather than a phrase borrowed from existing East Asian literary tradition. It names a syntactic choice: where natural Classical Chinese allows considerable word-order flexibility (postposed modifiers, split constructions, context-dependent omissions), Dan'a'yo regularizes the dominant Classical Chinese and Sino-Xenic tendency toward modifier-before-head into an absolute rule.

The choice reflects the Sinospheric languages Dan'a'yo is designed to serve. Japanese and Korean are strictly head-final — all modifiers precede without exception. Classical Chinese is predominantly head-final in its attributive constructions (A之B, relative clause + 之 + noun) but allows variation. Dan'a'yo adopts the stricter Japanese/Korean pattern, ensuring maximum structural overlap with the two most head-final members of the East Asian sphere while remaining grounded in the Classical Chinese construction 之.

The phrase 修飾先行 therefore names both a grammatical fact (this is how Dan'a'yo works) and a design declaration (this is how Dan'a'yo was made to work). It is elevated from tendency to rule.

## Cultural Notes

**Within Dan'a'yo**, 修飾先行 functions as a technical term of the language's own grammar — the phrase used to name and teach the modifier-first rule to learners and to invoke it as authority in grammatical discussion. It appears in the grammar book wherever word order is addressed, and is the single most structurally consequential rule for constructing Dan'a'yo phrases correctly.

**In the context of East Asian syntax**, the principle aligns Dan'a'yo most closely with Japanese and Korean, both of which are rigorously head-final. The Classical Chinese pattern is similar but less strict; the Dan'a'yo regularization represents a choice to harmonize with the Altaic members of the Sinosphere at the cost of some Classical Chinese flexibility. A speaker of Japanese or Korean will find the modifier-first rule entirely natural; a speaker of Mandarin (where postposed relative clauses are possible and common in speech) may need to adjust.

**Rhetorical effect**: because all modifying material precedes the head, Dan'a'yo sentences can build up a long stack of qualifications before the key element arrives. Complex ideas require patience from left edge to resolution point — but the payoff is absolute structural clarity once the head lands.

## Example Sentences

1. **Attributive construction** (from [[文法 - 05形態]]):
   捕已魚之男人 此処於在。
   *"The man who caught the fish is here."*
   Structure: [捕已魚] (caught-fish) → 之 → 男人 (man) → 此処於在 (is here)

2. **Determiner + noun:**
   此書 (this book), 其人 (that person), 毎日 (every day)
   All determiners precede without exception.

3. **Genitive:**
   天之道 — "the way of Heaven" (Heaven-LINK way)
   The possessor/qualifier 天 precedes the head 道.

4. **English (describing the rule):**
   In Dan'a'yo the rule is absolute — 修飾先行, modifier first, means that every attributive element, from a single adjective to an entire embedded clause, arrives before the word it qualifies.

## Sentiment

**Technical and definitional** — 修飾先行 is a grammatical rule, not a commendatory or critical phrase. Its register is precise and analytical. Within Dan'a'yo grammatical discourse it functions as authoritative: invoking the phrase closes a word-order question. Among learners it carries the same weight as any foundational rule of the language — not emotional but load-bearing. The phrase is also a quiet declaration of the language's design philosophy: clarity through regularity, structure through consistency.
