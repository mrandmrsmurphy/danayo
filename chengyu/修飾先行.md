## 修飾先行

### Readings
- Mandarin: xiū shì xiān xíng
- Cantonese: sau¹ sik¹ sin¹ hang⁴
- Japanese (hiragana): しゅうしょく せんこう
- Korean (Hangul): 수식선행 (su-sik seon-haeng)
- Vietnamese: tu sức tiên hành

### Literal structure
- 修飾: modification, attribution
- 先行: precede, go first

The phrase states a consistent ordering rule: modifiers come before what they modify.

### Interpretation
A head-final syntactic principle:

All modifiers precede their head, so structure builds leftward toward the main element.

### Operational meaning in Dan’a’yo
This governs phrase structure across categories:

1. **Nominal domain**  
    Determiners and quantifiers precede:
    - 此人 (this person)
    - 其書 (that book)
    - 毎 日 (every day)
    - 何 人 (which person)
    - 皆 民 (all people)
2. **Attributive constructions**  
    Clausal and phrasal modifiers precede the noun and are linked by **之**:
    - 捕 已 魚 之 男人  
        “the man [who caught the fish]”
    Here, the entire modifying clause comes before 男人, with 之 marking attribution.
3. **Generalized modifier-first rule**  
    Applies broadly:
    - adjectives before nouns
    - relative clauses before nouns
    - genitives before heads (A 之 B)
    - adverbials before verbs 
4. **Exceptions **
    - postpositions follow their complements
    - tense/aspect markers follow the verb
    These introduce limited right-branching elements within an otherwise head-final system.

## Example
- 捕已魚之男人 此処於在  
    “The man who caught the fish is here.”

Structure:
- [捕已魚] (caught fish) → modifier
- 之 → linker
- 男人 → head noun
- 此処於在 → predicate (“is at this place”)

### Underlying principles
- **Head-finality**: heads appear at the right edge of their phrase
- **Pre-nominal modification**: aligns with Classical Chinese and J/K patterns
- **Uniform parsing**: readers accumulate modifiers before resolving the head

### Effect
- High density of preposed information
- Strong predictability of structure
- Potential processing load shifted to the right edge (head resolution delayed)

### Concise definition
“A syntactic rule that all modifiers precede their heads, producing a predominantly head-final structure, with limited exceptions for postpositions and tense markers.”

### Note
The system is stricter than natural Classical Chinese, where word order can be more flexible. This is a deliberate regularization: 修飾先行 is elevated from tendency to _rule_.
