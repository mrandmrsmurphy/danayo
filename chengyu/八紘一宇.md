[[Misc. Chengyu]]
One of the earliest occurrences of “八纮” ([[八紘]]) is in _Liezi_ (《列子》), in the “Tang Wen / 汤问” chapter. The relevant passage:

> 汤又问：“物有巨细乎？ 有修短乎？ 有同异乎？”  
> 革曰：“渤海之东不知几亿万里，有大壑焉，实惟无底之谷，其下无底，名曰归墟。**八纮 九野之水**，天汉之流，莫不注之，而无增无减焉。”

**Literal meaning:** “The eight cords, one roof.”  
**Extended sense:** The idea that the world’s four quarters and eight directions are united under a single sovereign or order; later, a slogan for universal unity under imperial rule.

**Source:** From the **日本書紀** (720), in the passage:  
兼六合以開都、掩八紘一而為宇.

The phrase is embedded in early imperial mytho-historical narrative and later extracted as a compact ideological formula.

**Classical background:**  
六合 refers to the six directions, the four cardinal points plus above and below. 八紘 extends this spatial imagery, referring to the eight extremities of the world. 紘 originally denotes a cord or binding thread. 宇 means roof or eaves, and by extension, the space enclosed beneath them.

The compressed expression therefore imagines the entire world gathered beneath one roof. In its early literary setting, the language reflects cosmological kingship, not modern nationalism. It articulates a universalizing political theology common in East Asian imperial thought.

**Historical development:**  
In the twentieth century, 八紘一宇 was revived as an ideological slogan during the expansionist period of the Japanese state, especially in the Shōwa era. In that context it took on explicitly imperial and geopolitical meaning, far removed from its classical narrative origin. This later usage strongly colors contemporary perception of the phrase.

**Modern usage:**  
Today, 八紘一宇 appears primarily in historical discussion, especially in studies of Japanese political thought and wartime ideology. It is not used casually in everyday speech. Its tone is formal and historically charged.
