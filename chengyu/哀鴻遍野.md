## Literal Meaning
- [哀](characters/哀.md) — mournful, grieving, lamenting
- [鴻](characters/鴻.md) — wild goose; swan goose
- [遍 (char)](characters/遍%20(char).md) — everywhere, throughout, filling
- [野](characters/野.md) — wilderness, open country, the fields

Literally: "Mournful geese throughout the wilderness" — the open land is filled with the cries of grieving, wandering birds.

In classical Chinese poetry, wild geese (鴻雁) are the emblematic bird of displacement and longing: they fly in formation across vast distances, calling out, and their cry is the sound of separation. 哀鴻 — *mournful geese* — is a figure for displaced, lamenting people. 遍野 — *filling the wilderness* — conveys total scale: there is no corner of the open country not touched by this suffering.

## Extended Meaning
哀鴻遍野 describes a scene of mass, widespread suffering — specifically the kind produced by catastrophe: war, famine, flood, dynastic collapse — in which vast numbers of people have been driven from their homes and wander, destitute, across the land. It is not a figure for personal grief but for collective calamity at the scale of a population.

The idiom carries a specific visual and acoustic image: the cries of desperate people heard across the countryside, as pervasive as birdsong. It is used in formal and literary registers to characterize great historical disasters, and it implies both the scale (total, everywhere) and the sound (audible, not hidden) of suffering.

## Source and Origin
**《詩經·小雅·鴻雁》** (*Classic of Poetry*, Minor Odes, "Wild Geese"):

> 鴻雁于飛，哀鳴嗷嗷。
> 維此哲人，謂我劬勞。
> 維彼愚人，謂我宣驕。
>
> "The wild geese fly away, crying out in sorrow.
> The wise men say I am exhausted with toil;
> The foolish men say I am being arrogantly proud."

The poem 鴻雁 is a lament, traditionally interpreted as the complaint of displaced people — ministers exiled from court, or common people driven from their homes — who wander like migratory geese. The wild goose (鴻雁) is chosen because geese: migrate across vast distances, fly in formation (suggesting community and order now broken), and call out in voices that carry over open country. Their cry, 嗷嗷 (*áo'áo*, the sound of sustained wailing), becomes the voice of human suffering.

The fixed four-character form 哀鴻遍野 does not appear verbatim in the 詩經 but crystallized in later classical usage as an allusion to this poem. 哀鴻 condenses 哀鳴 ("mournful cries") + 鴻 (the bird species); 遍野 ("throughout the wilderness") extends the poem's scene to a totalized landscape of suffering. By the Tang and Song dynasties, the phrase was established vocabulary for any disaster that produced mass displacement.

The 詩經 is one of the Five Classics (五經), traditionally associated with Confucius's editorial work. The Minor Odes (小雅) include court songs, ceremonial hymns, and laments of officials — 鴻雁 is among the most emotionally direct.

## Cultural Notes
The wild goose is one of the richest symbols in classical East Asian poetry and art. In the Chinese tradition: geese migrate from north to south with the seasons, making them emblems of the passing of time and the separation of people from their homes; their formation flight suggests the loyalty and order of a community now broken; and their call — carrying clearly over open ground — is the sound of longing made audible. 鴻雁 specifically (the swan goose, *Anser cygnoides*) was associated in classical usage with long-distance communication, since messages were legendarily said to be carried by geese between separated lovers or officials.

哀鴻遍野 has been invoked throughout Chinese history to characterize each great catastrophe: the fall of dynasties, the Mongol conquests, the Taiping Rebellion (1850–64, which killed tens of millions), the famines and floods of the Republican era, the devastation of the Second World War and Civil War. The phrase is durable precisely because it is not attached to any single event — it names a pattern of human suffering that recurs.

In Japanese, あいこうへんや is a recognized classical allusion, appearing in historical and literary contexts, though less common in everyday speech than in Chinese. In Korean, 애홍편야 carries the same weight as a literary reference to collective suffering.

The pairing 哀鴻 / 遍野 has a structural elegance: the first half is sonic and avian (the birds and their cry), the second is spatial and total (all the land, everywhere). The image arrives in two beats — the sound, then the landscape — and the combination is more affecting than either alone.

## Example Sentences
1. 《詩經·小雅·鴻雁》:「鴻雁于飛，哀鳴嗷嗷。」
   "The wild geese fly away, crying out in sorrow." (The poem from which the idiom derives.)
2. 亂世之中，哀鴻遍野，難民流離失所，無以為歸。
   "In times of chaos, mournful wanderers fill the land — refugees displaced, with nowhere to return."
3. The chronicler described the aftermath of the Yellow River flood as 哀鴻遍野 — the plains emptied of farmers, the roads full of the dispossessed.

## Sentiment
Tragic and grave. 哀鴻遍野 is not casual hyperbole — it invokes the classical image of collective catastrophe and carries the weight of its source in the Book of Songs. It belongs to formal and literary registers; it is not used lightly or ironically. When it is deployed, the suffering it names is understood to be real, widespread, and deep.
