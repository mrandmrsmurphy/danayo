[[Biblical Chengyu]]

### Pronunciation
- **Mandarin**: jiā āi tòng chǎn
- **Cantonese**: gaa1 oi1 tung3 caan2
- **Japanese (hiragana)**: かあいつうさん
- **Korean (Hangul)**: 가애통산 (ga-ae-tong-san)
- **Vietnamese (Sino-Vietnamese)**: gia ai thống sản

### Literal Meaning
- **加** to add, increase
- **哀痛** sorrow and pain
- **産／產** childbirth, bringing forth

Literal: “To increase sorrow and pain in childbirth.”

### Figurative Meaning
Refers to the divine judgment upon Eve after the Fall: the intensification of pain and sorrow in childbearing. By extension, it may denote the burden of consequences following disobedience, especially those tied to family life, motherhood, or the created order.

### Scriptural Source
The phrase condenses **Genesis 3:16**:

> “I will greatly multiply thy sorrow and thy conception; in sorrow thou shalt bring forth children.”

Hebrew: _harbāh ’arbeh ‘itstebōnek_ — “multiplying I will multiply your pain.”

Traditional Chinese Bible translations render the verse with expressions such as “我必多多加增你懷胎的苦楚” or similar formulations emphasizing multiplication and pain. The chengyu form 加哀痛産 is a compact theological summary rather than a verbatim biblical quotation.

### Example Usage
> 人類背逆之後，始有加哀痛産之罰。  
> After humanity’s rebellion, there came the penalty of multiplied sorrow in childbirth.

### Cultural Notes
This idiom stands within the larger Christian doctrine of the Fall. It is not merely biological description but covenantal judgment within the Genesis narrative. Historically, Christian theology has interpreted this verse in several ways:
1. As literal physical pain in childbirth.
2. As encompassing emotional anguish in motherhood.
3. As part of a broader distortion of male–female relations introduced in Genesis 3.

In a Dan'a'yo context, the four-character form highlights the judicial aspect of the event. The verb 加 emphasizes intensification rather than creation *ex nihilo*; pain is multiplied, not invented. This aligns with the Hebrew emphatic construction.

### Tone and Sentiment
Solemn, judicial, tragic. It evokes the gravity of divine judgment while implying the broader narrative of fall and redemption.
