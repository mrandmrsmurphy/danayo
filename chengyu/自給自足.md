## Literal Meaning
- [自](characters/自.md) — self, oneself
- [給](characters/給.md) — supply, provide
- [自](characters/自.md) — self, oneself
- [足 (char)](characters/足%20(char).md) — sufficient, enough; foot

Literally: "Self-supply, self-sufficient" — supply yourself, and have enough from your own supply.

自給自足 follows the classical 自_自_ pattern: two parallel two-character compounds, each beginning with 自, that reinforce a single concept through repetition. 自給 (supply oneself) + 自足 (be sufficient from oneself) — the self both produces and is satisfied by what it produces. The structure emphasizes not just the act of self-provision but its completeness: nothing is lacking, nothing needs to come from outside.

## Extended Meaning
自給自足 describes a state of **complete self-sufficiency** — whether for an individual, a household, a community, a nation, or an economy. The entity in question produces all it consumes and consumes all it produces, with no net dependence on external supply.

The phrase applies at every scale:
- An individual who grows their own food, makes their own tools, and needs nothing from markets
- A village or commune that meets all its needs internally
- A nation pursuing **autarky** — economic independence from foreign supply chains, a policy goal in wartime or as an expression of sovereignty

The emotional register of 自給自足 is broadly positive — it evokes self-reliance, independence, and wholeness — though at the national-policy level it can also describe isolationist economic strategy with its associated inefficiencies.

## Source and Origin
自給自足 is a classical compound built from two established two-character words:
- **自給** (self-supply): appearing in classical texts in the sense of "sufficient to support oneself," as in administrative descriptions of households or territories that produce enough to sustain themselves without tax relief or redistribution
- **自足** (self-sufficiency, contentment): a concept with deep roots in both Confucian and Taoist thought — the Taoist 知足 (knowing sufficiency, being content with what one has) and the more general idea of 自得 (at ease in oneself, content with one's own condition)

The four-character compound 自給自足 fuses these two ideas into a single expression and became standard vocabulary in economic and agricultural discourse across the Sinosphere. It is used in modern Chinese, Japanese, and Korean with essentially identical meaning and range.

The phrase belongs to a family of 自_自_ chengyu built on the same pattern:
- [[自業自得]] — "one's deeds return as one's own fate; you reap what you sow"
- [[自由自在]] — "free and unconstrained"
- [[自暴自棄]] — "self-abandonment; giving up on oneself"

In each case the first 自 governs the first action and the second 自 receives its result — a rhetorical loop that emphasizes that the self is both cause and effect of its own condition.

## Cultural Notes
自給自足 carries different weight depending on historical context. In pre-modern agrarian East Asia, self-sufficiency was the default condition of most households and villages — to be 自給自足 was simply to be a functioning family farm. The phrase described normality rather than achievement.

In the 20th century, 自給自足 acquired political weight as governments pursued deliberate autarky policies. Japan's wartime economic mobilization, China's Great Leap Forward with its commune system, and North Korea's Juche (주체) ideology all invoked or required 自給自足 as a national goal. In these contexts the phrase describes not peaceful homesteading but the forced isolation of national economies from international trade — with consequences ranging from strategic resilience to catastrophic food shortage.

In contemporary usage, 自給自足 has been rehabilitated as an aspirational concept in sustainability discourse: food sovereignty, off-grid living, community-supported agriculture, and circular economies all invoke the self-sufficiency ideal. The homesteading revival in the United States and Europe, the 里山 (satoyama) sustainable agriculture movement in Japan, and the rural self-reliance movements across Asia all draw on the 自給自足 concept even when they do not use the phrase directly.

The Japanese じきゅうじそく is a standard four-character compound (四字熟語) with no particularly literary connotations — it is straightforwardly the economic and agricultural term. In Chinese, 自给自足 is similarly matter-of-fact. Neither language treats the phrase as especially elevated or poetic; it is useful and precise vocabulary.

## Example Sentences
1. 農家は米・野菜・果物を自分で作り、自給自足の生活を営んでいた。
   "The farming household grew its own rice, vegetables, and fruit, living a life of 自給自足."
2. 이 마을은 전쟁 중에도 자급자족으로 살아남았다.
   "This village survived even the war through 자급자족."
3. Complete 自給自足 is rarely achievable at scale — specialization and trade almost always produce more total goods than isolation, which is why autarkic economies tend toward scarcity.

## Sentiment
Broadly positive: 自給自足 evokes independence, wholeness, and resilience. The image of a household or community that needs nothing from outside carries the appeal of self-determination and freedom from dependency. In policy contexts the phrase is neutral-to-positive as a goal, though the historical record of forced national autarky tempers enthusiasm for it at the macro scale.
