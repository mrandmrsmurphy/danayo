<ruby>[今日安](/words/今日安.md)<rt>ㄍㄧㄇㄋㄧㄊ·ㄚㄋ</rt></ruby>!  Hello!  Welcome to Dan'a'yo, the pan-Asian language.  In this first less, we will learn the basic alphabet, those little squiggles above the complicated drawings.  This is a version of the _Bopomofo_ alphabet, in common use in Taiwan.

## Vowels
The easiest place to begin is with the six vowel letters, **a**, **e**, **i**, **o**, **u**, and **ǝ**.  These are able to stand alone, so we can write them with examples:
- ㄚ is /a/ as in "father".  Say to yourself, "Ah! A slingshot is aimed at me!"
- ㄝ is /e/ as in "bet".  Perhaps it is the edge of a bed.
- ㄧ is /i/ as in "ski".  It's a monitor with a flat line, making a beep.
- ㄛ is /o/ as in "coat".  I needed a hook to hang by coat on.
- ㄨ is /u/ as in "rune".  It's an x-shaped rune, it's true.
- ㄜ is /ǝ/ as in "butt".  Butt cuts suck.

If a syllable starts with one of these, but doesn't come first in a word, it is typical  to make it clear with a central dot just before it, i.e. ·ㄚ，·ㄝ，·ㄧ，·ㄛ，·ㄨ,·ㄜ。

## Semi-vowels
Most of these vowels have versions that can take an **on-glide**.  There are two on-glide: y and w.
### Y-Vowels
- **ya** is spelled ⼘
- **ye** is spelled ⼶
- **yo** is spelled ⼄
- **yu** is spelled ⼜
### W-Vowels
- **wa** is spelled ㆼ
- **we** is spelled ⼔
### Off-glides
In addition to on-glides, there are also off-glide.   Most combinations are possible, but not all actually occur.  If you've been paying attention, you already know the off-glide:
- **i** is ㄧ
	- **i** with an off-glide **i** is spelled ㄧㄜ/**iǝ**, like Mandarin ie.  It can be pronounced as **i** followed by just about anything that doesn't involve lip-rounding, pretty much.
	- The only glide of anything kind which ǝ can take is ㄧ off-glide.
- **u** is ㄨ
	- **eu** and **iu*** are not allowed.  
	- **u** with an off-glide of **u** is spelled ㄨㄛ/**uo**, like Mandarin uo.  It can be pronounced as **u** followed by just about anything that involves lip-rounded, pretty much

## Putting it all together
So, you should now be able to sound out the writing above the Chinese characters.  Each character has one and only one syllable for it's pronunciation, and it's always the same!  Syllables aren't allowed to effect each other, so don't say ㄋㄧㄋㄍㄚㄋ with a "ning".  Feel free to have a small pause between syllables to avoid this.

A syllable is, therefore, 
- Just one letter, if that letter is a vowel (ㄚ, ㄝ, ㄧ, ㄛ, ㄨ, ㄜ, ⼘, ㆼ, ⼶, ⼔, ⼄, or ⼜)
- Just two letters, if it is CV or VC 
- Maximally three letter, CVC
That description is a little bit cheating, because the final consonant can technically be another vowel, in the form of an off-glide.
