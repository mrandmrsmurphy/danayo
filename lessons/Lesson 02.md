Now that you are able to sound out words, it's time to tackle the 3000 kilogram elephant in the room: Chinese characters.  While there are 3310 of these in Dan'a'yo, that's actually all of them.  In Chinese and Japanese, these are open sets.  Technically any of the ~40,000 characters that haven been used over the last 4,000 years could appear at any time.  Our subset is limited in comparison.

The best way to get started is to learn some of the ancient categories for <ruby>[漢字](/words/漢字.md)<rt>ㄏㄚㄋㄐㄧ</rt></ruby>:
## Types
### Pictograms
Pictograms or 象形 are drawings of real like objects.  This is what most people think of when someone says <ruby>[漢字](/words/漢字.md)<rt>ㄏㄚㄋㄐㄧ</rt></ruby>.  However, they are a small minority of characters, around 300.
These 300 character, nevertheless, are some of the most essential in the language.  65 of them are in the most basic character set to learn first.  In turn, these are components in other character, the building blocks from which hundreds and hundreds of others will be built.

For example, 口 is picture of an open mouth, and means ... mouth!  It is then used to indicate "something to do with mouth" in a hose of characters, such as 吐 ("spit"), 咳 ("cough"), or 噫 ("belch").

### Symbols
Like modern emojis, 指事 or symbols are ancient drawings of ideas

- 上 is up
- 下 is down
- 三 is three
- 品 is articles

Again, many of these are very, very basic and super important.  There are about 55 of them.

### Compounds
会意 or compound-ideographs are two or more symbols put together to make a word.  This a very natural way to combine the previous two to extend the vocabulary:

- 休 “rest” = 人 (person) + 木 (tree)
- 明 “bright” = 日 (sun) + 月 (moon)
- 信 “trust” = 人 (person) + 言 (speech)

There are about 400 of these.  They are not all basic, and so need not all be tackled in the beginning.

### Phono-sematic compounds
形声 or phono-semantic compounds are the overwhelming majority of characters.  If something is not one of the previous three, it is this.  Show a Chinese person a random character, and they will say "it has something to do with x and it sounds something like y".  This is because part of the shape is for meaning, and part is for sound.

For example, suppose you encountered 凍 in the wild.  Eventually, you will learn to recognize the left two dots as the "ice radical", which are only there for semantic range.  The right side is something you will quickly learn is the word for "east", 東, which is pronounced ㄉㄛㄫ.  It turns out, this is the word for "freeze" and it is pronounced ㄉㄛㄫ!

## Radicals
Long ago, Chinese dictionaries were organized by radical, a set of 214 symbols which appear somewhere in every Chinese character (supposedly).  For example, 言 is the word for "say" and appears as a component in almost 100 characters. If you needed to look up 𧦅, you would (ideally) spot that radical 言, turn to that section of the dictionary, and then count the total number of strokes in 𧦅, which is 11.  In the 言, under heading 11, you would find your character

## SKIP
In 1999, _The Kodansha Kanji Learner's Dictionary_ came up with a superior method for organizing 漢字.  Every hanji is assigned a set of three numbers.  The first is how the character is organized.  A clear left-right division is 1.  A clear top-bottom division is 2.  A surrounding or wrap around components if 3.  Miscellaneous is 4.  The stroke count of the component parts are the next two numbers, for types 1 through 3.  For type 4, the stroke count is the second number, while the third is a classification system.

This system vastly speeds up searching for hanji and is highly recommended.
