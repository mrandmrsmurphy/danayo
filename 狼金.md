## Notes
**Form.** 狼金 is 狼 “wolf” + 金 “metal,” following the rule for transition elements.

**Etymological situation.** _Tungsten_ is split between two traditions:

- Swedish _tung sten_ “heavy stone” (the name _tungsten_)
- German _Wolfram_ (the source of the symbol **W**)

The German name comes from _wolf rahm_ (“wolf’s foam/soot”), referring to how the ore wolframite seemed to “devour” tin during smelting. This imagery, not the Swedish “heavy stone,” is what survives in the chemical symbol.

**Motivation for 狼.**  
Our choice follows the **symbolic lineage rather than the lexical one**:

- W → Wolfram → “wolf” → 狼

This is not a direct calque of _tungsten_, but a **semantic back-derivation from the symbol tradition**, which is itself historically grounded and widely recognized in chemistry.

**Type of formation.**  
A **symbol-driven semantic reconstruction**:

- modern symbol (W)
- traced to Wolfram
- reduced to its salient image (“wolf”)
- mapped to 狼

This is unusual but justified given the dual naming history.

**Fit within the system.**

1. Avoids collision with 重 (already used for barium), which would otherwise be the natural calque of “heavy stone.”
2. Provides a distinctive, high-frequency graph for symbol use.
3. Uses 金 appropriately for a d-block element.
4. Maintains a meaningful connection to international chemistry via the symbol W.

**Comparative CJKV forms.**

- Modern Chinese: 钨 (phonetic; historically 烏 “crow/black” also appears in compounds like 黑鎢)
- Japanese: タングステン (phonetic)
- Korean: 텅스텐 (phonetic)
- Vietnamese: vonfram / tungsten (mixed traditions)

Notably, Chinese **鎢** (“black metal”) reflects ore appearance rather than either Western etymology, showing that divergence from the original name is common.
